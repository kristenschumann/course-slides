Part 1

What do admissions committees need to see?

The three things that separate admits from waitlists.

0:30 - 0:45 | Before we talk about writing, we need to talk about what these committees are looking for. Not what they say in info sessions, but what they're scanning for when they read your essay at 10 PM with a stack of 40 more to go.

What Makes Essays Stand Out

Three Things That Matter

What drives you

Your values, your "why." Not what you've done, but what makes you do it. Stanford's Essay A lives here. So does HBS's growth question.

How you'll contribute

Show how your track record points to what you'll bring to campus. Wharton and Kellogg weight this heavily in their community essays.

Real-world impact

Not achievements on a resume. The change you've created and why it mattered. Booth and Columbia want to see this in your career vision.

0:45 - 2:15 | Three things. I tell every client this on our first call. Less than 1% of applicants nail all three in a single essay. Most people get one: they show what drives them, or they list their impact, or they talk about contribution. The ones who get into Stanford, HBS, Wharton? They find a way to weave all three into a single narrative. Think of it as a Venn diagram: your values, your contribution, your impact. The sweet spot is where all three overlap. That's your essay.

Part 2

How do you find the right tone?

Three steps to writing that sounds like a person, not a press release.

2:15 - 2:30 | OK, so you know what they want to see. The next question is how you say it. Tone is where most essays die. You can have the best story in the world and kill it with corporate language.

Effective Writing Style

Three Steps to the Right Voice

01

Bring people into your world

Don't talk about your life "in general." Write from your first-person POV. Don't lecture. Visualize. Say what it was like for you at that snapshot in time.

02

Write how you speak

Don't say "henceforth" or "thus." Make yourself clear with simple, loose language. Write with command, but without growing detached.

03

Reflect and re-center

The best essays don't simply tell what happened. They talk about how it shaped you, the lessons you drew, and how they shifted your values.

2:30 - 4:30 | Step one: bring people into your world. I don't want "I grew up in a diverse environment." I want the fluorescent lights in the hospital waiting room. I want the sound of your mother's pen on the clipboard. Put me there. Step two: write how you speak. Read your draft aloud. If it sounds like a LinkedIn post, rewrite it. "Henceforth" is not how you talk to your friends. "Thus" is not how you tell a story. Step three: reflect. This is what separates a good essay from a great one. Don't just tell me what happened. Tell me what you took from it. How did it shift the way you see the world? Stanford calls this "what matters most." HBS calls it "growth." Every school is asking the same question in different words.

The Golden Rule

Write like you're writing to a close friend. Put aside what you think they want to see.

4:30 - 5:30 | This is the single most important sentence in this entire video. Write like you're writing to a close friend. Not your boss. Not a committee. A friend. Someone who already knows you're smart, already knows your resume, and just wants to hear the real version. The moment you start writing for an imagined audience, trying to sound impressive, trying to hit the "right" keywords, you lose the thing that makes your essay yours. Put aside what you think they want to see. Show them who you are.

Authentic Voice

Sound Like Yourself

Resume Voice

"Leveraged cross-functional synergies to optimize operational efficiency across three business units, resulting in measurable improvements to stakeholder satisfaction."

Real Person Voice

"I called a meeting with engineering because our teams hadn't talked in three months. It was uncomfortable. It was also the most productive hour of my entire year."

5:30 - 6:30 | Here's what I mean. The left side? That could have been written by anyone. Or by ChatGPT. "Leveraged cross-functional synergies," no one talks like that. The right side? That's a person. You can see them. You can imagine being in that meeting. Here's my test: read your essay aloud to a friend. If any sentence makes you cringe, rewrite it. If it sounds like a corporate memo, rewrite it. Your voice, imperfections included, is the point. And I'll say this now: admissions committees are getting better at spotting AI-generated text. Your real voice is your best defense.

6:45 - 8:30 | This is adapted from a Stanford GSB "What matters most" essay. Look at the green highlight, the hook. "The fluorescent lights in the hospital waiting room made everything look slightly green." Twelve words and you're in the room. You can see the lights. You can feel the plastic chair. What matters is understanding why it works.

Evolution of an Essay

From Thesis Statement to World You Can Step Into

Early Draft

"What matters most to me is resilience. I overcame a difficult childhood, excelled in college despite financial hardship, and thrived at a demanding consulting firm."

Evolved Draft

"The fluorescent lights in the hospital waiting room made everything look slightly green. My mother sat next to me filling out insurance paperwork, and I watched her do math in the margins, figuring out what we could afford."

8:30 - 10:00 | Same applicant. Same story. Completely different impact. The early draft is a thesis statement: "What matters most to me is resilience." It then lists three examples in rapid succession. The committee finishes it and knows the applicant's argument but nothing about the applicant as a person. The evolved draft? You're in the waiting room. You can see the pen moving across the margins. That's the difference between telling someone your point and building a world they can step into. I see this with every client. First draft is always a thesis. My job is to find the scene underneath it. Your job is to do the same thing before you submit.

Common Trap

Admissions committees can tell when someone else wrote your essay.

Your voice, imperfections included, is the point.

Avoid AI tools for anything other than basic spelling and grammar.

10:00 - 10:45 | I need to say this directly. Every admissions office I've spoken to in the past year has mentioned AI-generated essays. They can tell. The writing is too smooth, too generic, too perfectly structured. It reads like a very competent stranger wrote it, because a very competent stranger did. Use AI for spell-check. Use it to catch typos. But the essay itself has to be yours. Your awkward phrasing, your specific references, your voice. That's what they're evaluating. If you hand that over to a tool, you've given away the one thing that makes your application different from the other 7,999.

Watch Out

Three Mistakes That Sink Essays

1

Listing achievements instead of showing judgment

They have your resume. Show what you chose, what you gave up, and what you learned when it didn't go as planned.

2

Submitting the same essay to every school

HBS tests leadership. Stanford tests values. Wharton tests community. Booth tests intellectual curiosity. Same essay won't work.

3

Polishing until your voice disappears

The tenth revision is often worse than the third. Keep the version that sounds like you.

10:45 - 12:30 | Three mistakes I see every single cycle. First: listing achievements instead of showing judgment. "I delivered a $5M project on time and under budget." That's your resume. What the committee wants to know is: what did you decide? What trade-off did you make? What did you learn when it went sideways? Second: submitting the same essay everywhere. I can't say this enough. HBS wants business-mindedness and leadership under pressure. Stanford wants your core values. Wharton wants to see how you'll contribute to the community. Booth wants intellectual curiosity. An essay that works for one will fail at another. You need to research each school's criteria and tailor your approach. Third, and this one surprises people, polishing until your voice disappears. I've seen clients revise ten times and the essay gets worse. The seventh draft sounds corporate. The third draft sounded like them. Keep the version that sounds like you.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Your essays are the only place to be a person, not a data point. Make them count.
  • Write like you're talking to a close friend. Bring people into your world with specific moments, not bullet points in prose.
  • Reflect before you draft. If you can't state your point in one sentence, you're not ready to write.
12:30 - 13:30 | Three things to walk away with. One: your essays are the only place in the application where you get to be a person. Your GMAT is a number. Your GPA is a number. Your resume is a list. The essay is where the committee hears your voice. Don't waste it writing another resume in paragraph form. Two: write like you're talking to a close friend. That means specific moments, not general claims. The fluorescent lights, the meeting you called, the conversation with your mother. Put me there. Three: reflect before you draft. Write your ending first. If you can't state the point of your essay in one sentence, you need more thinking time, not more writing time.
