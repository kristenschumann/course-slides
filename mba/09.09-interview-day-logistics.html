Virtual Interviews

Technical Setup Checklist

  • Test everything early: Camera, microphone, internet, and the platform itself.
  • Do a practice call: Use the same platform with a friend to test audio and video.
  • Have a backup plan: Keep your phone charged in case you need to switch to a call.
  • Keep materials handy: Resume visible, school info accessible, but don't read from notes.
1:00 - 2:15 | Most interviews now have a virtual option. Test everything at least a day before: camera, microphone, internet, and the platform. Do a practice call with a friend on the same platform. Have your phone charged and ready as a backup. Keep your resume visible and school info accessible, but don't read from notes. Know how to reach your interviewer if something breaks.

Virtual Interviews

Setting Up Your Space

01

Quiet Environment

No interruptions for 60-90 minutes. Phone on silent, tabs closed, roommates notified.

02

Clean Background

Neutral, professional setting. Good lighting facing you. Camera at eye level.

03

Professional Attire

Dress fully as you would in person. Even unseen attire helps your mindset.

2:15 - 3:15 | Three things for your space. First, a quiet environment with no interruptions. Tell roommates or family you need 60-90 minutes. Phone on silent, unnecessary tabs closed. Second, a clean, professional background with good lighting facing you and camera at eye level. Use books to raise your laptop if needed. Third, dress fully in professional attire. Even if they only see you from the waist up, dressing completely helps your mindset.

3:15 - 4:30 | For in-person interviews, plan to arrive 10-15 minutes early. Factor in traffic, parking, and transit delays. The Day Before

Set Yourself Up for Success

Do

  • Confirm interview time and format
  • Test all technology (virtual)
  • Research your interviewer on LinkedIn
  • Review key stories and talking points
  • Prepare your outfit and get good sleep

Don't

  • Cram new information at the last minute
  • Stay up late obsessing over details
  • Drink excessively the night before
  • Skip the mental prep: visualize success
4:30 - 5:30 | The day before, confirm the interview time and format. Test all technology for virtual interviews. Research your interviewer on LinkedIn if you know who it is. Review your key stories, prepare your outfit, and get good sleep. What not to do: don't cram new information, you're as prepared as you're going to be. Don't stay up late obsessing. Don't drink excessively. And don't skip the mental prep. Visualize the interview going well and remind yourself what you want the interviewer to remember about you.

Interview Day

Staying Calm and Focused

Eat something, limit caffeine, and do whatever calms you: walk, music, meditation. Review your key points one more time, then stop preparing and start trusting.

During the conversation: take a breath before speaking, smile, listen actively, and pause when you need to. It's a conversation, not a performance.

5:30 - 6:15 | On interview day, eat something so hunger doesn't distract you. Avoid excessive caffeine if it makes you jittery. Do whatever helps you feel calm. Review your key points one more time, then stop preparing. During the interview, take a breath before you speak, smile, show positive energy. It's okay to pause before answering. Listen actively. If you don't understand a question, ask for clarification. This is a conversation, not a performance.

Handling Problems

When Things Go Wrong

Tech Issues (Virtual)

Stay calm, acknowledge the issue, and suggest switching to phone or rescheduling. Schools understand.

Running Late

Call immediately with your ETA. Apologize briefly on arrival, then move on. Don't dwell on it.

Blanking on a Question

"Let me think about that for a moment." A brief pause is always better than rambling.

Feeling Overwhelmed

Ask for a moment to collect your thoughts. Take three slow breaths. Interviewers respect composure.

6:15 - 7:15 | Things will go wrong sometimes. For tech issues, stay calm, acknowledge it, suggest switching to phone or rescheduling. Schools are understanding. If running late, call immediately with your ETA. Apologize briefly and move on. If you blank on a question, say "Let me think about that for a moment." A pause is better than rambling. If you feel overwhelmed, it's perfectly fine to ask for a moment to collect your thoughts. Take three slow breaths. Interviewers would rather you settle and give a strong answer than push through in distress.

After the Interview

Your Next Three Steps

  • Thank the interviewer before leaving or hanging up.
  • Take notes while it's fresh, then give yourself a break before analyzing.
  • Send a thank-you email within 24 hours, referencing something specific.

Don't over-analyze. You often can't tell how it went from the inside.

7:15 - 7:45 | After the interview, three things. Thank the interviewer before you leave or hang up. Take a few notes while the conversation is fresh, then give yourself a break. Don't immediately analyze everything. And send a thank-you email within 24 hours. We cover the exact structure and tone of thank-you notes in the Post-Interview Communication video. The key principle: keep it brief, reference something specific, and reaffirm your enthusiasm.
