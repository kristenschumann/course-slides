Assumptions and
the Negation Test

0:00 - 0:05 | Title slide. This video covers how to identify assumptions and use the negation test to verify answers.

The Challenge

The trickiest CR question type

Assumption questions ask you to find something the argument takes for granted. The answer isn't stated anywhere. But there's a powerful technique to verify your choice.

Key Idea

The negation test: If you negate the correct answer, the argument should fall apart. If the argument still works after negation, the answer is wrong.

0:05 - 0:30 | Set up the challenge. Assumption questions are the trickiest because the answer is unstated. Introduce the negation test as a reliable technique for verifying answers.

Definition

What is an assumption?

An assumption is an unstated premise the argument depends on. It's the invisible support holding premises and conclusion together.

Example: "Company X's profits increased this quarter, so the new CEO must be doing a good job."

Assumption: The CEO's actions caused the profit increase, not market conditions, prior decisions, or luck.

Analogy

Premises are the bricks, the conclusion is the roof, and the assumption is the invisible support. If the assumption is false, the roof collapses.

0:30 - 2:00 | Define assumptions with the bricks/roof/support analogy. Walk through the CEO example: the stated premise is "profits increased" but the assumption is that the CEO caused it.

Argument Still Works

2:00 - 4:00 | Teach the three-step negation test. Walk through the CEO example: negate "The CEO's decisions significantly affected profits" to get "The CEO's decisions did NOT significantly affect profits." Does the argument still work? No. So this is the assumption.

Example

Applying the negation test

Argument: "Reading improves vocabulary, so students should read more to do better on standardized tests."

Choice A (Wrong)

"Reading is the only way to improve vocabulary."

Negate: "Reading is NOT the only way."

Argument still works. Reading can still help even if other methods also work. Not the assumption.

Choice B (Correct)

"Better vocabulary improves test performance."

Negate: "Better vocabulary does NOT improve test performance."

Argument collapses. If vocabulary doesn't help tests, reading more is pointless. This IS the assumption.

4:00 - 5:30 | Walk through the reading/vocabulary example. Choice A fails the negation test because the argument works without it. Choice B passes because negating it destroys the argument.

Negation Technique

How to negate properly

The negation test only works if you negate correctly. Here are the rules:

Statement Type Original Correct Negation
Simple All dogs bark Not all dogs bark (some don't)
Qualified Most X are Y It is not the case that most X are Y
Compound (A and B) A and B are true Not A or not B (De Morgan's)

Key trap: Negating "all" is "not all" (some aren't), NOT "none." Students who negate too strongly will reject correct answers.

5:30 - 7:00 | Teach proper negation. The biggest mistake: students negate "all" as "none" instead of "not all." Show the three patterns. Simple: add "not" or flip the quantifier. Qualified: prefix with "it is not the case that." Compound: use De Morgan's law. Emphasize the trap: negating too strongly leads to rejecting correct answers.

Negation Technique

Quick negation practice

1

"The drug is safe for all patients."

Negate: "The drug is not safe for all patients." (Some patients may be at risk.)

2

"Cost is the primary factor in the decision."

Negate: "Cost is not the primary factor in the decision." (Something else matters more.)

3

"The program improves skills and reduces turnover."

Negate: "The program does not improve skills or does not reduce turnover." (At least one fails.)

7:00 - 8:00 | Three quick practice negations. Walk through each one. Number 1: "safe for all" becomes "not safe for all" meaning some patients may be at risk. Number 2: straightforward "not" insertion. Number 3: De Morgan's law. "A and B" negates to "not A or not B." Only one needs to fail for the negation to hold.

Part 2

Assumption Types and Traps

5:30 - 5:35 | Section divider. Now cover common assumption patterns and wrong answer traps.

Patterns

4 common assumption types

No Interference

Nothing else is causing the effect. "The drug cured patients" assumes no other treatment or natural recovery occurred.

Representative Sample

Evidence from one group applies to the conclusion's group. College students may not represent the general population.

Valid Comparison

Two things being compared are similar in relevant ways. "Copy City B's policy" assumes the cities are comparable enough.

Clear Terms

Words mean what they seem. "Customer satisfaction increased" assumes satisfaction is measured meaningfully.

5:35 - 7:00 | Walk through the four common assumption types. These correspond to the gap types from Video 5.19. No interference is the most common. Representative sample appears in study-based arguments.

Watch Out

Wrong answer traps

Too Strong

An answer that would help the argument but isn't required. "Only this factor matters" when the argument just needs "this factor matters somewhat."

Irrelevant

An answer about something the argument doesn't actually depend on. It may sound related but doesn't connect to the gap.

Reversed

"B causes A" when the argument is about "A causes B." The direction of the relationship is wrong.

Wrong Target

Supports a premise rather than connecting premises to conclusion. It strengthens evidence but doesn't bridge the gap.

7:00 - 8:00 | Walk through the four wrong answer traps. "Too strong" is the most common: the answer goes beyond what the argument requires. "Reversed" is subtle and catches students who identify the right concepts but mix up the direction.

Distinction

Necessary vs. sufficient assumptions

Necessary Assumption

Must be true for the argument to work. Without it, the argument fails.

This is the minimum requirement. It doesn't prove the conclusion, it just keeps the argument from falling apart.

Sufficient Assumption

Completely proves the conclusion. More than the argument requires.

This would make the conclusion definitely true, not just possible. GRE rarely asks for these.

Key Idea

GRE assumption questions typically ask for necessary assumptions. The negation test finds necessary assumptions.

8:00 - 9:00 | Distinguish necessary from sufficient. Most GRE assumption questions ask for necessary assumptions. A necessary assumption is the floor: without it, the argument collapses. A sufficient assumption would prove the conclusion outright.

Practice

Find the assumption

Argument: Because the new software is more efficient than the old software, the company should switch to the new software immediately.

The argument assumes which of the following?

(A) The new software is less expensive than the old software.

(B) Efficiency is an important factor in software selection.

(C) The company has the resources and ability to switch software.

(D) Employees prefer the new software.

(E) The old software is outdated.

~1 min | Read the argument aloud, then the question and choices. Give the viewer a moment to think. Encourage them to pause and try the negation test on their top pick before moving on.

Practice

Negation test walkthrough

Correct: (C)

Negate C: "The company does NOT have the resources or ability to switch software."

If the company can't switch, the conclusion — "should switch immediately" — collapses entirely. You can't switch if you can't switch.

Why not (A)? Negate: "New software is NOT less expensive." The argument is about efficiency, not cost — still works.

Why not (B)? Negate: "Efficiency is NOT an important factor in software selection." Seems damaging, but the premise already establishes efficiency as the reason. The argument doesn't depend on efficiency being important in general — it assumes the company CAN switch. (B) is baked into the premise, not a hidden assumption.

Why not (D)? Negate: "Employees do NOT prefer the new software." Preference isn't the argument's basis — still works.

Why not (E)? Negate: "The old software is NOT outdated." The argument never claims it is — still works.

Strategy

The Negation Test is your verification tool. Always negate your answer choice and ask: "Does the argument fall apart?" If yes, that's your assumption.

~1.5 min | Reveal the answer: C. Walk through the negation of C and show how the argument collapses. Then quickly show why A, B, D, and E fail the negation test — the argument survives without them. For B, note that efficiency is already stated in the premise so it's not a hidden assumption. Reinforce the strategy: negate, check for collapse.

Key Takeaways

Assumptions and the negation test

  • Assumptions are unstated premises - the invisible support connecting evidence to the conclusion, without which the argument fails
  • Use the negation test to verify - negate the answer choice and check if the argument collapses or still works
  • Argument collapses = correct assumption - argument still works = wrong answer, the argument doesn't depend on it
  • Watch for "too strong" answers - the correct assumption is the minimum requirement, not an answer that would prove the conclusion entirely
  • The negation test finds necessary assumptions - use it to confirm your top choice or decide between two close options
9:00 - 10:00 | Summary slide. The negation test is the most reliable tool for assumption questions. Negate the answer. If the argument falls apart, you've found the assumption. When stuck between two choices, negate both.
