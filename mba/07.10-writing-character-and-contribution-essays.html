What Committees Want

Three Qualities They Evaluate

Self-Awareness

Can you honestly assess your role, including what you did wrong? Reflection matters more than performance.

Growth

What did you learn? How did you change? They want to see evolution, not stories where you were already perfect.

Impact on Others

How did your actions affect the people around you? This is the "contribution" part most applicants miss.

1:00 - 2:15 | These essays are the most behavioral type you'll encounter. Schools evaluate three things. Self-awareness: can you honestly assess your role, including mistakes? Reflection matters more than performance. Growth: what did you learn and how did you change? They don't want stories where you were already perfect. Impact on others: almost every character prompt has a relational dimension. It's not just about what happened to you, it's about how your actions affected those around you. This is the contribution part many applicants miss entirely.

Selecting Stories

Choose Revealing Over Impressive

  • Growth over glory: The messy middle beats the highlight reel every time.
  • Specificity over scope: One meaningful conversation outweighs a 200-person initiative.
  • Recent over distant: Draw from the last 2-3 years of your professional life.
2:15 - 3:30 | Story selection is where most applicants go wrong. They pick the most impressive story rather than the most revealing one. Three principles. Growth over glory: the story where the team was falling apart and you course-corrected is far better than the story where everything went perfectly. Specificity over scope: a story about one meaningful conversation with a struggling team member is more powerful than leading a huge initiative. Recent over distant: draw from the last 2-3 years. Schools are admitting the current version of you.

Selecting Stories

The Story Selection Test

Shows Hidden Character

Does this reveal something about you that isn't obvious from your resume?

Demonstrates Growth

Did you change or grow because of this experience?

Feels Authentically You

Would someone who knows you well recognize this as genuinely "you"?

All Three? Strong Story

If you answer yes to all three questions, you have found a winning essay topic.

3:30 - 4:30 | Here's a simple test for any story you're considering. Ask three questions. Does this show something about my character that isn't obvious from my resume? Did I change or grow because of this experience? Would someone who knows me well recognize this story as genuinely me? If you answer yes to all three, you've found a strong story. If not, keep looking.

Writing About Failure

How to own it, show growth, and avoid common traps

4:30 - 4:35 | Now let's talk about the essay type that terrifies most applicants: the failure essay.

Failure Essays

The Four-Step Framework

1

Own It

No hedging or passive voice. "I made a mistake."

→
2

Show Impact

What actually happened? Don't minimize consequences.

→
3

Explain Learning

Be specific about what changed in how you think or act

→
4

Prove the Change

Show a later moment where you acted differently

4:35 - 6:00 | Here's the framework that works. Step one: own it clearly. No hedging, no passive voice. "I made a mistake" is more powerful than "mistakes were made." Step two: show the impact honestly. If people were hurt or set back, say so. Minimizing consequences suggests you haven't fully processed it. Step three: explain what you learned, specifically. Not "I learned to communicate better" but "I learned that pushing forward without checking in creates a disconnect that undermines my goals." Step four: show the change in action. Show a subsequent moment where you did things differently. This proves the growth was real.

Failure Essays

Common Traps to Avoid

Trap What It Looks Like Why It Fails
Humble brag "My biggest failure was working too hard" It's a disguised strength, not a failure
Blame shift "Leadership didn't give us resources" Focus on your role, not others' shortcomings
Ancient history "When I was a sophomore in college..." Doesn't reflect who you are now
Overdrama Making a small setback sound catastrophic Authenticity means being proportionate
6:00 - 7:15 | Four traps to avoid. The humble brag: "my biggest failure was working too hard" is not a failure, it's a disguised strength. Readers see through this immediately. The blame shift: even if leadership did fail you, the essay should focus on your role and your learning. What could you have done differently within your constraints? Ancient history: unless you're a very recent graduate, draw from your professional life. And overdrama: you don't need to make it sound worse than it was. A small failure that taught you a meaningful lesson is perfectly valid.

Leadership Essays

Show, Don't Label

Telling

"I am a natural leader who inspires teams to excel."

Requires the reader to take your word for it. Generic and forgettable.

Showing

"When three team members threatened to quit during our tightest deadline, I spent an evening calling each of them individually."

Lets the reader see your leadership in action. Specific and memorable.

Focus on how you lead, not that you lead. Include others' words about your leadership for added credibility.

7:15 - 8:30 | Leadership essays are a trap. Too modest and you sound passive. Too bold and you sound arrogant. The sweet spot is showing through action and impact. Compare these two examples. Telling: "I am a natural leader who inspires teams to excel." Showing: "When three team members threatened to quit during our tightest deadline, I spent an evening calling each individually to understand what was going wrong." The first requires you to take the writer's word for it. The second lets you see it. Focus on how you lead, not that you lead. And include other people's words about your leadership. Someone else's assessment carries more weight than your own.

Teamwork Essays

Zoom In on Your Specific Role

  • Set up the team: Who was involved, what was the goal, and what was the tension?
  • Your specific contribution: Not your title, but what you actually did to change the dynamic.
  • How it affected the group: Did collaboration change? Did relationships shift?
  • Your insight about teamwork: Reveal what you learned about working with others.
8:30 - 9:45 | Several schools ask specifically about teamwork, collaboration, or community contribution. The biggest mistake is describing what the team did without clarifying what you contributed. "We launched the product and exceeded targets" tells the reader nothing about you. Instead, use this framework. Set up the team and the challenge. Identify your specific role and contribution, not your title but your actual impact. Show how your contribution affected the group dynamic. And reflect on what you learned about working with others. For contribution prompts from Wharton, Fuqua, Darden, and Cornell, schools want to see that you're a giver, not just a taker.

Writing Process

Start From the Inside Out

Brainstorm 8-10 stories, map them to prompts, check for variety across your application, then write each draft starting with the specific moment, not the background.

Most prompts are 200-500 words. Cut ruthlessly. Every sentence must earn its place. After drafting, ask: could someone else have written this? If yes, it needs more of you.

9:45 - 10:45 | Here's the writing process. Step one: brainstorm 8 to 10 stories involving challenge, growth, failure, leadership, or contribution. Step two: map stories to your specific school prompts. Some stories work for multiple schools with different angles. Step three: check for variety. If every essay is about work leadership, you're one-dimensional. Mix in community, personal growth, relationships. Step four: write each draft from the inside out. Start with the specific moment, the scene, conversation, or decision. Build outward to context and reflection. Don't start with background. And step five: cut ruthlessly. Most prompts are 200-500 words. You don't have room for preamble. After drafting, ask yourself: could someone else have written this? If yes, add more of your specific perspective and voice.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Choose growth over glory when selecting stories
  • Own failure completely with no hedging or blame-shifting
  • Show leadership through action, not self-applied labels
  • Zoom in on your specific role in team stories
  • Match your story to each school's values and culture
  • Be specific, be recent, and be honest above all
10:45 - 12:00 | Key takeaways. Choose growth over glory. Committees want to see how you learn, not just how you win. Own failure completely. No hedging, no blame-shifting, no humble-bragging. Show leadership through action. Stop telling people you're a leader and show them what you do. Zoom in on your specific role in team stories. Your contribution matters, not the team's result. Match your story to the school's values. HBS wants you to invest in others. Kellogg wants values-driven decisions. Columbia wants inclusion. And above all: be specific, be recent, and be honest. These essays reveal who you are when it counts. Make sure the person on the page is the real you.
