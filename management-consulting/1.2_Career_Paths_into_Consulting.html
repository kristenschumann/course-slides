02

Compensation

$90K-$110K base salary, $100K-$125K total comp

03

Program Length

0:30 - 1:30 | If you're in college or recently graduated, this is your entry point. Base salary ranges from $90K to $110K, with total comp around $100K to $125K. This is a 2-3 year program. Most analysts are "up or out" when that clock runs. Recruiting is almost entirely school-driven. Target schools get on-campus interviews.

Undergrad Path Trade-Offs

Advantages

  • Elite training at 22 with world-class problem-solving frameworks
  • Exceptional exit opportunities into PE, tech, startups, corporate strategy
  • Doors stay open for a decade after two years at MBB

Disadvantages

  • "Up or out" pressure with a 2-3 year expiration date on your role
  • Limited strategic ownership as the real work happens above you
  • Target school advantage makes non-target recruiting significantly harder
1:30 - 2:00 | The upside: you get elite training at 22. Exit opportunities are absurd. Two years at McKinsey opens doors that stay open for a decade. The downside: you're learning, not leading. The real strategic work happens above you. And the 2-3 year expiration date is real.

The MBA Path

01

Role Title

Associate at all MBB firms, with path to partner track

02

Base Salary

$190K-$192K base with $30K signing bonus

03

Total Comp

$260K-$285K first year including performance bonus

2:00 - 3:30 | The MBA path is the default into consulting. You enter as an Associate with a base of $190K to $192K. Add a $30K signing bonus and $40K to $63K performance bonus, and total first-year comp hits $260K to $285K. Recruiting is locked to your school's schedule. M7 programs dominate MBB placement. This is the most common path onto the partner track.

The Experienced Hire Path

01

Entry Levels

Associate (3-5 yrs) to Engagement Manager (7+ yrs) depending on background

02

Recruiting

Rolling deadlines year-round with no structured calendar

03

Requirements

3+ years of relevant industry experience, airtight case prep

3:30 - 4:30 | The hardest path and the least understood. No structured recruiting calendar. Firms hire laterally year-round on a rolling basis. No deadline means no urgency. No cohort means no built-in support. Your entry level depends on your experience. You need to position your non-consulting background as directly relevant to the firm's practice areas.

Structured vs. Lateral Recruiting

Structured Paths (UG + MBA)
  • Fixed recruiting calendar with clear deadlines
  • Cohort-based onboarding and training
  • School brand provides built-in credibility
  • Standardized entry level and compensation
Experienced Hire
  • Rolling applications with no fixed deadlines
  • No cohort or structured support network
  • Must position industry expertise as relevant
  • Entry level varies based on background
4:30 - 5:00 | The key difference between structured paths and experienced hire: structured paths give you a calendar, a cohort, and school-driven credibility. Experienced hires have flexibility but face fierce per-slot competition with no built-in support network. If you're taking this path, case prep needs to be airtight.

Career Ladder at MBB

Years 0-3

Entry Level

BA/Associate: learn frameworks, execute analysis, build core skills

Years 3-7

Mid-Level

Associate to EM/PL: run workstreams, manage teams, own client relationships

Years 7+

Senior Leadership

AP/Principal to Partner: drive firm strategy, $500K+ base, $1M+ total

5:00 - 5:30 | Once you're in, the ladder is the same regardless of entry path. At McKinsey: BA, Associate, Engagement Manager, Associate Partner, Partner, Senior Partner. At BCG and Bain: Associate, Consultant, Project Leader, Principal, Partner. The money scales fast. EMs earn $200K-$270K base, up to $350K total. Partners clear $500K base and over $1M total.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

Your path determines your entry point, not your ceiling. An undergrad analyst and an MBA associate can both make partner.

The undergrad path gives you optionality early. The MBA path gives you higher comp and a faster track to leadership.

The experienced hire path is viable but unforgiving. Position yourself as someone who fills a gap the firm cannot fill with a fresh MBA.

Know the numbers. Understanding the actual progression and economics signals you have done your homework.

6:00 - 7:00 | Four takeaways. First: your path determines entry point, not ceiling. Second: think about what you want at 25 versus 30. Third: experienced hire path needs specific positioning, not generic transition stories. Fourth: know the numbers and show you understand the progression when speaking with firms.
