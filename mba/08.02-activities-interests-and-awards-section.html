Activities, Interests,
and Awards

0:00 - 0:30 | The activities, interests, and awards section doesn't get as much attention as essays or test scores. It probably won't make or break your candidacy on its own. But it can reinforce your narrative, show dimensionality, and give the admissions committee a sense of who you are beyond work. Let me show you how to approach this section strategically.

MBA Admissions Course

Why This Section Matters

Rounds Out Your Story

Shows who you are outside of work. Your essays show the professional you; this adds dimension.

Supports Your Narrative

If you claim to care about education access, your activities should provide evidence for that claim.

Signals Campus Fit

Schools want students who will join clubs, participate in events, and build community.

0:30 - 1:30 | Three reasons this section matters. First, it rounds out your story. Your essays and resume show your professional self. This section shows what you do with your time outside of work. Second, it supports your narrative. If your essays emphasize a passion, your activities should provide evidence. A disconnect here is a red flag. Third, it signals campus fit. Schools want students who will be involved on campus. If you've been active in undergrad or your community, that suggests you'll be involved in business school too.

Three Themes to Cover

  • Desire to give back: Volunteering, nonprofit work, mentoring, community organizing
  • Desire to be involved: Professional associations, alumni groups, clubs, campus activities
  • Diversity of perspective: Unique hobbies, creative pursuits, athletic achievements, cultural activities

Cover 2-3 of these themes across your activity slots. Each entry should reveal something different about you.

1:30 - 2:45 | Most applications give you limited slots with tight character limits. You can't include everything, so cover 2 to 3 of these themes. First, desire to give back through volunteering, nonprofit board membership, community organizing, or mentoring. Second, desire to be involved through professional associations, alumni groups, or campus activities. Third, diversity of perspective through unique hobbies, creative pursuits, athletic achievements, or cultural activities. Each slot should reveal something different about you that isn't shown elsewhere in the application.

Show Impact, Not Just Activity

Weak Description Strong Description
"Volunteer at local food bank" "Led 15 volunteers in redesigning distribution, reducing wait times 40%"
"Member of marketing club" "Organized quarterly events averaging 150 attendees, grew membership 40%"
"Enjoy baking" "Completed 6-month Escoffier pastry program, now supply a local coffee shop weekly"

Lead with impact, use numbers, skip obvious context, and use action verbs. Draft in a separate document, not the tiny application text box.

2:45 - 4:15 | Character limits are tight, so every word matters. Look at the difference between weak and strong descriptions. "Volunteer at local food bank" tells nothing. "Led 15 volunteers in redesigning distribution, reducing wait times 40%" shows leadership and quantified impact. The pattern is always the same: lead with what you accomplished, include numbers, skip obvious context, and use action verbs. Led, organized, launched, increased, developed. Draft in a separate document and edit ruthlessly before pasting into the application.

Awards Are Broader Than You Think

Make a Big List

  • Dean's list, scholarships, honors societies
  • Work awards, top performer, promotions
  • Competition wins (case comps, hackathons)
  • Certifications and selective programs
  • Athletic or personal milestones

What Each Represents

  • Dean's list = intellectual capability
  • Case comp winner = strategic thinking
  • Top performer = high execution standards
  • Marathon finisher = discipline and grit
  • Choose variety across strengths

Don't leave this blank. Even without traditional awards, you have achievements. Think creatively about what qualifies.

4:15 - 5:30 | Awards stress people out. "I haven't won anything!" But awards and honors are broader than trophies. Start by making a big list of everything you're proud of: academic honors, work recognition, competition wins, certifications, anything selective. Then think about what each represents. The committee doesn't care about the award itself, they care what it says about you. Dean's list shows intellectual capability. A case competition win shows strategic thinking. Top performer at work shows high standards. Pick awards that represent different strengths. And don't leave it blank. Completing a marathon is an achievement. Getting into a competitive program is an achievement.

Specific Interests Stand Out

"Reading" is forgettable. "Working through the Harvard Classics, currently on volume 14" is memorable. Specific interests give interviewers something to ask about and make you human.

Your interests don't need to be expensive or exotic. They need to be specific enough that someone can picture you doing them. But be honest. Don't invent interests you don't have.

5:30 - 6:30 | The interests portion is your chance to be memorable. Generic interests like reading, travel, and fitness tell nothing. Specific interests create conversation. "Completed 5 ultramarathons across 3 continents." "Building a YouTube channel about Korean street food with 15K subscribers." "Competitive amateur chess, rated 1800 FIDE." The pattern is always the same: turn a generic interest into a specific one. Instead of cooking, say hosting monthly dinners featuring cuisines from countries you've visited. Instead of fitness, say trained for and completed your first Olympic triathlon at age 35. But be honest. The point is to present what genuinely interests you in a compelling way, not to invent interests you don't have.

Five Mistakes to Avoid

Treating It as an Afterthought

This section is small but not insignificant. Give it real thought and time.

Disconnecting from Narrative

If activities contradict your essays, that's a red flag. Ensure consistency.

Listing Without Impact

"Member of club" tells nothing. Show what you did and what resulted.

Padding with Weak Items

Three strong entries beat six weak ones. Don't include things just to fill space.

6:30 - 7:30 | Four common mistakes. Treating this section as an afterthought, when it deserves real thought. Disconnecting from your narrative, where your activities contradict what you say in essays. Listing activities without impact, like "member of marketing club" with no details. And padding with insignificant items. Three strong entries beat six weak ones every time. Also remember: being generic about interests is a missed opportunity. "Travel, reading, sports" is forgettable. Be specific.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Choose activities that reinforce your overall narrative.
  • Cover different themes: giving back, involvement, unique interests.
  • Awards show qualities, not just achievements. Choose for variety.
  • Descriptions should show impact with numbers and action verbs.
  • Make interests specific and memorable, not generic.
  • Small section, meaningful opportunity. Give it the thought it deserves.
