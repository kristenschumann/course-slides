Category Examples

4:00 - 4:45 | Here are six common theme categories. Most strong applications have 2-3 themes that appear across multiple parts of your life. If a theme only shows up once, it might not be core to who you are. Pick the ones that genuinely resonate.

Theme Validation

Theme Validation Checklist

  • Appear across multiple areas of your life (work, personal, community)
  • Connect naturally to your MBA goals
  • Be authentically yours, not what you think schools want to hear
  • Differentiate you from typical applicants in your background
  • Provide evidence for the skills and qualities schools seek
4:45 - 5:15 | Run your themes through this checklist. Every theme you use should pass all five tests. The third point is critical. Authenticity always wins over telling schools what you think they want to hear.

The Three Whys

The Three Essential Whys

01

Why MBA?

Why this degree versus other paths to your goals.

02

Why Now?

Why this is the right moment in your career for an MBA.

03

Why This School?

What makes this specific program the best fit for you.

6:50 - 7:30 | Your narrative must clearly answer all three of these questions. Miss any one, and there's a gap in your story. Let's look at each one and the common mistakes applicants make.

Why MBA

Why MBA: Avoid vs. Instead

Avoid Say Instead
"I want to learn business" "I need cross-functional leadership skills to scale product teams"
"Everyone gets an MBA" "An MBA bridges my engineering expertise with business strategy"
"I want to earn more" "I've explored alternatives, but the MBA network is essential for VC"
"I want to pivot" "I'm moving from consulting to health tech product management"
7:30 - 8:15 | The left column shows four common pitfalls for "Why MBA." On the right, the stronger alternatives. Notice the pattern: vague reasons fail. Specific, personal reasons win. Your answer should make it clear that an MBA is the only logical next step.

Why This School

Demonstrating School Fit

  • Specific courses or professors relevant to your goals
  • Clubs and activities you'd join and contribute to
  • Student culture and values that align with yours
  • Alumni network doing what you aspire to do
  • Geographic and recruiting advantages for your career path
8:15 - 8:50 | For "Why This School," go far beyond rankings. Show you've done real research. Name specific professors, clubs, or programs. Explain how the school's culture matches who you are. This is where you prove genuine interest.

8:50 - 9:30 | Your short-term goals need all four qualities. Specific means naming the industry, function, and company type. Realistic means it's achievable with your MBA and background. Logical means it connects to your past. Exciting means it shows genuine ambition.

Career Goals

The Goals Formula

"After my MBA, I plan to join [type of company] in [function/role], focusing on [specific area], where I can leverage my experience in [relevant background] while developing [new skills]."

Your long-term goal should extend logically from your short-term goal. It should be visionary, connected, impactful, and aligned with your core themes.

9:30 - 10:10 | Here's a proven formula for articulating short-term goals. Fill in each bracket with your specifics. For long-term goals, paint a bigger picture that extends naturally from your short-term plan. Both should feel authentic to your themes.

Goals Red Flags

Watch for These Warning Signs

Red Flag

Don't Require an MBA

If you can reach your goal without the degree, rethink your reasoning.

Red Flag

Disconnected from Past

Goals that ignore your background feel random and unconvincing.

Red Flag

Too Vague

"Be a leader" tells admissions nothing about your actual plans.

Red Flag

Unrealistically Specific

"Be CEO of Google" undermines your credibility with the committee.

10:10 - 10:50 | Watch for these four red flags. Each one signals a weak narrative. Goals that don't need an MBA raise questions about motivation. Disconnected goals feel incoherent. Vague goals show a lack of reflection. Unrealistic goals hurt credibility.

Authenticity Check

The Authenticity Pressure Test

  • Friend test: Would your friends recognize this story as you?
  • Uniqueness test: Could another applicant tell the same story?
  • Transparency test: Are you hiding anything important?
  • Enthusiasm test: Does your genuine passion come through?
10:50 - 11:40 | Before you submit, run your narrative through these four tests. The friend test is the most important. If your closest friends wouldn't recognize the person in your essays, you've drifted too far from authenticity.

Authenticity Check

The Dinner Table Test

Imagine sitting at dinner with an admissions committee member. Would you tell your story the same way?

Your written narrative should feel like an elevated version of how you'd actually talk about yourself. The best narratives are the most coherent and authentic. Take the time to craft yours thoughtfully.

11:40 - 12:15 | This is the ultimate gut check. If your written narrative sounds nothing like how you'd describe yourself in conversation, something is off. Authentic doesn't mean casual. It means genuine, honest, and true to who you really are.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Your narrative has 3 purposes: answer the prompt, show who you are, communicate fit
  • Use the past-present-future framework to structure your arc
  • Identify 2-3 core themes using the theme discovery exercise
  • Answer the three whys: Why MBA, Why Now, Why This School
  • Craft specific, connected goals using the formula
  • Pressure-test for authenticity. Your friends should recognize you
12:15 - 15:00 | Let's recap. Your narrative serves three purposes. Structure it with past-present-future. Find 2-3 themes that are genuinely yours. Answer all three whys convincingly. Set specific, connected goals. And always test for authenticity. The best MBA narratives aren't the most impressive. They're the most honest and coherent.
