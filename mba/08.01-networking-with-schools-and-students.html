1:00 - 2:30 | Four reasons networking matters. First, it demonstrates genuine interest. Schools have CRM systems and track who attends sessions and visits campus. Second, it strengthens your essays. Generic "why this school" essays fail because applicants don't know enough. Real conversations give you specific stories. Third, it helps you assess fit. Better to learn about culture before you accept than after you arrive. Fourth, if you impress a current student enough, they might send an unsolicited note to admissions on your behalf. You can't ask for this, but you can earn it by being prepared and genuine.

Start with Who You Know

  • College alumni: People from your undergrad who attended your target MBA programs
  • Colleagues: Current or former coworkers who have MBAs
  • Extended network: Friends of friends and professional contacts from events

Warm connections respond more often and give more candid advice. Ask: "Do you know anyone who went to [school]? I'd love an introduction."

2:30 - 3:45 | Before you reach out cold to strangers, mine your existing network. College alumni who attended your target programs, colleagues with MBAs, friends of friends, professional contacts from conferences. These warm connections are more likely to respond and give candid advice. A referral from someone in your network carries more weight than cold outreach. Ask people: do you know anyone who went to this school? You'll be surprised how often the answer is yes.

The LinkedIn Playbook

Target club leaders specifically. Consulting Club, Finance Club, and affinity group presidents are visible, engaged, and often happy to talk. Cross-reference school websites with LinkedIn.

Sample message: "Hi Sarah, I'm applying to Booth and saw you lead the Entrepreneurship Club. I'm a PM interested in startups post-MBA and would love to hear about the New Venture Challenge. Would you have 15 minutes for a call?"

3:45 - 5:30 | LinkedIn is your most powerful tool. Search for current students by filtering for the school name and "currently attending." Target club leaders specifically, like the Consulting Club or Finance Club presidents. They're visible, engaged, and happy to talk. Also look for admissions ambassadors who have volunteered to speak with prospective students. Keep your outreach brief, specific, and personalized. Show you've done research. Make a reasonable ask. You may need to message 10 people to get 3 responses, and that's normal. But don't copy-paste the same generic message. People can tell.

Questions Worth Asking

Ask This Why It Works
"What surprised you most about the program?" Reveals insider perspective you can't find online
"What drew you to this school over others?" Uncovers differentiators for your essays
"What's the student culture really like?" Gets beyond marketing language to real culture
"How do students support each other?" Shows community dynamics and collaboration
"What would you do differently applying now?" Practical advice from someone who's been through it
5:30 - 7:00 | Focus on learning, not extracting admissions tips. Ask what surprised them about the program. Ask what drew them to this school over others. Ask what the culture is really like beyond the marketing. Ask how students support each other. Ask what they'd do differently if they were applying now. These questions get you real insights that will make your essays specific and authentic. After each conversation, send a thank-you note. And if you're admitted, let them know.

7:10 - 9:00 | Schools invest heavily in helping prospective students learn about their programs. Three main channels. First, events and webinars, both online and in-person. Attend the ones for schools you're serious about. Ask thoughtful questions during Q&A. Schools notice who attends and who asks good questions. Second, campus visits. If you can swing it, visit. Schedule an official tour, sit in on a class, meet a student ambassador. This is one of the highest-signal demonstrations of interest. Third, virtual engagement. Most T15 schools now offer virtual class visits, one-on-one ambassador chats, and recorded sessions. If you can't visit in person, these carry real weight. Schools track all of it.

Networking Etiquette

Do
  • Be genuine in your interest
  • Keep calls to 15-20 minutes
  • Prepare thoughtful questions
  • Follow up with thanks
  • Reference conversations in essays
Don't
  • Send the same generic message to many people
  • Ask for insider admissions tips
  • Be transactional or pushy
  • Exaggerate your interactions
  • Pester people who don't respond
9:00 - 10:30 | Here's the etiquette. Do be genuine, keep calls brief at 15 to 20 minutes, prepare thoughtful questions, follow up with thanks, and reference what you learned in your essays. Don't send generic copy-paste messages, ask for admissions tips, be transactional, exaggerate your interactions, or pester people who don't respond. Admissions officers can tell the difference between "I spoke with six students and here's what they all said," which is performative, versus "My conversation with Mark changed how I think about Kellogg's team-based approach," which is genuine.

Turn Conversations into Essays

"When I spoke with current student Alex Chen, she described how the Entrepreneurship Lab let her test a business concept while getting real-time feedback from professors and peers. That hands-on approach resonates with how I develop best."

This is specific, authentic, and shows you did the work. Use the same approach in interviews by referencing conversations about culture and community.

10:30 - 12:00 | Here's where networking pays off in your application. In your "why this school" essay, instead of generic statements, you can write something like this example: referencing a specific conversation about a specific program. It's specific, it shows you did the work, and it's authentic. Use the same approach in interviews. Reference conversations about culture and community. What you learn also helps you decide where to apply, how to position yourself, and what aspects of the school to emphasize.
