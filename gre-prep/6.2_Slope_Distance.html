Part 1

Slope

Measuring steepness and direction

0:30-0:40 | Transition to slope formula.

Slope

Rise over run

$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$

0:40-1:20 | Slope = rise over run = change in y over change in x. The green line rises from left to right (positive slope), the red line falls (negative slope). GRE tip: perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals (e.g., 2/3 and -3/2). Parallel lines have equal slopes. QC questions often test whether you know this without needing to compute actual slopes.

Slope

Calculating slope

Positive Slope

Through $(2, 3)$ and $(6, 11)$

$m = \frac{11 - 3}{6 - 2} = \frac{8}{4} = $ $2$

Negative Slope

Through $(-1, 5)$ and $(3, -3)$

$m = \frac{-3 - 5}{3 - (-1)} = \frac{-8}{4} = $ $-2$

1:20-2:00 | Positive slope: line rises left to right. Example gives slope 2. Negative slope: line falls left to right. Example gives slope -2.

Slope

Special cases and relationships

Type Slope Example
Horizontal line $0$ $y = 5$
Vertical line Undefined $x = 3$
Parallel lines $m_1 = m_2$ Same slope
Perpendicular lines $m_1 \times m_2 = -1$ Negative reciprocals

Example If slope is 3, the perpendicular slope is $-\frac{1}{3}$

2:00-3:00 | Special slopes: horizontal lines have slope 0, vertical lines are undefined. Parallel lines share the same slope. Perpendicular lines have slopes that multiply to -1 (negative reciprocals). If slope is 3, perpendicular is -1/3.

Part 2

Distance Formula

The Pythagorean theorem in disguise

3:00-3:10 | Transition to distance formula.

Distance

Length between two points

$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$   This IS the Pythagorean theorem: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$

3:10-3:50 | Distance formula is the Pythagorean theorem applied to coordinates. The horizontal and vertical distances form the legs of a right triangle, and the distance is the hypotenuse.

Distance

Pythagorean triples in action

3-4-5 Triple

$(1, 2)$ to $(4, 6)$

$\sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{25} = $ $5$

6-8-10 Triple

$(-2, 3)$ to $(4, -5)$

$\sqrt{6^2 + 8^2} = \sqrt{100} = $ $10$

3:50-4:30 | First example: deltas are 3 and 4, recognizing the 3-4-5 triple gives distance 5. Second example: deltas are 6 and 8, a multiple of 3-4-5, giving distance 10.

Distance

Recognize Pythagorean triples

Memorize common triples to skip the square root calculation entirely.

Triple Common Multiples
3-4-5 6-8-10, 9-12-15, 12-16-20
5-12-13 10-24-26
8-15-17 16-30-34
4:30-5:10 | Pythagorean triples let you skip the square root entirely. The big three: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17. Any multiple also works. Spot these to save time on the GRE.

Part 3

Midpoint Formula

Average the coordinates

5:10-5:20 | Transition to midpoint formula.

Midpoint

Center of a line segment

Midpoint $= \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\; \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$   Average each coordinate.

5:20-5:50 | Midpoint = average the x-coordinates, average the y-coordinates. The midpoint is the exact center of the line segment. GRE speed tip: the midpoint formula works backwards too. If you know one endpoint and the midpoint, set up equations to find the other endpoint. This "reverse midpoint" pattern appears frequently on the GRE.

Midpoint

Finding midpoints & endpoints

Find Midpoint

$(2, 5)$ to $(8, 11)$

$\left(\frac{2+8}{2}, \frac{5+11}{2}\right) = $ $(5, 8)$

Find Endpoint

Midpoint $(4, 7)$, one end $(1, 3)$

$x_B = 2(4) - 1 = 7$, $y_B = 2(7) - 3 = 11$. B $= $ $(7, 11)$

5:50-6:30 | Example: (2+8)/2 = 5, (5+11)/2 = 8, so midpoint is (5, 8). Reverse problem: given midpoint and one endpoint, solve for the other. Double the midpoint and subtract the known endpoint.

Example 1

Testing for isosceles

Problem

Triangle ABC: A(0,0), B(6,0), C(3,4). Is it isosceles?

Solution

$AB = 6$ (horizontal distance)

$AC = \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$

$BC = \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$

$AC = BC = 5$, so yes, isosceles

6:30-7:30 | Application: use distance formula to test triangle properties. AB = 6 (just horizontal distance). AC and BC both equal sqrt(9+16) = 5. Two equal sides means isosceles.

Example 2

Circle center and radius

Problem

P(1,2) and Q(7,10) are endpoints of a diameter. Find the center and radius.

Solution

Center $= $ midpoint $= \left(\frac{1+7}{2}, \frac{2+10}{2}\right) = (4, 6)$

Diameter $= \sqrt{6^2 + 8^2} = \sqrt{100} = 10$

Radius $= \frac{10}{2} =$ $5$

7:30-8:30 | Circle problem: center is the midpoint of the diameter = (4,6). Diameter = distance = sqrt(36+64) = 10. Radius = half the diameter = 5. Note the 6-8-10 triple again.

Example 3

Proving a right triangle

Problem

A(1,1), B(4,5), C(8,2). Is this a right triangle?

Solution

$AB = \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$,  $BC = \sqrt{16+9} = 5$

$AC = \sqrt{49 + 1} = \sqrt{50}$

Check: $AB^2 + BC^2 = 25 + 25 = 50 = AC^2$ ✓

Right angle at B

8:30-9:30 | Proving a right triangle: find all three side lengths with the distance formula, then check if the Pythagorean theorem holds. AB = BC = 5 and AC = sqrt(50). 25 + 25 = 50, confirmed. Right angle at vertex B.

Key Takeaways

Slope, distance & midpoint

  • Slope - $m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$, positive = rising, negative = falling, zero = horizontal
  • Parallel vs perpendicular - Same slope = parallel. Slopes multiply to $-1$ = perpendicular
  • Distance - $d = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}$, the Pythagorean theorem on coordinates
  • Pythagorean triples - Recognize 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17 to skip calculations
  • Midpoint - Average x-coordinates and average y-coordinates
9:30-10:00 | Summary: three formulas that form the foundation of coordinate geometry. Slope for steepness, distance for length, midpoint for center. Recognize Pythagorean triples to save time.
