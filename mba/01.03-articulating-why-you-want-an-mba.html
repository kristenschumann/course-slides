Why Goals Matter

What Admissions Committees Assess

Fit

Does this person know what they want, and does our program help them get there?

Trajectory

Is this person thinking clearly about their future? Vague goals suggest vague thinking.

Contribution

What will this person bring to our community from day one?

1:00 - 2:15 | From my experience sitting on the other side of this process, adcoms are really asking one question: will this person succeed? Your goals help them assess three things. Fit: do you know what you want, and can our program get you there? I have seen strong applicants get dinged because they wanted tech but applied to a finance-heavy school. Trajectory: are you thinking clearly? Vague goals signal vague thinking, and adcoms pick up on that fast. Contribution: what do you bring from day one? When you have direction, you show up to clubs, recruiting panels, and classroom discussions with purpose.

Short-Term Goal

What Makes a Strong Post-MBA Goal

  • Specific: Name the industry, function, and type of company
  • Realistic: Achievable given your background plus an MBA
  • Logical: Connects to your past experiences and recurring themes
  • Exciting: Shows real ambition and the impact you want to have
2:15 - 3:30 | Your short-term goal is the first 3 to 5 years post-MBA. When I work with clients, I push for four qualities. Specific: not "I want to work in consulting" but "I want to join a strategy consulting firm like Bain or McKinsey, focusing on healthcare clients." Realistic: achievable given your background plus the MBA. Logical: there should be a clear thread connecting your past to this next step. And exciting: show ambition. I want to feel your energy when you talk about this direction.

Short-Term Goal

A Formula That Works

"After my MBA, I plan to join [type of company] in [function/role], focusing on [specific area], where I can leverage my experience in [relevant background] while developing [new skills the MBA provides]."

This structure shows specificity, connection to your past, and a clear reason why you need the MBA.

3:30 - 4:30 | This is the formula I walk every client through. "After my MBA, I plan to join [type of company] in [function or role], focusing on [specific area], where I can leverage my experience in [relevant background] while developing [new skills the MBA provides]." It hits specificity, connection to your past, and the reason you need the MBA. You do not have to use these exact words, but every strong goal statement I have seen covers these elements.

Common Misconceptions

What Applicants Get Wrong

Misconception Reality
"My goals must be set in stone" Schools know plans change. They want thoughtful direction, not predictions.
"Say what they want to hear" Inauthentic goals show in essays and interviews. Write what you actually care about.
"More specific is always better" "Head of Product at Spotify" is too narrow. "Product at a consumer tech company" is right.
"I need unique goals" Consulting and banking are fine. What differentiates you is why and what you bring.
"Traditional goals are always safe" If your target industry is being reshaped by AI, acknowledge the transformation.
7:00 - 8:15 | These are misconceptions I correct constantly in my coaching. "My goals must be set in stone." No. Schools know plans change. They want thoughtful direction. "Say what they want to hear." I cannot stress this enough: inauthenticity shows. Adcoms read thousands of essays and they can spot borrowed goals immediately. "More specific is always better." There is a sweet spot. "Head of Product at Spotify" is too narrow. "I want to work in tech" is too vague. Aim for the middle. "I need unique goals." Consulting and banking are totally fine. What differentiates you is your why. And "traditional goals are always safe." If your target industry is being reshaped by AI, acknowledge it. That shows forward thinking.

Red Flags

Goals That Raise Concerns

  • No MBA needed: If you can achieve objectives without it, why invest $100K-$300K?
  • Disconnected from background: Random pivots without explanation are not compelling.
  • Too vague: "I want to be a leader" says nothing specific.
  • Unrealistically specific: "CEO of Apple by 2035" is not credible.
  • Only about money: "Maximize earning potential" may be true, but do not lead with it.
8:15 - 9:15 | These are the red flags I watch for. ðŸ’¡ Share a story about a client who came in with red-flag goals and how you worked together to fix them. Goals that do not require an MBA: if you can get there without business school, why invest $100K to $300K? I always push clients to frame the MBA as a necessary accelerant. Disconnected goals: random pivots without a narrative are not compelling to adcoms. Too vague: "I want to be a leader" tells them nothing. Unrealistically specific: "CEO of Apple by 2035" is not credible. And money-only goals: "maximize earning potential" may be honest, but it should never be your lead.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Specificity matters: name the industry, function, and type of company
  • Connection matters: your goals should link to your past
  • Credibility matters: the path from here to there should make sense
  • Authenticity matters: you have to actually care about this direction
  • Evolution is okay: your goals can and probably will change
  • The goal is to demonstrate thoughtful direction, not a perfect 10-year plan

Credibility: the path from here to there has to make sense. Authenticity: you have to actually care about this direction, and trust me, it shows when you do. And evolution is okay. Your goals will probably change, and that is perfectly fine. What I tell every client: you are not writing a prediction. You are demonstrating that you think seriously about your future and can chart a course. That is what gets you in.
