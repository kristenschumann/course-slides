Interview Purpose

Five Things They're Evaluating

  • Communication: Can you articulate thoughts clearly and tell a coherent story?
  • Self-awareness: Do you know your strengths, weaknesses, and how others see you?
  • Authenticity: Does the person match the application?
  • Fit: Will you thrive in this community and contribute to it?
  • Judgment: Can you think on your feet and handle tough questions?
1:00 - 2:15 | Your application tells them what you've done. The interview tells them who you are. They're evaluating five things. Communication: can you speak clearly and tell a story? Self-awareness: do you know yourself honestly? Authenticity: does the real you match what's on paper? Fit: will you contribute to the community? Judgment: can you handle challenging questions with maturity? The interview is another data point, not a separate evaluation. Consistency between your application and interview matters.

2:15 - 3:45 | Different schools use different formats. Blind interviews mean the interviewer hasn't read your app, so you shape the narrative. HBS is known for this. Application-based means they'll ask about specific experiences and may probe weaknesses. Alumni interviews tend to be conversational. Staff interviews are more structured. Wharton uses a unique team-based discussion where you collaborate with other applicants. Some schools like Kellogg and MIT Sloan also include video essays. Always research your school's format before you prepare.

Common Questions

The Big Three to Prepare First

"Tell me about yourself"

60-90 seconds covering where you are, how you got here, where you're heading, and why an MBA now

"Why do you want an MBA?"

Be specific about what you need: skills, access, credibility, network. Connect it to your goals

"Why this school?"

Reference specific programs, courses, or community aspects. Show you've done your research

3:45 - 5:15 | Three questions come up in virtually every MBA interview. First, "Tell me about yourself." Have a 60-90 second answer ready covering where you are professionally, a brief arc of how you got there, where you're heading, and why the MBA. Don't recite your resume. Tell a story. Second, "Why do you want an MBA?" Be specific about what you need. Third, "Why this school?" Know the school's differentiators. Reference specific programs and community aspects that matter to you.

Common Questions

Other Categories to Prepare

Behavioral

"Tell me about a time you led a team." "Describe a time you failed." Use the STAR method: Situation, Task, Action, Result

Self-Reflection

"What are your strengths and weaknesses?" "How would colleagues describe you?" Be honest and specific

Application Follow-Up

"Tell me more about your role at [company]." "Can you explain this gap?" Know your app cold

Your Questions

Most interviews end with "What questions do you have?" Prepare 2-3 thoughtful questions not answered by the website

5:15 - 6:30 | Beyond the Big Three, prepare for four other categories. Behavioral questions ask about specific situations. Use the STAR method, which we cover in depth in the Behavioral Interview Frameworks video. Self-reflection questions probe how well you know yourself. Application follow-up questions dig into details from your app, so know it cold. And always prepare 2-3 thoughtful questions to ask the interviewer.

Story Bank

Test Each Story Before You Use It

For each story, ask: Can I describe what I specifically did? Is there a measurable outcome? Did I learn something I can articulate?

If you answer yes to all three, it's a strong story. If any answer is no, find a better example or rework the story to highlight your specific role.

8:00 - 8:45 | Here's a quick quality test for every story in your bank. Three questions. Can I describe what I specifically did, not the team? Is there a measurable or meaningful outcome? Did I learn something I can articulate? If you answer yes to all three, you have a strong story. If any answer is no, either find a better example or rework the story to highlight your specific contribution and takeaway.

Interview Mindset

How to Show Up on the Day

  • It's a conversation, not an interrogation. The interviewer wants you to succeed.
  • You've passed the hard part. The interview means your application was strong.
  • Be yourself, professionally. Authenticity matters more than polish.
  • Energy and enthusiasm count. Show genuine excitement about the opportunity.
8:45 - 9:30 | A few mindset shifts for interview day. It's a conversation, not an interrogation. The interviewer wants you to succeed. Remember you've already passed the hard part by getting the invitation. Be yourself, professionally. Authenticity matters more than polish. And bring energy and enthusiasm. Show that you're genuinely excited about this school and opportunity. It's also okay not to know everything. Honest uncertainty beats a weak bluff.
