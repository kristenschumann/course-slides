Essay Purposes

Every Essay Must Do Three Things

01

Answer the Prompt

If your essay could work for a different prompt, it's too generic. Read carefully and respond directly.

02

Show Who You Are

Reveal dimensions of yourself that can't be captured in bullet points. Dreams, values, growth.

03

Communicate Fit

Your values, goals, and approach should align with the school. They should think: "This person would thrive here."

Core Principle

The Mindset Shift

Go deep, not broad. One meaningful story beats ten surface-level achievements. Admissions committees remember how your essay made them feel, not the facts.

Most applicants try to cram in every accomplishment. The result is a shallow summary that reads like a resume. Great essays pick one story and let the reader feel something real.

2:30 - 3:45 | Here's the mindset shift that separates good essays from great ones. Go deep, not broad. Most applicants try to cram in every accomplishment. The result is a shallow summary that tells the reader nothing they couldn't find on a resume. Great essays pick one thing and go deep. Committees read thousands of applications. What sticks? Not the person who listed ten achievements. The person who shared one meaningful story that revealed something true about themselves.

Common Pitfalls

Mistakes That Sink Applications

  • Regurgitating your resume: Walking through your resume in essay form adds no value.
  • Highlight reel mode: Listing accomplishments without humility or growth makes readers tune out.
  • Forcing a theme: If you keep explaining why something fits, the theme is wrong.
  • Guessing what they want: Inauthenticity is obvious. Find what's true for you.
3:50 - 5:30 | Four pitfalls that sink applications. First, regurgitating your resume. Walking through your resume in essay form is boring and adds no value. Pick one or two experiences and go deep. Second, highlight reel mode. If all you do is talk about how amazing you are, readers get turned off. Show humility and growth. Third, forcing a theme that doesn't fit. If you're constantly explaining why something fits your theme, the theme is wrong. Fourth, guessing what they want to hear. Admissions committees can tell when you're not being authentic. Find what's true for you.

Authentic Voice

Write Like Yourself

Signs of Inauthentic Writing

  • Buzzwords without substance
  • Generic statements anyone could write
  • Overstated accomplishments
  • Sentences that sound impressive but say nothing

Signs of Authentic Writing

  • Specific details only you would know
  • Moments of vulnerability or uncertainty
  • Your actual thought process, not just outcomes
  • Language that matches how you think

The "dinner table test": would you tell your story this way sitting across from an admissions officer?

10:15 - 11:45 | Your essay should sound like you. An elevated version, but recognizably you. On the left, signs of inauthentic writing: buzzwords, generic statements, overstated accomplishments, sentences that sound impressive but say nothing. On the right, authentic writing: specific details only you would know, moments of vulnerability, your actual thought process, language that matches how you think. Use the dinner table test: if you were sitting at dinner with an admissions officer, would you tell your story this way? If your essay sounds like a press release, rewrite it.

Writing Process

Seven Steps to a Strong Essay

01

Brainstorm

Get ideas on paper without filtering. What stories define you?

02

Outline

Map your structure. Know your ending before you begin.

03

Draft and Rest

Write quickly without editing. Then walk away for at least a day.

04

Revise and Polish

Go deeper, get feedback, then polish for clarity and flow.

11:45 - 13:30 | Here's a practical process. Step one, brainstorm without filtering. Get ideas on paper. What stories could you tell? Step two, create an outline. Map your structure and know your ending. Step three, write a rough draft quickly. Don't edit as you go. The first draft is supposed to be messy. Then let it sit for at least a day. Step four, revise for depth. Are you going deep enough? Can you add more specific details? Then get feedback from trusted readers. Do they understand your point? Does it feel like you? Finally, polish for clarity, flow, and grammar. Read it aloud to catch anything awkward.
