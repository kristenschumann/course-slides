The Basics

What the calculator can do

The GRE calculator is basic. It handles five operations, and every calculation requires multiple mouse clicks.

What It Has

  • Addition, subtraction, multiplication, division
  • Square roots
  • Parentheses for order of operations
  • Memory function (M+, MR, MC)

The Trade-Off

No exponents, no trig, no fractions. On-screen only, click with your mouse.

Every calculation requires multiple clicks. Accuracy comes at the cost of time.

Strategic Choices

When to use the calculator

  • Large or messy numbers: $847 \times 36$ or $127.5 \div 8.5$ are calculator jobs
  • Decimal arithmetic: Let the calculator handle decimal places
  • Checking your work: Quick verification of hand calculations
  • Multi-step chains: (15% of 340) + (23% of 280) prevents arithmetic mistakes in the chain
Key Idea

Use the calculator when the numbers are messy or when you need exact values and hand calculation would be slow or error-prone.

Mental Math

When scratch paper is faster

  • Simple arithmetic: $48 \div 6$, $7 \times 8$, $125 - 39$ are faster by hand or in your head
  • Fractions: Keep them as fractions on paper. Don't convert $\frac{3}{7}$ to a decimal just to use the calculator
  • Estimation: When answer choices are far apart, rough mental math is faster
  • Setting up problems: Write your equation on paper first, then decide if you need the calculator
Rule of Thumb

If you can solve it in your head in under 5 seconds, don't use the calculator.

Watch Out

4 calculator traps

1. Order of Operations

Enter things wrong, get wrong answers. Use parentheses explicitly.

2. Early Fraction Conversion

Entering $\frac{1}{3}$ as 0.333 creates rounding errors when you multiply.

3. Typing Errors

Clicking fast makes it easy to hit the wrong button. If your answer seems odd, retry.

4. Over-Reliance

Using the calculator for everything wastes time and doesn't build number sense.

Summary

Calculator vs. scratch paper

  • Use the calculator for messy numbers - large multiplication, decimals, and multi-step chains
  • Use scratch paper for simple math - basic arithmetic, fractions, and estimation are faster by hand
  • Apply the 5-second rule - if you can solve it in your head in under 5 seconds, skip the calculator
  • Set up on paper first - write your equation before deciding whether you need the calculator
  • Watch for traps - order of operations errors, premature fraction conversion, and typing mistakes
