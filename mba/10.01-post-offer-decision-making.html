Decision Framework

Seven Factors to Evaluate

  • Career alignment: Placement data for your specific target industry
  • Culture and community: Collaborative vs. competitive, cohort size, social life
  • Financial package: Total cost of attendance, not just tuition minus scholarship
  • Location, academics, network, and gut feeling

Career alignment is the most important and most underweighted factor. Don't ask "which school is best?" Ask "which school is best for what I want to do?"

1:00 - 2:30 | Seven factors, roughly in order of importance. Career alignment is number one, and it's the one most applicants underweight. Look at employment reports for your target industry specifically, not aggregate numbers. A school placing 25% into tech is materially different from one placing 8%. Culture and community is number two. Collaborative vs. competitive, cohort size, learning approach, social culture. You'll live in this environment for two years. Financial package is third. Calculate total cost of attendance, not just tuition minus scholarship. Then location, academic strengths, alumni network, and gut feeling. Don't ask "which school is best?" Ask "which school is best for what I want to do?"

Career Alignment

What to Research at Each School

Recruiting Pipelines

Which target companies recruit on campus? Does the school have formal relationships with your target employers?

Career Services

Dedicated advisors for your target industry? Active support for consulting, banking, tech, or your field?

Alumni Placement

Where are alumni from the last 5-10 years? Are they in the companies and roles you're targeting?

Student Perspective

Talk to second-years in your target industry. Ask: "How was recruiting? What did the school do well?"

2:30 - 3:45 | Let's go deeper on career alignment. Four things to research. Recruiting pipelines: which companies recruit on campus, and do your target employers have formal relationships with the school? Career services: does the career development office have dedicated advisors for your target industry? Alumni placement: where are graduates from the last 5 to 10 years working? Are they in the companies and roles you want? And the student perspective: talk to second-years and recent alumni in your target industry. Ask them directly about their recruiting experience and what the school did well.

The formal information sessions will all sound great. What matters is how you feel in unstructured settings: dinner with students, a bar after admit weekend, a casual hallway conversation.

Seek out students not on the welcome committee. Ask: "What's one thing you'd change?" and "What surprised you most about the experience?"

3:45 - 4:45 | Culture is the factor most applicants underestimate until they experience it firsthand. Collaborative vs. competitive. Cohort size. Learning approach. Social culture. The best way to evaluate it is to interact with current students in unstructured settings. The formal sessions will all sound great. What matters is how you feel when you're hanging out with students over dinner or at a bar. Seek out students who aren't on the admissions ambassador team. 4:45 - 6:00 | Build a side-by-side spreadsheet for every school. Include tuition and fees, living expenses, health insurance, books, then subtract your scholarship to get the net cost. Compare estimated loans and median salary in your target industry. This exercise often reveals that the "most expensive" school on paper isn't actually the most expensive when you factor in living costs and aid. A $50K scholarship at one school might seem generous, but if that school's total cost is $80K more than another, the scholarship school is actually pricier. If schools lead to similar career outcomes, the one that costs less is the better financial decision. Don't overpay for marginal prestige.

6:00 - 7:15 | Admit weekends are your best opportunity to evaluate culture firsthand. Attend at every school you're seriously considering. Three priorities. First, attend everything, especially the informal events. Dinners and happy hours reveal the real culture. Second, go off-script. The admissions ambassadors will give you the polished pitch. You'll think you'll remember the differences, but after visiting three or four schools, the details blur. Write down both rational observations and gut feelings.

7:15 - 8:15 | Understanding the timeline is critical. R1 admits typically get decisions in December with deposits due late January or early February. R2 decisions come in March with deposits due mid-to-late April. R3 admits have shorter windows, sometimes just two to three weeks. Deposits are typically $1,000 to $2,000 and non-refundable. Most top programs participate in the MBA CSEA agreement, which is designed to give R1 admits time to hear back from R2 applications. But specifics vary by school and year, so check each school's deadline. If you need an extension, ask. A polite email explaining your situation usually gets a positive response.

The Final Call

Five Ways to Break a Tie

  • Build a weighted decision matrix to clarify your priorities and score each school.
  • Talk to alumni 5+ years out, who can tell you what mattered most long-term.
  • Imagine yourself three years from now at each school's graduation.
  • Set a personal deadline a few days before the deposit due date, then commit.
8:15 - 9:45 | If you've done all the research and still can't decide, here are four ways to break the tie. First, build a weighted decision matrix. List your criteria, assign weights based on your priorities, and score each school. The math won't decide for you, but it clarifies your thinking. If the numbers come out close, that tells you the decision is a genuine toss-up on rational grounds, and you should lean into intangibles. Second, talk to alumni who are 5+ years out. They can tell you which aspects of the school mattered most long-term and which didn't matter at all. Third, imagine yourself three years from now at each school's graduation. Which scenario keeps coming back to you? Fourth, set a personal deadline a few days before the deposit due date. Make the call and commit fully. Stop second-guessing. Every school on your list is excellent.

Your subconscious is processing information that's hard to articulate analytically. After doing the rational analysis, your instinct about where you belong carries real weight.
