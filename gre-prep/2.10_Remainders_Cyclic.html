Remainder Basics

The division algorithm

Dividend = Divisor $\times$ Quotient + Remainder, or $n = dq + r$ where $0 \leq r < d$

Example

When we divide 17 by 5:

$17 = 5 \times 3 + 2$

Quotient $= 3$, Remainder $= 2$

GRE Language

"17 leaves a remainder of 2 when divided by 5"

This means $17 = 5q + 2$ for some integer $q$.

0:40-1:40 | The division algorithm: Dividend = Divisor x Quotient + Remainder. Walk through 17 divided by 5: quotient 3, remainder 2. The GRE phrases this as "17 leaves a remainder of 2 when divided by 5." This translates to 17 = 5q + 2.

Example 1

Working with remainder expressions

Problem

When $n$ is divided by 7, the remainder is 4. What is the remainder when $2n$ is divided by 7?

Solution

Step 1: Express: $n = 7q + 4$

Step 2: Double: $2n = 14q + 8 = 7(2q) + 8$

Step 3: Since $8 = 7(1) + 1$: $2n = 7(2q+1) + 1$

Remainder: $1$

1:40-2:40 | Example 1. Express n = 7q + 4. Double both sides: 2n = 14q + 8. Rewrite 8 as 7(1) + 1. So 2n = 7(2q+1) + 1. The remainder is 1.

Example 4

Units digit of $7^{100}$

Problem

What is the units digit of $7^{100}$?

Solution

Step 1: 7's cycle: 7, 9, 3, 1 (length 4)

Step 2: $100 \div 4 = 25$ remainder $0$

Step 3: Remainder 0 $\rightarrow$ position 4 in cycle

Units digit: $1$

6:10-6:50 | Example 4. 7's cycle is 7, 9, 3, 1 with length 4. Divide exponent by cycle length: 100 / 4 = 25 remainder 0. Remainder 0 means the last position in the cycle, which is 1.

Step 2: $47 \div 4 = 11$ remainder $3$

Step 3: Position 3 in cycle $\rightarrow$ 7

6:50-7:25 | Example 5. 3's cycle: 3, 9, 7, 1 with length 4. 47 divided by 4 is 11 remainder 3. Position 3 in the cycle is 7.

Example 6

Units digit of a product

Problem

What is the units digit of $13^{25} \times 17^{18}$?

Solution

$13^{25}$: base ends in 3, cycle 3,9,7,1. $25 \div 4 = 6$ R $1 \rightarrow$ 3

$17^{18}$: base ends in 7, cycle 7,9,3,1. $18 \div 4 = 4$ R $2 \rightarrow$ 9

Multiply units digits: $3 \times 9 = 27$

Units digit: $7$

7:25-8:30 | Example 6. Find each units digit separately. 13^25: base ends in 3, cycle is 3,9,7,1. 25/4 = 6 R 1, position 1 is 3. 17^18: base ends in 7, cycle is 7,9,3,1. 18/4 = 4 R 2, position 2 is 9. Multiply: 3 x 9 = 27, units digit is 7.

Example 7

Remainder of a product

Problem

If $x$ leaves remainder 3 when divided by 7, and $y$ leaves remainder 5 when divided by 7, what is the remainder when $xy$ is divided by 7?

Solution

Step 1: Multiply remainders: $3 \times 5 = 15$

Step 2: $15 \div 7 = 2$ remainder $1$

Remainder: $1$

8:30-9:15 | Example 7. Multiply the remainders: 3 x 5 = 15. Then find the remainder of 15 divided by 7, which is 1.

Example 9

Remainder pattern in powers

Problem

What is the remainder when $2^{100}$ is divided by 3?

Solution

Find pattern: $2^1 \rightarrow$ R $2$, $2^2 \rightarrow$ R $1$, $2^3 \rightarrow$ R $2$, $2^4 \rightarrow$ R $1$

Cycle: 2, 1, 2, 1... (length 2)

100 is even $\rightarrow$ same position as $2^2$

Remainder: $1$

10:15-11:15 | Example 9. Find the remainder pattern: 2^1 gives remainder 2, 2^2 gives 1, 2^3 gives 2, 2^4 gives 1. The cycle is 2, 1 with length 2. Since 100 is even, same position as 2^2, remainder is 1.

Key Takeaways

Remainders & cyclic patterns

  • Division algorithm. $n = dq + r$ where $0 \leq r < d$
  • Remainder arithmetic. Replace numbers with their remainders before adding or multiplying
  • Units digit cycles. Bases 2, 3, 7, 8 cycle every 4; bases 4, 9 cycle every 2; bases 0, 1, 5, 6 never change
  • Finding position in cycle. Divide exponent by cycle length, use the remainder as position (remainder 0 means last position)
  • Product of units digits. Find each units digit separately, then multiply and take the units digit of the result
  • Remainder cycles. Not just units digits; remainders of powers also cycle, useful for any divisor
11:15-13:00 | Summary: The division algorithm expresses any division as n = dq + r. Replace numbers with remainders before operating. Units digit cycles repeat with lengths 1, 2, or 4. Divide exponent by cycle length to find position. Remainder 0 means the last position in the cycle. [Leland+ Tip] Think of remainders like clock math (modular arithmetic). If it is 10 AM and you add 15 hours, the clock reads 1 AM because 15 mod 12 = 3, and 10+3 = 13, which is 1 on a 12-hour clock. The GRE uses the same logic with different divisors. Test small cases to find the cycle, then use division to find position.
