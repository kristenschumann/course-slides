Tell Me About Yourself

The answer that sets the tone for every interview

5:00 - 5:15 | Section transition. Let the title land.

Job Search Bootcamp

The Sweet Spot

90 seconds

The ideal length for your "tell me about yourself" answer

Too short and you seem unprepared. Too long and you lose the interviewer. 90 seconds is the sweet spot.

5:15 - 6:15 | Big stat reveal. Let the number anchor. COACH: "Here's your target. 90 seconds. That's it. Most people either give a 30-second answer that says nothing or a 4-minute monologue that loses the room. We're going to nail the sweet spot." Key point: 90 seconds is both a creative constraint and a performance target.

Job Search Bootcamp

The 3-Part Formula

01

Your Past

Where you've been. 1-2 sentences about your background and the thread that connects your experience.

02

Your Present

What you're doing now. Why you're making a move. What's driving your search.

03

Your Future

Where you're headed. Why this role and company are the right next step.

6:15 - 8:00 | Introduce the 3-part formula. Walk through each card. COACH: "Past, present, future. That's the whole structure. Your past builds credibility. Your present explains motivation. Your future shows intentionality. Every great answer follows this arc." Key point: This structure works for every industry, every level.

Job Search Bootcamp

Two Approaches

The Resume Recital

Walking through every job in chronological order.

"So I graduated from State U in 2018, then I worked at Company A for two years doing marketing, then I moved to Company B where I..."

The Narrative Arc

A clear story with a connecting thread.

"I've spent the last six years helping early-stage companies figure out their growth engine. That work taught me that I love the 0-to-1 stage, which is why I'm focused on product roles at Series A startups."

8:00 - 10:00 | Contrast the two approaches. Read both examples aloud. COACH: "Read the left side. Sound familiar? This is what most people do. They recite their resume. The interviewer already has your resume. What they want is the story behind it. The right side gives them that story." Key point: Interviewers want narrative, not chronology.

Job Search Bootcamp

What Makes It Land

  • A connecting thread across your experience (not a list of jobs)
  • Specific details that stick ("I grew the team from 3 to 12")
  • Energy and conviction in your delivery (flat voice kills great content)
  • A clear reason why this role is the right next step
  • Practiced enough to feel natural, not memorized
10:00 - 11:30 | Walk through the five elements. Emphasize energy and delivery. COACH: "Five things to aim for. The biggest one most people miss is number three: energy. You can have the perfect script, but if you deliver it like you're reading a textbook, the interviewer checks out. We'll practice this tonight." TRANSITION: "Let me show you what this sounds like in practice."

The Specificity Test

If someone else in your field could say the same sentence, it's too generic.

Replace "I'm passionate about helping companies grow" with "I helped my last company go from $2M to $8M in ARR by rebuilding their outbound sales process." Specificity builds credibility.

17:00 - 18:00 | Key insight. Let it land before transitioning to practice. COACH: "Here's the test. Read each sentence of your answer and ask: could someone else say this? If yes, rewrite it. The goal is that only you could deliver this answer."

Job Search Bootcamp

Common Pitfalls

Starting with College

Unless you graduated recently, skip the undergraduate story. Start with the experience that matters most.

Being Too Humble

This isn't bragging. It's professional context. Own your results with confidence.

No Clear "Why"

Every answer needs a reason you want this role. Without it, the interviewer wonders if you're just applying everywhere.

Part 3

The STAR Method

Transform your accomplishments into interview-ready answers

40:00 - 40:15 | Section transition.

Job Search Bootcamp

STAR Framework

1

Situation

Set the scene. Company, team, challenge. 2 sentences max.

2

Task

Your specific responsibility. What were you asked to do?

3

Action

What you did. Be specific about YOUR decisions and steps.

4

Result

The outcome. Quantify it. What changed because of your work?

40:15 - 42:00 | Walk through the four steps. Emphasize Action and Result. COACH: "STAR. Situation, Task, Action, Result. You've probably heard this before. The difference between a decent STAR answer and a great one comes down to two things: specificity in the Action and quantification in the Result. Most people rush through those." Key point: Action is where most people are too vague. Result is where most people forget numbers.

Job Search Bootcamp

Weak vs. Strong STAR

Vague Answer

Generic action, no measurable result.

"We had a problem with customer churn. I worked with the team to fix it. We improved retention."

Specific Answer

Clear action steps, quantified outcome.

"Monthly churn hit 8%. I analyzed the exit surveys, identified onboarding as the gap, and redesigned the first-week experience. Churn dropped to 4.5% in 60 days."

42:00 - 44:00 | Side-by-side comparison. Read both aloud. COACH: "Read both. The weak version could be said by anyone on that team. The strong version could only be said by the person who did the work. That's the standard we're aiming for." Key point: The strong version has three things the weak one doesn't: a number, a specific action, and a measurable result.

Job Search Bootcamp

STAR Mistakes to Avoid

Situation

Too Much Setup

Keep context to 2 sentences. The interviewer wants to hear what you did, not 3 minutes of background.

Task

Team vs. You

Say "I" not "we." Interviewers want your contribution, not the group project.

Action

Skipping the How

Don't jump from problem to result. Walk through your decision-making process step by step.

Result

No Numbers

Every result needs a metric. Revenue, percentage, time saved, team size. If you can't quantify it, estimate it.

44:00 - 46:00 | Walk through each mistake. One sentence per card. COACH: "Four mistakes I see in almost every first draft. Too much setup, saying 'we' instead of 'I,' skipping the how, and forgetting to quantify the result. Watch for all four as you build your answers." TRANSITION: "Let me show you how to fix these with a real example."

How Mock Rounds Work

Groups of 3

One interviewer, one interviewee, one observer. Rotate after each round.

2 Questions Each

Interviewer picks from the question bank. Interviewee answers in STAR format. 2-3 minutes per answer.

Feedback Focus

Observer gives feedback on: structure (did it follow STAR?), specificity (were there numbers?), and energy (did they sound confident?).
