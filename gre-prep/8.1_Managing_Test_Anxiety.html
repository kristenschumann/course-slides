The Reality

Test anxiety is normal

Test anxiety affects almost everyone. The good news: anxiety is manageable, and some of it is actually helpful.

The Problem

Thinking "I'm nervous, something is wrong" makes anxiety worse

The Reframe

Thinking "My body is getting ready to perform" improves your score

Research

Students who reframe anxiety as excitement score higher on tests.

0:05 - 1:30 | Normalize anxiety. The nervous feeling before a test is the same response athletes have before competition. The key is how you interpret it. Research shows reframing anxiety as excitement leads to better performance.

Long-Term Prep

Days before the test

Practice Under Test Conditions

Take at least one practice test with real timing and environment. Familiarity reduces fear.

Protect Your Sleep

Get 7-8 hours the two nights before. Never sacrifice sleep for last-minute studying.

Watch Your Caffeine

Caffeine mimics anxiety symptoms. If already nervous, extra coffee makes it worse.

Have a Plan

Know what you are bringing, how you are getting there, and what to eat. Planning removes uncertainty.

1:30 - 3:00 | Walk through each long-term preparation strategy. The best anxiety reducer is preparation itself. These four actions in the days before the test make a real difference.

Day-Of Technique

Grounding and self-talk

Grounding (5-4-3)

If panic rises, focus on physical sensations.

  • Notice 5 things you can see
  • 4 things you can touch
  • 3 things you can hear

Pulls you out of anxious thoughts and into the present.

Positive Self-Talk

Replace negative thoughts with realistic ones.

Instead ofSay
"I'm going to fail""I've prepared"
"I don't know anything""I know a lot"
"Everyone is smarter""I belong here"
4:00 - 5:00 | Two more day-of techniques. Grounding pulls you out of anxious spirals by focusing on physical sensations. Self-talk replaces catastrophic thoughts with realistic ones. Both take under a minute.

During the Test

When anxiety spikes

  • If you feel stuck: Mark the question, move on, and come back later. One wrong answer does not ruin your score.
  • If your heart is racing: Pause for 5 seconds. Take one deep breath. Then continue. You do not need to calm down completely.
  • If you feel overwhelmed: Focus only on the question in front of you. Not the test, not your score, just this one question.
  • If you blank on something: Skip it. Move to easier questions first. The answer often comes back when you stop forcing it.
5:00 - 6:00 | Cover in-test anxiety management. The key message: you have strategies for every anxiety trigger. The worst thing you can do is spiral on a hard question.

Reset between sections

Why it works

Even one round of this resets your nervous system and shifts your focus to the next section. Don't analyze how you did — just breathe and move forward.

6:00 - 6:30 | The 1-minute break between sections is a reset opportunity. Students often spend it replaying the last section. Instead: do one round of 4-4-4 breathing and mentally start fresh. The goal is to enter the next section focused on it, not distracted by the previous one.

Perspective

Keep it in context

The GRE is one part of your application. A bad day will not ruin your career.

You can retake the test. You can use ScoreSelect. Many successful grad students had imperfect GRE scores.

Mindset

Trust your preparation. Stay focused on the current question. Don't think ahead about your score.

6:30 - 7:30 | Reassure students that the GRE is important but not the only factor. Mention retake options and ScoreSelect. Key mindset: trust the preparation you have done. Focus only on the question in front of you. Thinking about score while testing hurts performance.

Key Takeaways

Managing test anxiety

  • Reframe nervousness as your body getting ready to perform, not a sign that something is wrong
  • Prepare thoroughly and practice under real test conditions so nothing feels unfamiliar
  • Protect your sleep the two nights before the test, and avoid extra caffeine
  • Use box breathing before and during the test to calm your nervous system in under a minute
  • Focus on one question at a time and mark-and-move when you feel stuck
  • Use the 1-minute break to reset with 4-4-4 breathing — enter each new section fresh, not distracted by the last one
7:30 - 8:00 | Summary slide. Recap all six key strategies. End with reassurance: anxiety is normal, manageable, and does not have to control your performance.
