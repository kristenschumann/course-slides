Section 1

What a Cap Table Is and Why VCs Obsess Over It

1:15 | Transition into cap table fundamentals.

VC Recruiting Course

The basic structure

Rows = Shareholders

Founders, institutional investors, angel investors, option pool holders. Every person or entity with an ownership stake gets a row.

Columns = Attributes

Name, share class (common, Series A preferred, Series B preferred), number of shares, percentage ownership, and fully diluted ownership.

Share Classes

Common stock goes to founders and employees. Preferred stock goes to investors. Each funding round creates a new class of preferred with distinct rights.

1:15 - 2:00 | A cap table is the ownership blueprint for a company. [Click] Rows are shareholders: founders, investors, option pool holders. [Click] Columns track attributes: name, share class, share count, percentage ownership. At seed stage this might be ten rows in a Google Sheet. By Series C it can be 200 rows across multiple share classes. [Click] Common stock for founders and employees, preferred stock for investors. Each round creates a new class of preferred.

VC Recruiting Course

Three reasons cap tables matter

Ownership Drives Economics

When a VC invests $5M at a $20M pre-money, they are buying 20%. But 20% of what? The cap table tells you exactly what that percentage looks like in shares, price, and rights.

Cap Tables Reveal History

A seed investor who owns 30% after putting in $500K signals something went wrong in negotiation. The cap table is a forensic record of every deal the company has done.

Cap Tables Determine Outcomes

A founder who owns 15% on paper might get less than an investor who owns 10%, depending on liquidation preferences. The cap table shows who gets paid and in what order.

2:00 - 2:45 | Three reasons VCs obsess over cap tables. [Click] Ownership drives economics. Without the cap table, ownership is a guess. [Click] Cap tables reveal history. I can look at one and tell whether the founders negotiated well or got taken advantage of. A seed investor at 30% for $500K is a red flag. [Click] Cap tables determine outcomes. The cap table tells you what people own. Liquidation preferences tell you what they actually get. We will cover that distinction in Section 4.

Section 2

How Dilution Actually Works

3:30 | Transition into dilution mechanics.

VC Recruiting Course

Dilution is mechanical, not mysterious

New ownership % = Your shares / New total shares outstanding

When a company issues new shares, existing shareholders' percentage ownership goes down. Their number of shares stays the same. But the total pie got bigger, so their slice is a smaller fraction of the whole. Dilution happens through share issuance, not through losing shares.

3:30 - 4:00 | Dilution is straightforward once you see it mechanically. Your new ownership equals your shares divided by the new total shares outstanding. Your share count does not change. The denominator gets bigger. That is all dilution is.

VC Recruiting Course

Smaller slice, bigger pie

The Math That Matters

A founder who owns 40% of a $50M company has $20M in value. Dilute to 30% of a $200M company and that is $60M. They own less of a much bigger pie. The absolute dollar value tripled.

The Fundraising Lever

A founder who raises $3M at $12M pre gives up 20%. The same $3M at $27M pre gives up 10%. Same cash, half the dilution. Raising at the right valuation is the most consequential financial decision a founder makes.

5:30 - 6:00 | Key insight: dilution is not inherently bad if the value of your remaining shares grows faster than your percentage shrinks. [Click] A founder at 40% of a $50M company has $20M. Dilute to 30% of $200M and that is $60M. The absolute value tripled. That is the trade: dilution is the cost, growth is the return. [Click] And founders who raise at higher valuations give up less equity per dollar. Same cash, half the dilution. That is why fundraising at the right valuation matters.
Section 3

Option Pools: The Hidden Dilution Event

6:00 | Transition into option pool mechanics.

VC Recruiting Course

What an option pool is

A block of shares reserved for future employees. Stock options for engineers, executives, and salespeople as part of compensation. Every venture-backed startup has one.

The standard at Series A is about 14% of fully diluted shares. Late-stage companies often carry pools closer to 20%. The pool gets created as part of the financing. The fight is over how big it should be and when it gets created.

6:00 - 6:30 | An option pool is shares reserved for future employees. Every venture-backed startup has one. The standard at Series A is about 14%. The contentious part is not the pool itself, it is the timing of when it gets created.

VC Recruiting Course

Why pre-money pool creation matters

Without Option Pool

$10M pre-money, $2.5M investment, $12.5M post-money. Investor gets 20%. Founders retain 80%. Clean and simple.

With 15% Pre-Money Pool

Same terms, but a 15% option pool is created before the investment closes. The pool comes out of the founders' side. Founders end up with 65%, not 80%. Investor still owns 20%. Pool owns 15%.

6:30 - 7:15 | Here is the key mechanic. Investors almost always require the pool to be created pre-money. [Click] Without a pool: $10M pre, $2.5M investment, founders keep 80%, investor gets 20%. [Click] With a 15% pre-money pool: the dilution falls entirely on the founders. They end up with 65% instead of 80%. The investor still owns exactly 20%. This is a negotiation point and a real one.

VC Recruiting Course

How founders push back

The Investor's Play

Push for a larger pool (20%+) so the company can hire longer without expanding the pool, which would dilute the investor. An oversized pool is effectively a free call option at the founders' expense.

The Founder's Counter

Size the pool to the actual 12-18 month hiring plan. "We need a VP Engineering at 1.5%, three senior engineers at 0.3% each, a head of sales at 0.8%." Specificity is the founders' best weapon.

7:15 - 8:00 | Founders push for smaller pools, investors push for larger ones. [Click] An investor who demands 20% without reference to the hiring plan is building a buffer at the founders' expense. Unallocated pool shares dilute everyone now but serve no purpose if they are never granted. [Click] Sophisticated founders counter with a specific hiring plan. That specificity justifies a 12% pool instead of 20%. The interview answer: the pool dilutes existing shareholders, not the new investor. That is the key point.
Section 4

Liquidation Preferences and Waterfall Analysis

8:00 | Transition into how money actually flows at exit.

VC Recruiting Course

1x non-participating preferred (standard)

Preferred shareholders get paid before common shareholders. The preference defines the minimum amount the investor receives before anyone else sees a dollar.

With 1x non-participating preferred, the investor chooses whichever is higher: take their money back (the preference) or convert to common stock and take their pro-rata share. At low exits, they take the preference. At high exits, they convert. The preference is downside protection, not upside capture.

8:00 - 8:30 | A liquidation preference determines who gets paid first at exit. The standard is 1x non-participating. The investor has a choice: take their money back, or convert to common and take their percentage. They pick whichever is higher. At low exits, the preference protects them. At high exits, they convert. Simple.

8:30 - 9:00 | Walk through the waterfall. Investor put in $10M, owns 20%. [Click] At a $30M exit, the preference gives them $10M. [Click] Converting gives them 20% of $30M, which is $6M. [Click] They take the preference. $10M is better than $6M. [Click] At $100M, converting gives $20M, which beats the $10M preference. They convert. Founders get $80M. At high outcomes, the preference becomes irrelevant.

Participating vs. non-participating at $100M exit

Structure Preference Participation Investor Total Founders Get
Non-participating $10M or convert -- $20M (converts) $80M
Participating $10M off the top + 20% of $90M $28M $72M
9:00 - 9:45 | Now the aggressive version. [Click] Non-participating: investor converts, takes $20M at a $100M exit. Standard. [Click] Participating preferred: investor gets their $10M back AND 20% of the remaining $90M. That is $28M instead of $20M. The extra $8M comes directly from the founders. This is called double dipping. [Click] Participating with a 3x cap limits the total payout, but at this exit value the investor is under the cap anyway. Participating preferred is less common but still shows up in later-stage deals.

VC Recruiting Course

How to run a waterfall step by step

  • Pay senior preferences first. Later rounds usually have seniority. Series B gets paid before Series A.
  • Pay junior preferences next. Work through each investor class in order of seniority.
  • Check for conversion. If any preferred investor would do better converting to common, they convert.
  • Distribute remaining proceeds. Whatever is left flows to common holders (founders, employees with exercised options).
9:45 - 10:15 | In an interview, go step by step. Pay senior preferences first, then junior, check if anyone is better off converting, then distribute the remainder to common. Write it out. Do not try to do it in your head. The candidate who works methodically beats the candidate who tries to be fast and gets the wrong answer.

VC Recruiting Course

Key takeaways

  • A cap table is the financial DNA of a company. Every negotiation, every round, every equity hire is encoded in it. Reading one fluently is non-negotiable for VC work.
  • Dilution is the cost of funding growth. The question is never whether the founder got diluted. It is whether they got diluted at a valuation that reflects progress.
  • Option pools dilute existing shareholders, not the incoming investor. The pre-money vs. post-money distinction is the single most important thing to understand about pool mechanics.
  • Ownership percentages lie without liquidation preferences. Two investors can own the same percentage and walk away with dramatically different payouts.
  • In interviews, write out the waterfall step by step. Methodical beats fast. The candidate who works through the math carefully wins.
