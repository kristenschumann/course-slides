Mixture Formula

The weighted table

Core principle: Total pure substance $=$ sum of pure substance from each part.

Component Concentration Amount Pure Substance
Solution 1 $c_1$ $v_1$ $c_1 \times v_1$
Solution 2 $c_2$ $v_2$ $c_2 \times v_2$
Mixture $c_{\text{mix}}$ $v_1 + v_2$ $c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2$
0:40-2:00 | The weighted table. Fill in concentration, amount, and pure substance for each component. The mixture row totals the amounts and the pure substance. The key equation: pure substance in = pure substance out.

Example 1

Basic concentration problem

Problem

10 gal of 30% alcohol mixed with 5 gal of 60% alcohol. What is the mixture concentration?

Solution

Pure alcohol: $0.30(10) + 0.60(5) = 3 + 3 = 6$

Concentration $= \frac{6}{15}$

Answer: $40\%$ (not $45\%$)

2:00-3:00 | Example 1: the hook problem. Pure alcohol from each part: 3 + 3 = 6 gal. Total volume = 15 gal. Concentration = 6/15 = 40%. Not 45% because there is more of the weaker solution.

Example 2

Adding pure substance

Problem

A 20 L solution is 15% salt. How many liters of pure salt must be added to make 25%?

Solution

Original salt: $0.15(20) = 3$ L. Added: $x$ L at 100%

$3 + x = 0.25(20 + x)$

$3 + x = 5 + 0.25x$, so $0.75x = 2$

Answer: $x = \frac{8}{3} \approx 2.67$ L

3:00-4:20 | Example 2: adding pure salt. Original has 3 L of salt. After adding x L of pure salt (100%), total salt = 3 + x, total volume = 20 + x. Set equal to 25% and solve.

Replacement Problems

Example 3

Drain and replace

Problem

100 gal of 40% antifreeze. Some is drained and replaced with water. Result is 25%. How much was drained?

Solution

Original antifreeze: $40$ gal

Drain $x$ gal (removes $0.4x$ antifreeze)

$40 - 0.4x = 25$, so $0.4x = 15$

Answer: $x = 37.5$ gal

4:30-5:40 | Example 3: replacement problem. We drain x gallons of 40% solution and replace with water (0%). Water adds no antifreeze. Remaining antifreeze = 40 - 0.4x = 25 (since final is 25% of 100 gal). Solve for x = 37.5 gallons.

Example 4

Price mixture

Problem

Brand A (\$8/lb) mixed with Brand B (\$12/lb) for 20 lbs at \$9.50/lb. How much of each?

Solution

A $= x$ lb, B $= 20 - x$ lb

$8x + 12(20 - x) = 190$

$8x + 240 - 12x = 190$, so $-4x = -50$

A $=$ $12.5$ lb, B $=$ $7.5$ lb

5:50-7:00 | Example 4: price mixture. Let A = x, B = 20 - x (LEGO inference). Total cost = $190. Solve 8x + 12(20-x) = 190 to get x = 12.5 lbs of A and 7.5 lbs of B.

Example 5

Finding the mixing ratio

Problem

Cashews (\$10/lb) and peanuts (\$4/lb). What ratio gives \$6/lb?

Solution

Cashews: \$10, diff from \$6 $= +4$

Peanuts: \$4, diff from \$6 $= -2$

Ratio (inverse of diffs): $2{:}4$

Cashews:Peanuts $=$ $1{:}2$

7:00-7:50 | Example 5: the allegation shortcut. Find differences from the target price. The mixing ratio is the inverse of those differences. Cashews to peanuts = 2:4 = 1:2. Coaching tip: the allegation method (also called the alligation diagram) is the fastest approach for any two-component mixture. Draw the X-diagram: put the two concentrations on the left, the target in the middle, and cross-subtract. The results give you the mixing ratio directly.

Shortcut

The allegation method

Ratio of Low to High $= |\text{High} - \text{Target}| : |\text{Low} - \text{Target}|$

Example A

Mix 30% and 60% to get 45%

$|60-45| : |30-45| = 15:15 =$ $1:1$

Example B

Mix 20% and 50% to get 30%

$|50-30| : |20-30| = 20:10 =$ $2:1$

7:50-9:00 | The allegation method for quickly finding mixing ratios. The ratio of the low-concentration to the high-concentration equals the absolute difference of the high from target to the low from target. Walk through both examples.

Key Takeaways

Mixture problem toolkit

  • Weighted table - Fill in concentration, amount, and pure substance for each part
  • Key equation - Pure substance in $=$ pure substance out ($c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 = c_{\text{mix}} \cdot v_{\text{total}}$)
  • Replacement problems - Draining removes pure substance, adding water adds none
  • Price mixtures - Same formula with price replacing concentration
  • Allegation shortcut - Ratio of Low:High $= |\text{High} - \text{Target}| : |\text{Low} - \text{Target}|$
9:00-10:00 | Summary: the weighted table works for all mixture problems. Track pure substance. Use the allegation shortcut for quick ratio finding. This applies to concentration, price, and weighted average problems. Remember: mixtures are closely related to weighted averages. The alligation method and the sum-of-deviations-equals-zero concept are two sides of the same coin. If you master one, the other follows naturally.
