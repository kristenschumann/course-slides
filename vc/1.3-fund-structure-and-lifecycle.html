Core Concept

A VC Fund Is a Clock

Every dollar has a timestamp, and every phase of the fund's life changes what a GP can and cannot do. Understanding that clock is how you understand why VCs behave the way they do.

[0:00-0:30] Hook — frame the central metaphor. A fund is not a bank account. It has a fixed lifespan, and the phase of the fund determines GP behavior. A GP who loves a deal in year two might pass on the exact same deal in year six — because the clock changed, not the deal.

Portfolio Companies

Where capital is deployed

[2:30-3:15] The centerpiece document is the Limited Partnership Agreement — the LPA. It outlines fund duration, management fee structure, carried interest terms, the distribution waterfall, and investment restrictions. [click] GP entity at the top. [click] The Management Company handles day-to-day operations — employs the investment team, pays rent, covers travel — funded by management fees. As covered in 1.1, management fees typically run at 2% of committed capital during the investment period. [click] The Fund LP is the actual investment vehicle. [click] Capital flows down into portfolio companies.

Raising Capital

Closes, Commitments, and Skin in the Game

First & Final Close

First close at 40-60% of target lets deployment begin. Fundraising continues until final close.

GP Commitment

GPs contribute 1-3% of total fund size. For a $200M fund, that is $2-6M from the GP team's own pockets.

[3:15-4:30] Fundraising mirrors startup fundraising — pitch decks, data rooms, LP meetings. [click] The first close — typically 40 to 60 percent of target — lets the GP start deploying while continuing to raise. [click] GP commitment of 1-3% is skin in the game that LPs insist on. As covered in 1.1, this alignment of incentives is fundamental to the LP-GP relationship. For a $200M fund, that is $2-6M from the partners personally. This shapes who can and cannot start a fund — you need personal capital or partners who have it.

The Investment Period

Deploying capital and building the portfolio

[4:30] Transition to the investment period — the most active phase.

Years 1-5

Deploying Capital Just-in-Time

Capital Call

GP issues notice; LPs wire pro-rata share within 10-15 business days

Deal Flow & Screening

Sourcing and filtering investment opportunities

Due Diligence

Deep analysis of target companies

Term Sheet & Wire

Negotiate terms and deploy capital

[4:30-5:15] LPs do not write a single check on day one. As covered in 1.1, capital is called as needed — just-in-time. [click] When the fund closes a deal, the GP sends a capital call notice, and LPs wire their pro-rata share within 10-15 business days. LPs committed across multiple funds can face overlapping capital calls in the same quarter. [click] Deal flow and screening. [click] Deep due diligence. [click] Term sheet negotiation and wiring the investment.

Critical Decision

Reserves Separate the Best Funds

The $200M Split

  • $80-100M for initial investments (40-50%)
  • $100-120M reserved for follow-on rounds (50-60%)

Why It Matters

  • Without reserves, a fund cannot exercise pro-rata rights on its own winners
  • Deploying too fast means getting diluted out of the best deals
  • Management fees run at full rate: 2% of committed capital
[5:15-6:00] This is where funds live or die. [click] Best practice: reserve 50-60% of capital for follow-on investments in strongest portfolio companies. A $200M fund deploys $80-100M into initial checks and holds $100-120M for follow-ons. [click] Without reserves, a fund finds a winner, holds pro-rata rights, and cannot exercise them. Getting this ratio right is one of the most consequential portfolio construction decisions a GP makes. During this period, management fees run at the full 2% of committed capital.
Part 4

Portfolio Management

Supporting winners and cutting losses

[6:00] Transition to portfolio management phase.

Years 3-8

Where GPs Earn Their Carry

Board-Level Work

Governance decisions, strategic pivots, crisis management, executive hiring, business development, follow-on financing strategy.

Quarterly Monitoring

KPIs, burn rates, revenue growth, customer metrics, company health versus underwriting assumptions.

[6:00-7:00] Starting around year three, the balance shifts from sourcing new deals to supporting existing ones. GPs take board seats — they are in the room for governance, pivots, and crises. [click] Board-level work includes executive hiring, opening doors for BD, advising on financing strategy, and having direct, uncomfortable conversations when needed. [click] On the monitoring side, quarterly portfolio reviews track KPIs, burn rates, revenue, customer metrics, and overall health relative to original underwriting assumptions.

Industry Wisdom

"Lemons Ripen Early"

Underperformers show themselves within the first 2-3 years. Missed milestones, founder-market fit questions, and burn rates that do not match traction are early warning signals. Recognizing this early and reallocating follow-on capital to the winners separates great fund managers from average ones.

[7:00-7:30] Classic industry saying. The companies that will struggle tend to signal it well before the winners become obvious. This forces some of the hardest decisions in venture — cutting losses on underperformers and concentrating follow-on capital into the portfolio companies showing real traction and momentum.
Part 5

Exit and Harvesting

Turning paper gains into real returns

[7:30] Transition to exits and distributions.

Years 5-10+

Three Primary Exit Paths

Exit Type Description Key Consideration
M&A Larger company acquires portfolio company Most common exit route
IPO Company lists on public exchange Headline-grabbing; less frequent
Secondary Sale GP sells stake to another investor Structured transaction or direct share sale
[7:30-8:15] Exits are when everything becomes real. "Marks are opinions. Distributions are cash." [click] M&A is the most common path — a larger company acquires the portfolio company. [click] IPOs grab headlines but are less frequent. [click] Secondary sales — selling stakes to other investors — offer liquidity outside of traditional exits. Timing exits is part art, part science: balancing remaining growth potential against market conditions and the fund's own timeline pressures.

Follow the Money

How Profits Flow Back

Return of Capital

LPs receive every contributed dollar back first

Preferred Return

If applicable — 8% hurdle is standard in PE but rare in VC. When present, LPs receive this before carry kicks in.

Carried Interest

Remaining profits split 80/20 (LP/GP)

Fee Step-Down

Management fees drop to 1-1.5% on invested capital

[8:15-9:00] The distribution waterfall is defined in the LPA and governs the order profits flow. [click] First: LPs get every dollar of contributed capital back. No one else sees a dime. [click] Then the preferred return — if applicable. An 8% annual hurdle is standard in PE buyout funds, but many VC funds do not include one. When present, LPs receive this return before carry kicks in. [click] Only after those hurdles are cleared does carry kick in: 80% to LPs, 20% to the GP. Carry is the reward for performance — not for showing up, not for trying, but for actually returning cash above the hurdle. [click] Management fees also step down after the investment period — from 2% of committed to 1-1.5% of invested capital, reflecting reduced operational intensity.

Putting It All Together

1-3 Deploy $80M into 22 deals (enterprise software + fintech) $80M out
3-5 $40M in follow-on rounds to strongest performers $40M out
4 First exit: M&A returns $15M $15M in

Final $500M total returned on $200M fund 2.5x TVPI, ~15% net IRR
[9:00-10:30] Tying it all together with a hypothetical $200M Fund I. [click] Year zero: raise $200M with a first close at $120M. Years 1-3: deploy $80M into 22 initial investments. Years 3-5: deploy $40M in follow-ons to the strongest companies, keeping $80M in reserve. [click] Year 4: first exit, an M&A deal returning $15M. Solid but not a fund-maker. Year 7: a portfolio company IPOs — the fund's stake generates $90M. That is the kind of outcome that defines a fund. [click] Year 10: remaining positions wound down. Final math: $500M total value on $200M invested. 2.5x TVPI, approximately 15% net IRR — a strong result, comfortably above most LP benchmarks. Note the overlapping fund structure: by years 8-10, the GP has usually raised and begun deploying Fund II, III, even Fund IV.

Return Patterns

The J-Curve Explained

NAV Over Fund Life

Plot Net Asset Value over 10 years. The curve dips below zero in years 1-3, inflects upward years 4-6, then rises sharply through years 6-10+.

Early Years: Negative Returns

Capital deployment, management fees, early write-offs pull NAV below contributed capital.

Inflection (Years 4-6)

First meaningful exits and follow-on markups.

Harvest (Years 6-10+)

Strong exits drive NAV sharply upward.

[10:30-11:15] Most people expect steady growth from day one. It does not work that way. [click] Plot NAV over ten years and you see the J-curve. [click] Early years: returns are negative. Deploying capital, paying fees, absorbing early write-offs. This is the model working as designed. [click] Inflection around years 4-6 as first exits materialize and surviving companies get marked up from follow-on rounds. [click] Then the harvest years, where strong exits drive NAV sharply upward. The J-curve is also why the industry uses vintage year comparisons — much like wine. A 2019 fund is compared to other 2019 funds because macro conditions, valuations, and exit markets affect all funds from the same era equally. As covered in 1.1, vintage year is essential context for benchmarking performance.

Years 10+

Wind-Down, Extensions, and Overlapping Funds

Fund Extensions

Most LPAs include 1-2 year extension provisions. GPs request extensions to maximize exit value on remaining positions rather than forcing fire sales.

Orderly Wind-Down

Remaining portfolio companies are exited via M&A, secondaries, or in some cases written off. Final distributions are made and the fund formally dissolves.

Overlapping Funds

By years 8-10, a GP is typically managing Fund I wind-down, Fund II portfolio management, and Fund III deployment — simultaneously.

[11:15-11:45] The 10-year fund life is a starting point, not a hard stop. [click] Most LPAs include provisions for 1-2 year extensions, typically requiring LP advisory committee approval. GPs use these to avoid forced exits at unfavorable valuations. [click] During wind-down, remaining positions are exited through M&A, secondary sales, or write-offs. Final distributions flow through the waterfall, and the fund formally dissolves. [click] Here is the reality of a successful GP's life: they are simultaneously managing wind-down on Fund I, active portfolio management on Fund II, and deploying capital from Fund III. This overlapping fund structure means GPs are perpetually in multiple phases of the lifecycle at once — which is why understanding the full cycle matters so much.

Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • A VC fund is a clock, not a bank account — the 10-year lifecycle creates urgency at every phase
  • Know the LPA cold — it governs fees, carry, distributions, and restrictions
  • Follow-on reserves (50-60%) separate the best funds from the rest
  • The J-curve means negative early returns are a feature, not a bug
  • Understanding the lifecycle explains all VC behavior — aggressive in year two, cautious in year eight, always fundraising
[11:45-12:15] Recap the five key takeaways. [click] The fund is a clock — every phase changes what the GP can and should do. [click] The LPA is the governing document for everything. In interviews, you must speak to LPA mechanics. [click] Reserving 50-60% for follow-ons sounds conservative until a fund gets diluted out of its own best deal. [click] The J-curve: patience is literally engineered into the venture model. [click] When you understand the lifecycle, you understand VC behavior — why they act differently at each stage, and why they are always fundraising.
