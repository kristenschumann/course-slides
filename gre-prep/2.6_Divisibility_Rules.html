GRE Applications

Using divisibility rules to solve problems

5:00-5:10 | Transition: let's apply these rules to GRE-style problems.

Example 1

Missing digit problem

Problem

The 5-digit number $5a{,}2b4$ is divisible by 36. What is $a + b$?

$36 = 4 \times 9$, so test both rules.

Solution

By 4: Last two digits $b4$ divisible by 4, so $b \in \{0,2,4,6,8\}$

By 9: $5+a+2+b+4 = 11+a+b$ must be divisible by 9

Combine: $11+a+b$ must be 18 or 27, so $a+b = 7$ or $a+b = 16$

Both valid: $a+b=$ $7$ or $16$ (e.g. $a=1,b=6$ or $a=8,b=8$)

5:10-6:30 | Example 1: 36 equals 4 times 9, so test both rules. By 4, the last two digits b4 must form a number divisible by 4, so b is even. By 9, the digit sum 11 plus a plus b must be divisible by 9. Since a and b are single digits (0-9), 11+a+b ranges from 11 to 29. The multiples of 9 in that range are 18 and 27. So a+b equals 7 or a+b equals 16. For a+b=7 with b even: (a=7,b=0), (a=5,b=2), (a=3,b=4), (a=1,b=6). For a+b=16 with b even: (a=8,b=8). Both solutions are valid. This teaches thoroughness — always check all possible multiples of 9 in the range. [Leland+ Tip] For composite divisors, always break into prime-power components.

Example 2

Eliminate by divisibility

Problem

Which is NOT divisible by 15?

(A) 105   (B) 225   (C) 335

(D) 345   (E) 405

$15 = 3 \times 5$. Check both.

Solution

All end in 5, so all are divisible by 5.

Check digit sums for divisibility by 3:

105: 6 Yes | 225: 9 Yes | 335: 11 No | 345: 12 Yes | 405: 9 Yes

Answer: (C) 335

6:30-7:30 | Example 2: 15 equals 3 times 5. All choices end in 5, so all pass the 5 test. Check digit sums for 3: 105 sums to 6, passes. 225 sums to 9, passes. 335 sums to 11, fails. 345 sums to 12, passes. 405 sums to 9, passes. Answer is C, 335.

Example 3

Greatest 3-digit multiple

Problem

What is the greatest 3-digit number divisible by both 6 and 9?

Solution

Step 1: Find LCM: $6 = 2 \times 3$, $9 = 3^2$, so LCM $= 2 \times 3^2 = 18$

Step 2: Divide: $999 \div 18 = 55.5$

Step 3: Round down: $55 \times 18$

Answer: $990$

7:30-8:20 | Example 3: divisible by both 6 and 9 means divisible by the LCM. 6 is 2 times 3, 9 is 3 squared, LCM is 18. Divide 999 by 18 to get 55.5. Round down to 55, multiply by 18 to get 990.

Key Takeaways

Divisibility rules

  • By 2 - Last digit is even
  • By 3 and 9 - Sum the digits. Divisible by 3? By 9? Same test, different threshold.
  • By 4 and 8 - Check last 2 digits (for 4) or last 3 digits (for 8)
  • By 5 and 10 - Last digit is 0 or 5 (for 5), or just 0 (for 10)
  • Composite divisors - Break into coprime factors and check both (6 = 2 and 3, 12 = 3 and 4)
8:20-9:00 | Summary: the most useful rules are for 2, 3, 4, 5, and 9. For composite divisors, break them into coprime factors and check both conditions. These rules turn long division into instant pattern recognition on the GRE.
