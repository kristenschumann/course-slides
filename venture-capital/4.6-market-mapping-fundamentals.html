Section 1

What a Market Map Actually Is

1:00 | Transition into defining a real market map.

VC Recruiting Course

A list of logos is not a market map

The Bad Map

Company logos grouped by vague categories like "infrastructure," "platform," and "applications." No funding data. No competitive positioning. No insight about where the market is heading. This is a directory, not an analytical tool.

The Good Map

Companies organized by value chain position with segmentation by customer type, business model, and stage. Includes funding data, competitive notes, and explicit white space callouts. A reader immediately understands where capital has concentrated.

1:00 - 1:45 | I see this mistake constantly. Someone puts 30 logos on a slide, organizes them into three columns, and calls it a market map. [Click] That is a logo dump. No insight, no analysis. [Click] A real market map uses value chain logic to show competitive dynamics, opportunity gaps, and where value is being captured.

VC Recruiting Course

Partners want to see where the opportunity is

Descriptive Map

Shows you who is in the market. Company names, logos, basic categories. Available to anyone with a PitchBook login. Does not differentiate you as a candidate.

Analytical Map

Shows you where the opportunity is. Includes your competitive assessments, moat indicators, and white space hypotheses. Candidates who bring this to an interview immediately differentiate themselves.

[Click] An analytical map shows where the opportunity is. Your judgment, your competitive notes, your white space flags. That is unique to you.

The Seven Dimensions of a Great Map

2:45 | Transition into the seven dimensions.

VC Recruiting Course

Position, customer, and stage

Value Chain Position

Where does each company sit in the delivery of value? Each layer has different economics and defensibility.

Customer Segment

Enterprise, SMB, consumer, developer, government? Two identical technologies become different businesses depending on the buyer.

Stage and Funding

Pre-seed through public. Ten seed-stage companies and zero Series A companies in a category tells you something important about market maturity.

VC Recruiting Course

Business model and defensibility

Business Model

SaaS, usage-based, marketplace, transaction-fee, hardware-plus-software. Two companies in the same category with different models have radically different margin profiles and scaling characteristics.

Moat Indicators

Barriers to entry, switching costs, network effects, proprietary data, regulatory advantages. A category full of companies with no defensibility means margins will compress and most players will struggle.

4:15 - 4:45 | [Click] Dimension 4: business model is the economic engine. Don't group a SaaS company and a marketplace in the same bucket without noting the structural difference. [Click] Dimension 5: moat indicators. Not every company has a moat. That is an important finding.

VC Recruiting Course

Your judgment and the gaps

Competitive Notes

Your assessment of each company's strengths, weaknesses, and trajectory. "Strong product but weak GTM. Selling to enterprise with a bottoms-up tool. Likely needs to rebuild sales motion." This qualitative layer is where your unique value lives.

White Space Flags

Categories with no companies. Customer segments nobody is serving. Value chain positions where no startup has planted a flag. When you identify a gap, you have found a potential opportunity.

Section 4

White Space Analysis

7:15 | Transition into white space, the whole point of the exercise.

VC Recruiting Course

Where demand meets opportunity

High Demand + High Supply

Crowded Category

Pick the winner, not the category. Example: AI chatbots for customer support.

High Demand + Low Supply

Sweet Spot

Clear need, few startups. Example: AI-native compliance tools for community banks.

Low Demand + High Supply

Danger Zone

Lots of companies, no customer pull. Example: Blockchain for supply chain.

Low Demand + Low Supply

Watch List

Could be dead or too early. Look for an emerging catalyst in the next 12-24 months.

7:15 - 8:15 | White space is where market maps become investment tools. Four quadrants. [Click] High demand, high supply: crowded. The thesis is about picking the winner. [Click] High demand, low supply: the sweet spot. Clear need, few startups. [Click] Low demand, high supply: danger zone. Lots of building, no customer pull. [Click] Low demand, low supply: requires the most judgment. Is there an emerging catalyst?

VC Recruiting Course

Turn every gap into a testable thesis

"No company is addressing [specific problem] for [specific customer segment] because [specific reason]. I believe this will change when [specific catalyst occurs], creating an opportunity for [specific type of company]."

That is a white space hypothesis. It connects to the value chain: when you see a step that every company handles manually or outsources to legacy providers, that is structural white space. It is not just an absence of startups. It is an absence of modern solutions at a specific point in the value delivery process.

8:15 - 9:00 | When you identify white space, document your hypothesis using this structure. This is the building block of an investment thesis. When you see a gap in the chain, a step handled manually or by legacy providers, that is the highest-conviction kind of white space.

Key takeaways

  • A market map is not a logo collection. It is a structured analytical tool built on value chain logic. If your map lacks competitive notes, moat indicators, and white space flags, it is a directory, not a map.
  • Include all seven dimensions. Value chain position, customer segment, stage, business model, moat indicators, competitive notes, and white space flags.
  • White space is the entire point. High demand plus low startup supply is the quadrant that generates the best investment opportunities. Train your eye to look for gaps, not just logos.
  • Source beyond databases. PitchBook and CB Insights are starting points. The most valuable companies on your map will come from YC batch lists, founder Twitter, and patent filings.
  • Keep it alive. Update weekly, refresh quarterly, time-stamp everything. A living map is a career asset that compounds. A stale one is a liability.
