Premise, Conclusion
and the Gap

0:00 - 0:05 | Title slide. This video teaches how to break arguments into premises, conclusions, and the gap between them.

The Framework

Every argument has a gap

Every argument on the GRE has the same structure: premises that are supposed to lead to a conclusion. But most arguments have a gap, something that must be true for the conclusion to follow.

Key Idea

Finding the gap is the key to answering strengthen, weaken, and assumption questions. It tells you what the argument takes for granted.

0:05 - 0:30 | Set up the framework. Arguments have premises (evidence), a conclusion (claim), and a gap (unstated assumption). Finding the gap unlocks three major question types.

Definitions

Premises vs. conclusions

Premises

The facts, evidence, or reasons given. The "because" part of the argument.

Signal words: because, since, given that, as shown by, for the reason that

Conclusion

The claim the argument is trying to prove. The "therefore" part of the argument.

Signal words: therefore, thus, so, hence, consequently, we can conclude that

Tip

Sometimes there are no indicator words. Ask yourself: "What is being claimed, and what is offered as support?"

0:30 - 2:30 | Define premises and conclusions with signal words. Premises are the evidence. The conclusion is the claim. Walk through the coffee example: "caffeine increases alertness" is a premise; "coffee will make you more productive" is the conclusion.

Core Concept

Finding the gap

The gap is the logical space between premises and conclusion. It's an assumption the argument makes but doesn't state.

Example

Premise: "Caffeine increases alertness."

Conclusion: "You'll be more productive."

Gap: Does alertness necessarily lead to productivity? Maybe you're more alert but distracted.

How to Find It

  1. Identify exactly what the premises establish
  2. Identify exactly what the conclusion claims
  3. Ask: What must be true for the conclusion to follow?
2:30 - 5:00 | Teach the gap concept with the coffee/productivity example. The premises say "alertness" but the conclusion says "productivity." The gap is the assumption that alertness equals productivity.

Part 2

Common Gap Types

5:00 - 5:05 | Section divider. Now cover the four most common types of gaps in GRE arguments.

Patterns

4 common gap types

Term Shift

The conclusion uses a term that wasn't in the premises. "Satisfaction" in the premise becomes "turnover" in the conclusion.

Causal Gap

The premise shows correlation, but the conclusion assumes causation. Does A cause B, or does B cause A, or does C cause both?

Representativeness

Evidence is from one group, but the conclusion applies to another. Are college students representative of "most people"?

Implementation

Premise shows something works in testing, but the conclusion says it will work in practice. Will real-world conditions differ?

5:05 - 7:00 | Walk through each gap type with examples. Term shift is the most common. Causal gaps appear in any argument that draws conclusions from correlations. Representativeness gaps appear in arguments based on studies or surveys.

Application

Using the gap for argument-based questions

Question Type What the Correct Answer Does Relationship to the Gap
Assumption Fills the gap States what must be true for the conclusion to follow
Strengthen Supports the assumption Provides evidence that the gap isn't a problem
Weaken Attacks the assumption Shows the gap IS a problem
Example

"Sales rose after the ad campaign, so the campaign worked." Gap: the campaign caused the increase. Weaken: "A competitor went out of business at the same time."

7:00 - 8:30 | Show how the gap concept applies to three question types. Assumption questions ask for the gap itself. Strengthen supports the assumption. Weaken attacks it. Use the ad campaign example for all three.

Practice

Finding the gap in action

"City X should hire more garbage collectors. City X has more residents per collector than City Y, and City Y has cleaner streets."

Breakdown

  • P1: X has more residents per collector than Y
  • P2: Y has cleaner streets
  • Conclusion: X should hire more collectors
  • Gap: Collector ratio causes the difference

Predictions

  • Weaken: "Y has stricter littering laws"
  • Strengthen: "Only difference is collector count"
  • Assumption: "Collector ratio affects cleanliness"
8:30 - 9:30 | Walk through a full practice example. Identify the premises, conclusion, and gap. Then predict answers for all three question types. This shows how one gap analysis unlocks multiple questions.

Key Takeaways

Premise, conclusion, and the gap

  • Identify the premises - the facts, evidence, or reasons given to support the argument's claim
  • Identify the conclusion - the claim being made, often signaled by "therefore," "thus," or "so"
  • Find the gap - ask what must be true for the conclusion to follow from the premises
  • Recognize gap types - term shifts, causal claims, representativeness issues, and implementation assumptions
  • Use the gap to predict answers - assumptions fill the gap, strengthen supports it, weaken attacks it
9:30 - 10:00 | Summary slide. The gap is the central concept for argument-based reading questions. Find it, and you can predict the correct answer for assumption, strengthen, and weaken questions.
