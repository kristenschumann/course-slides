Interpreting Graphs:
Bar, Line, Pie & More

0:00-0:20 | Hook: Every GRE includes 2-3 data interpretation sets. They use bar graphs, line graphs, pie charts, stacked bars, and scatter plots. Knowing what each reveals and hides is essential.

Bar Graphs

Comparing categories

  • Vertical & horizontal bars: Compare heights or lengths directly. Most common on the GRE
  • Stacked bars: Show parts within each bar. Subtract to find individual segment sizes
  • Grouped bars: Compare multiple variables side by side across categories
0:20-1:30 | Four types of bar graphs. Vertical is most common. Horizontal for many categories. Stacked shows composition within each bar. Grouped compares multiple variables side by side. Strategy tip: before looking at any questions, spend 30 seconds scanning the display for title, labels, units, scale, and legend. Check if the y-axis starts at zero. This prevents the most common DI mistakes.

Example 1

Bar graph: Percent increase

By what percent did revenue increase from Q2 to Q4?

Solution

Change: $\$65\text{M} - \$55\text{M} = \$10\text{M}$

Percent increase: $\frac{10}{55} \times 100\%$

Answer: $\approx 18.2\%$

1:30-2:20 | Example: percent increase from Q2 to Q4. The change is 10M. Divide by the original (Q2 = 55M). 10/55 is approximately 18.2%.

Watch Out

The truncated axis trap

Y-axis starts at 0

Y-axis starts at 50

Trap

A bar that appears twice as tall does NOT mean twice the value. Always check where the axis starts.

Solution

Read the actual values from the scale, not the visual proportions.

2:20-3:00 | Critical trap: truncated axes. When the y-axis does not start at zero, visual comparisons are misleading. A bar that looks twice as tall might only represent a 10% difference. Always read the actual numbers.

Part 2

Line Graphs

Trends over time and rate of change

3:00-3:10 | Transition: Line graphs are the go-to for showing change over time.

Line Graphs

Interpreting slopes and trends

Slope Meaning What to Look For
Steep upward Rapid increase Large change over short time
Gentle upward Slow increase Gradual growth
Flat No change Stable period
Downward Decrease Declining trend

Watch out: Do not interpolate between plotted points unless the line is confirmed to be straight.

3:10-4:20 | How to read line graph slopes. Steep upward means rapid increase. Flat means no change. Downward means decrease. Important: do not assume values between plotted points follow a straight line.

Example 2

Average annual increase

What is the average annual increase in profit from Year 1 to Year 5?

Solution

Total increase: $\$18\text{M} - \$10\text{M} = \$8\text{M}$

Number of intervals: 4 (Year 1 to Year 5)

Average: $\frac{\$8\text{M}}{4} =$ $\$2\text{M/year}$

4:20-5:00 | Example: average annual increase. Total change is 8M over 4 intervals (not 5 years, but 4 gaps). Average increase is 2M per year.

Pie Charts

Parts of a whole

A company's $500,000 budget is allocated as shown. How much goes to Marketing?

Key Formulas

Value: Percent $\times$ Total

Angle: $\frac{\text{Percent}}{100} \times 360°$

Difference: Subtract percents, then multiply by total

5:00-6:30 | Pie charts show parts of a whole. The entire pie is 100%. To find a value, multiply percent by total. To find an angle, multiply percent by 360 degrees. Example with a $500K budget broken into four categories.

More Graph Types

Stacked bars and scatter plots

Stacked Bar

Each bar is divided into segments showing composition.

  • Read segment heights, not top of bar
  • Total bar height = sum of all parts
  • Segmented (100%) bars show proportions
Scatter Plot

Each point represents two variables for one observation.

  • Look for positive, negative, or no trend
  • Identify outliers far from the cluster
  • Do not assume causation from correlation
6:30-7:30 | Two more graph types you may see. Stacked bar charts divide each bar into segments. Read individual segment heights carefully by subtracting, not just reading the top. Segmented (100%) bars normalize everything to proportions. Scatter plots show the relationship between two variables. Look for the overall trend direction and any outliers.

Combined Displays

Multi-graph questions

What were Company A's sales in 2020?

From bar graph: total market = $100M

From pie chart: Company A share = 35%

Sales $= 35\% \times \$100\text{M} =$ \$35M

6:30-7:30 | Combined display questions require data from multiple sources. Step 1: find total market from the bar graph. Step 2: find the company share from the pie chart. Step 3: multiply to get the answer.

Checklist

Before you calculate, always check

1. Labels

What is being measured on each axis?

2. Units

Thousands? Millions? Check the title/caption.

3. Scale

Does the y-axis start at zero?

4. Legend

What do colors and patterns represent?

7:30-8:30 | The pre-calculation checklist. Always verify: axis labels, unit multipliers, starting point of the scale, and legend color coding. This prevents the most common Data Interpretation errors.

Limitations

What each graph hides

Graph Type Best For Watch Out For
Bar Comparing categories Truncated axes exaggerate differences
Line Trends over time Interpolation between points may be wrong
Pie Parts of a whole 3D distortion, hard to compare small slices
Stacked bar Composition over categories Must subtract to find individual segments
Scatter Relationships between variables Correlation does not imply causation
Key Rule

Always verify visual impressions with actual numbers. What looks bigger may not be.

8:30-9:30 | Limitations of each graph type. Bar graphs can mislead with truncated axes. Line graphs tempt you to interpolate. Pie charts distort proportions in 3D. Always verify visual impressions with numbers.

Key Takeaways

Interpreting graphs

  • Bar graphs -- Compare categories; watch for truncated axes
  • Line graphs -- Show trends; slope steepness = rate of change
  • Pie charts -- Parts of a whole; Value = Percent x Total
  • Stacked bars & scatter -- Read segment heights carefully; look for trend direction
  • Combined displays -- Match each question to the right graph first
  • Always check -- Labels, units, scale, and legend before calculating
9:30-10:00 | Summary: Bar graphs compare categories, line graphs show trends, pie charts show composition. For combined displays, identify which graph to use first. Always check labels, units, scale before calculating.
