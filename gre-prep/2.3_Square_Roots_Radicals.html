Breaking Apart

Factor and separate

Factor the number inside the radical, put each factor under its own radical sign, then simplify.

The Rule

$\sqrt{a \times b} = \sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{b}$

Multiplication distributes over radicals.

Example

$\sqrt{12} = \sqrt{4 \times 3}$

$= \sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{3} = $ $2\sqrt{3}$

Watch Out

This does NOT work with addition: $\sqrt{a + b} \neq \sqrt{a} + \sqrt{b}$

1:10-2:00 | Method 1: factor and separate. The square root of a product equals the product of the square roots. Factor 12 into 4 times 3, take the root of 4 to get 2, and you have 2 root 3. Important: this does NOT work with addition.

Example 1

Combining like radicals

Problem

$(\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{27})^3$

Solution

Step 1: Simplify: $\sqrt{27} = \sqrt{9 \times 3} = 3\sqrt{3}$

Step 2: Combine: $\sqrt{3} + 3\sqrt{3} = 4\sqrt{3}$

Step 3: Cube it: $(4\sqrt{3})^3 = 64 \times 3\sqrt{3}$

Answer: $192\sqrt{3}$

2:00-3:00 | Example 1: simplify root 27 to 3 root 3, combine like radicals to get 4 root 3, then cube it. 4 cubed is 64, root 3 cubed is 3 root 3. Multiply to get 192 root 3.

Example 2

Divide the root into exponents

Problem

$\sqrt[5]{x^5 y^{10} z^{23}}$

Solution

$x$: $5 \div 5 = 1$ (one $x$ comes out)

$y$: $10 \div 5 = 2$ ($y^2$ comes out)

$z$: $23 \div 5 = 4$ remainder $3$ ($z^4$ out, $z^3$ stays)

Answer: $xy^2z^4\sqrt[5]{z^3}$

3:00-4:00 | The fast way for variable radicals: divide the root into each exponent. For the fifth root: 5 into 5 is 1, so x comes out. 10 divided by 5 is 2, so y squared comes out. 23 divided by 5 is 4 remainder 3, so z to the 4th comes out and z cubed stays inside. Answer: x y^2 z^4 times the fifth root of z^3.

Fractional Exponents

The conversion rule

The denominator of the fractional exponent = the root of the radical.

Basic Conversions

RadicalExponent
$\sqrt{3}$$3^{1/2}$
$\sqrt[3]{3}$$3^{1/3}$
$\sqrt[4]{3}$$3^{1/4}$

With Inner Exponents

RadicalExponent
$\sqrt{7^9}$$7^{9/2}$
$\sqrt[3]{7^2}$$7^{2/3}$
$\sqrt[5]{x^3}$$x^{3/5}$
4:10-5:00 | The conversion rule: the root becomes the denominator of the fractional exponent. Square root of 3 is 3 to the 1/2. If there is an exponent inside the radical, it becomes the numerator. Square root of 7^9 is 7 to the 9/2.

Example 3

Mixed radicals and exponents

Problem

$\sqrt{3} \times 9^2$

Solution

Step 1: Convert: $\sqrt{3} = 3^{1/2}$

Step 2: Get bases same: $9^2 = (3^2)^2 = 3^4$

Step 3: Add exponents: $3^{1/2} \times 3^4 = 3^{9/2}$

Step 4: Simplify: $3^{9/2} = 3^4 \times 3^{1/2} = 81\sqrt{3}$

5:00-6:20 | Example 3: convert root 3 to 3^(1/2), rewrite 9^2 as 3^4, add exponents to get 3^(9/2). Split: 3^4 times 3^(1/2) equals 81 root 3.

Radicals of Decimals

Count and divide decimal places

Count the decimal places, then divide by the root to find decimal places in the answer.

Square Root

$\sqrt{0.000016}$

6 decimal places $\div$ 2 = 3 places

$\sqrt{16} = 4$, so answer: $0.004$

Cube Root

$\sqrt[3]{0.000027}$

6 decimal places $\div$ 3 = 2 places

$\sqrt[3]{27} = 3$, so answer: $0.03$

6:20-7:40 | Radicals of decimals: count decimal places and divide by the root. For square roots, the answer has half the decimal places. 0.000016 has 6 decimal places, half is 3, root of 16 is 4, answer is 0.004. For cube roots, divide by 3. 0.000027 has 6 places, one-third is 2, cube root of 27 is 3, answer is 0.03.

Radicals

Roots greater than 1

The root of ANY number greater than 1 MUST be greater than 1.

Why?

Numbers less than 1 multiply to give less than 1:

$0.9 \times 0.9 = 0.81$

$0.9999 \times 0.9999 = 0.9998...$

GRE Application

If you see $\sqrt{1.0000000987}$, you do not need to calculate.

Answer: Greater than 1

7:40-9:00 | The root of any number greater than 1 must be greater than 1. Why? Because numbers less than 1 multiply to give less than 1. 0.9 times 0.9 is 0.81. No matter how many nines you add, you cannot reach 1 by multiplying decimals less than 1. On the GRE, if you see root of 1.0001, you know instantly it is greater than 1.

Example 4

Simplify a fractional exponent

Problem

Simplify $3^{9/2}$

Solution

Step 1: Split the exponent: $3^{9/2} = 3^{4 + 1/2}$

Step 2: Separate: $3^4 \times 3^{1/2}$

Step 3: Evaluate: $81 \times \sqrt{3}$

Answer: $81\sqrt{3}$

9:00-9:50 | Example 4: simplify 3 to the 9/2. Split the exponent into 4 plus 1/2. Separate into 3^4 times 3^(1/2). That is 81 times root 3. Answer: 81 root 3.

Rationalization

Rationalizing the denominator

GRE answer choices never have radicals in the denominator. If your answer does, you need to rationalize.

Simple

Multiply by $\dfrac{\sqrt{n}}{\sqrt{n}}$

$\dfrac{5}{\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{5}{\sqrt{3}} \times \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = $ $\dfrac{5\sqrt{3}}{3}$

Conjugate

Multiply by $\dfrac{a - b}{a - b}$ to use difference of squares

$\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{5}+\sqrt{2}} \times \dfrac{\sqrt{5}-\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{5}-\sqrt{2}} = $ $\dfrac{\sqrt{5}-\sqrt{2}}{3}$

9:50 - 11:00 | Two rationalization methods. Simple: multiply top and bottom by the same radical. 5 over root 3 times root 3 over root 3 gives 5 root 3 over 3. Conjugate: for sums or differences of radicals in the denominator, multiply by the conjugate. 1 over root 5 plus root 2, multiply by root 5 minus root 2 over itself. The denominator becomes 5 minus 2 equals 3 by difference of squares.

Rationalization

Why this matters on the GRE

Answer choices are always rationalized. If your answer has a radical in the denominator, it won't match any choice until you rationalize.

Your Work

$\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{2}}$

This won't appear as an answer choice.

Answer Choice

$3\sqrt{2}$

$\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{2}} \times \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{6\sqrt{2}}{2} = 3\sqrt{2}$

11:00 - 12:00 | Show why this matters practically. If you solve a problem and get 6 over root 2, you won't find that in the answer choices. Rationalize: multiply by root 2 over root 2 to get 6 root 2 over 2, which simplifies to 3 root 2. That's the form you'll see in the choices. If your answer doesn't match any choice, try rationalizing.

Key Takeaways

Square roots & radicals

  • Factor and separate - $\sqrt{ab} = \sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{b}$, but NOT over addition
  • Pull out pairs - For variable radicals, divide the root into each exponent
  • Fractional exponents - The denominator is the root: $\sqrt[n]{a^m} = a^{m/n}$
  • Decimal radicals - Count decimal places, divide by the root
  • Roots of numbers $> 1$ - Always greater than 1, no calculation needed
  • Rationalize the denominator - Multiply by $\sqrt{n}/\sqrt{n}$ or use the conjugate so your answer matches GRE choices

Convert to fractional exponents when mixing with exponent rules. Count decimal places for decimal radicals. And the root of any number greater than 1 is always greater than 1. [Leland+ Tip] A common trap: sqrt(a+b) does NOT equal sqrt(a) + sqrt(b). If you forget this rule on test day, verify with easy numbers: sqrt(9+16) = sqrt(25) = 5, but sqrt(9) + sqrt(16) = 3+4 = 7. Use this self-check technique for any rule you are unsure about.
