Thank-You Notes

01

Express Gratitude

One sentence thanking them for their time. Simple and direct.

02

Reference Something Specific

Mention a story, class, or exchange from your conversation. This proves you were engaged.

03

Reaffirm Enthusiasm

Close with genuine excitement for the program. Reference a specific aspect you discussed.

1:00 - 2:15 | A strong thank-you note has three elements. First, express gratitude. One sentence thanking them for their time. Second, reference something specific from the conversation. Did they share a story about the program? Did you discuss a particular class or club? Mention it. This separates a good note from a generic one. Third, reaffirm your enthusiasm. Close with a brief statement about your excitement for the program, ideally referencing a specific aspect you discussed.

Thank-You Notes

What a Good Note Looks Like

"Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today. I really enjoyed our conversation about the small group learning model, and your perspective on how the cohort experience shaped your approach to leadership was incredibly insightful. I left our conversation even more excited about the program."

Send within 24 hours. Three to five sentences total. This is a note, not an essay.

2:15 - 3:15 | Here's what a good thank-you note looks like. It's short, specific, and genuine. Notice: one sentence of thanks, one reference to something specific from the conversation, one statement of enthusiasm. That's it. Send it within 24 hours, ideally the same evening or next morning. Email is the right format. Handwritten notes arrive too late to be relevant in admissions.

Thank-You Notes

Four Mistakes to Avoid

  • Recapping your candidacy: They know who you are after 30-60 minutes together.
  • Adding new information: Squeezing in forgotten talking points comes across as desperate.
  • Asking about next steps: The admissions team will communicate timelines officially.
  • Writing too much: If it's longer than a short paragraph, it's too long.
3:15 - 4:15 | Equally important is what not to include. Don't recap your entire candidacy. They just spent 30 to 60 minutes with you. Don't add new information you forgot to mention. The interview is over, and trying to squeeze in additional points feels desperate. Don't ask about your application status or next steps. The admissions team will communicate through official channels. And don't write a lengthy message. If your note is longer than a short paragraph, it's too long. Respect their time.

Thank-You Notes

Adjusting for Your Interviewer

Alumni Interviewers

Slightly more conversational and personal. They volunteered their time, and the dynamic is more peer-to-peer. A warm thank-you email is appreciated.

Admissions Staff

Keep it a bit more formal. They're conducting dozens of interviews, so brevity is especially valued. Tightly focused email.

LinkedIn connection requests are appropriate for alumni interviewers (wait a few hours, personalize your note). For admissions staff, stick with email only.

4:15 - 5:15 | Adjust your approach based on who interviewed you. For alumni interviewers, you can be slightly more conversational and personal. They're professionals who volunteered their time. A LinkedIn connection request is also appropriate, but wait at least a few hours and always include a personalized note. For admissions staff, keep it more formal and brief. They're conducting dozens of interviews. Stick with email only, no LinkedIn request. The relationship dynamic is different.

Part 2

The HBS Post-Interview Reflection

A unique, required component with real weight

5:15 - 5:20 | Now let's talk about something unique to Harvard Business School: the post-interview reflection.

HBS Reflection

Three-Part Structure

01

Opening

2-3 sentences. Brief thanks and your genuine reaction to the interview. How did you feel?

02

The Highlight

150-200 words. A specific moment that resonated. Go deep on why it mattered, not just what happened.

03

Closing

2-3 sentences. Reaffirm enthusiasm for HBS. Add one brief point if it genuinely adds value.

300-450 words total. Due within 24 hours. Should read like a thoughtful email, not a polished essay.

5:20 - 6:30 | The HBS reflection has three parts. Opening: 2-3 sentences with a brief thank-you and your genuine reaction. The highlight: 150-200 words on a specific moment that resonated. This is the core. Pick a question that made you think differently, a perspective your interviewer shared that connected to something you care about, or a moment where you articulated something new about yourself. Go deep on why it mattered. Closing: 2-3 sentences reaffirming enthusiasm, with one brief addition if it genuinely adds value. Total: 300-450 words, due within 24 hours.

The most important step is the first one. Your reflection quality depends on the specificity of your recall.

6:30 - 7:45 | Here's the process. Step one: the moment you leave your interview, sit down and spend 15-20 minutes writing down everything. Questions asked, stories told, key moments, your emotional reactions. Do this before you call anyone or decompress. Your memory fades quickly. Step two: take a short break, an hour or two. Let the experience settle. Step three: review your notes, identify the highlight, and write a first draft. Write conversationally, as if telling a friend about the best part of your interview. Step four: edit once for clarity, clean up the structure, then submit. Don't over-polish. If it reads like it was written in a day, that's the point.

If your reflection reads like a polished essay that could have been written before the interview, it will hurt you. They want authentic, real-time thinking. Specific moments from the conversation. LinkedIn Protocol

Connecting After the Interview

For alumni interviewers, a LinkedIn request is welcome. Wait a few hours, always include a personalized note, and never connect before the interview.

For admissions staff, skip LinkedIn entirely. A thank-you email is the right channel. Researching your interviewer beforehand is smart, but sending a request before you've met crosses a boundary.

8:45 - 9:30 | Should you connect on LinkedIn? For alumni interviewers, yes. It's a natural extension of a professional conversation. But wait at least a few hours, and always include a personalized note referencing something from your conversation. A blank request after a personal conversation feels impersonal. Never connect before the interview. Researching them beforehand is smart, but sending a request before you've met is inappropriate. For admissions staff, skip LinkedIn. A thank-you email is more appropriate. The relationship dynamic is different from an alumni conversation.
