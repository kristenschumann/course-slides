Choosing Your Round

Which Round Fits Your Situation?

Well Prepared

Strong scores, polished essays, recs lined up. Apply Round 1 for best odds and most scholarships.

Need More Time

Still prepping for the GMAT or refining essays. Round 2 is solid but expect less financial aid.

International Applicants

Apply Round 1 or 2 only. Visa processing makes Round 3 risky.

Reapplicants

Take the time to genuinely improve. Address feedback, strengthen weak areas, don't rush.

5:00 - 6:30 | Which round fits you? If you are well prepared with strong scores, polished essays, and recs ready, go Round 1 for the best odds and most scholarships. If you need more time for the GMAT or essays, Round 2 is solid but expect less aid. International applicants should apply Round 1 or 2 only because visa processing makes Round 3 risky. Reapplicants should take time to genuinely improve. Address feedback, strengthen weak spots. Rushing a reapplication rarely works.

Common Mistakes

Five Timing Traps to Avoid

  • Delaying test prep: Underestimating prep time leaves no room for retakes.
  • Last-minute rec requests: Two weeks' notice produces generic letters.
  • Rushing essays: Starting one month before the deadline produces shallow responses.
  • Chasing rankings over fit: Committees can tell when you haven't researched the school.
  • Applying Round 3 out of desperation: A strong R1 next year beats a weak R3 now.
6:30 - 8:15 | Five timing traps. First, delaying test prep. People underestimate how long it takes and end up rushing with no retake buffer. Second, last-minute rec requests. Two weeks' notice puts your recommenders in a tough spot and produces generic letters. Third, rushing essays. Starting a month before the deadline almost always produces shallow responses. Fourth, chasing rankings over fit. Admissions committees can tell when you haven't researched why their school specifically. Fifth, applying Round 3 out of desperation. If you missed Round 2, it is often better to wait for next year's Round 1.

Your Decision

The Right Question to Ask

"When is the soonest I could submit a strong application? Not a rushed one. A strong one."

There is no shame in taking an extra year to prepare. A strong Round 1 application next year beats a weak Round 2 application this year.

8:15 - 9:15 | Here is the question to ask yourself: When is the soonest I could submit a strong application? Not a rushed one. A strong one. That means competitive test scores, polished essays, and recommenders who have had time to write thoughtful letters. There is no shame in taking an extra year. A strong Round 1 application next year beats a weak Round 2 this year. Work backward from "strong" and let that determine your round.
