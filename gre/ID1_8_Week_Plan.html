Before You Start

This Plan Is Flexible

This plan is designed around a typical two-month timeline, but you don't need exactly 8 weeks.

If you have 6 weeks, or 10 weeks, that's fine. The plan is flexible. What matters is working through the material consistently, at whatever pace fits your schedule.

By the end of this video, you'll know what to expect, how to structure your study time, and how to track progress.

What You'll See

Four Types of Content

01

Skill Lessons

Videos that teach specific GRE concepts. Each lesson focuses on one skill. This is where you learn new material.

02

Reference Sheets

One-page reminders of what you learned. Think of them as cheat sheets you can glance at when you need a refresher.

03

Drills

Short, focused practice on a single topic, usually 10-15 questions. Do these right after a skill lesson to lock in what you learned.

04

Practice Sets

Longer than drills, often mixed-topic. These train you to shift between question types, just like on the real test.

Learn from videos → Reference sheets when needed → Practice with drills and sets

Daily Routine

How to Structure Your Study Time

For a two-month timeline, aim for roughly 15-20 hours per week

Short 15-30 min

Quick Review

Vocab review or revisiting concepts you've already learned. Easy to fit into a busy day.

Medium 45-90 min

Learn + Practice

Watch a skill lesson, then do the drill while the material is fresh. This is your core study time.

Long 2+ hours

Deep Focus

Practice tests and intensive study. Plan for uninterrupted time when scheduling these.

Consistency matters more than perfection. Some days you'll have more time, some days less. That's fine.

4:25 - 6:25 | Explain the three block types. Medium blocks are the core (45-90 min learn + practice). Short blocks for maintenance. Long blocks for practice tests. Key message: consistency over perfection.

Measuring Progress

Practice Tests Are Diagnostic Tools

When to Take Them
  • Take them consistently throughout prep, not just at the end
  • Some people do one every week, others every two weeks
  • Find a rhythm that works for you
How to Use Them
  • The point isn't just to get a score
  • After each test, analyze what went wrong
  • Every mistake is data telling you where to focus next

Don't get discouraged by early scores. The first few tests establish a baseline. They're not proving you're ready.

Summary

What This Plan Does for You

It gives you a structured path from wherever you're starting to being ready for test day.
