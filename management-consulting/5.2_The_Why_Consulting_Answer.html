The Three Components

Motivation

Why consulting over banking, tech, or industry? Rooted in a personal story.

Firm Fit

Why this specific firm? Must pass the interchangeability test.

Evidence

Proof from your background that backs up every claim you make.

0:35 - 1:15 | Most candidates nail one of the three. Strong answers weave all three into a two-minute response, roughly one minute on why consulting, one minute on why this firm, with evidence threaded throughout.

Break Into Management Consulting

Generic vs. Strong Answers

Generic Answer

Could be said by any candidate about any firm.

"I want to be a consultant because I love problem-solving, working with smart people, and I want exposure to different industries."

Strong Answer

Personal story, names the firm, references real conversations.

"My dad ran a small manufacturing business, and I watched him struggle with problems he didn't have the tools to solve. At BCG, I spoke with three consultants who described building original analytical approaches for each client."

1:15 - 2:15 | Side-by-side comparison. Two pressure tests: the interchangeability test (swap out the firm name) and the memorability test (can the interviewer describe your answer in one sentence?).

Break Into Management Consulting

Build Your Motivation

  • Start with an inflection point: a specific moment consulting became your goal
  • Connect to unique value: what consulting offers that other paths don't
  • Use your personal lens: not brochure language, but your lived experience
  • Show trade-off awareness: travel, hours, ambiguity signal maturity
2:25 - 3:15 | Motivation starts with an inflection point. Story must be real. Fabricated stories collapse under follow-up questions. Frame consulting benefits through your personal lens. Mentioning trade-offs signals maturity.

3:15 - 4:30 | Each MBB firm has genuine differentiators. Pick one or two that connect to your values and story. Don't mention all of them. "I'm drawn to Bain because of the collaborative culture" fails the interchangeability test.

Break Into Management Consulting

Making It Believable

  • Your accomplishments: specific projects showing consulting-relevant skills
  • Networking conversations: "I spoke with [Name] in the [City] office about [topic]"
  • Firm research: specific publications, practice areas, or events you engaged with
4:30 - 5:15 | Claims without evidence are just assertions. Three sources of evidence: accomplishments, networking conversations (most powerful), and firm-specific research. Evidence should show patterns, not isolated incidents.

Break Into Management Consulting

Mistakes That Kill Your Answer

  • Generic, interchangeable reasons: "great people, steep learning curve" tells interviewers nothing unique
  • Zero networking evidence: no conversations means your answer sounds like it came from a blog post
  • Rambling without structure: four minutes of unstructured enthusiasm is worse than two tight minutes
5:15 - 5:45 | Three mistakes seen repeatedly in candidate reviews. If your "Why McKinsey?" works equally well for BCG, the interviewer knows you didn't do the work. Motivation, firm fit, evidence. Two minutes. Done.

Key Takeaways

Build a Retellable Answer

  • Three components: personal motivation story, firm-specific fit that passes the interchangeability test, and concrete evidence from your background.
  • Start with your inflection point: the real moment consulting became your goal. That story is the backbone of a memorable answer.
  • Firm-specific means firm-specific: McKinsey's global staffing, BCG's bespoke approach, Bain's results-driven culture. Pick differentiators that connect to your experience.
  • Networking is your strongest evidence: "I spoke with [Name] in the [City] office" separates serious candidates from everyone else.
5:45 - 6:00 | Four key takeaways. Three components, inflection point, firm-specific differentiators, and networking evidence. If you haven't had those conversations yet, that's your next action item.
