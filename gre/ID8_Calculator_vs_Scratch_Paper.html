Calculator vs.
Scratch Paper

0:00 - 0:30 | Hook: The GRE gives you an on-screen calculator. That sounds like good news, but the calculator can actually slow you down if you don't use it strategically.

The Basics

What the Calculator Can Do

The GRE calculator is basic. It handles:

Addition Subtraction Multiplication Division Square Roots

Also supports parentheses and a memory function. That's it. No exponents, no trig, no fractions.

The Trade-Off

The calculator is on-screen only. You click buttons with your mouse. No keyboard shortcuts.

Every calculation requires multiple clicks. Accuracy comes at the cost of time.

Rule of thumb: If you can solve it in your head in under 10 seconds, don't use the calculator.

Strategic Choices

When to Use Each

Use the Calculator

1.

Large or messy numbers

847 x 36, 127.5 / 8.5

2.

Decimal arithmetic

Avoid decimal place errors

3.

Checking your work

Quick verification of hand calculations

4.

Multi-step chains

(15% of 340) + (23% of 280)

Use Scratch Paper

1.

Simple arithmetic

48 / 6, 7 x 8, 125 - 39

2.

Fractions

Keep as fractions, don't convert to decimals

3.

Estimation

When answer choices are far apart

4.

Setting up problems

Write the equation first, then decide

1:30 - 4:30 | Calculator: large numbers, decimals, checking work, multi-step chains. Scratch paper: simple math, fractions, estimation, setting up problems.

Watch Out

Calculator Traps

1

Order of Operations Errors

The calculator follows PEMDAS, but if you enter things wrong, you get wrong answers. Use parentheses explicitly.

2

Converting Fractions Too Early

Entering 1/3 as 0.333 creates rounding errors when you multiply. Keep fractions as fractions.

3

Typing Errors

When clicking fast, it's easy to hit the wrong button. If your answer seems weird, clear and try again.

4

Over-Reliance

Reaching for the calculator for everything wastes time and doesn't develop number sense that makes you faster.

4:30 - 5:30 | Four traps: 1) Order of operations errors, 2) Converting fractions to decimals too early, 3) Typing errors when clicking fast, 4) Over-reliance on calculator for everything.

Summary

The Strategy

Use calculator: Large/messy numbers, decimals, checking work

Use scratch paper: Simple arithmetic, fractions, estimation

Always: Set up the problem on paper before reaching for calculator

Watch out for: Order of operations errors and rounding issues

The calculator is a tool. But so is your brain. Use both strategically.

5:30 - 6:00 | Summary: Calculator for messy numbers, scratch paper for simple math. Always set up on paper first. Watch for order of operations and rounding. Use both tools strategically.
