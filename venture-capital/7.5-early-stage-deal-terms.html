Six safes, one ugly surprise

In 2018, a YC-backed startup raised $3 million across six different SAFEs, each with a different valuation cap. The founders thought they had sold about 15% of the company. When the Series A closed and every SAFE converted, they discovered they had actually sold closer to 40%.

SAFEs are marketed as simple. They are simple individually. Stack five or six with different terms, and you have a conversion puzzle that can wreck a founder's ownership. If you want to work in early-stage VC, you need to understand these instruments cold.

Section 1

Why Early-Stage Deals Use Different Instruments

1:00 | Transition into the rationale for different deal instruments.

VC Recruiting Course

Why not just price every round?

Cost and Complexity

A priced equity round costs $30K-$50K in legal fees and takes 4-8 weeks. On a $500K-$2M seed raise, that is 5-10% of the capital spent on lawyers. The overhead is absurd for an early-stage company.

Valuation Uncertainty

At seed, neither founder nor investor knows what the company is worth. There is no revenue, no established metrics, no comparable transaction. Forcing a precise valuation when the data does not exist creates a false sense of precision.

1:00 - 1:45 | Two reasons early-stage deals use simpler instruments. [Click] Cost. A priced round runs $30K-$50K in legal fees and takes weeks. That is 5-10% of a small seed raise just on lawyers. [Click] Uncertainty. At seed, nobody knows what the company is worth. There is no revenue model and no comparable transaction. SAFEs and notes defer the valuation question until a priced round happens and more data exists.

VC Recruiting Course

Why speed matters for both sides

For Founders

A SAFE can close in a day with a two-page document. Seed rounds have closed within 48 hours of a handshake. Two signatures, a wire transfer, done. The founder gets back to building instead of sitting in a lawyer's office.

For Investors

If you can close on a SAFE in three days while a competitor needs four weeks for a priced round, you win the deal. Speed of capital deployment is one of the most underrated competitive dynamics in early-stage investing.

1:45 - 2:30 | Beyond cost and uncertainty, speed is a competitive weapon. [Click] For founders, a SAFE closes in a day. I have seen seed rounds close in 48 hours. Two signatures and a wire transfer. [Click] For investors, speed wins deals. If you compete for a hot seed deal and you can close in three days while another fund needs four weeks, you win. The instrument choice is not just legal efficiency. It is market positioning.
Section 2

SAFEs: The Standard Early-Stage Instrument

2:30 | Transition into SAFE mechanics.

VC Recruiting Course

A SAFE is not debt

A SAFE is a contractual right to receive equity in the future, triggered by the next priced round. No interest rate. No maturity date. No obligation to repay.

The investor gives the company money now. The company promises to issue shares later, at a discount to whatever price the Series A investors pay. If the company never raises a priced round, the SAFE just sits there. This is the most common misconception candidates have in interviews.

2:30 - 3:00 | The most common misconception: a SAFE is not a loan. No interest, no maturity date, no repayment obligation. It is a contractual right to receive equity later, triggered by a priced round. The investor gives cash now, the company issues shares later at a discount to the Series A price.

VC Recruiting Course

YC's standard SAFE variants

Valuation Cap

The most common. Investor and founder agree on a cap. SAFE converts at the cap or the Series A price, whichever is lower. The cap rewards early risk-taking.

Discount

Investor gets a percentage discount (typically 20%) to the Series A price. Less common because the cap provides better upside. A 20% discount is the same regardless of how high the Series A prices.

MFN (Most Favored Nation)

No cap, no discount. The investor adopts the terms of any later SAFE the company issues. Used for the earliest, smallest checks. Elegant but creates administrative complexity.

3:00 - 3:45 | Three SAFE variants in YC's standard documents. [Click] Valuation cap. By far the most common. The investor converts at the cap or the Series A price, whichever is lower. A $10M cap on a $30M Series A means the SAFE investor converts at $10M and gets 3x more shares. [Click] Discount. A flat percentage off the Series A price. Less common because the upside is capped at the discount percentage. [Click] MFN. No cap, no discount, but the investor gets whatever the best future SAFE terms are. Used for small early checks. Spend 80% of your interview prep on the cap SAFE. That is what you will get asked about.

If the company rockets to a $100M Series A, the SAFE investor still converts at $10M. Massive effective return.

VC Recruiting Course

The 2018 shift that changed everything

Pre-Money SAFE (Original)

Valuation cap refers to the pre-money valuation before the SAFE investment. Problem: with multiple SAFEs, dilution compounds in ways that are nearly impossible to calculate. Founders had no idea what they actually sold until the priced round.

Post-Money SAFE (2018+)

Valuation cap includes the SAFE money itself. $1M SAFE at a $10M cap = exactly 10% ownership. Math is transparent. But all dilution from additional SAFEs falls on the founders. Transparency came at a cost.

4:15 - 5:00 | In 2018, YC released the post-money SAFE. This was a fundamental change. [Click] The original pre-money SAFE: the cap referred to the pre-money valuation. Multiple SAFEs created circular dilution math that was nearly impossible to calculate. Founders discovered what they sold only at conversion. [Click] The post-money SAFE: the cap includes the SAFE money. $1M at a $10M cap is exactly 10%. Clean and transparent. But the trade-off: each SAFE investor's ownership is protected and all the dilution from additional SAFEs falls on the founders. Investors won this negotiation. Founders got transparency in exchange for worse economics.

The post-money SAFE investor owns exactly 10% no matter what. The transparency is dramatically better, but the economics shifted toward investors. This is the most important distinction in early-stage deal structures and a common interview question.

Three key differences

Feature SAFE Convertible Note
Interest None 4-8% (accrues, converts to equity)
Maturity date None Typically 18-24 months

5:30 - 6:30 | Three differences that matter in interviews. [Click] Interest. Notes accrue interest, typically 4-8%. On a $500K note at 6% over 18 months, an extra $45K converts to equity. Not huge, but not nothing. [Click] Maturity date. Notes have one. If a company has not raised a priced round by maturity, the investor technically can demand repayment. Most extend, but it is leverage. [Click] Balance sheet. Notes are debt. SAFEs are not. For a pre-revenue startup, the practical difference is small. For a company starting to report financials, $2M in debt looks different than $2M in SAFEs. [Click] Both convert at the next priced round. [Click] SAFEs are cheaper and faster to close.

VC Recruiting Course

Choosing the right instrument

SAFE

Standard for most seed rounds. No debt, no maturity pressure, cleaner balance sheet. Dominates for raises under $2M. Two-page document that closes in days.

Convertible Note

Best for bridge rounds between priced rounds. Investor wants debt protection or specific tax advantages. Still common outside Silicon Valley where lawyers prefer debt instruments.

Priced Seed Round

For larger raises above $3M where legal costs are a rounding error. Provides cap table certainty from day one. Signals founder confidence in the valuation.

6:30 - 7:00 | When to use each. [Click] SAFE for most seed rounds, especially under $2M. It is the default. [Click] Convertible notes for bridge financing between priced rounds, or when investors want debt protection. Still used outside Silicon Valley. [Click] Priced seed rounds for larger raises above $3M. On a $4M raise, $40K in legal fees is 1% of the round. That is a rounding error for the cap table certainty you get.

VC Recruiting Course

The trend toward priced seed rounds

Cap Table Certainty

Everyone knows what they own the day the round closes. No conversion uncertainty, no circular math, no surprises. For larger raises above $3M, the legal costs are a rounding error.

Signals Confidence

A priced round says the founders believe the company is worth $15M and are willing to set that price formally. A SAFE with a $15M cap says something weaker. In competitive situations, the willingness to price can differentiate a company.

8:15 - 8:45 | The stacking problem is driving more founders toward priced seed rounds, especially for larger raises. [Click] Cap table certainty. Everyone knows ownership from day one. No conversion puzzles. [Click] There is also a signaling dimension. Pricing the round signals confidence. In competitive deal situations, that differentiation matters.

VC Recruiting Course

The right to maintain your position

The standard YC SAFE does not include pro-rata rights. Investors who want the right to invest in future rounds to maintain their ownership percentage need a separate side letter.

A SAFE investor without pro-rata rights gets diluted at Series A with no ability to defend their position. Some seed investors make pro-rata a condition of investing. Others negotiate for it at Series A. This matters because pro-rata from existing investors can crowd out room for a new lead investor in later rounds.

8:45 - 9:00 | One more SAFE nuance for interviews: pro-rata rights. The standard YC SAFE does not include them. Investors need a separate side letter. Without pro-rata, the SAFE investor gets diluted at Series A with no recourse. Some investors make pro-rata a condition. Others negotiate later. This becomes contentious when every existing investor exercises pro-rata in a competitive follow-on and there is no room for a new lead.

Key takeaways

  • The post-money SAFE is the version you need to know inside and out. The shift from pre-money to post-money changed who bears the dilution. Investors got ownership protection. Founders got transparency in exchange for worse economics.
  • The valuation cap on a SAFE functions as a de facto valuation. Everyone in the industry knows this. The legal fiction of deferred valuation saves $40K in legal fees and three weeks of negotiation.
  • Know three differences between SAFEs and convertible notes. Interest accrual, maturity date, and debt classification. That is sufficient for any interview question.
  • SAFE stacking is the answer when someone asks"What is the risk of raising on SAFEs?" Cumulative dilution from multiple instruments at different caps. That answer demonstrates understanding beyond the surface level.
  • All of these instruments eventually hit the cap table from 7.4. And the term sheet in 7.6 is where these terms get formalized in a priced round.
