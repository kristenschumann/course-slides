Balancing Test Prep
with Applications

0:00 - 0:30 | Here is a scenario I see constantly: someone starts their MBA applications a couple months before deadlines, realizes they need to retake the GMAT or GRE, and suddenly they are cramming for a test while also trying to write essays and manage recommenders. It is a recipe for stress and mediocre results on all fronts. Let me show you how to sequence these so you are not doing everything at once.

0:50 - 2:00 | Here is the ideal sequence. At 6+ months before your deadline, start test prep. Take diagnostic tests, identify weaknesses, build a study plan. At 4 to 5 months out, take your first official test. Based on results, decide if you need a retake. We recommend retaking if you are below the median at your top-choice program. 2:00 - 3:15 | Plan for 2 to 4 months of dedicated study depending on how far you are from your target. Someone starting with a 300 diagnostic GRE who needs a 330 will need more time than someone starting at 320. Most successful test-takers study 10 to 15 hours per week. That is about 2 hours on weeknights plus longer sessions on weekends. Take a full-length practice test every 2 weeks to track progress. Your first diagnostic tells you where you are, and subsequent tests show whether your prep is working.

3:15 - 4:15 | Should you start applications before finishing test prep? Some overlap is necessary, but be cautious about investing too much before getting your target score. You can start brainstorming essay topics, researching schools, identifying recommenders, and building your resume. But hold off on writing final essay drafts, asking recommenders to submit, filling out applications, and committing to your final school list. Why? Because your school list might change based on your score. Once your test is finalized, you can fully commit to targeted essays.

Score Recovery

Three Options If Your Score Falls Short

  • Retake the test: Analyze your score report, target weak areas, give yourself 4-6 weeks of focused prep.
  • Adjust your school list: Move reach schools up, add more targets. Strong essays and recs can offset a lower score.
  • Apply with what you have: Schools evaluate whole profiles. Consider whether Round 2 or a gap year makes sense.
4:15 - 5:30 | If your score falls short, you have three options. First, retake the test. Analyze your score report, find where you lost points, target those areas, and give yourself 4 to 6 weeks of focused prep before retaking. Many applicants improve significantly on their second attempt. Second, adjust your school list. Move some reach schools to super reach, add more target and safety schools. A below-average test score is not a death sentence. Strong essays and recommendations can offset it. Third, apply with what you have. Sometimes the score is what it is. Schools evaluate whole profiles. But be strategic about whether Round 2 gives you more time or whether a gap year makes sense.

Avoiding Pitfalls

Set a "Good Enough" Threshold

Before your first test, define your floor: "If I score X or above, I move forward. If below X, I retake once. If my second score is still below X, I adjust my list and apply anyway."

A concrete rule: if you are within 20 GMAT points or 3 GRE points of your target school's median, apply. The rest of your application can close that gap.

5:30 - 6:15 | Here is a critical piece of advice. Before your first official test, define your floor. Set a rule: if I score X or above, I move forward. If below, I retake once. If the second score is still below, I adjust my school list and apply anyway. This prevents the open-ended retake cycle that drains time and energy. A concrete rule: if you are within 20 GMAT points or 3 GRE points of your target school's median, apply. The rest of your application can close that gap.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Start studying 4-6 months before deadlines and test early
  • Build in retake buffer from the start
  • Block 10-15 hours per week for dedicated study
  • Focus 70% of study time on your weakest areas
  • Adjust your school list if your score is lower (or higher) than expected
  • A strong Round 2 beats a weak Round 1
7:15 - 8:00 | Key takeaways. Start studying 4 to 6 months before deadlines. Ideally take your first official test 4 months out. Build in retake buffer from the start. Block 10 to 15 hours per week for dedicated study. Focus 70% of your time on your weakest areas. Adjust your school list if your score comes in lower or higher than expected. And remember, a strong Round 2 application always beats a weak Round 1. Do not rush. Sequence your work, and you will produce stronger results on every front.
