Even & Odd Logic
(Parity)

0:00-0:30 | Hook: QC Problem. If a and b are positive integers and a + b = 11, compare ab vs 25. Parity tells you one of a,b is even and one is odd, so the product is always even. But that alone doesn't determine the comparison. You need to plug in specific values: 5 x 6 = 30 > 25, but 1 x 10 = 10 < 25. Since the relationship changes, the answer is D.

Parity Rules

Addition and subtraction

OperationResult
Even $+$ EvenEven
Odd $+$ OddEven
Even $+$ OddOdd
Memory Trick

Same parity $\rightarrow$ Even result

Different parity $\rightarrow$ Odd result

Subtraction follows the same rules as addition. Same parity gives even, different gives odd.

0:30-1:30 | Addition rules: Even + Even = Even, Odd + Odd = Even, Even + Odd = Odd. Memory trick: same parity gives even, different parity gives odd. Subtraction follows the same rules.

Parity Rules

Multiplication

OperationResult
Even $\times$ EvenEven
Even $\times$ OddEven
Odd $\times$ OddOdd
Memory Trick

If any factor is even, the product is even.

The only way to get an odd product is odd $\times$ odd.

1:30-2:30 | Multiplication rules. Even times anything is even. The only way to get an odd product is odd times odd. This is the most important parity rule for the GRE.

Parity Rules

Powers and exponents

Even Base

Even raised to any positive power is even.

$2^{100}$ is even

$4^{99}$ is even

Odd Base

Odd raised to any positive power is odd.

$3^{100}$ is odd

$7^{53}$ is odd

2:30-3:15 | Powers: even base raised to any positive power stays even, odd base raised to any positive power stays odd. This is because multiplying evens always gives even, multiplying odds always gives odd.

Example 1

Parity of an expression

Problem

If $n$ is a positive odd integer, what is the parity of $n^2 + n$?

Solution

$n$ is odd $\rightarrow$ $n^2$ is odd

odd $+$ odd $=$ even

Or factor: $n^2 + n = n(n+1)$, one of $n, n+1$ is even $\rightarrow$ even

3:15-3:55 | Example 1. n is odd, so n^2 is odd. Odd + odd = even. Alternative approach: factor as n(n+1). Consecutive integers always include one even, so the product is even.

Example 2

Product of consecutive integers

Problem

If $a$, $b$, and $c$ are consecutive positive integers, is $abc$ even or odd?

Solution

Three consecutive integers always include at least one even number.

If any factor is even, the product is even.

Answer: Always even

3:55-4:30 | Example 2. Three consecutive integers always include at least one even number. Since any even factor makes the product even, abc is always even.

Example 3

Quantitative comparison

$x$ is a positive integer

Quantity A

$(-1)^x$

vs

Quantity B

$(-1)^{x+1}$

x Even

$(-1)^x = 1$ and $(-1)^{x+1} = -1$

A > B

x Odd

$(-1)^x = -1$ and $(-1)^{x+1} = 1$

A < B

Answer: D (Cannot be determined)

4:30-5:30 | Example 3: QC problem. If x is even, (-1)^x = 1 and (-1)^(x+1) = -1, so A > B. If x is odd, the reverse. Since the relationship changes, answer is D.

Example 4

Sum of five consecutive integers

Problem

The sum of five consecutive integers is always divisible by:

(A) 2
(B) 4
(C) 5
(D) 10
(E) 15

Solution

Test: $1+2+3+4+5 = 15$

Test: $2+3+4+5+6 = 20$

Both divisible by 5. Sum $= 5 \times$ middle number.

Answer: (C) 5

5:30-6:30 | Example 4. Test with actual numbers: 1+2+3+4+5 = 15, 2+3+4+5+6 = 20. Both divisible by 5 but 15 is not divisible by 2, 4, 10, or 15. The sum of 5 consecutive integers always equals 5 times the middle number. Answer is C.

Key Takeaways

Even & odd logic (parity)

  • Addition/subtraction. Same parity gives even, different parity gives odd
  • Multiplication. If any factor is even, the product is even
  • Powers. Even base stays even, odd base stays odd, regardless of exponent
  • Consecutive integers. Always include at least one even, so their product is even
  • GRE strategy. Test both even and odd cases to see if the answer changes
6:30-7:00 | Summary: Same parity addition gives even. Any even factor makes a product even. Even or odd base stays that way under exponents. Consecutive integers always produce an even product. On the GRE, test both cases to determine if the answer is fixed. [Leland+ Tip] Common GRE traps with parity: zero IS even (many students forget this). Also, when plugging in numbers on QC problems, always test negatives and fractions, not just positive integers. The GRE writers deliberately exploit the assumption that test-takers only try positive integers.
