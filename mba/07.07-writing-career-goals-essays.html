School Perspective

Five Things Schools Evaluate

  • Clarity: Can you articulate a clear direction? Vague goals suggest vague thinking.
  • Logic: Do your goals connect to your background? Random pivots need explanation.
  • Credibility: Are your goals ambitious but achievable given your experience?
  • Need for MBA: Could you achieve this without spending two years and $200K?
  • Fit: Do your goals align with what this specific school offers?
1:00 - 2:30 | Five things schools evaluate. Clarity: can you articulate a clear direction? Vague goals suggest vague thinking. Logic: do your goals connect to your background? A marketing person wanting healthcare consulting needs to explain the thread. Credibility: are your goals realistic? Ambitious but achievable. Need for MBA: is the MBA actually necessary? You need to show why the MBA specifically enables your path. Fit: do your goals align with what this school offers? If you want tech and you're applying to a finance school, explain the fit.

Articulating Goals

From Vague to Compelling

Level Short-Term Goal Example
Vague "I want to work in technology"
Better "I want a product management role at a consumer tech company"
Compelling "I want a PM role at a high-growth consumer tech company like Airbnb or Stripe, focusing on growth, combining my engineering background with business strategy"

Your short-term goal should answer: what function, what industry, what type of company, and what will you be doing day-to-day?

2:30 - 4:00 | Let me show you the difference between vague and compelling goals. Vague: "I want to work in technology." This tells the reader nothing. Better: "I want a product management role at a consumer tech company." Now they can picture it. Compelling: "I want a PM role at a high-growth consumer tech company like Airbnb or Stripe, focusing on growth initiatives, combining my engineering background with business strategy." Now the reader sees the full picture. Your short-term goal should answer four questions: what function, what industry, what type of company, and what will you be doing day-to-day? Long-term goals can be broader but should flow logically from your short-term.

Part 2

Why MBA, Why Now?

Making the case for business school

4:00 - 4:05 | Now let's tackle the hardest part for many applicants: making the case for why you need an MBA and why now.

Why MBA?

Weak Reasons vs. Strong Reasons

Too Generic

  • "I want to learn business skills"
  • "An MBA opens doors"
  • "All successful people have MBAs"
  • "I want to expand my network"

Specific and Concrete

  • "I need financial modeling skills to evaluate acquisition targets"
  • "Consulting recruiting requires structured MBA access"
  • "The MBA bridges my credibility gap for an industry pivot"
  • "I need a network in healthtech, where I have zero connections"
4:05 - 5:30 | Here's where many applicants struggle. On the left, weak reasons. "I want to learn business skills" is too vague. "An MBA opens doors" is obvious. Everyone knows this. On the right, strong reasons that connect to specific gaps. "I need financial modeling skills to evaluate acquisition targets." "Consulting recruiting requires structured MBA access." "The MBA bridges my credibility gap for an industry pivot." Notice the pattern: strong reasons connect to specific skill gaps, specific access needs, or specific credibility bridges. Generic reasons don't work.

Timing

Addressing "Why Now?"

Strong Timing Reasons

"I've maximized learning in my current role." "I have enough experience to contribute meaningfully to class." "I've clarified what I want, and the MBA is the fastest path."

Industry Inflection

"The industry is at an inflection point, and now is the time to position for the next wave." Show you understand market shifts.

Address AI Disruption

If your goal is in consulting, finance, or tech, acknowledge AI is reshaping these fields. Show why core MBA skills remain relevant.

Experience Sweet Spot

3-7 years of experience is the typical MBA sweet spot. Enough to contribute to discussions, young enough to pivot.

5:30 - 7:00 | You also need to address timing. Why is this the right moment? Strong reasons: you've maximized learning in your current role, you have enough experience to contribute to class, you've clarified what you want. If the industry is at an inflection point, say so. And a note about AI: if your goal is in consulting, finance, or tech, these fields are being reshaped. Show you understand the disruption and have a thesis for why your skills plus MBA training remain relevant. For consulting, explain why structured thinking, client management, and cross-industry pattern recognition still matter even as deliverables change.

Essay Structure

A Format That Works

Hook + short-term goal → Background connection → Why MBA? → Long-term vision → Why this school (if asked). Every sentence must earn its place in 300-500 words.

Cut generic statements, obvious facts about the school, backstory that doesn't connect to goals, and anything you say elsewhere in the application.

8:30 - 9:30 | Here's a structure that works. Open with something engaging, a moment or realization, then state your short-term goal clearly. Next, briefly explain how your past led you here. This establishes credibility. Then explain specifically why you need the MBA. What will it give you that you don't have? Share your long-term vision and connect it to impact. If the prompt asks, explain why this school specifically. Many schools give you 300 to 500 words. That's tight. Every sentence must earn its place. Cut generic statements, obvious facts, backstory that doesn't connect, and anything you say elsewhere in the application.

Common Mistakes

Three Errors That Sink Goals Essays

Goals Too Vague

"I want to make an impact in healthcare" tells the reader nothing specific about your plan.

Disconnected Arc

Goals that don't connect to your background. If anyone could write this essay, it's missing "you."

No "Why MBA?" Logic

If the reader can't see why you need an MBA specifically, the essay fails its core purpose.

9:30 - 10:45 | Three common errors. Goals too vague: "I want to make an impact in healthcare" tells the reader nothing. What specifically do you want to do? Disconnected arc: if your essay could be written by anyone, it's missing the "you." Why do your experiences make these your goals? No "why MBA?" logic: if the reader can't understand why you need an MBA specifically, and why you can't just get promoted or take a course, the essay fails its core purpose.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Be specific about goals: function, industry, company type
  • Connect past to future with a logical thread through the present
  • Make "why MBA?" concrete with specific skill gaps and access needs
  • Explain "why now" with a clear timing justification
  • Keep it tight. Every sentence must earn its space in 300-500 words
  • Make it yours. Only you could write this essay
