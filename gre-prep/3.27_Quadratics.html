Factoring

Solving

Factor, then set each factor equal to zero.

Discriminant

$b^2 - 4ac$ tells you the number of real solutions.

0:30-1:30 | Three core skills: factoring (find two numbers), solving (set factors to zero), and the discriminant (b^2 - 4ac determines how many solutions). Let's drill each one. Math trick: when the leading coefficient is not 1 (like 6x^2), multiply a times c, factor that product normally, then divide back. This avoids the guesswork of trial-and-error factoring.

Problem 1

Difference of squares

Problem

Factor: $x^2 - 9$

Solution

Pattern: $a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b)$

$x^2 - 9 = x^2 - 3^2$

Answer: $(x+3)(x-3)$

1:30-2:10 | Problem 1: difference of squares. Recognize x^2 - 9 as x^2 - 3^2. Apply the pattern a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b). Answer: (x+3)(x-3).

Problem 2

Standard trinomial factoring

Problem

Factor: $x^2 + 7x + 12$

Solution

Find two numbers: multiply to 12, add to 7

$3 \times 4 = 12$ and $3 + 4 = 7$

Answer: $(x+3)(x+4)$

2:10-2:50 | Problem 2: standard trinomial. Find two numbers that multiply to 12 and add to 7. That is 3 and 4. Answer: (x+3)(x+4).

Problem 3

Perfect square trinomial

Problem

Factor: $x^2 - 8x + 16$

Solution

Check: $\frac{8}{2} = 4$, and $4^2 = 16$

This is a perfect square trinomial

Answer: $(x-4)^2$

2:50-3:30 | Problem 3: perfect square trinomial. Half of 8 is 4, and 4 squared is 16. This confirms the pattern. Answer: (x-4)^2.

Problem 4

Factor out the GCF first

Problem

Factor completely: $2x^2 + 10x + 12$

Solution

Step 1: Factor out 2: $2(x^2 + 5x + 6)$

Step 2: $2 \times 3 = 6$ and $2 + 3 = 5$

Answer: $2(x+2)(x+3)$

3:30-4:10 | Problem 4: always factor out the GCF first. Factor out 2, then factor the remaining trinomial. 2 and 3 multiply to 6 and add to 5. Answer: 2(x+2)(x+3).

Problem 5

Sum and product shortcuts

Problem

Solve $x^2 - x - 12 = 0$. What is the sum of the solutions?

Solution

Shortcut: Sum of roots $= \frac{-b}{a} = \frac{-(-1)}{1}$

Verify: $(x-4)(x+3) = 0$, roots are 4, $-3$

Answer: Sum $= 1$

GRE Tip

Sum of roots $= \frac{-b}{a}$. Product of roots $= \frac{c}{a}$. No factoring needed.

4:10-5:10 | Problem 5: sum of solutions shortcut. Use -b/a = 1. Verify by factoring: roots are 4 and -3, sum is 1. The product shortcut is c/a. These save time on the GRE.

Problem 6

Using the discriminant

Problem

How many real solutions does $x^2 - 4x + 5 = 0$ have?

Solution

Discriminant: $b^2 - 4ac$

$(-4)^2 - 4(1)(5) = 16 - 20 = -4$

Negative discriminant: no real solutions

Answer: $0$ real solutions

5:10-5:50 | Problem 6: discriminant. Calculate b^2 - 4ac = 16 - 20 = -4. Negative means no real solutions. Positive means two, zero means exactly one.

Reference

Discriminant decision table

Discriminant $b^2 - 4ac$ Number of Solutions What It Means
Positive ($> 0$) 2 real solutions Parabola crosses x-axis twice
Zero ($= 0$) 1 real solution Parabola touches x-axis once
Negative ($< 0$) 0 real solutions Parabola misses x-axis entirely
5:50-6:30 | Discriminant reference table. Positive gives two solutions, zero gives one, negative gives none. The GRE frequently asks "how many solutions" or "for what value of k does this have exactly one solution."

Problem 7

Finding k for one solution

Problem

For what positive value of $k$ does $x^2 + kx + 9 = 0$ have exactly one real solution?

Solution

Step 1: Set discriminant $= 0$

Step 2: $k^2 - 4(1)(9) = 0$

Step 3: $k^2 = 36$, so $k = \pm 6$

Answer: $k = 6$

6:30-7:30 | Problem 7: finding k. For exactly one solution, set discriminant = 0. k^2 - 36 = 0, k = plus or minus 6. The positive value is 6.

Problem 8

Sum and product of roots

Problem

If $r$ and $s$ are solutions of $x^2 - 5x + 3 = 0$, find $r^2 + s^2$ without solving.

Solution

Step 1: $r + s = 5$ and $rs = 3$

Step 2: $r^2 + s^2 = (r+s)^2 - 2rs$

Step 3: $= 25 - 6$

Answer: $r^2 + s^2 = 19$

7:30-8:30 | Problem 8: using sum and product of roots. r + s = 5 and rs = 3 from Vieta's formulas. Use the identity r^2 + s^2 = (r+s)^2 - 2rs = 25 - 6 = 19. No need to find individual roots.

Problem 9

Quadratic in disguise

Problem

Solve: $x^4 - 13x^2 + 36 = 0$

Solution

Step 1: Let $y = x^2$: $y^2 - 13y + 36 = 0$

Step 2: $(y-4)(y-9) = 0$, so $y = 4$ or $y = 9$

Step 3: $x^2 = 4 \rightarrow x = \pm 2$; $x^2 = 9 \rightarrow x = \pm 3$

Answer: $x = \pm 2, \pm 3$

8:30-9:30 | Problem 9: quadratic in disguise. Substitute y = x^2 to convert to a standard quadratic. Factor to get y = 4 or y = 9. Back-substitute: x = plus or minus 2 and plus or minus 3. Four solutions total.

Quadratic Formula

When factoring fails

$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$

Problem

Solve: $2x^2 + 3x - 1 = 0$

Solution

Step 1: $a = 2$, $b = 3$, $c = -1$

Step 2: $x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{9 + 8}}{4} = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{17}}{4}$

Answer: $x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{17}}{4}$

GRE Tip

Use the formula when the trinomial does not factor neatly. If the discriminant is a perfect square, factoring would have worked.

9:30-10:30 | The quadratic formula: x = (-b plus or minus sqrt(b^2 - 4ac)) / 2a. Use it when the trinomial does not factor cleanly. Here a=2, b=3, c=-1. Discriminant = 9+8 = 17, not a perfect square, so the formula is the right approach.

Completing the Square

Converting to vertex form

Problem

Solve: $x^2 + 6x + 2 = 0$

Solution

Step 1: Move constant: $x^2 + 6x = -2$

Step 2: Half of 6 is 3, square it: add 9 to both sides

Step 3: $(x + 3)^2 = 7$

Answer: $x = -3 \pm \sqrt{7}$

When to Use

The GRE tests completing the square when asking for vertex form or when a problem says "rewrite in the form $(x + a)^2 = b$."

10:30-11:30 | Completing the square. Move the constant, take half the linear coefficient (6/2 = 3), square it (9), add to both sides. Factor the left side as a perfect square. Take the square root. GRE tests this for vertex form and "rewrite" problems.

Key Takeaways

Quadratics

  • Difference of squares: $a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b)$
  • Standard trinomial: Find two numbers that multiply to $c$ and add to $b$.
  • GCF first: Always factor out the greatest common factor before trying other methods.
  • Discriminant: $b^2 - 4ac$ determines the number of real solutions (2, 1, or 0).
  • Sum/product shortcuts: Sum $= \frac{-b}{a}$, product $= \frac{c}{a}$. Use for $r^2 + s^2$ problems.
  • Quadratic formula: $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ when factoring is not obvious.
  • Completing the square: Half the linear coefficient, square it, add to both sides.
  • Quadratics in disguise: Substitute $y = x^2$ for fourth-degree equations.
