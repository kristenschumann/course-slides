School Perspective

Three Things Schools Evaluate

Genuine Interest

Have you done your homework? Do you understand what makes this school different from the rest?

Fit

Do your goals and values align with their offerings? Will you thrive and contribute to this community?

Yield Prediction

Schools track who accepts offers. Demonstrating informed interest signals you're likely to enroll.

1:00 - 2:15 | When schools ask "why here?", they're evaluating three things. Genuine interest: have you done your homework? Do you actually understand what makes this school different? Fit: do your goals and values align with what they offer? Will you thrive in this environment and contribute to the community? Yield prediction: schools track who accepts their offers. Demonstrating genuine, informed interest signals you're not just using them as a backup. Generic essays fail on all three counts. Specific essays succeed because they show: "I understand exactly what makes you different, and here's why that matters for my specific goals."

Research Framework

How to Research Each School

  • Start with basics: Curriculum, concentrations, special programs, employment reports.
  • Go deeper: Professor profiles, course descriptions, student blogs, info sessions.
  • Talk to people: Current students and alumni are invaluable. Ask what surprised them.
  • Take notes: When something specific excites you, write it down. These details power your essay.
2:15 - 3:45 | The foundation of a strong "why this school" essay is research. Start with the basics: school website, curriculum, concentrations, special programs, employment reports. Go deeper: professor profiles, course descriptions, student blogs, information sessions, campus visits if possible. Talk to people. Nothing beats conversations with current students or recent alumni. Ask them what makes this school different and what surprised them. And take notes. When you find something specific that excites you, write it down. These details will power your essay.

Essay Content

What to Include in Your Essay

Category Example Connection
Academics "Professor Chen's data-driven decision making course addresses the analytical gap I need to fill"
Clubs/Activities "The Healthcare Club conference brings together the industry leaders I need in my network"
Community "The collaborative culture Maya described matches how I've always worked best"
Geography "The school's location gives direct access to the tech employers I'm targeting"
Contribution "My experience in emerging markets would add perspective to the Global Initiatives Lab"
3:45 - 5:15 | Your essay should have specific, concrete connections between your goals and the school's offerings. Academics: name a specific course and explain why it matters for your goals. Clubs and activities: explain how a specific club or practicum aligns with your post-MBA plans. Community and culture: reference a conversation with a student or a specific aspect of the culture that resonates. Geography and industry connections: explain how the location gives access to your target employers. And contribution: this is often overlooked but important. Admissions wants to know what you'll bring, not just what you'll take. Notice the pattern: every point connects something specific about the school to something specific about you.

What to Exclude

Generic Praise vs. Real Connection

Skip These

  • "Wharton has an excellent reputation"
  • "The faculty is world-class"
  • "Stanford is located in Silicon Valley"
  • "Diverse community and global perspectives"

Write These Instead

  • "Professor Bradlow's Marketing Analytics fills my specific skill gap"
  • "The Entrepreneurship Lab aligns with my goal to found a company"
  • "Three alumni in healthtech are doing exactly what I want to do"
  • "The learning team structure builds relationships I value"
5:15 - 6:30 | What to exclude. On the left: generic praise that every applicant says. "Wharton has an excellent reputation." "The faculty is world-class." "Stanford is in Silicon Valley." These are true but meaningless. They don't differentiate you. On the right: specific connections. A named professor whose course addresses your skill gap. A specific lab that aligns with your long-term goal. Named alumni doing what you want to do. If you can swap the school name and the essay still works, it's too generic.

6:30 - 7:45 | Here's a structure that works. Start with a personal hook: a conversation with an alum, an event you attended, a moment when this school clicked. Then 2 to 3 specific connections, each linking an aspect of the school to your goals or values. Structure each point: here's something specific about this school, here's why it matters for my goals, and optionally, here's how I'll contribute. School-Specific Tips

Positioning for Top Programs

HBS / Stanford

HBS: emphasize leadership and the case method. Stanford: focus on personal development, authenticity, and their mission to "change lives."

Wharton / Booth

Wharton: analytical rigor and flexible curriculum. Booth: intellectual curiosity and the freedom to create your own path.

Kellogg / Sloan

Kellogg: teamwork and collaboration. MIT Sloan: innovation and action-oriented "mind and hand" learning.

Your Approach

Prompts evolve yearly. Use these as starting points, but always check current prompts and do fresh research each cycle.

7:45 - 9:00 | Quick positioning tips for top schools. HBS: emphasize leadership and impact, reference the case method. Stanford: focus on personal development and authenticity, reference their mission. Wharton: highlight analytical rigor and the flexible curriculum. Booth: emphasize intellectual curiosity and freedom to create your own path. Kellogg: focus on teamwork and collaboration. MIT Sloan: highlight innovation and action-oriented learning. Important caveat: essay prompts, school priorities, and cultural emphasis evolve from year to year. Use these as starting points, but always check the current cycle's prompts and do fresh research.

Common Mistakes

Five Errors That Weaken Essays

  • Interchangeable essay: If you can swap the school name and it still works, start over.
  • All take, no give: Focus only on what you'll gain with nothing about what you'll contribute.
  • Namedropping without substance: Mentioning a professor without connecting to your goals.
  • Sounding like a brochure: Don't repeat marketing copy. Add your own insight.
9:00 - 10:00 | Five common mistakes. First, the interchangeable essay. If you can swap the school name and it still works, it's too generic. Start over. Second, all take, no give. Focusing only on what you'll gain with nothing about what you'll contribute to the community. Third, namedropping without substance. Mentioning a professor or course without explaining why it matters for your goals. Fourth, sounding like a brochure. Don't repeat marketing copy back to the school. Add your own insight and perspective.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Do deep research. Conversations with students and alumni are invaluable
  • Be specific. Name courses, professors, clubs, and alumni
  • Connect everything to your specific goals and values
  • Show what you'll contribute, not just what you'll take
  • Exclude generic praise that could apply to any school
  • Make it personal. Only you could have written this essay
10:00 - 10:30 | Key takeaways. Do deep research. Conversations with students and alumni are invaluable. Be specific. Name courses, professors, clubs, alumni. Connect everything to your specific goals and values. Show what you'll contribute, not just what you'll take. Exclude generic praise that could apply to any school. And make it personal. Only you could have written this essay. This essay proves you understand the school and yourself. Make that clear.
