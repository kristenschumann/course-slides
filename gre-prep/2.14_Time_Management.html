on the GRE

0:00 - 0:30 | Hook: The GRE gives you a fixed amount of time per section. Knowing how to budget that time across question types is one of the biggest score levers. Today we cover section-level benchmarks, the mark-and-return strategy, and how to pace yourself on Text Completion questions specifically.

Section Benchmarks

Know your time budget

Verbal Reasoning

23 minutes, ~15 questions

Average: ~1.5 min/question

TC and SE should take less, giving you more time for Reading Comprehension passages.

Quantitative Reasoning

26 minutes, ~15 questions

Average: ~1.75 min/question

Data Interpretation sets take longer. Budget extra time for those and move faster on arithmetic.

Key insight: These are averages. Some questions should take 30 seconds, others 3 minutes. The skill is knowing which is which.

0:30 - 1:30 | Section-level benchmarks. Verbal: 23 minutes for about 15 questions, averaging 1.5 minutes each. Quant: 26 minutes for about 15 questions, averaging 1.75 minutes each. But these are just averages. The real skill is spending less time on easy questions so you have more time for hard ones.

1:30 - 3:00 | The mark-and-return strategy. First pass: answer everything you can quickly. If stuck for more than 30 seconds, guess, mark, and move on. Second pass: return to marked questions with whatever time remains. Never leave anything blank since there is no penalty for wrong answers. The biggest mistake is getting stuck on one hard question and losing time for easier questions later in the section.

Know the Format

The three types of text completion

Single-Blank

One blank with five answer choices. Tests your ability to understand sentence meaning and find the word that fits.

Double-Blank

Two blanks with three choices each. You must get BOTH right to earn credit. No partial points.

Triple-Blank

Three blanks with three choices each. Again, all three must be correct for any credit.

Key Insight

The strategy is the same for all types: look for clues in the sentence that tell you what kind of word belongs in each blank.

0:30 - 2:00 | Three flavors of TC: single-blank (5 choices), double-blank (3 choices each, no partial credit), triple-blank (3 choices each, no partial credit). Good news: multi-blank questions have fewer choices per blank. The strategy is the same for all three types.

The Method

Predict-then-match

Step 1: Read for structure

Read the entire sentence first. Understand the overall meaning and logical structure.

Step 2: Find the clue

Every blank has a clue: a word or phrase that tells you what the blank must mean.

Step 3: Predict your word

BEFORE looking at choices, predict what word fits. "Good," "bad," "increase" are fine.

Step 4: Match

Find the choice matching your prediction. Predicted "criticize" and see "censure"? That's it.

2:00 - 3:30 | The four-step method: Read for structure and meaning, find the clue (similar meaning, opposite, cause/effect, example), predict your own word before looking at choices, then match. This prevents you from being swayed by attractive wrong answers. Key coaching insight: do NOT look at answer choices before predicting. Students who peek at choices first often latch onto a word that colors how they read the sentence, leading to errors.

Quick Reference

Signal words speed up predictions

Signal words tell you whether the blank matches or contrasts the clue. Spotting them instantly saves time.

Same Direction

and, moreover, in fact, thus, therefore

Opposite Direction

but, however, although, despite, rather

Advanced Tips

Multi-blank strategy

  • Solve the easiest blank first. Don't go in order. Start with whichever blank has the clearest clue or most obvious answer
  • Use process of elimination. For multi-blank questions, eliminate choices for one blank to narrow options for the others
  • Read the completed sentence. After filling all blanks, read the whole thing. Does it make logical sense from start to finish?
  • Don't overthink partial matches. If two blanks are clear but the third is uncertain, trust the two you're confident about and pick the best remaining option
6:30 - 7:30 | Multi-blank tips: solve the easiest blank first, use elimination, always read the completed sentence for sense check, and don't overthink when two blanks are clear. These strategies save time and reduce errors on the hardest TC questions. Pro tip: use scratch paper even on verbal. Write down your predicted word for each blank before looking at choices, then mark ABCDE and cross out as you go. Externalizing your thinking frees up mental energy for the next question.

Key Takeaways

Time management on the GRE

  • Know your benchmarks: ~1.5 min/question on Verbal, ~1.75 min/question on Quant
  • Mark and return: Never get stuck. Guess, mark, move on, and come back with remaining time
  • Predict-Then-Match for TC: Read, find the clue, predict your word, then match to choices
  • TC time targets: Single-blank 30-45 sec, double-blank 60-90 sec, triple-blank 90-120 sec
  • Protect easy points: Spending 4 minutes on one hard question means rushing 2-3 easier ones
8:00 - 8:30 | Summary. Know your section benchmarks. Use mark-and-return so you never get stuck. Predict-Then-Match is your core TC method. Stick to time targets per question type. The overarching principle: protect your easy points by not spending too long on any single question. For detailed signal word strategies, see Lesson 2.21.
