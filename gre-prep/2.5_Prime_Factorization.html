Prime Factorization

The method

Every positive integer can be written as a product of primes. Keep dividing by the smallest prime that works.

Example: Factor 180

$180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5$

GCF

Greatest common factor

GCF = product of shared primes, using the LOWEST power of each.

Problem

Find the GCF of 48 and 180

$48 = 2^4 \times 3$

$180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5$

Solution

Shared primes: 2 and 3 (5 is not in 48)

Lowest powers: $\min(4,2) = 2^2$ and $\min(1,2) = 3^1$

GCF $= 2^2 \times 3 =$ $12$

2:30-4:00 | GCF: product of shared primes at the lowest power. Prime factorize both: 48 is 2^4 times 3, 180 is 2^2 times 3^2 times 5. Shared primes are 2 and 3. Take the lower power of each: 2^2 and 3^1. GCF is 2^2 times 3 equals 12.

LCM

Least common multiple

LCM = product of ALL primes, using the HIGHEST power of each.

Problem

Find the LCM of 48 and 180

$48 = 2^4 \times 3$

$180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5$

Solution

All primes: 2, 3, and 5

Highest powers: $2^4$, $3^2$, $5^1$

LCM $= 16 \times 9 \times 5 =$ $720$

Check

$\text{GCF} \times \text{LCM} = 12 \times 720 = 8{,}640 = 48 \times 180$ ✓

4:00-5:30 | LCM: product of all primes at the highest power. Take every prime that appears in either number: 2, 3, and 5. Use the highest power of each: 2^4, 3^2, 5^1. Multiply: 16 times 9 times 5 equals 720. Check: GCF times LCM equals 12 times 720 equals 8640, which equals 48 times 180.

Comparison

GCF vs LCM at a glance

Task Which Primes? Which Power?
GCF Only shared primes Lowest power
LCM All primes from both Highest power

Useful formula: $\text{GCF}(a,b) \times \text{LCM}(a,b) = a \times b$

5:30-6:00 | Quick comparison: GCF uses shared primes at lowest powers. LCM uses all primes at highest powers. And the product of GCF and LCM always equals the product of the two numbers. This formula is tested on the GRE. [Leland+ Tip] Always break numbers down to primes before dealing with GCF/LCM problems. The GRE rewards this approach over listing multiples. Remember the mnemonic: Fewer Factors, More Multiples. Factors are always smaller; multiples go to infinity.

Example 1

Using the GCF-LCM formula

Problem

The GCF of 36 and some positive integer $n$ is 12. The LCM of 36 and $n$ is 180.

What is $n$?

Solution

Step 1: Use formula: $\text{GCF} \times \text{LCM} = 36 \times n$

Step 2: $12 \times 180 = 36n$, so $2{,}160 = 36n$

Step 3: $n = 2{,}160 \div 36$

Answer: $n = 60$

6:00-6:50 | Example 1: use the GCF times LCM formula. GCF is 12, LCM is 180. 12 times 180 is 2160. 2160 equals 36 times n, so n is 60. Verify: 36 is 2^2 times 3^2, 60 is 2^2 times 3 times 5. GCF is 12, LCM is 180. It checks out.

Example 2

How many factors?

Problem

For how many positive integers $n$ is $\frac{180}{n}$ an integer?

Translation: how many factors does 180 have?

Solution

Step 1: Factor: $180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5^1$

Step 2: Add 1 to each exponent and multiply

$(2+1)(2+1)(1+1) = 3 \times 3 \times 2$

Answer: $18$ factors

6:50-7:30 | Example 2: 180/n is an integer means n divides 180 evenly. How many factors does 180 have? Prime factorize: 2^2 times 3^2 times 5^1. Add 1 to each exponent and multiply: (2+1)(2+1)(1+1) equals 3 times 3 times 2 equals 18 factors.

Key Takeaways

Prime factorization, GCF & LCM

  • Prime factorize - Divide by the smallest prime repeatedly until you reach a prime
  • GCF - Take shared primes at the lowest power
  • LCM - Take all primes at the highest power
  • Check formula - $\text{GCF} \times \text{LCM} = $ product of the two numbers
  • Count factors - Add 1 to each exponent in the prime factorization, then multiply
7:30-8:00 | Summary: prime factorization converts hard arithmetic into simple comparisons of exponents. GCF takes shared primes at lowest powers. LCM takes all primes at highest powers. Use the check formula to verify or solve for unknowns. Count factors by adding 1 to each exponent and multiplying.
