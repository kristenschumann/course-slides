The headline terms

1:00 - 1:45 | The headline terms are straightforward. Pre-money plus investment equals post-money. Price per share comes from pre-money divided by fully diluted shares. The nuance is in "fully diluted," which includes all outstanding options, warrants, convertible instruments, and the option pool. If the term sheet specifies a 15% option pool pre-money, that pool is in the share count before the price is calculated. From 7.4: the pool dilutes the founders, not the new investor.

VC Recruiting Course

What to look for in the preference clause

1x Non-Participating

The standard. Investor gets their money back OR converts to common and takes pro-rata. Whichever is higher. Downside protection, not upside capture.

1x Participating

The aggressive version. Investor gets money back AND participates in remaining proceeds. Double dipping. Material departure from standard at Series A.

Participating with Cap

The compromise. Investor participates until total return hits a cap (2x or 3x), then converts. Limits the double dip but still favors the investor.

1:45 - 2:30 | Liquidation preferences. We covered the mechanics in 7.4. On a term sheet, here is what to look for. [Click] 1x non-participating is the standard. At Benchmark or Sequoia, this is all you will see. [Click] 1x participating is a material departure. The investor gets money back AND pro-rata participation. Shows up at less competitive firms, in tougher markets, or in later-stage deals. [Click] Participating with a cap is the middle ground. Limits the double dip to 2x or 3x total return. Still favors the investor over the standard.

VC Recruiting Course

Down round protection

Broad-Based Weighted Average

The standard. Investor's conversion price adjusts downward, but accounts for the size of the down round relative to the overall cap table. Partial protection. Not a windfall.

Full Ratchet

The aggressive version. Investor's price resets entirely to the new lower price, regardless of how small the down round is. A $10 to $2 repricing creates a 5x increase in share count. Red flag on any term sheet.

2:30 - 3:15 | Anti-dilution protects the investor if a down round happens. [Click] Broad-based weighted average is standard. The adjustment is proportional to the down round size. Partial protection that is fair to both sides. [Click] Full ratchet is a red flag. The investor's entire position reprices to the new lower price. If they bought at $10 and a tiny bridge prices at $2, full ratchet reprices everything to $2. That is a 5x share increase that massively dilutes founders. Rare in competitive markets. Always flag it.

Governance Terms: Where the Power Is

4:00 | Transition into governance terms.

VC Recruiting Course

Board seats: the most underrated term on the sheet

Stage Common Seats Investor Seats Independent Total Control
Series A 2 1 2 5 Balanced
Series B 2 2 1 5 Power shift
Series C 2 3 2 7 Founders minority
4:00 - 4:45 | Board composition evolves and never in the founders' favor. [Click] At Series A, the standard is 2 common seats, 1 investor seat, 2 mutually selected independents. Neither side has outright control. [Click] At Series B, the new investor wants a seat. Now it is 2-2-1. The founders lost their advantage. [Click] By Series C, the board might be 2-3-2. Founders are a minority. Each incremental board seat shifts power permanently. That is why the Series A structure matters so much. It sets the baseline every subsequent round negotiates from.

VC Recruiting Course

What the board can block

Standard (Reasonable)

Issuing senior equity. Taking on debt above a threshold. Changing the certificate of incorporation. Selling the company. Changing board size. These prevent the founder from undermining the investor's position.

Non-Standard (Watch For)

Blocking executive hires and fires. Approving the annual budget. Preventing new lines of business. These shift operational control from the CEO to the boardroom. Budget approval rights effectively give the investor a veto over strategy.

4:45 - 5:30 | Protective provisions give preferred stockholders a veto over specific actions. [Click] Standard provisions are reasonable. Block issuing senior equity, taking on major debt, selling the company, changing the charter. These protect the investor without restricting operations. [Click] Non-standard provisions shift operational control. Budget approval, hiring and firing approval, new business lines. A founder who agrees to budget approval has given the investor a veto over strategy. That is a significant concession. Flag it.

VC Recruiting Course

Four more terms to know

Pro-Rata Rights

The right to invest in future rounds to maintain ownership. Standard for lead investors. Becomes contentious when all existing investors exercise and crowd out a new lead.

Information Rights

Quarterly and annual financials, cap table updates. Standard. Monthly financials are more burdensome but not unusual. Often tied to a "major investor" ownership threshold.

Drag-Along Rights

If a specified majority approves a sale, all shareholders must participate. Prevents a minority from blocking an acquisition. Watch the threshold: majority of preferred alone is more aggressive than majority of both preferred and common.

Tag-Along and ROFR

Tag-along lets investors sell alongside a founder in a secondary. ROFR gives the company first right to buy shares before they are sold externally. Both control who ends up on the cap table.

5:30 - 6:30 | Four more governance terms. Pro-rata rights: standard for the lead, contentious when existing investors crowd out new capital. Information rights: quarterly financials and cap table updates, standard. Drag-along: forces all shareholders to participate if a majority approves a sale. Watch whether the threshold requires common votes or just preferred. Tag-along and ROFR: control who can sell and who ends up on the cap table. Both are standard fairness provisions.

VC Recruiting Course

Valuations get renegotiated, board seats do not

I have seen founders who were thrilled with their $25M valuation discover two years later that the protective provisions gave their investor an effective veto over a strategic acquisition the founders wanted to pursue.

A founder who wins on valuation but loses on board control has not won. Valuation is a number you negotiate once. Board seats and protective provisions are structures you live with for the life of the company. The best VCs offer clean terms because their competitive advantage is brand, network, and portfolio, not aggressive deal provisions.

6:30 - 7:00 | The governance section is where experienced founders spend the most time. Valuation is a one-time negotiation. Board seats and protective provisions are permanent. I have watched founders celebrate a high valuation only to discover the governance terms gave their investor a veto over strategy. The valuation did not matter at that point. The governance did.

Reading a Term Sheet in an Interview

7:00 | Transition into the interview application.

VC Recruiting Course

How VCs actually read term sheets

Step 1: Economics

Pre-money valuation, investment amount, liquidation preference, anti-dilution. Calculate ownership. Note participating vs. non-participating. Check anti-dilution type.

Step 2: Governance

Board composition and protective provisions. Who controls the board? What can the investor block unilaterally? These two provisions define the power dynamic.

Step 3: Form an Opinion

Is this fair, aggressive, or founder-friendly? Why? Most candidates describe terms but do not assess them. The interviewer wants your judgment.

Who controls what? [Click] Step three is where most candidates stop short. Form an opinion. Is the term sheet fair, aggressive, or founder-friendly? The interviewer does not want you to recite definitions. They want your assessment and your reasoning.

An aggressive, investor-friendly term sheet

Term Value Assessment
Pre-money / Investment $15M / $5M Fine
Liquidation preference 1x participating (3x cap) Aggressive
Anti-dilution Full ratchet Red flag
Board 1 common, 1 preferred, 1 independent Investor blocking power
Protective provisions Includes budget approval Shifts operational control
8:15 - 8:45 | Now an aggressive one. Participating preferred with a 3x cap is unusual at Series A. Full ratchet is a significant red flag. Three-person board with one investor seat gives the investor blocking power since they only need the independent vote. Budget approval shifts operational control. If advising the founder, push hard to convert the preference to non-participating and the anti-dilution to broad-based weighted average. Those are the two terms that matter most.

Term sheet traffic light

Standard (Green)

1x non-participating. Broad-based weighted average. 5-person board (2/1/2). Standard protective provisions. Non-cumulative dividends.

Negotiate (Yellow)

Participating with cap. Option pool above 15%. Cumulative dividends. 3-person board. Pro-rata on small checks.

Red Flag

Full ratchet anti-dilution. Participating uncapped. Investor-controlled board. Budget approval rights. Executive hire/fire approval.

8:45 - 9:15 | A quick reference. [Click] Green: everything standard. 1x non-participating, broad-based weighted average, balanced board. This is what you see from tier-one funds. [Click] Yellow: negotiate these. Participating with a cap, oversized option pool, cumulative dividends. Not deal-breakers but worth pushing back. [Click] Red: full ratchet, uncapped participation, investor-controlled board, budget approval. These fundamentally shift economics or control. Flag them immediately in an interview and explain what you would negotiate.

VC Recruiting Course

Diplomacy in the interview

If the interviewer's firm uses aggressive terms and you call out those exact terms as red flags, you might create an awkward moment. Frame your analysis in terms of trade-offs, not judgments.

"Participating preferred shifts economics toward the investor in moderate outcomes, which makes sense if the investor is providing significant value-add beyond capital." That is diplomatic without being dishonest. The skill is having the opinion and knowing when and how to express it.

9:00 - 9:15 | One practical note. If you are interviewing at a firm known for participating preferred and you call it a red flag, you have created a problem. Frame it as a trade-off. "Participating preferred makes sense if the investor provides significant value beyond capital." Diplomatic without being dishonest. The skill is having the opinion and knowing how to deliver it.

Key takeaways

  • Economic terms and governance terms are two different negotiations on the same document. A founder who wins on valuation but loses on board control has not won.
  • 1x non-participating with broad-based weighted average is the standard. Anything beyond that is a departure, and departures need justification. If you can explain why a term departs and what it means for both sides, you understand term sheets at associate level.
  • Board composition is the most underrated term. Valuations get renegotiated. Liquidation preferences get restructured. Board seats, once given, are almost never taken back.
  • When reading a term sheet in an interview, do not recite. Prioritize. Identify the two or three departures from standard. Explain who they favor. Offer a specific opinion on whether the package is fair. That is the skill being tested.
