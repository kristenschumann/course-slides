Vocabulary Efficiently

0:00 - 0:30 | Hook: Vocabulary knowledge accounts for roughly 50% of your GRE Verbal score. Not just because questions directly test it, but because understanding words is essential for TC, SE, and RC. This video covers how to build vocabulary efficiently.

The Foundation

Why Vocabulary Matters

Vocabulary affects every question type on GRE Verbal, not just the ones that directly test words.

Text Completion & SE

Directly test whether you know words. If you don't know what "perfunctory" means, you'll struggle with questions that use it.

Reading Comprehension

Stumbling over unfamiliar words kills comprehension and wastes time. You can't analyze what you can't understand.

Logical Structure

Words like "notwithstanding," "albeit," and "ostensibly" signal specific logical relationships. Miss the word, miss the meaning.

0:30 - 2:00 | Three ways vocabulary matters: TC/SE directly test it, RC becomes harder without it, and logical structure words signal relationships. Vocabulary is the foundation for all of Verbal.

Common Mistakes

What Doesn't Work

Memorizing Long Lists

Going through alphabetical lists means you remember "abase" and "aberration" but forget everything by "zealot." Your brain needs variety.

Definitions Without Context

Knowing "sanguine" means "optimistic" isn't enough. You need to see it in sentences and understand its connotations.

Passive Review

Looking at a flashcard and thinking "yeah, I know that" doesn't create lasting memory. You need active retrieval.

Save Your Time

These approaches feel productive but don't build the deep word knowledge the GRE requires.

2:00 - 3:30 | Three common mistakes: memorizing alphabetical lists, learning definitions without context, and passive flashcard review. All feel productive but don't create lasting, usable vocabulary knowledge.

Part 2

The CUES Method

3:30 - 3:45 | Transition: Now that we know what doesn't work, here's the system that does. The CUES method gives you a framework for learning words that actually stick.

Active Learning

The CUES Method

C - Context First

Read the word in a sentence before looking up the definition. Try to guess the meaning.

U - Understand, Don't Memorize

Learn roots, connotations, and related words. "Bellicose" shares a root with "rebel" and "belligerent."

E - Examples Matter

Create your own sentence or think of a real person. "Loquacious" = talkative.

S - Spaced Repetition

Review at intervals: 1, 3, 7, 14 days. Each recall strengthens the memory.

3:45 - 5:30 | The CUES method: Context first (read in sentence, guess meaning), Understand don't memorize (learn roots and word families), Examples matter (personal associations), Spaced repetition (increasing intervals). Demonstrate with "perfunctory": context sentence, root per- + fungi (going through motions), related words cursory/superficial, personal example.

Beyond Definitions

Connotation and Tone

Words have emotional weight beyond their definitions. The GRE often tests whether you can match tone.

Concept Positive Neutral Negative
Thin Slender Skinny Gaunt
Self-assured Confident Assertive Arrogant
Careful with money Frugal Thrifty Miserly

Study Tip

When learning a new word, ask: Is this positive, negative, or neutral? Tone helps eliminate wrong answers.

7:00 - 8:30 | Connotation matters: words with similar definitions can have very different emotional weight. Slender vs gaunt, confident vs arrogant, frugal vs miserly. On the GRE, the right answer often depends on matching tone, not just definition.

Part 3

Your Study Plan

8:30 - 8:45 | Transition: Now let's put this into a concrete, actionable study plan you can start today.

8-10 Week Plan

Your Vocabulary Study Plan

Daily: New Words

Learn 10-15 new words using the CUES method. That's 700-1,000 words total, covering high-frequency GRE vocabulary.

Daily: Review

Review 20-30 old words using spaced repetition. Focus on words you've missed before.

Weekly: Test Yourself

Take a vocabulary quiz under pressure. Testing creates stronger memories than passive review.

Consistency Wins

Fifteen minutes daily beats two hours weekly. Vocabulary is built through repeated exposure, not cramming.

8:45 - 10:00 | Concrete study plan: 10-15 new words daily with CUES method, review 20-30 old words with spaced repetition, weekly quizzes. Sample schedule: morning 10 min for new words, lunch 5 min for yesterday's review, evening 5 min for older words. Consistency beats cramming.

Active Use

Cement Your Vocabulary

The best way to lock in vocabulary is to actively use it. Three ways to practice:

Practice Questions

Do TC and SE questions regularly. When you miss one because of vocabulary, add that word to your list.

Challenging Reading

Read The Economist, The Atlantic, or academic abstracts. Notice how words you've studied are used in context.

Practice Writing

Deliberately use new words in practice essays. Using a word in your own writing creates the strongest memory.

10:00 - 11:00 | Three ways to actively cement vocabulary: practice TC/SE questions and add missed words to your list, read challenging material and notice studied words in context, and use new words in practice essays for the strongest memory formation.
