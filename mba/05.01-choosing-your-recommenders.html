Selection Criteria

The Six Must-Haves

  • Knows you well: Worked closely with you and observed your performance firsthand.
  • Direct supervisor: At least one must be a current or former manager.
  • Complementary perspectives: Each brings a different dimension of your candidacy.
1:00 - 2:15 | There are six must-have requirements. First, your recommenders need to know you well enough to write specifically about your strengths. They should have worked with you closely and observed your professional performance firsthand. Second, at least one must be a direct manager or supervisor. This is especially important for HBS and GSB, who are specific about this. Third, your recommenders should complement each other but not overlap. They should come from different parts of your professional life so each letter brings something uniquely powerful.

Selection Criteria

Three More Must-Haves

  • Credible: Professional standing matters, but alumni status is not required.
  • Understands the stakes: Knows what top programs look for (you can coach them).
  • Wants you to succeed: Genuinely invested in your outcome, not indifferent.
2:15 - 3:30 | Fourth, your recommenders need to be credible. This means professional standing, not personal connections. Alumni or MBA status is neither the only nor the best way to prove credibility. A recommender with personal, powerful stories about you is more important than having MBA after their name. Fifth, they need to understand what it takes to get into a top program. Even if they've never written a recommendation before, you can and should coach them. Sixth, your recommender needs to genuinely want you to succeed. When their incentives are aligned with yours, that's when the magic happens.

Working Professionals

Your Strongest Recommender Types

Direct Managers

Current or former supervisors who oversaw your day-to-day work and performance

Senior Colleagues

People who worked closely with you on significant projects or cross-functional teams

Clients or Partners

External stakeholders from consulting, sales, or client-facing roles

Mentors

People who have observed your professional growth over an extended period

3:30 - 4:45 | As a working professional, your strongest recommenders will typically be current or former direct managers, senior colleagues who worked closely with you on major projects, clients or external partners if you were in consulting or client-facing roles, and mentors who have observed your growth over time. While professors can work if you've taken recent executive education, admissions committees are most interested in how you perform in a professional setting.

4:45 - 6:00 | Here is the step-by-step process. First, make a list of every recommender who fits the criteria. Second, have initial conversations with your top 3-5 candidates. Reach out, share that you're applying, gauge their enthusiasm, and get a sense of whether they'd write a strong letter. Third, score each potential recommender on the six must-haves using a simple 1-3 scale. Fourth, overlay two strategic questions: do my top two recommenders cover different dimensions? And does either have a school tie that adds weight? This turns a gut decision into a structured one.

Language Matters

Weak vs. Strong Recommendations

Weak

"Evelyn is very mature and gets positive feedback from everyone she works with."

Strong

"Evelyn's level of maturity exceeds that of her much older colleagues. She received extremely positive feedback across the broader company, and we extended her an offer at a higher level than we typically offer to new grads."

7:00 - 8:15 | Look at the difference specificity makes. The weak version says Evelyn is mature and gets positive feedback. It's vague and forgettable. The strong version says her maturity exceeds much older colleagues, she got positive feedback across the broader company, and they extended an offer at a higher level than typical for new grads. Specific claims backed by evidence. The best recommendations use bold language and concrete stories. These schools are not in the business of accepting average people, so the recommendation should not describe you as average.

Recommender Mindset

They're Fighting for You

Great recommenders understand they are on your team. You are not asking for an unbiased evaluation. You are drafting them to help you get into business school.

How well your recommender knows you and the words they are willing to write about you is significantly more important than who they are. Alumni status is nice-to-have, not a must-have.

8:15 - 9:15 | Here is the most important principle. Business schools try to trick recommenders into providing an objective evaluation, but that is not what you're asking for. You want your recommenders to be truthful, but you're drafting them onto your team. Great recommenders fully understand that, fight for you, and involve you throughout the process. And remember: how well your recommender knows you and the words they're willing to write is significantly more important than who they are. An alumni who barely knows you will never outperform a manager who can tell powerful stories about your work.

Strategic Coverage

Cover Three Dimensions

Technical Excellence

One recommender who speaks to your functional skills and domain expertise

Leadership Skills

One who speaks to your interpersonal abilities and how you lead and collaborate

Growth Potential

One who speaks to your trajectory, ambition, and potential for future impact

9:15 - 9:45 | Think strategically about coverage. Ideally, have one recommender who speaks to your technical and functional excellence, one who covers your leadership and interpersonal skills, and one who can speak to your growth potential and future impact. Your creativity and thoughtfulness here will round out the rest of your application. Allow 3 to 4 months from first outreach to deadline. It can be done in two months, but we don't recommend less. Give recommenders at least a month from receiving prep materials to the due date, and six weeks if applying to three or more schools.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Apply the six must-haves: knows you well, supervisor, complementary, credible, understands stakes, wants you to succeed
  • How well they know you matters more than who they are
  • Use the four-step selection process: list, explore, score, decide
  • Allow 3-4 months from first outreach to application deadline
  • Look for bold claims backed by specific stories
  • Draft them onto your team. They're fighting for you, not evaluating you
9:45 - 10:00 | Key takeaways. Apply the six must-haves when choosing recommenders. Remember that how well they know you matters far more than their title or alumni status. Use the four-step selection process to turn a gut decision into a structured one. Allow 3 to 4 months for the full process. Look for bold, specific language backed by real stories. And draft your recommenders onto your team. Choose wisely and set them up for success.
