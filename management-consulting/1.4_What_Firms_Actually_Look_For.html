Leadership is assessed through behavioral stories. Cultural fit is the one nobody talks about openly but everyone evaluates.

Evaluation Criteria

The Airport Test

Cultural fit is the criterion nobody talks about openly, but every interviewer evaluates. Consulting is a people business: you travel, work late, and present alongside your team.

The "Airport Test": Would I want to be stuck in an airport with this person after a tough client meeting? An unofficial but real evaluation criterion at every major firm.

1:30 - 2:30 | Partners ask themselves a simple question: would I want to be stuck in an airport with this person? It's literally called the airport test, and it influences more decisions than any firm will admit.

Evaluation Weighting

How Components Weight

01

Case Performance

50-60% of the decision. Your analytical and communication skills under pressure.

02

Fit / Behavioral

~30% of the decision. Your stories, your energy, your interpersonal presence.

03

Resume / Credentials

~10% of the decision. Got you in the door, but barely matters now.

2:30 - 4:00 | No firm publishes an official weighting, but after years of interviewing and calibration sessions, case performance is about half the decision. The surprise is how much fit matters: ~30%. Most candidates are bunched on case performance, so fit becomes the tiebreaker. Resume got you the interview, but once you're sitting across from an interviewer, nobody is thinking about your GPA.

Leadership & Drive

What Counts as Leadership

What Works

Evidence of impact and influence with specific, measurable outcomes.

"Built a student org from 5 to 50 members and secured university funding"

What Falls Flat

Titles and roles without evidence of what you actually changed or built.

"I was president of the consulting club" or "I managed a team of 8 people"

4:00 - 4:45 | Leadership is not about titles. It's about evidence of impact and influence. The best stories have specific, measurable outcomes. "I grew revenue by 15%" beats "I was responsible for the P&L" every time.

Structured Thinking

What Structured Thinking Looks Like

  • Break the problem into MECE components (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive)
  • State a hypothesis early and test it with data
  • Prioritize which branch to explore first, and explain why
  • Synthesize your findings into a recommendation, even with incomplete information
4:45 - 5:30 | Most candidates think the goal is to get the right answer. It's not. The goal is to show how you think. An interviewer would rather see you build a clean structure and reach a reasonable answer than watch you stumble onto the perfect answer through disorganized thinking. Consultants structure problems for a living. That's the audition.

5:30 - 7:00 | McKinsey values "obligation to dissent" and hypothesis-driven thinking. BCG leans into creativity and going off-script. Bain's culture is the most team-oriented: they watch whether you incorporate feedback and make the interview feel like a partnership. A one-size-fits-all approach won't work if you're targeting all three. Note: McKinsey updated their PEI dimensions in summer 2025 to Connection, Drive, Leadership, and Growth. If you're prepping from a guide published before mid-2025, your categories may be outdated.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Case performance is half the decision, but fit is the tiebreaker. Thirty percent of your evaluation happens in behavioral interviews. Prepare for it with the same rigor you bring to cases.
  • Structured thinking beats the right answer. Show your interviewer how you organize a problem, not just where you land.
  • Leadership is about impact, not titles. Your stories need specific outcomes. "I built, I changed, I influenced" beats "I was responsible for."
  • Each firm has a distinct culture that shapes scoring. McKinsey rewards rigor, BCG rewards creativity, Bain rewards collaboration.
7:00 - 8:00 | Wrap up: Your resume gets you in the room. Your case skills keep you competitive. But the thing that actually gets you the offer is the interviewer walking into the decision meeting and saying, "I want this person on my team." That's what firms actually look for.
