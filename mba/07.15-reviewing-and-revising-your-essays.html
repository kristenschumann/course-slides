Self-Revision

Five Techniques That Work

  • Read aloud: Your brain auto-corrects when you read silently. Your ears won't.
  • Print it out: Paper makes errors more visible. Grab a pen and mark it up.
  • Change the format: Read in a different font, on your phone, or as a PDF.
  • Read backward: Go paragraph-by-paragraph in reverse. Forces you to judge each one on its own.
  • "So what?" test: After each paragraph, ask why it matters. If you can't answer, cut or clarify.
2:00 - 3:30 | Five techniques I actually recommend. Read aloud — your brain auto-corrects silently, but your ears won't let you get away with clunky sentences. Print it out — grab a pen, mark it up. Revision Passes

Six Passes, One at a Time

Pass Focus Key Questions
1. Big Picture Structure and arc Does it answer the prompt? Does the ending land?
2. Content Depth and specifics Is every paragraph necessary? Are there concrete details?
3. Flow Transitions and pacing Do paragraphs connect? Is the pacing right?
4. Voice Tone and authenticity Does this sound like you? Anything generic or cliched?
5. Line Editing Clarity and word choice Can sentences be tighter? Any filler words?

Proofreading comes last. Don't polish sentences you might cut.

3:30 - 5:00 | Don't try to fix everything at once. Six passes, each with a different lens. Pass 1 — big picture: does it answer the prompt? Does the ending land? Pass 2 — content: is every paragraph pulling its weight? Real details or just filler? Pass 3 — flow: do transitions work? Pacing feel right? Pass 4 — voice: does this sound like you, or like an essay? Pass 5 — line editing: tighten sentences, cut filler words. Pass 6 — proofreading. And don't start proofreading until the content is locked. No point perfecting the grammar of a paragraph you might delete.

External Feedback

Who to Ask and What to Ask

Who to Ask

  • Close friends or family who know you well
  • Professional colleagues who know your work
  • People with MBA experience (if available)
  • A Leland MBA coach

What to Ask

  • "Does this answer the prompt fully?"
  • "What's the one thing you remember?"
  • "Is there anything confusing?"
  • "Does this sound like me?"
  • "Where did your attention drift?"
5:05 - 6:30 | Mix up who you ask. People who know you well — they'll catch when something doesn't sound like you. Colleagues who've seen your work. Someone with MBA experience if you can find them. And a Leland MBA coach — they know what adcoms are looking for and can give you expert-level feedback. But be specific about what you're asking. "What do you think?" gets you nothing useful. Instead: does this answer the prompt? What's the one thing you remember? Where did your attention drift? Those questions get you actionable feedback.

Using Feedback

Three Rules for Feedback

Look for Patterns

If three people say the same thing, listen. If one person has a gripe, it might just be taste.

Protect Your Voice

If feedback pushes you toward something that doesn't feel like you, push back. This is your essay.

Know When to Stop

At some point, more rounds of feedback just make you second-guess yourself. If it's solid, send it.

6:30 - 7:45 | Three rules for using feedback well. Look for patterns — if three people flag the same thing, it's real. If one person has a concern, it might just be their taste. Common Mistakes

Five Revision Traps

  • Revising while drafting: Write first, revise later. Perfectionism kills momentum.
  • Asking too many people: Five opinions are more useful than fifteen.
  • Only asking people who'll say it's great: You need honest readers, not cheerleaders.
  • Skipping the break: Fresh eyes are essential. Don't revise what you just wrote.
8:45 - 9:30 | Four traps I see all the time. Revising while drafting — kills your momentum. Write first, fix later. Asking too many people — five opinions beat fifteen. Too much feedback just creates noise. Only asking people who'll tell you it's great — you need honest readers, not cheerleaders. If nobody's pushing back, you're not getting real feedback. And skipping the break — fresh eyes are non-negotiable. Give yourself at least a day between major rounds.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Expect to revise multiple times. First drafts aren't supposed to be good.
  • Give yourself at least a day between drafts for fresh perspective.
  • Use different revision passes for different levels of editing.
  • Get external feedback from varied sources with specific questions.
  • Look for patterns in feedback and protect your authentic voice.
  • Know when to stop. Perfect is the enemy of done.
9:30 - 10:00 | To wrap up: your first draft isn't supposed to be good — give yourself permission for that. Take time between rounds. Use different passes for different levels of editing — don't try to fix everything at once. Get feedback from varied sources with specific questions, not "what do you think?" Look for patterns and protect your voice — if it doesn't sound like you, revert. And know when to stop. Perfect is the enemy of done. Revision is where good essays become great. Put in the time.
