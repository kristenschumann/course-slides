The Right Mindset

An Honor, Not an Imposition

You're not asking for a favor. You're offering an opportunity to be part of your success story. When you internalize that, the conversation becomes much easier.

When someone you've worked with says, "I respect you so much that I want your name on the most important application of my career," that's a genuine compliment. Most managers and mentors view this as an honor.

1:00 - 2:15 | Let's address the elephant in the room. Most applicants dread this conversation. They worry they're imposing or they're afraid of hearing no. But think about it from the other person's perspective. You're saying: your opinion of me matters more than almost anyone else's. Most managers and mentors view this as an honor. They've invested in your growth and being asked is a signal that their investment paid off. The mindset shift is this: you're not asking for a favor. You're offering an opportunity to be part of your success story.

Best Day: Tuesday-Wednesday

Pad Your Deadline

Tell them your deadline is one week before the actual due date for a buffer

2:15 - 3:15 | Before we get into what to say, let's talk about when to say it. The ideal timeline is 3-4 months before your deadline. This gives time for the initial conversation, prep materials, and breathing room for them to write. At minimum, give them 6-8 weeks. They'll probably write everything on one random Saturday, but they need a few weeks to find that Saturday. Mid-week works best for the conversation. And tell them your deadline is one week before the actual due date for a cushion.

Part 1

The Six-Step Framework

How to structure the conversation

3:15 - 3:20 | Now let's walk through the six-step framework for making the ask. This is not something you should do over a quick Slack message. Set up a dedicated time for a real conversation.

Conversation Framework

Steps 1-3: Build Up to the Ask

01

Set the Stage

Catch up naturally, then share that you've decided to apply to business school

02

Ask for Perspective

Ask about their experience with MBA applications. It flatters them and reveals their knowledge level

03

Mention the Stakes

Share that recommendations are the one part you can't control, so you're being very thoughtful

3:20 - 5:00 | Step 1: Set the stage. Catch up, ask about their work and life. Then transition naturally. Something like, "I've decided to apply to business school this year and I've been working on it for a while now." This shows you're serious and already in motion. Step 2: Ask for their perspective. Before asking them to write anything, ask for their advice about the process. This flatters them and gives you a sense of how much coaching they'll need later. Step 3: Mention the stakes. Share that recommendations are the one part you don't control, and because of that you're being incredibly thoughtful about who you ask. This signals that you're going to get in with or without them, but they have the opportunity to be part of it.

Conversation Framework

Steps 4-6: The Ask and Follow-Through

  • Make the ask: "I would be honored if you'd be willing to write a recommendation for me." Be specific about why you chose them.
  • Give them space: Don't pressure for an immediate answer. Mention you're considering a few people so they can think it over.
  • Set expectations: Promise a detailed prep document with deadlines, questions, and examples so they can focus on writing.
5:00 - 6:30 | Step 4: Make the ask. Say something like, "I've really valued working with you, and some of the most meaningful professional experiences I've had have been under your guidance. I would be honored if you'd be willing to write a recommendation for me." Notice: you're specific about why, you use the word honored, and you give them an easy way to opt out. Step 5: Give them space. Don't pressure for an immediate answer. Mention you're considering a few people. This removes pressure and, counterintuitively, makes them more likely to say yes. Step 6: Set expectations. Immediately reassure them that you'll put together a detailed prep document with everything they need. You've just neutralized the scariest part, the unknown time burden.

Sensitive Situations

When Your Boss Can't Know

Option Who Best When
Former manager Previous boss who supervised meaningful work You left on good terms and they know you well
Senior colleague Project lead, client partner, or skip-level They worked closely with you on visible projects
Cross-functional leader Dotted-line supervisor from another team You collaborated on cross-team initiatives

If asked why your current supervisor isn't included, keep it simple: "Disclosing at this stage could impact my role. I've selected recommenders who can speak in detail to my professional capabilities."

6:30 - 7:45 | This is one of the most common scenarios. You need a supervisor as a recommender, but telling your current boss could jeopardize your job. Admissions committees understand this completely. Option one: use a former direct manager. This is the cleanest solution. Option two: use a senior colleague, a project lead, a client partner, or a skip-level manager. Option three: use a cross-functional leader from a dotted-line relationship. If an application asks why your current supervisor isn't included, keep it brief and honest. Don't overthink it. Admissions committees read this explanation hundreds of times every cycle. One important note: don't misrepresent the relationship. If they're not your supervisor, don't call them one.

Reading the Room

Handling Less-Than-Ideal Responses

Lukewarm Yes

If they hedge or pause too long, give them an easy out. A reluctant recommender writes a reluctant letter.

"I'm Too Busy"

Respect it. Thank them sincerely and leave the door open. Sometimes they circle back later.

"Am I the Right Person?"

Ask who they'd suggest. They may point you to an even stronger recommender you hadn't considered.

Outright No

Rare, but don't take it personally. Thank them sincerely and move to your next candidate.

7:45 - 8:45 | Not every ask goes perfectly. If you get a lukewarm yes with hedging or long pauses, pay attention. Give them an easy out by saying you completely understand if the timing doesn't work. A reluctant recommender writes a reluctant letter. If they say they're too busy, respect it and thank them. Sometimes they circle back later. If they ask whether they're the right person, ask who they would suggest. They often know your network better than you think. An outright no is rare, but if it happens, don't take it personally. Thank them and move on.

8:45 - 9:30 | Once they say yes, lock in the logistics. First, send a thank-you note within 24 hours. Reiterate that you'll make the process easy and you'll be in touch soon. Second, confirm your padded deadlines in writing and make sure they're on their calendar. Third, schedule a 30-45 minute follow-up meeting for 1-2 weeks later where you'll walk them through your prep document. Fourth, once the process is underway, check in every 7-14 days. Not to nag, but to offer support. A couple friendly emails keep you on their radar without being annoying.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Reframe the ask: you're honoring them, not imposing on them
  • Time it right: 3-4 months out is ideal, 6-8 weeks is the minimum
  • Use the six-step framework: stage, perspective, stakes, ask, space, expectations
  • If your boss can't know, use a former manager or senior colleague
  • Read the room on lukewarm responses and give them an easy out
  • Lock in logistics: thank-you, deadlines, prep meeting, regular check-ins
9:30 - 10:00 | Key takeaways. Reframe the ask as an honor, not an imposition. Time it 3-4 months out. Use the six-step conversation framework. If your boss can't know, use a former manager and keep the explanation simple. Read the room on lukewarm responses. And lock in the logistics once they say yes. Approach this with confidence, give them space to say yes enthusiastically, and set the stage for a great recommendation process.
