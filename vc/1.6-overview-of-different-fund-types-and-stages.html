Section 1

Investment Stages Defined

Five stages, five different languages, five different cultures

[1:00] Transition into the five investment stages. Each one has its own language, metrics, and culture. A partner at Benchmark and a partner at General Atlantic are both "venture capitalists" — but they might as well be in different industries.

Stage 1

Pre-Seed: Investing in Ideas

Team Conviction

Evaluation is almost entirely about the founders — domain conviction, resilience, genuine insight about a market

Investors: Angels, accelerators (YC, Techstars), pre-seed funds

Market Insight

Round size under $2M, checks $25K-$500K. SAFEs and convertible notes — not priced rounds

Companies: Often pre-product, almost always pre-revenue

[1:00–1:30] Pre-seed is conviction investing in its purest form. [Click 1] Evaluation is almost entirely about the founders: do they have a genuine insight about a market? Companies are often pre-product, almost always pre-revenue. Investors are angels, accelerators like Y Combinator and Techstars, and dedicated pre-seed funds. [Click 2] Rounds under $2M, checks from $25K to $500K. Instruments are SAFEs or convertible notes, not priced rounds.

Stage 2

Seed: Early Traction Signals

  • Round size: $2-5M
  • Check size: $500K-$3M
  • Investors: First Round Capital, Precursor, Initialized, angels
  • Ownership target: 10-15%

What "traction" actually means at seed

  • 500 DAUs with 40% week-over-week retention
  • $15K MRR growing 20% month-over-month
  • 10,000-person waitlist with zero revenue
  • The job: deciding which signals are real vs. vanity metrics
[1:30–2:15] The company has a product — maybe rough, but real users are touching it. [Click 1] Rounds $2-5M, checks $500K-$3M, targeting 10-15% ownership. Key investors: First Round Capital, Precursor Ventures, Initialized Capital. [Click 2] "Traction" is overused — here's what it actually looks like at seed. Evaluation shifts toward product quality, early traction signals, and founder-market fit. The question: is this specific founder uniquely positioned to win in this specific market?

Stage 3

Series A: The Great Filter

  • Round size: $10-25M | Check size: $5-15M
  • Ownership target: 15-25%
  • Investors: Benchmark, Greylock, Bessemer, multi-stage firms
  • Metrics that matter: Revenue growth rates, unit economics trajectory, go-to-market efficiency
  • Key question: Is the LTV-to-CAC ratio heading in the right direction?
[2:15–2:50] Series A is the great filter — companies need repeatable revenue and a go-to-market motion that works consistently. [Click 1] Rounds $10-25M, checks $5-15M. [Click 2] Ownership targets 15-25%. [Click 3] The classic early-stage venture firms: Benchmark, Greylock, Bessemer. [Click 4] The evaluation framework shifts: revenue growth rates, unit economics trajectory, GTM efficiency. [Click 5] Data starts to matter as much as narrative — can you acquire customers at a cost that makes economic sense?

Stage 4

Series B: Can This Scale?

Execution Risk

Round size: $30-60M | Check size: $15-40M

Ownership target: 10-20%

Investors: a16z, Lightspeed, growth-oriented early-stage firms

Management Evaluation

Seed risk: Will this company survive? (Existential)

Series B risk: Can this team handle hypergrowth? (Execution)

Evaluation expands to VP of Sales, CFO — not just founders

[2:50–3:25] The model is proven — question becomes: can this scale? SaaS companies at this stage have $5-20M ARR. [Click 1] Rounds $30-60M, checks $15-40M, ownership 10-20%. Investors include a16z and Lightspeed. [Click 2] The nature of risk changes fundamentally. At seed, risk is existential — will this survive? At Series B, it's execution risk — can the management team handle hypergrowth without the wheels coming off? Evaluation expands to the leadership bench, not just founders. Strong unit economics, clear market expansion potential, evidence the team can manage rapid scaling.

Stage 5

Growth Equity: Path to IPO

  • Round size: $80M+ | Check size: $40-200M+
  • Ownership target: 5-15%
  • Investors: General Atlantic, Insight Partners, Tiger Global, crossover funds
  • Evaluation: Detailed financial metrics, public company comparables, path to profitability, competitive moat durability
  • Almost like public market analysis — a fundamentally different discipline from seed investing
[3:25–4:00] Growth stage — investing in market leaders with a clear path to IPO or large strategic exit. [Click 1] Rounds $80M+, checks $40M to $200M+. [Click 2] Ownership targets 5-15%. [Click 3] General Atlantic, Insight Partners, Tiger Global, and crossover funds that invest in both private and public markets. Tiger Global became famous — and then infamous — for the velocity at which they deployed capital at this stage. [Click 4] Evaluation looks almost like public market analysis — detailed financials, public comps, profitability path, moat durability. [Click 5] This is a fundamentally different discipline from seed investing.

Section 2

Fund Types and Strategies

The fund you join shapes your entire experience

[4:00] Same landscape, different lens. Instead of investment stages, think about fund types — because the fund you join shapes your entire experience.

Small Funds

Micro VC and Seed Funds

Micro VC / Solo GP

Under $50M fund | 1-3 person teams

Thesis-driven, high volume of pre-seed and seed checks

Hustle Fund, Weekend Fund, Precursor Ventures

You do everything — sourcing, diligence, portfolio support, LP reporting

Seed Funds

$50-250M fund | 5-15 person teams

Defined roles: sourcing, operations, platform team

First Round Capital, Initialized Capital, Floodgate

Strong sourcing skills and product intuition required
[4:00–5:00] [Click 1] Micro VCs are 1-3 person teams managing under $50M. Often deeply thesis-driven — a solo GP with 15 years in healthcare investing exclusively in digital health, for example. Important economic reality: a $30M fund at 2% fee generates $600K/year — covering office, legal, travel, and all salaries. The GP bets everything on carry that won't mature for 7-10 years. That affects comp, team size, and daily operations. [Click 2] Seed funds are the professional-scale version — $50-250M with 5-15 people in defined roles. Need strong sourcing skills and genuine product intuition. Evaluating companies with minimal data, so judgment matters as much as spreadsheets.

Established Platforms

Early-Stage and Multi-Stage Funds

Early-Stage Funds

$250M-$1B | Series A and B focus

Deep diligence, meaningful board seats, small experienced partnerships

Benchmark, Greylock, Founders Fund

Analytical rigor + sector expertise + complex deal execution

Multi-Stage Funds

$1B+ | Seed through growth

Large teams organized by stage and/or sector. Massive platform services — recruiting, marketing, engineering.

a16z ($35B+ AUM), Sequoia, Lightspeed, Bessemer

Specialist roles — hired for growth team, fintech vertical, or platform org
[5:00–5:45] [Click 1] Early-stage funds ($250M-$1B) focus on Series A and B. Benchmark, Greylock, Founders Fund. Small, highly experienced partnerships running deep diligence and taking board seats. You need to build a detailed market model and defend it to partners who've done this for decades — if your analysis has a gap, they'll find it fast. [Click 2] Multi-stage funds ($1B+) invest across the entire spectrum. a16z has $35B+ AUM. Large teams organized by stage, sector, or both. They also run massive platform services for portfolio companies. Candidate profile skews toward specialists.

Later Stage

Growth Equity and Corporate VC

Growth Equity

$2B+ funds | Closer to PE than traditional VC

Heavily metrics-driven, detailed financial models, public market knowledge essential

General Atlantic, Insight Partners, Tiger Global

Natural entry point from investment banking or PE

Corporate Venture Capital

Strategic + financial returns for parent company

Technology integration, market intelligence, strategic positioning

Google Ventures, Salesforce Ventures, Intel Capital

Domain expertise in parent company's business is key

Trade-offs: CVCs can move slower due to internal corporate approval. Some top startups won't take CVC money to avoid a competitor on their cap table.

[5:45–6:30] [Click 1] Growth equity firms operate much closer to private equity. $2B+ funds, heavily metrics-driven. General Atlantic, Insight Partners, Tiger Global. If you're coming from banking or PE, this is often the most natural entry point — skill sets overlap significantly. [Click 2] CVCs — Google Ventures, Salesforce Ventures, Intel Capital. Strategic investors seeking both financial returns and strategic value. Candidate profile favors domain expertise in the parent company's business. Important trade-off: CVCs can move slower on deals because of internal corporate approval processes. Some top startups won't take CVC money because they don't want a potential acquirer or competitor on their cap table — that shapes which deals you'll see and which you won't.

Section 3

What Each Fund Wants in You

Where context becomes personal

[6:30] This section should directly change how you target your job search. Everything above is context — this is where it gets personal.

Seed Funds

Hustlers and Scouts Wanted

  • Sourcing ability: Find great companies before anyone else — events, launch platforms, founder relationships
  • Product intuition: Evaluate potential from limited data — 10 users, 6 months of runway
  • Founder empathy: Startup operating experience translates to understanding founders deeply
  • Generalist mindset: Small teams mean you do everything — sourcing, portfolio support, LP meetings

The minimum bar: Candidates who get hired already have a deal flow engine running. They've tracked startups, built founder relationships, and can point to three companies they would have invested in — with reasoning.

[6:30–7:15] Seed funds want sourcing ability above all else. [Click 1] Can you find great companies before anyone else? Being deeply embedded in the startup ecosystem. [Click 2] Product intuition — evaluating potential from minimal data. [Click 3] Founder empathy — operating experience is a huge advantage. [Click 4] Generalist mindset required because teams are small. [Click 5] The real minimum bar: already having a deal flow engine running before the interview. Tracking startups in a space, having founder relationships, being able to name three companies you would have invested in and explain why.

Early-Stage Funds

Analysts and Strategists Wanted

  • Analytical depth: Build rigorous market models and defend them under scrutiny
  • Sector expertise: Genuine, differentiated knowledge — dev tools, climate tech, fintech infrastructure
  • Deal judgment: Distinguish between a good company and a good investment — they're not always the same thing
  • Board-ready thinking: Even at junior levels, firms expect the ability to add strategic value in board settings

Standout candidates have a point of view. They can explain why a market is about to inflect — backed by data and primary research, not a TechCrunch article.

[7:15–7:50] Series A/B funds raise the evaluation bar significantly. [Click 1] Analytical depth — can you build a rigorous market model and defend it? [Click 2] Sector expertise — genuine, differentiated knowledge in a specific area. [Click 3] Deal judgment — the subtle but critical skill of distinguishing a good company from a good investment. A great company at the wrong price or stage isn't a great investment. [Click 4] Board-ready thinking, even at the junior level. [Click 5] The standout factor: having a point of view backed by data and primary research, not surface-level reading.

Growth Funds

Finance Athletes Wanted

  • Financial modeling: Three-statement models with revenue scenarios — "Show me this company at a $5B market cap"
  • Data-driven evaluation: Cohort analysis, unit economics deep dives, customer concentration risk
  • Market sizing precision: Credible bottoms-up TAM — underwriting to specific outcomes
  • Transaction experience: Investment banking or PE background translates directly — language and execution under pressure
[7:50–8:30] Growth investing is the most analytically rigorous part of VC. [Click 1] Detailed financial modeling — build a three-statement model and show how the company looks at a $5B market cap. [Click 2] Data-driven evaluation: cohort analysis, unit economics deep dives, customer concentration risk. [Click 3] Market sizing precision matters because you're underwriting to specific outcomes — need credible bottoms-up TAM. [Click 4] Transaction experience from banking or PE is highly valued — you speak the language and have executed deals under pressure.

How Hiring Works

Recruiting Varies by Fund Type

Seed & Early-Stage

Hire through relationships — less structure, more serendipity

Founder introduction → coffee with partner → offer in weeks

No formal application process

Applying cold to a seed fund means you've already lost. You need a warm introduction.

Growth & Multi-Stage

Structured recruiting cycles

Formal applications, case studies, modeling tests, multiple rounds

Familiar cadence for banking and consulting backgrounds

The fund type you're targeting tells you not just what to study, but how to position yourself.

[9:15–9:40] The recruiting process itself varies dramatically — most candidates don't realize this. [Click 1] Seed and early-stage funds hire through relationships. Less structure, more serendipity — introduced by a founder, coffee with a partner, offer within weeks. Cold applications to seed funds rarely work — you need a warm introduction. [Click 2] Growth funds and multi-stage firms run structured recruiting: formal cycles, case studies, modeling tests, multiple rounds. If coming from banking or consulting, this cadence will feel familiar. Module 4 covers investment thesis development that early-stage funds value; Module 5 covers interview prep for each fund type.

Remember This

Key Takeaways

  • Five investment stages from pre-seed through growth each have distinct check sizes, metrics, and evaluation criteria. Know them cold before any interview.
  • Fund types range from solo GPs managing $20M to multi-stage platforms managing tens of billions. The daily experience and career trajectory are radically different.
  • Seed investing is conviction-based and relationship-driven. Growth investing is metrics-driven and analytically rigorous. Almost different professions.
  • Match your background to the right fund type and stage. Play to your strengths first, then build toward where you want to end up.
  • When asked "why this fund?" — show you understand what makes their stage, strategy, and fund size different from every other firm.
[9:40–9:55] Five takeaways. [Click 1] Know the five stages cold — mixing up a seed metric with a growth metric is an instant credibility killer. [Click 2] Fund types shape daily experience — pick the one matching how you actually want to spend your time. [Click 3] Seed vs. growth is almost two different professions. Respect the distinction. [Click 4] Match background to fund type. Strengths first, then build toward your goal. [Click 5] The "why this fund?" answer needs to show genuine understanding of their specific stage, strategy, and fund size. That specificity is what separates candidates who get callbacks.
