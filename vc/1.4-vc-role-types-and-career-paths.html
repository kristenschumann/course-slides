Section 1

The VC Career Ladder

From analyst to general partner

[1:00] Transition into the career ladder breakdown.

Career Ladder

Analyst (0-2 Years)

What You Do

  • Research engine of the firm: market research, deal screening, financial modeling, competitive analysis
  • Read hundreds of inbound decks, flag the best for partners, write investment memos
  • Building the muscle of pattern recognition

What to Know

  • At 90% of firms, this is a two-year program — not partner track
  • Expectation: learn, then move on to business school, a startup, or a company
  • Compensation: $80K-$120K base + modest bonus. No carry.
[1:00-1:45] The analyst is where most people first touch VC. [Click 1] Day to day, analysts are the research engine -- market research, deal screening, financial modeling, competitive analysis. Reading hundreds of inbound decks, flagging the best ones, writing one-pagers. Most are obvious passes within 30 seconds. This is where pattern recognition begins. [Click 2] Critical context: at 90% of firms, the analyst role is a two-year program. Not partner track. The expectation is to learn and move on. Comp is $80K-$120K base plus bonus. No carry at this level.

Career Ladder

Associate (2-5 Years)

What You Do

  • First level with genuine deal involvement
  • Sourcing companies, running due diligence, executing deals from term sheet through close
  • Often the "first call" — the person entrepreneurs reach when connecting with the firm
  • Supporting partners on portfolio company board work

What to Know

  • Post-MBA or promoted from analyst
  • Your judgment is on the record — deals you champion are tracked
  • Compensation: $150K-$250K base + bonus
  • May begin receiving carry allocation for the first time
[1:45-2:30] [Click 1] The associate role is where things get real. This is the first level with genuine deal involvement -- sourcing companies, running diligence, executing deals from term sheet through close. Associates often become the "first call" that entrepreneurs reach. Also supporting partners on board work, helping startups with hiring, go-to-market strategy. [Click 2] This is post-MBA or promoted. Key shift: your judgment is now on the record. The deals you champion are tracked. Comp steps up meaningfully -- $150K-$250K plus bonus, and possibly first carry allocation. Small, but a first taste of real VC economics.

Career Ladder

Senior Associate / VP (4-7 Years)

What You Do

  • Leading deals end-to-end: sourcing through close
  • Developing your own investment thesis, not just supporting a partner's
  • Building a personal brand and deal flow network
  • Accumulating a track record of investments

The Key Question

  • The firm evaluates: genuine investment judgment, or great executor?
  • A great executor runs a flawless diligence process
  • Someone with investment judgment sees what an early-stage company could become
  • This distinction determines partner track vs. plateau
  • Comp: $200K-$350K + carry
[2:30-3:10] [Click 1] At Senior Associate / VP, you are leading deals end-to-end. Not just supporting a partner's thesis -- developing your own. Building a personal brand, establishing deal flow networks, accumulating a track record. [Click 2] This is the level where the firm asks a critical question: genuine investment judgment, or very good executor? A great executor runs a flawless diligence process. Someone with investment judgment looks at a messy early-stage company and sees what it could become. That distinction determines whether you make partner or plateau. Comp is $200K-$350K plus carry.

Career Ladder

Principal to General Partner

Principal / Director (6-10 Years)

  • Full deal autonomy — champion deals through the partnership
  • Clear sector thesis (e.g., developer tools, climate tech, fintech infrastructure)
  • Personal brand that founders recognize
  • Comp: $300K-$500K + meaningful carry

Partner / GP (10+ Years)

  • Final decision maker at partnership meetings
  • Sits on boards, represents firm publicly, drives LP fundraising
  • Responsible for fund strategy and returns
  • Comp: share of 2% management fee + 20% carry on profits
  • Top firms: $1M+ annually in cash, with carry in millions on successful funds
  • Struggling funds: partners can earn less than senior associates at hot firms
[3:10-4:00] [Click 1] Principals have full deal autonomy. They champion deals through the partnership. Expected to have a clear sector thesis -- maybe the firm's person on developer tools, climate tech, or fintech infrastructure. Founders come to you specifically, not just the firm. Comp is $300K-$500K plus meaningful carry. [Click 2] The GP is the final decision maker. Partners vote yes or no in partnership meetings. They sit on boards, drive LP fundraising, own fund strategy and returns. Comp is highly variable -- share of the 2% management fee plus 20% carry. At a top-performing firm, total comp can exceed $1M annually in cash with carry in the millions. But at a struggling fund, partners can take home less than a senior associate at a hot growth-stage firm. The variance is enormous.

Section 2

Established vs. Emerging Funds

The type of firm shapes experience more than title

[4:00] Transition. The type of firm shapes day-to-day experience more than title.

Firm Types

Two Very Different Experiences

Established Fund

e.g., a16z, Sequoia, Benchmark

  • Structure: Clear workflows, specialized roles
  • Learning: Deep expertise in one area
  • Brand: Name opens doors — founders take your call
  • Career Path: Structured but intensely competitive
  • Ownership: Specialized stage or sector focus

Emerging Fund

Pre-Fund III, solo GP, small team

  • Structure: Everyone does everything
  • Learning: Steep and broad across full lifecycle
  • Brand: Must hustle for every meeting and deal
  • Career Path: Less defined, more potential upside
  • Ownership: Full investment lifecycle from day one
[4:00-5:00] This comparison matters more than most career guides suggest. [Click 1] Established funds -- Andreessen Horowitz, Sequoia, Benchmark. Clear workflows, specialized roles. You might spend two years sourcing before touching diligence. Learning is deep but narrow. The brand is extraordinary -- founders take your call because of the name. Career path is structured but intensely competitive. [Click 2] Emerging funds -- pre-Fund III, small teams. Everything is different. No sourcing team vs. diligence team. You could be sourcing Monday, running diligence Tuesday, helping a portfolio company with their pitch deck Wednesday. From week one. Learning curve is steeper and broader. But the brand does not open doors -- every meeting and deal requires hustle.

Key Insight

Neither Path Is Objectively Better

Choose Established If...

  • You want deep expertise in one area
  • You value a prestigious credential
  • You thrive in structured environments

Choose Emerging If...

  • You want to understand the full lifecycle of venture investing
  • You are comfortable with ambiguity
  • You want breadth of exposure early on
[5:00-5:30] Neither is objectively better. [Click 1] The established fund is hard to beat for deep expertise and brand. [Click 2] Emerging funds provide breadth of exposure that is hard to replicate. The choice depends on learning style, not prestige. Breadth early and brand later is one common strategy, but the reverse works too.

Entry Pathways

What Each Background Brings

Investment Banking

Goldman, Morgan Stanley — 2-3 years

Strength: Rock-solid financial modeling, deal execution

Gap: Product and market intuition, evaluating founder-market fit

Management Consulting

McKinsey, Bain, BCG

Strength: Strategy thinking, market analysis

Gap: Deal mechanics experience — the rhythm of how VC deals get done

Startup Operating

Product leads, early employees at Series A-C startups

Strength: Founder empathy, authentic team evaluation

Gap: Financial modeling, cap table mechanics

[5:50-6:30] [Click 1] Investment banking is the most traditional route. Two to three years at Goldman or Morgan Stanley provides rock-solid financial modeling and deal execution experience. The gap: bankers sometimes lack product and market intuition. Modeling a cap table is one skill -- evaluating founder-market fit is entirely different. [Click 2] Management consulting -- McKinsey, Bain, BCG produce many VCs. Strategy thinking and market analysis translate directly. Similar gap: may lack deal mechanics experience, the actual rhythm of how a VC deal gets done. [Click 3] Startup operating is increasingly valued. Three years as a product lead at a Series B startup means understanding what building a company actually feels like. Founder empathy and authentic team evaluation come from lived experience. The gap: financial modeling and cap table mechanics require sharpening.

Entry Pathways

Founders and Non-Traditional Paths

Former Founders

  • Firms like Andreessen Horowitz and Founders Fund were built on this premise
  • Strength: First-hand understanding of the builder's journey, strong startup networks
  • Typical entry: Principal or Partner level

Non-Traditional Backgrounds

  • Journalists who covered tech, engineers who built products, policy experts who understand regulation
  • Unique domain expertise as a genuine differentiator

"For long-term success in this business, you have to think more generally and push yourself out of your comfort zone." — David Cowan, Bessemer

[6:30-7:00] [Click 1] Founders moving into VC is a growing trend. Andreessen Horowitz and Founders Fund were essentially built on this premise. Former founders bring first-hand understanding of the builder's journey and strong ecosystem networks. They typically enter at Principal or Partner level. [Click 2] Non-traditional paths -- journalists, engineers, policy experts. Unique domain expertise can be a genuine differentiator. As David Cowan at Bessemer puts it: "For long-term success in this business, you have to think more generally and push yourself out of your comfort zone." What VCs Actually Look For

The qualities that matter most in hiring

[7:00] Transition. Regardless of background, the best firms evaluate a specific set of qualities.

Hiring Criteria

What the Best Firms Evaluate

  • Strong awareness of technology trends
  • "Situational awareness" — the ability to read dynamics in any meeting (James Swartz, Accel)
  • Investment judgment — can you pick winners?
  • Optimism balanced with realism, plus an inherent bias for action

"A good VC has some ADD." — Seth Levine, Foundry Group

#1 Factor: Relationships. Not transactional networking — real relationships where value is added before anything is asked. The people who break in have been building genuine connections in the ecosystem for years before they ever apply.

[7:00-7:30] Regardless of background, the best firms evaluate these qualities. [Click 1] Strong awareness of technology trends -- living and breathing this space. What James Swartz at Accel calls "situational awareness" -- the ability to read dynamics in any meeting. Investment judgment. Optimism balanced with realism. Bias for action. [Click 2] Seth Levine at Foundry Group puts it well: "A good VC has some ADD." Curious about everything, always moving, always learning. [Click 3] But the number one factor is relationships. Not transactional networking -- real relationships where value is added before anything is asked. The people who break into VC are almost always the ones who have been building genuine connections for years before they ever apply. That is the prerequisite. Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • Nonlinear path is the norm: The career ladder runs Analyst to Partner, but almost nobody climbs it in a straight line. Plan for a nonlinear path.
  • Analyst roles are time-boxed: Pre-MBA analyst roles are typically two-year stints. Use those two years to learn everything, build relationships, and figure out next steps.
  • Firm type matters: Emerging funds give breadth. Established funds give brand and depth. Choose based on learning style.
  • No "right" background: IB, consulting, operating, founding all work. Close the gaps your background leaves.
  • Judgment and relationships: Investment judgment and relationships are the two things that matter most. Everything else is trainable. Start building both now.
[7:30-7:50] Five key takeaways. [Click 1] Nonlinear path is the norm -- people jump firms, leave for startups, return at higher levels. [Click 2] If looking at a pre-MBA analyst role, know it is a two-year stint. Maximize learning and relationships. Do not assume it leads to partner. [Click 3] Firm type matters as much as title. Emerging = breadth. Established = brand and depth. Pick based on learning style, not LinkedIn optics. [Click 4] No right background. Every path works. Be honest about the gaps and close them. [Click 5] Investment judgment and relationships are the two pillars. Everything else can be trained. These take years to build -- start now.
