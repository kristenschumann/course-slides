Why It Matters

Three Ways the GRE Tests Vocabulary

01

Direct Testing

Text Completion and Sentence Equivalence directly test whether you know words. If you don't know "perfunctory," you'll struggle with questions that use it.

02

Comprehension

Reading Comprehension becomes much harder if you're stumbling over unfamiliar words. Guessing word meanings mid-passage costs you time and accuracy.

03

Logic Signals

Words like "notwithstanding," "albeit," and "ostensibly" signal logical relationships. Miss those signals, and you'll miss the meaning.

The takeaway: This isn't about memorizing the dictionary. It's about strategic learning of high-value words.

0:55 - 2:15 | Walk through the three ways vocabulary impacts your score. Direct testing is obvious, but emphasize points 2 and 3 which students often miss.

Common Mistakes

The Wrong Way to Study Vocabulary

1

Memorizing Long Lists in Order

Going through alphabetical lists means you'll remember "abase" and "aberration" but forget everything by "zealot." Your brain needs variety.

2

Learning Definitions Without Context

Knowing that "sanguine" means "optimistic" isn't enough. You need to see it in sentences, understand its connotations, and know what situations it describes.

3

Passive Review

Looking at a flashcard and thinking "yeah, I know that one" doesn't create lasting memory. You need to actively retrieve the meaning.

2:15 - 3:30 | Explain the three common mistakes. These are relatable, most students will recognize themselves in at least one. This sets up the solution coming next.

The Right Way

The CUES Method

C

Context First

Read the word in a sentence before looking up the definition. Try to guess the meaning. This primes your brain for learning.

U

Understand, Don't Memorize

Learn roots, connotations, and related words. "Bellicose" shares a root with "rebel" and "belligerent." Now you know a word family.

E

Examples Matter

Create your own sentence. Think of a real person or situation the word describes. "Loquacious" = that friend who never stops talking.

S

Spaced Repetition

Review at increasing intervals: 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days. Each successful recall strengthens the memory.

Active engagement beats passive exposure every time.

3:30 - 5:30 | Introduce the CUES method. Walk through each letter with the examples. This is the core framework students should remember.

Power Strategy

Learn Word Roots to Decode New Words

BENE/BON good benevolent, bonus, boon
MAL/MALE bad malicious, malevolent, malady
CRED believe credible, incredulous, credence
DICT say dictate, contradict, verdict
SPEC/SPIC look inspect, conspicuous, retrospect
VERT/VERS turn convert, versatile, aversion
DUC/DUCT lead conduct, deduce, induce
MIT/MISS send transmit, dismiss, remit
SCRIB/SCRIPT write describe, manuscript, prescribe
VOC/VOK call evoke, advocate, revoke

If you know "bene-" means good, you can decode "benevolent," "benefactor," "beneficial," and "benediction," even if you've never seen them.

Power Strategy

Immerse Yourself in Advanced Content

Flashcards are useful, but nothing beats encountering words in their natural habitat. Make advanced vocabulary part of your daily life.

Read

  • The Economist or The Atlantic for current events
  • The New Yorker for longform essays
  • Scientific American for science writing
  • Classic literature (Dickens, Austen, etc.)

Listen

  • Podcasts: Radiolab, Freakonomics, Hidden Brain
  • Audiobooks: Non-fiction by Malcolm Gladwell, Mary Roach
  • Lectures: TED Talks, university lectures
  • News analysis programs (NPR, BBC)

When you encounter an unfamiliar word, pause and look it up. Then keep reading. This context-first approach is far more effective than flashcards alone.

6:30 - 7:30 | Encourage immersion in advanced content. This is about making vocabulary learning part of daily life, not just study sessions.

Power Strategy

The Memory Palace Technique

Link new vocabulary to vivid mental images in familiar places. The stranger the image, the better you'll remember it.

1

Choose a Location

Pick a place you know well: your home, school route, or favorite coffee shop.

2

Create Vivid Images

For "bellicose" (warlike), imagine a bell at your front door dressed in battle armor.

3

Walk Through

Mentally walk through your palace. Each location triggers the word and its meaning.

Example: To remember "obsequious" (excessively eager to please), picture an OBoe player at your kitchen table, SEQUInS on their outfit, bowing and nodding frantically trying to please everyone.

Your brain evolved to remember locations and images, not abstract definitions. Use this to your advantage.

7:30 - 8:30 | Explain the memory palace technique. Emphasize that bizarre, vivid images are key. This ancient technique is still used by memory champions.

Beyond Definitions

Connotation Matters

Words have emotional weight beyond their definitions. Consider these three words that all mean "thin":

Slender

Positive, elegant

Skinny

Neutral to slightly negative

Gaunt

Negative, unhealthy

On the GRE, you'll often choose between words with similar meanings but different connotations.
The right answer often depends on matching the tone, not just the definition.

Always ask, is this word positive, negative, or neutral? Would I use it to praise or criticize?

8:30 - 9:30 | Explain connotation using the slender/skinny/gaunt example. This is a concept many students miss but it's crucial for GRE success.

Your Action Plan

Daily Vocabulary Routine

Daily targets: 10-15 new words + review 20-30 old words = 700-1000 words over 8-10 weeks

Morning 10 min

Learn New Words

Study 10-15 new words using the CUES method. Context first, understand roots, create examples.

Midday 5 min

Quick Review

Review yesterday's words. Can you recall definitions without looking? Mark any you missed.

Evening 5 min

Spaced Review

Review older words due for repetition. Focus on words you've missed before.

Consistency beats cramming. Fifteen minutes every day beats two hours once a week.

9:30 - 10:30 | Present the concrete daily schedule. Morning for new words, midday for yesterday's review, evening for spaced repetition. Emphasize consistency.

Summary

Key Takeaways

Vocabulary is half the battle on GRE Verbal. It affects every question type.

Use the CUES method: Context, Understand, Examples, Spaced repetition.

Learn word roots to decode unfamiliar words. Immerse yourself in advanced content.

Use the memory palace for difficult words. Pay attention to connotation, not just definition.

Most importantly: Study a little bit every day. Consistency builds vocabulary, not cramming.
