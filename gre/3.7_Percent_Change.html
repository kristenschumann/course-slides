Part 1

The Percent Change Formula

Always divide by the original

0:30-0:40 | Transition into the percent change formula.

Percent Change

The formula and the multiplier

$\text{Percent Change} = \frac{\text{New} - \text{Old}}{\text{Old}} \times 100\%$

Increase

Increase of $x\%$

Multiply by $(1 + \frac{x}{100})$

Decrease

Decrease of $x\%$

Multiply by $(1 - \frac{x}{100})$

0:40-2:00 | The formula: (New - Old) / Old times 100%. Always divide by the original. The multiplier method: increase of 20% means multiply by 1.20, decrease of 15% means multiply by 0.85.

Example 1

Finding the percent increase

Problem

A stock price rises from \$40 to \$50. What is the percent increase?

Solution

$\frac{50 - 40}{40} \times 100\%$

$= \frac{10}{40} \times 100\% = 0.25 \times 100\%$

Answer: $25\%$ increase

2:00-2:40 | Example 1: stock goes from $40 to $50. Change is 10, divide by old (40), get 25% increase.

Part 2

Percent More vs. Times as Much

The critical distinction

2:40-2:50 | Transition into the percent-more vs times-as-much distinction.

Percent More vs. Times

Reading the language precisely

Statement Meaning Multiplier
A is 50% more than B $A = 1.5B$ $\times 1.5$
A is 50% of B $A = 0.5B$ $\times 0.5$
A is 3 times B $A = 3B$ $\times 3$
A is 200% more than B $A = 3B$ $\times 3$
Key

3 times as much $=$ 200% more (not 300%). "X times more" adds X copies of the original.

2:50-4:20 | The critical distinction. 50% more means multiply by 1.5. 3 times as much means multiply by 3, which is the same as 200% more. Walk through each row. Emphasize that "3 times more" = 4 times total is debated, but on the GRE, "X% more" always means add X% to the original.

Example 2

Applying "percent more"

Problem

Sarah earns 40% more than Tom. Tom earns \$5,000. How much does Sarah earn?

Solution

"40% more" $\rightarrow$ multiply by 1.40

Sarah $= 1.40 \times \$5{,}000$

Answer: $\$7{,}000$

4:20-4:50 | Example 2: 40% more means multiply by 1.40. Sarah = 1.40 x $5,000 = $7,000.

Part 3

Successive Percent Changes

Multiply the multipliers, never add the percents

4:50-5:00 | Transition into successive changes.

Example 3

Successive discounts

Problem

A \$100 shirt is 20% off, then another 30% off the sale price. Final price?

Solution

First discount: $\$100 \times 0.80 = \$80$

Second discount: $\$80 \times 0.70 = \$56$

Total discount: $44\%$ (not $50\%$!)

Common Error

Adding percents: $20\% + 30\% = 50\%$. The second discount applies to the reduced price.

5:00-6:00 | Example 3: successive discounts. 20% off gives $80. Then 30% off of $80 gives $56. The combined multiplier is 0.80 x 0.70 = 0.56, which is a 44% discount, not 50%. Quick formula for two successive changes x% and y%: net change = x + y + (xy/100). For -20% and -30%: -20 + -30 + (600/100) = -44%. This shortcut saves time on the GRE.

Successive Change

The quick formula

For two successive changes of $x\%$ and $y\%$, the net percent change is:

Net change $= x + y + \frac{xy}{100}$

Example

A 20% increase followed by a 10% decrease.

Solution

$x = 20$, $y = -10$

$20 + (-10) + \frac{(20)(-10)}{100}$

$= 10 + (-2) = 8$

Answer: $8\%$ net increase

5:30 - 6:00 | The quick formula for successive percent changes: x + y + xy/100. Use signed values (negative for decreases). This saves time compared to multiplying decimals on the GRE.

Example 4

Successive increases

Problem

A population increases by 10% one year, then 20% the next. Total percent increase?

Solution

Year 1 multiplier: $1.10$

Year 2 multiplier: $1.20$

Combined: $1.10 \times 1.20 = 1.32$

Answer: $32\%$ increase (not $30\%$)

6:00-6:50 | Example 4: successive increases. Multiply 1.10 by 1.20 to get 1.32, meaning 32% total increase, not 30%.

Successive Change

The return trip trap

The Trap

Increase by 50%, then decrease by 50%:

$\$100 \times 1.50 = \$150$

$\$150 \times 0.50 = $ $\$75$

Why

The decrease applies to a larger base.

To return to \$100 from \$150:

Need $\frac{50}{150} = $ $33.3\%$ decrease

6:50-7:50 | The return trip trap. Increase by 50% then decrease by 50% does NOT get you back. You end up at $75, not $100. The decrease is calculated from a larger base. To return to $100 you need only a 33.3% decrease. Coaching tip: a percent decrease always hurts more than a percent increase of the same amount helps. This is guaranteed to appear as a trap answer on the GRE. Think of it as fractions: a 50% increase is multiplying by 3/2, so the reverse is 2/3, which is a 33.3% decrease.

Example 5

Increase then decrease

Problem

A price increases by 25%, then decreases by 20%. What is the net change?

Solution

Increase 25%: $\times 1.25$

Decrease 20%: $\times 0.80$

Combined: $1.25 \times 0.80 = 1.00$

Answer: $0\%$ change (back to original)

7:50-8:30 | Example 5: increase 25% then decrease 20%. Combined multiplier is 1.25 x 0.80 = 1.00. Net change is 0%. This specific pair happens to cancel exactly.

Example 6

Finding the original price

Problem

After a 25% increase, a price is \$75. What was the original price?

Solution

Original $\times 1.25 = \$75$

Original $= \frac{\$75}{1.25}$

Answer: $\$60$

8:30-9:10 | Example 6: working backwards. If the new price is $75 after a 25% increase, divide by 1.25 to get $60.

Key Takeaways

Percent change toolkit

  • Percent change formula - $\frac{\text{New} - \text{Old}}{\text{Old}} \times 100\%$, always divide by the original
  • Multiplier method - Increase $x\%$ means $\times (1 + \frac{x}{100})$, decrease means $\times (1 - \frac{x}{100})$
  • Percent more vs. times - 3 times as much $=$ 200% more, not 300%
  • Successive changes - Multiply the multipliers, never add the percents
  • Return trip trap - Equal increases and decreases do not cancel out
9:10-10:00 | Summary: always use the multiplier method. Multiply multipliers for successive changes. Watch for the percent-more vs times-as-much distinction. Remember the return trip trap.
