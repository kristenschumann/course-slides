The Challenge

30 minutes to defend a position

The Issue Essay asks you to take a position on a claim and defend it in 30 minutes. The structure can be templated.

Every Issue prompt has two parts: 1) A statement expressing a position. 2) Instructions for what to do with it (agree/disagree, or address counterarguments).

You Can

  • Fully agree
  • Fully disagree
  • Agree with qualifications

Best Practice

Pick the position you can support most easily. A clear, one-sided argument scores better.

0:05 - 1:30 | Explain the Issue prompt structure. Emphasize that students should read the instructions carefully because some prompts ask them to address counterarguments. Any position can earn a high score.

Template

The 5-paragraph structure

ParagraphContentWords
IntroductionRestate issue, state thesis, preview reasons~50
Body 1First reason + specific example + explanation~100
Body 2Second reason + specific example + explanation~100
Body 3Counterargument OR third reason~80
ConclusionRestate thesis, summarize, broader implication~50
Key Idea

Total: 380-400 words. Enough for a good score. You do not need 600 words.

1:30 - 3:30 | Present the template. This is the core of the Issue Essay strategy. Students should memorize this structure so they can focus on content, not organization, on test day.

Examples

Where to find supporting evidence

Build a bank of flexible examples in advance. Three strong categories:

Historical

Events, figures, movements from history.

Current Events

Recent news, trends, and social changes.

Personal

Your own life and observations.

Prep Tip

Prepare 4-5 flexible examples: a historical leader, a business case, a scientific discovery, a social movement, a personal experience.

3:30 - 5:00 | Teach example generation. The five types of examples (historical, current, personal, hypothetical, literary) can be adapted to many prompts. Students should prepare a bank of 4-5 flexible examples before test day.

Examples

Explain the connection

Do not just mention an example. Show how it supports your argument.

Weak Example

"For example, Abraham Lincoln was a great president."

No connection to the argument. Just a name drop.

Strong Example

"Lincoln faced enormous criticism during the Civil War, yet his willingness to persist despite opposition ultimately preserved the Union. This shows that leaders must maintain convictions even when popular opinion turns against them."

5:00 - 6:00 | Show the difference between a weak and strong example. The key is the explanation sentence that connects the example back to the thesis. Without that sentence, the example proves nothing.

Walkthrough

Applying the template

Prompt

"The best way to teach is to praise positive actions and ignore negative ones."

Position

Disagree: ignoring negatives is not always effective.

Reason 1

Some negatives need correction for safety.

Reason 2

Ignoring negatives can reinforce them.

Counterargument

Praise works best combined with boundaries.

Time Management

Your 30-minute plan

PhaseTimeActivity
Read and Plan4-5 minUnderstand prompt, take position, outline
Write Intro3-4 minHook, thesis, preview of reasons
Write Body 15-6 minFirst reason with example
Write Body 25-6 minSecond reason with example
Write Body 34-5 minThird point or counterargument
Proofread2-3 minFix obvious errors only
Warning

If not done outlining by minute 5, start writing. An imperfect essay beats an incomplete one.

8:30 - 9:30 | Present the timing breakdown. The biggest timing mistake is spending too long planning. Stick to the plan and get writing.

Pitfalls

5 mistakes to avoid

  • Not taking a clear stance. Do not end up arguing both sides equally. Commit to a position.
  • Examples without explanation. "For example, Steve Jobs" does not prove anything. Explain the connection.
  • Running out of time. If your outline is not done by minute 5, start writing immediately.
  • Trying to be too clever. Simple, clear writing scores well. Do not use fancy vocabulary you might misuse.
  • Skipping counterarguments when asked. Some prompts specifically ask you to address opposing views.

Template in Action

Sample body paragraph

Technology has fundamentally transformed access to educational resources. For instance, platforms like Khan Academy and Coursera now offer university-level courses to millions of students worldwide who would otherwise lack access to such instruction. This democratization of knowledge means that a student in rural India can learn calculus from the same quality materials as a student at MIT, breaking down geographic and economic barriers that have historically limited educational opportunity. While access alone does not guarantee learning outcomes, it represents a necessary first step toward educational equity.

Key Takeaways

Issue essay strategy

  • Take a clear position and commit to it, even if you see both sides
  • Use the 5-paragraph template so you never waste time deciding on structure
  • Prepare 4-5 flexible examples in advance that can adapt to many different prompts
  • Explain the connection between each example and your thesis, not just the example itself
  • Manage your time with a strict plan: 5 min planning, 22 min writing, 3 min proofreading
  • Clarity and logic matter more than fancy writing or lengthy essays
