The Key Insight

The Twist Many Students Miss

You're not just looking for two words that fit the blank. You're looking for two words that create sentences with the SAME MEANING.

This means the right answers are almost always synonyms. Once you understand this, SE questions become much more predictable.

Today's strategy: Use synonym pairs to your advantage. Find the pair first, then verify it fits.

0:10 - 0:55 | Hook explaining the key insight that SE requires same-meaning sentences.

Know the Difference

How SE Differs from Text Completion

Text Completion

  • 1-3 blanks
  • Select ONE answer per blank
  • 5 choices (single) or 3 per blank (multi)
  • Find the word that fits

Sentence Equivalence

  • Always 1 blank
  • Select TWO answers
  • 6 choices total
  • Find a PAIR that creates equivalent sentences

Key difference: Getting just one right earns zero points. You need the pair.

The Method

The Two-Pass Approach

1

Synonym Identification

Before re-reading the sentence, scan all six choices:

  • Group words that are synonyms
  • Mark "orphan" words with no synonym partner
  • If a word has no synonym, it CANNOT be correct
2

Context Matching

Now apply Text Completion skills:

  • Find the clue in the sentence
  • Note signal words (contrast or same-direction)
  • Match your prediction to one of the synonym pairs

This two-pass method is faster than testing each word individually and dramatically reduces errors.

Important Distinction

"Equivalent Meaning" ≠ Identical Sentences

The two correct answers must create sentences that convey the same basic idea, not identical wording.

Works: Same Meaning

"The professor was harsh in her criticism."

= "The professor was severe in her criticism."

Both mean: The criticism was very negative/strong.

Doesn't Work: Different Meanings

"The professor was harsh in her criticism." (negative criticism)

≠ "The professor was thorough in her criticism." (comprehensive criticism)

Thorough criticism could be positive or negative. These aren't equivalent.

Watch Out

Common SE Traps

1

"Same Topic" Words

"Hungry" and "eating" relate to food, but they're not synonyms that create equivalent sentences.

2

"Almost Synonyms"

"Careful" and "fearful" both involve caution, but they have different implications.

3

Synonym Pair That Doesn't Fit

A synonym pair might exist in the choices but not fit the sentence context. Always verify both words work.

4

One Word Fits But Has No Pair

A word might fit perfectly, but if it has no synonym partner among the choices, it cannot be correct.

Putting It Together

Worked Example

"The politician's speech was so _______ that even supporters found it difficult to discern her actual position on the issue."

lucid ambiguous eloquent vague inspiring divisive

PASS 1: Identify Pairs

  • Ambiguous/vague = both mean unclear
  • Orphans: lucid, eloquent, inspiring, divisive

PASS 2: Context Match

  • Clue: "difficult to discern her position"
  • Prediction: Something meaning unclear

Answer: Ambiguous and Vague. Both create equivalent sentences meaning the speech was unclear.
