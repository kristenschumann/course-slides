The math is table stakes. The thinking gets you hired.

Having interviewed over 50 candidates, the ones who struggled weren't the ones who got the math wrong. They were the ones who couldn't explain their reasoning.

They'd give an answer, "$150 million pre-money," but when pushed back with "Why not $100 million?" they couldn't articulate the assumptions driving their number. Section 3

How To Structure Your Answers

4:30 | Transition into the answer framework.

VC Recruiting Course

State, assume, calculate, pressure test

State the Approach

Tell the interviewer how you'll think about the problem before touching any numbers. "I'd look at comparable public companies, adjust for a private discount, then run the return math."

Make Assumptions Explicit

This is where most candidates fail. Say it out loud: "I'm assuming growth stays above 50% for three years, giving $80M ARR. I'm assuming a 10x exit multiple."

Calculate

Do the math. Back-of-envelope is fine. "$80M ARR times 10x is $800M exit. We own 15% after dilution. Proceeds: $120M on a $20M investment. That's 6x."

Pressure Test Yourself

Before the interviewer pokes holes, poke your own. "The risk is growth decelerating. If ARR hits $50M, we're looking at 3x, which is decent but not fund-making."

They're testing process, not precision

Two candidates can arrive at different valuations and both pass, if they both showed clear thinking, reasonable assumptions, and self-awareness about risks.

Interviewers are not looking for the right number. They're looking for the right process. The candidates who state assumptions, run the math, and pressure test themselves are the ones who get offers. The ones who skip to a number and hope it's right get follow-up questions they can't answer.

Section 4

The Five Mistakes That Sink Candidates

6:15 | Transition into common mistakes.

VC Recruiting Course

Misunderstanding the game

Treating VC Math Like Banking Math

A candidate tries to build a three-statement model for a pre-revenue startup. That's not how this works. VC math is simpler in mechanics but harder in judgment. Think ownership, dilution, exit scenarios, and fund-level returns.

Not Knowing Your Fund's Strategy

A seed fund needs 100x outcomes. A growth fund targets 3-5x with consistency. The technical questions differ because the investment strategy differs. Research the fund's stage, check size, and portfolio construction.

6:15 - 6:45 | Five predictable mistakes. [Click] Mistake 1: treating VC math like banking math. Don't build a three-statement model for a pre-revenue startup. VC math is simpler mechanically but harder in judgment. [Click] Mistake 2: not knowing your fund's strategy. If you're at a seed fund, you need to understand why 100x on a single deal matters. If you're at a growth fund, 3-5x with consistency is the game. The context shapes every technical answer.

VC Recruiting Course

Execution errors in the room

Giving Answers Without Assumptions

"I'd value this at $200 million." Based on what? Silence. Dead. Always lead with your assumptions. The number without reasoning tells the interviewer nothing.

Not Connecting Math to the Decision

The question is never just "what's the return?" It's "does this return justify the risk at this price?" A 3x on a Series A might be fine. A 3x on a pre-revenue seed deal is terrible given the risk profile.

Panicking when you don't know something

No one expects you to know everything. If a partner asks about waterfall distributions and you're an analyst candidate, honesty beats bluffing every time.

Say: "I haven't worked through a full waterfall model, but my understanding is that proceeds flow first to the LP preferred return, then to the GP catch-up, then split by carry terms. I'd want to model that out for a precise answer." That's honest, demonstrates partial knowledge, and shows intellectual humility. That answer is better than making something up.

Key takeaways

  • VC technical interviews test investor thinking, not spreadsheet speed. Your ability to reason through a problem with incomplete data matters more than getting the exact number right.
  • The six domains are your study guide. VC math, valuation, cap tables, deal terms, unit economics, and modeling. You need fluency in most and depth in two or three.
  • Always make your assumptions explicit. State them, run the math, then pressure test your own answer before the interviewer does.
  • Know your fund before you walk in the room. The technical bar is different at a seed fund, a Series A fund, and a growth equity fund. Your answers should reflect that.
  • The biggest differentiator is connecting technical knowledge to investment judgment. Explain why ownership matters in the context of fund-level returns and power-law dynamics.
