CAC: The Cost Nobody Calculates Honestly

1:15 | Transition into CAC methods and manipulation.

VC Recruiting Course

Three CAC methods, three different numbers

Method What's Included Example CAC What It Tells You
Blended Total S&M spend / all new customers $400 Most common, most honest, but blunt
Paid Only Paid spend / paid-channel customers $200 Excludes organic, flatters the number
Fully Loaded S&M + sales salaries + tools + onboarding $650 What VCs actually care about
1:15 - 2:00 | CAC sounds simple but is the most manipulated metric in any pitch deck. [Click] Blended: total spend divided by total new customers. Honest but blunt. [Click] Paid only: excludes organic customers from the denominator while keeping organic costs low. A company says paid CAC is $200 but spends $500K/month on brand marketing feeding the organic funnel. The real number is $600. [Click] Fully loaded: includes sales salaries, tools, onboarding. This is the number VCs care about and companies least want to show. In an interview, always ask: is that blended or paid, and does it include sales comp?

VC Recruiting Course

CAC rises as companies scale

The Scaling Trap

The cheapest customers are acquired first. First 1,000 come from founder networks. Next 10,000 from efficient digital channels. Next 100,000 require sales teams and competitive paid acquisition. If current CAC is $300, ask what it was 12 months ago.

CAC by Channel

A company might have $150 CAC from content marketing and $2,000 CAC from outbound sales. If the growth plan calls for tripling the outbound team, that plan is a plan to destroy unit economics. Channel mix determines where CAC is headed.

2:00 - 2:45 | Two critical patterns. [Click] CAC rises as companies scale. The cheapest customers come first. Founder networks, then word of mouth, then digital channels, then paid and outbound. Always ask what CAC was 12 months ago. The trend tells more than the current number. [Click] CAC by channel matters enormously. Content marketing at $150 vs outbound at $2,000. If the growth plan leans into the expensive channel, unit economics are about to get worse.

VC Recruiting Course

Interview strategy: Start with CAC, not LTV

When a partner says "walk me through the unit economics," most candidates start with LTV. Start with CAC instead. CAC is the input the company controls. It reveals management discipline, channel efficiency, and customer understanding.

LTV is a function of product quality, market dynamics, and customer behavior, all variables harder to influence directly. Starting with CAC frames you as someone who thinks from the operator's perspective, not just the spreadsheet. That framing signals you understand how businesses actually work.

2:45 - 3:15 | Quick interview tip. When asked to walk through unit economics, start with CAC, not LTV. Most candidates start with LTV. But CAC is the spending decision. It reveals management discipline and channel efficiency. Starting with CAC signals you think like an operator, not an analyst. That framing gets you hired.

LTV and the Ratios That Matter

3:45 | Transition into LTV formula and the key ratios.

VC Recruiting Course

Lifetime value: The formula and its inputs

Formula

LTV = ARPU x Gross Margin / Churn Rate

$500 ARPU x 80% Gross Margin / 2% Monthly Churn = $20,000 LTV

Simple math, but every input is an assumption, and assumptions compound. Reducing monthly churn from 3% to 2% increases LTV by 50%.

3:45 - 4:15 | The LTV formula. Average revenue per user times gross margin divided by monthly churn rate. A SaaS company with $500 ARPU, 80% margins, and 2% monthly churn has $20,000 LTV. Simple math, but every input is an assumption. Reducing churn from 3% to 2% increases LTV by 50%. Small changes compound dramatically.

VC Recruiting Course

Two inputs to pressure-test first

Gross Margin

Software should be above 70%. If a "SaaS" company has 50% margins, it's not really software. It has services or infrastructure costs embedded in delivery. Managed services disguised as SaaS is one of the most common bait-and-switches in pitch decks.

Churn Rate

2% monthly sounds small but annualizes to 21.5%. Enterprise SaaS: under 1% monthly. SMB SaaS: under 3% monthly. Consumer: higher churn is tolerable only if CAC is proportionally low. Above 5% monthly means replacing half your base every year.

4:15 - 5:00 | Two inputs to pressure-test. [Click] Gross margin. The benchmark is 70%+ for software. Below that, the company has meaningful services or infrastructure costs. Managed services disguised as SaaS is a common bait-and-switch. The revenue recurs, but the margins are not software margins. [Click] Churn. 2% monthly annualizes to 21.5%. You lose a fifth of your base every year. Enterprise SaaS needs under 1% monthly. SMB under 3%. Consumer can tolerate more if CAC is cheap enough.

VC Recruiting Course

Contribution margin: Beyond gross margin

Contribution margin subtracts all variable costs: support, payment processing, account management. For marketplaces, the gap between gross margin and contribution margin is where the real economics live.

A food delivery marketplace might have a 25% take rate but after driver incentives, support, and refunds, contribution margin per order is 5%. The unit economics live in that 5%, not the 25%. Always ask for the contribution-margin view when evaluating businesses with meaningful variable costs beyond COGS.

5:00 - 5:30 | Related concept: contribution margin. Gross margin accounts for delivery costs. Contribution margin goes further, subtracting support, payment processing, refunds. For SaaS, the two are close. For marketplaces, the gap is enormous. A food delivery marketplace with a 25% take rate might have 5% contribution margin per order after driver incentives and support. The real economics live in that 5%.

VC Recruiting Course

CAC payback is a liquidity metric in disguise

The Cash Problem

A 5:1 LTV/CAC ratio with a 30-month payback means the company finances acquisition for 2.5 years before seeing returns. Burning $5M/month on acquisition requires surviving 30 months before those customers pay for themselves.

The Formula

CAC / (Monthly ARPU x Gross Margin). Under 18 months is good. Under 12 is great. Under 6 usually means the company should invest more aggressively. Over 24 months is a structural problem requiring massive cash reserves or continuous fundraising.

6:00 - 6:30 | Why CAC payback often matters more than LTV/CAC. [Click] It's a liquidity metric in disguise. A company with beautiful 5:1 LTV/CAC but 30-month payback will run out of cash before those economics materialize. The cash goes out today, the lifetime value trickles in over years. [Click] The formula: CAC divided by monthly ARPU times gross margin. Under 18 months good, under 12 great, under 6 usually means under-investment. Over 24 months is structural trouble.

Cohort Analysis

6:30 | Transition into cohort analysis.

VC Recruiting Course

Three ways to read a cohort table

Read Horizontally

Follow a single row across time. A steep early drop that flattens is fine. A steady decline that never flattens is fatal.

Read Vertically

Compare the same month across cohorts. Vertical improvement means better onboarding and targeting. Vertical degradation means expanding into weaker segments.

Read Diagonally

If revenue increases over time, customers are spending more. This is expansion revenue driving NDR above 100%. Numbers that increase as you read across are the dream.

6:30 - 7:15 | Every metric so far can be gamed in aggregate. Cohort analysis is how you see through the averages. A cohort is a group of customers who started in the same period. [Click] Read horizontally: follow one cohort over time. Steep early drop then flattening is fine. Steady decline is fatal. [Click] Read vertically: compare the same month across cohorts. Is month-3 retention improving? That means the company is learning. [Click] Read diagonally: if revenue increases over time, you have expansion revenue. That's what drives NDR above 100%.

VC Recruiting Course

The best and most dangerous patterns

Healthy Pattern

Early cohorts have mediocre retention, and each subsequent cohort improves. The company is learning its ICP, improving onboarding, and building features that drive stickiness. The trajectory of improvement matters more than any single number.

Dangerous Pattern

Excellent early cohorts that degrade over time. Initial customers were design partners or early adopters with unusually high engagement. As the company expanded to a broader market, the product didn't resonate. Early success was an anomaly, not a baseline.

7:45 - 8:15 | Two patterns to recognize. [Click] Healthy: early cohorts are mediocre but each one improves. This is a company that's learning. [Click] Dangerous: great early cohorts that degrade over time. The first customers were design partners with unusually high engagement. As the company expanded to a broader market, retention fell. The early success was an anomaly. In an interview, saying "I'd want to see the cohort data before judging these aggregate numbers" is the analytical instinct that gets you hired.

Unit Economics by Business Model

9:00 | Transition into how unit economics differ across business models.

VC Recruiting Course

SaaS: Net dollar retention is the separator

The Core Metrics

MRR/ARR as the currency. LTV/CAC, CAC payback, gross margin, churn as the standard toolkit. NDR above 120% means existing customers generate 20% more revenue each year without any new logos. Snowflake hit 150%+ at IPO. Datadog runs 130%+.

The Magic Number

Net new ARR divided by prior quarter's S&M spend. Measures how efficiently growth spending converts to recurring revenue. Above 0.75 is healthy. Above 1.0 means the engine is firing. Below 0.5 means you're pouring into a leaky bucket.

9:00 - 9:30 | SaaS first. [Click] The standard toolkit: LTV/CAC, CAC payback, gross margin, churn. But the metric that separates elite SaaS is net dollar retention. NDR above 120% means existing customers grow 20% annually without new logos. Snowflake hit 150%+ at IPO. These companies can slow acquisition and still grow. [Click] The Magic Number: net new ARR divided by prior quarter's S&M spend. Above 0.75 is healthy, above 1.0 is excellent, below 0.5 is a leaky bucket.

VC Recruiting Course

Marketplaces: The unit is a transaction

GMV and Take Rate

Total transaction value is GMV. The marketplace captures a percentage as take rate. $100M GMV at 15% take rate = $15M net revenue. Take rates vary enormously: ride-sharing 20-30%, wholesale marketplaces 3-5%.

Supply vs. Demand Economics

Which side is harder to build? Subsidize the hard side, monetize the easy side. Uber subsidized drivers, monetized riders. Once both sides reach critical mass, the marketplace self-reinforces and unit economics improve because you stop subsidizing.

9:30 - 10:00 | Marketplaces. [Click] The unit isn't a customer, it's a transaction. GMV is total transaction value. Take rate is what the marketplace captures. Huge variation: ride-sharing at 20-30%, wholesale at 3-5%. Contribution margin per transaction is the key: revenue minus payment processing, support, fraud, insurance. If contribution margin is negative, you're losing money on every transaction and hoping volume saves you. It won't. [Click] Supply vs demand: subsidize the hard side, monetize the easy side. Once critical mass hits, both sides reinforce each other.

VC Recruiting Course

Consumer: Engagement drives everything

DAU/MAU Ratio

Engagement frequency. Above 50% is exceptional (Facebook-level). Most apps sit at 15-25%. A daily user is worth 10x a weekly user regardless of monetization model. This ratio predicts everything downstream.

The Consumer Loop

Acquisition cost, trial conversion, monthly churn, ARPU. Thinner margins and higher churn mean tighter tolerances. A consumer sub with 6-month average lifetime and $10/month ARPU has $60 LTV. CAC needs to stay under $20 for the math to work.

10:00 - 10:30 | Consumer. [Click] DAU/MAU is the engagement metric. Above 50% is Facebook-level. Most apps are 15-25%. A daily user is worth 10x a weekly user for monetization purposes. [Click] Consumer subscription loop: acquisition, conversion, churn, ARPU. Thinner margins and higher churn mean the LTV equation produces smaller numbers. A 6-month average lifetime at $10/month ARPU gives $60 LTV before margin adjustments. CAC must stay under $20. Much tighter tolerances than SaaS.

VC Recruiting Course

Key takeaways

  • Unit economics is the language of VC technical interviews. Walk through CAC, LTV, payback, and cohort analysis for any business model, and you can handle any case study they throw at you.
  • Never accept a single LTV/CAC number at face value. Ask how CAC is calculated, what assumptions drive LTV, and ask to see the cohort data. Pressure-test the inputs, not the outputs.
  • CAC payback matters as much as LTV/CAC. A beautiful ratio means nothing if the payback period is 30 months and the company runs out of cash before those economics materialize.
  • Cohort analysis is the single most powerful tool in VC diligence. Aggregate metrics lie. Cohorts tell the truth. If interview data includes cohort information, start there.
  • The benchmarks are your shorthand. LTV/CAC above 3x. CAC payback under 18 months. Gross margin above 70%. NDR above 120%. Magic Number above 0.75. Memorize them and know when each applies.
