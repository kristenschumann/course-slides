Measures of Spread

Range: The simplest measure

Range = Maximum - Minimum

Set A

70, 72, 75, 78, 80

Range $= 80 - 70 =$ $10$

Set B

50, 60, 75, 90, 100

Range $= 100 - 50 =$ $50$

Limitation

Range uses only two values, so a single outlier can make it misleading.

0:20-1:20 | Range is the simplest spread measure: maximum minus minimum. Set A has range 10 (tightly clustered), Set B has range 50 (widely spread). Limitation: range only uses two data points and is sensitive to outliers.

Standard Deviation

Measuring spread from the mean

Standard deviation (SD) measures how far values spread from the mean. You will not calculate SD on the GRE, but you must understand it.

Low SD

48, 49, 50, 51, 52

Values cluster near the mean (50). Small SD.

High SD

30, 40, 50, 60, 70

Values spread far from the mean (50). Large SD.

1:20-2:30 | Standard deviation measures how spread out values are from the mean. Small SD means values cluster near the mean. Large SD means values are widely spread. The GRE tests conceptual understanding, not calculation.

Standard Deviation

Three key properties

Always $\geq 0$

SD equals zero only when all values are identical.

Add a Constant

Adding $c$ to every value does NOT change SD.

Multiply by $c$

Multiplying every value by $c$ multiplies SD by $|c|$.

Example

If a set has mean 50 and SD 10, doubling every value gives mean 100 and SD 20.

2:30-3:40 | Three key SD properties. First, SD is always non-negative and equals zero only when all values are the same. Second, adding a constant shifts every value but does not change the spread. Third, multiplying by a constant scales the SD by that factor.

The bell curve and the 68-95-99.7 rule

Normal Distribution

The 68-95-99.7 rule

For a normal (bell-curve) distribution centered at mean $\mu$ with standard deviation $\sigma$:

Within 1 SD

68%

$\mu - \sigma$ to $\mu + \sigma$

Within 2 SDs

95%

$\mu - 2\sigma$ to $\mu + 2\sigma$

Within 3 SDs

99.7%

$\mu - 3\sigma$ to $\mu + 3\sigma$

3:50-5:00 | The empirical rule: 68% of data within 1 SD, 95% within 2 SDs, 99.7% within 3 SDs. This is the most commonly tested normal distribution fact on the GRE.

Example 1

Applying the empirical rule

Problem

Test scores are normally distributed with $\mu = 500$ and $\sigma = 100$. What percent score above 700?

Solution

$700 = 500 + 200 = \mu + 2\sigma$

95% within 2 SDs, so 5% outside

By symmetry, half above $\mu + 2\sigma$

Answer: $2.5\%$

5:00-5:50 | Example: scores with mean 500 and SD 100. 700 is 2 SDs above the mean. 95% fall within 2 SDs, so 5% are outside. By symmetry, 2.5% are above 700.

Comparing Distributions

Skewness: Mean vs. median

Symmetric

Mean $\approx$ Median

Normal distributions, balanced data.

Skewed Right

Mean $>$ Median

Long tail to the right (e.g., income).

Skewed Left

Mean $<$ Median

Long tail to the left (e.g., retirement age).

GRE Tip

The mean is always pulled toward the tail. If mean $>$ median, the tail is on the right.

5:50-7:30 | Three shapes of distributions. Symmetric: mean equals median. Skewed right: mean is pulled above median by high outliers (income). Skewed left: mean is pulled below median by low outliers. The GRE tests whether you can identify skewness from mean vs median.

Key Takeaways

Spread & distributions

  • Range -- Maximum minus minimum; simple but sensitive to outliers
  • Standard deviation -- Measures spread from the mean; you won't calculate it on the GRE
  • Adding a constant -- Shifts the mean but does not change SD
  • Multiplying by a constant -- Scales both the mean and SD by that factor
  • 68-95-99.7 rule -- Memorize these percentages for normal distribution questions
  • Skewness -- The mean is always pulled toward the longer tail
7:30-8:00 | Summary: Range and SD measure spread. Adding a constant does not change SD; multiplying does. The 68-95-99.7 rule is essential. Skewness tells you which way the tail goes.
