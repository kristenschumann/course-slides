Strengthen and Weaken
New Info Rules

0:00 - 0:05 | Title slide. This video covers how new information can strengthen or weaken GRE arguments.

The Concept

New information changes arguments

Strengthen and weaken questions ask: "What new info would make this argument better or worse?" The answer is something additional that, IF TRUE, changes how convincing the argument is.

Strengthen

Makes the gap smaller. Gives you reason to believe the assumption is true.

Weaken

Makes the gap bigger. Gives you reason to doubt the assumption.

Rule

You're not deciding if the argument is TRUE. You're deciding if the new info makes it MORE or LESS convincing.

0:05 - 1:30 | Set up the framework. Strengthen makes the gap smaller. Weaken makes it bigger. Crucial rule: you're judging effect on the argument, not truth.

Part 1

Strengthen Questions

1:30 - 1:35 | Section divider. Now cover strengthen questions in detail.

Strengthen

Types of strengtheners

Eliminates Alternatives

If A caused B, a strengthener shows C didn't cause B. "No other factors changed during that period."

Additional Evidence

More data supporting the conclusion. "A larger follow-up study confirmed the effect."

Supports Mechanism

Explains HOW the conclusion could be true. "Reading activates memory brain regions."

Trap

Answers that strengthen a premise but don't connect to the conclusion. Make sure it addresses the gap.

1:35 - 4:00 | Walk through the three types of strengtheners. Eliminating alternatives is the most common. Additional evidence is straightforward. Supporting the mechanism explains the causal link. The common trap: strengthening a premise instead of the conclusion.

Part 2

Weaken Questions

4:00 - 4:05 | Section divider. Now cover weaken questions in detail.

Weaken

Types of weakeners

Alternative Cause

If A caused B, a weakener shows C might have caused B. "A new bonus system started at the same time."

Flawed Evidence

Shows the evidence is unreliable. "The survey only included existing customers."

Breaks the Logic

Even if premises are true, the conclusion doesn't follow. "City A underreports crimes."

Trap

Answers that weaken a premise rather than the argument. The correct answer questions the connection between premise and conclusion.

4:05 - 6:30 | Walk through the three types of weakeners. Alternative cause is the most common pattern. Flawed evidence attacks the data. Breaking the logic shows the conclusion doesn't follow even with true premises. The common trap: weakening a premise rather than the connection.

Critical Rule

The "if true" rule

Accept the answer choice as true, even if it seems implausible.

You're not judging whether the answer is realistic. You're judging its effect on the argument.

Right Mindset

"Drinking cold water temporarily increases metabolism." IF this is true, it strengthens the weight-loss argument, even if the effect seems small.

Wrong Mindset

"That can't be a big enough effect to matter." Don't bring outside knowledge to judge answer choices.

6:30 - 8:00 | Teach the "if true" rule. Students often dismiss correct answers because they seem implausible. But the question says "if true" - accept it and judge the effect, not the plausibility. Don't bring outside knowledge.

Choosing

Pick the strongest effect

The question asks for the answer that "most" strengthens or weakens. Multiple answers may have some effect. Choose the biggest impact.

Comparison Factor Stronger Answer Weaker Answer
Scope Addresses the main gap Addresses a minor point
Directness Directly affects the conclusion Indirectly related
Evidence quality Strong, specific evidence Vague or tangential information
Key Idea

An answer addressing the central assumption is always stronger than one addressing a peripheral issue.

8:00 - 9:00 | Teach how to compare competing answers. Scope, directness, and evidence quality determine strength. The answer that addresses the main gap wins over one that addresses a side issue.

Worked Example

Strengthen: breakfast and test scores

Argument: "Studies show that students who eat breakfast perform better on standardized tests. Therefore, schools should provide free breakfast to improve test scores."

Premise: Breakfast correlates with better scores. Conclusion: Free breakfast will improve scores.

Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?

(A) Students who eat breakfast also tend to sleep more — alternative cause, not direct support
(B) In a pilot program, schools that added free breakfast saw a 12% score increase — CORRECT: direct evidence the policy works
(C) Breakfast provides essential nutrients for brain function — mechanism, but doesn't prove causation
(D) Most high-performing students already eat breakfast — restates the premise, doesn't help
(E) Test scores have declined nationally over the past decade — irrelevant to the argument
9:00 - 10:30 | Walk through the full worked example. Read the argument and identify the gap: correlation vs. causation. The conclusion assumes providing breakfast will cause improvement. Choice A suggests an alternative cause. Choice B gives direct evidence from a pilot program, making it the strongest strengthener. Choice C supports the mechanism but doesn't prove the policy works. Choice D restates the premise. Choice E is irrelevant.

Worked Example

Why (B) is the strongest answer

Choice (B)

"In a pilot program, schools that added free breakfast saw a 12% score increase."

Why it wins: It directly tests the conclusion. A real program tried free breakfast and scores went up. This bridges the gap between correlation and causation.

Choice (C) - Runner Up

"Breakfast provides essential nutrients for brain function."

Why it loses: It explains a possible mechanism but doesn't show the policy actually works. Mechanism without evidence is weaker than direct evidence.

Takeaway

Direct evidence of the conclusion working > explaining why it could work. Always prefer answers that address the gap most directly.

10:30 - 11:30 | Compare B and C directly. B provides direct evidence that the policy works. C only explains a mechanism. On the GRE, direct evidence always beats mechanism-only answers. This maps to the "directness" row from our comparison table earlier.

Key Takeaways

Strengthen and weaken rules

  • Strengthen answers make the gap smaller - they eliminate alternatives, provide additional evidence, or support the mechanism
  • Weaken answers make the gap bigger - they suggest alternative causes, question the evidence, or break the logic chain
  • Accept the "if true" rule - don't judge whether an answer is realistic, judge its effect on the argument
  • Focus on the conclusion and the gap - answers that address the central assumption are stronger than those addressing side issues
  • Choose the most significant effect - when multiple answers have some impact, pick the one that most directly affects the conclusion
9:00 - 10:00 | Summary slide. The gap is central to both question types. Strengthen supports the assumption. Weaken attacks it. Always accept answer choices as true and judge their effect on the argument.
