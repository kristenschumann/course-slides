What Schools Want

What They Evaluate (and Don't)

They evaluate
  • Clear communication under pressure
  • Authenticity and genuineness
  • Energy and classroom presence
  • Cultural fit with the program
They don't evaluate
  • Video production quality
  • Perfect, polished delivery
  • Acting or performing ability
  • Memorized word-for-word scripts
1:00 - 2:15 | Video essays let committees see the real you beyond polished written materials. They evaluate four things: communication skills under pressure, authenticity and genuineness, energy and classroom presence, and cultural fit. What they are NOT evaluating: video production quality, perfect polish, acting ability, or memorized scripts. Some natural hesitation is human. They want spontaneous, not robotic. Knowing this should take some pressure off.

Video Formats

Common School Formats

Timed Q&A

Prompt on screen, 20-30 sec prep, 60-90 sec response. No re-recording. Tests thinking on your feet.

Self-Introduction

60-second video introducing yourself to classmates. More controlled, but must be a single take with no edits.

Open Format

Pre-recorded video with more flexibility on format and length. Less common at top US programs.

2:15 - 3:30 | Schools use three main formats. Timed Q&A, used by schools like Kellogg and Yale SOM: you see a prompt, get 20-30 seconds to prepare, and record a 60-90 second response. No re-recording. This tests your ability to think on your feet. Self-introduction, used by schools like MIT Sloan: you record a brief 60-second video introducing yourself to future classmates. More controlled, but must be a single take. Open format, used by some European programs: you submit a pre-recorded video with more flexibility. Less common at top US schools. Always check your target school's current cycle requirements. Formats change year to year.

3:30 - 5:00 | Here's a structured 7-day practice plan. Days 1-2: just get comfortable with the camera. Record yourself answering easy questions like "what did you do last weekend?" for 60 seconds each. Watch the playback and notice what you want to improve. Do 10 recordings each day. Days 3-4: practice with MBA-relevant prompts. "Why are you pursuing an MBA right now?" "Tell us about a challenge you overcame at work." Give yourself 20 seconds of prep time and 60 seconds to respond. Record every attempt. Days 5-6: simulate test conditions using the exact format of your target school. Do 3-4 full simulations and have a friend watch one and give feedback. Day 7: one full simulation in your actual recording setup with proper lighting, camera, and background. Review and adjust.

Preparation

Prepare Themes, Not Scripts

Know the 3-4 main things you want an admissions committee to understand about you. When you get a question, look for ways to incorporate these themes naturally.

Don't memorize answers. Memorized responses sound robotic. Instead, practice responding to different framings of common topics so you stay flexible and conversational.

5:00 - 5:45 | The key to preparation is developing themes, not scripts. Know the 3-4 main things you want the committee to understand about you. When you get a question, look for ways to incorporate these themes naturally. Don't memorize word-for-word answers. That leads to robotic delivery and if the question is slightly different from what you practiced, you'll freeze. Instead, practice responding to different framings of common topics so you stay flexible and conversational.

Technical Setup

Get the Basics Right

Location

Quiet space, clean neutral background, good lighting from a window. Avoid backlighting.

Equipment

Webcam at eye level, decent audio, stable internet. Close other applications to avoid slowdowns.

Appearance

Business casual. Solid colors on camera. Check for distracting jewelry or patterns.

Test Everything

Do a full test recording before the real thing. Check video, audio, lighting, and framing.

5:45 - 7:00 | Get the technical basics right so they don't distract from your content. Location: quiet space with no interruptions, clean neutral background, good natural light from a window, and avoid backlighting. Equipment: webcam at eye level, use books to raise your laptop if needed. Built-in mic is usually fine but test it. Stable internet, close other apps. Appearance: business casual, solid colors look better on camera, check for distracting jewelry or busy patterns. And most importantly, test everything. Do a full test recording before the real thing and check video quality, audio, lighting, and framing.

Performing Well

When the Camera Is On

  • Look at the camera lens, not your own image on screen. This creates eye contact.
  • Be conversational. Imagine talking to a friendly classmate, not performing for judges.
  • Start with a breath. When the timer starts, breathe before speaking to avoid a rushed start.
  • Embrace imperfection. A few "ums" are fine. Recover and continue confidently.
7:00 - 8:30 | When the camera is on, four things to remember. Look at the camera lens, not your own image on screen. This creates eye contact with the viewer. Be conversational. Imagine you're talking to a friendly classmate, not performing for judges. Let your personality come through. Start with a breath. When the timer starts, take a breath before you speak. This prevents rushed, nervous starts. And embrace imperfection. A few ums are fine. A brief pause to think is natural. What matters is that you recover and continue confidently. Don't let small stumbles derail you. Use a simple structure for most answers: one-sentence direct answer, brief context or example, and what you learned or why it matters.

Common Mistakes

What Trips People Up

Mistake What Happens The Fix
Over-rehearsing Memorized answers sound robotic Practice themes and structure, not scripts
Wrong eye line Looking at yourself creates no eye contact Train yourself to look at the lens
Speaking too fast Nerves make everything rush together Consciously slow down. Pauses are okay.
Off-topic answers Giving a prepared answer that doesn't fit Listen carefully, then respond to what was asked
Technical failures Setup issues cause panic on recording day Test everything well in advance
8:30 - 9:45 | Five common mistakes. Over-rehearsing: memorized answers sound robotic. Practice themes and structure, not word-for-word scripts. Wrong eye line: looking at yourself instead of the camera creates a lack of eye contact. Train yourself to look at the lens. Speaking too fast: nerves make people rush. Consciously slow down. Pauses are okay. Off-topic answers: in the rush, some candidates give a prepared answer that doesn't fit the actual question. Listen carefully, then respond to what was asked. And technical failures: don't leave setup to the last minute. Test everything well in advance so you're confident on recording day.
