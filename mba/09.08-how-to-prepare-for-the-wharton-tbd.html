The Format

How the Wharton TBD Works

The Group

5-6 applicants discuss a business prompt together for ~35 minutes while an admissions observer takes notes

The Prompt

Revealed at the start. Everyone gets the same information. A few minutes to read before discussion begins

The Evaluation

No single "right answer." The discussion itself is the point. You're evaluated on how you engage

The Setting

In person or virtual depending on the cycle. Core dynamics are the same either way

1:00 - 2:30 | Here's how the TBD works. You're placed in a group of about 5-6 other applicants. You're given a business-related prompt, typically a case or discussion question. The group has about 35 minutes to discuss and reach some form of conclusion. A Wharton admissions team member observes and takes notes but doesn't participate. The prompt is revealed at the start and everyone gets the same information. There's no single right answer. The discussion is the point. The TBD may be in person or virtual. Check your invitation for specifics. If virtual, look at the camera when speaking and use participant names.

Evaluation Criteria

What Wharton Is Looking For

  • Collaboration: Do you build on others' ideas and help the group move forward?
  • Communication: Can you articulate clearly and listen actively?
  • Leadership: Can you guide without dominating? Synthesize, structure, unstick.
  • Critical thinking: Do you contribute substantive ideas and consider multiple angles?
  • Awareness: Are you reading the room and adapting to the group?
2:30 - 4:00 | Wharton calls it "contribution to the learning environment." Five things they evaluate. Collaboration: do you work well with others and build on their ideas? Communication: can you speak clearly and do you actually listen? Leadership, but appropriately. Can you guide the group when needed without dominating? Critical thinking: do you have substantive ideas and consider multiple perspectives? Awareness: are you reading the room? They are NOT evaluating who talks the most, who has the right answer, or who wins arguments. The core question is: would I want to learn with this person?

Strategies

Seven Ways to Stand Out

Strategy What It Looks Like
Contribute early Be among the first few to speak, building on what's been said
Build on others "Building on what Sarah said about X, I wonder if we should consider Y"
Invite others in "I'd love to hear what others think. Michael, do you have a perspective?"
Suggest structure "Maybe we should identify key criteria, then evaluate each option against them"
Synthesize progress "So far we've discussed three factors: cost, speed, and buy-in. Are we missing anything?"

Also: disagree respectfully when you have a different view, and don't fight for airtime. Graciously deferring shows confidence.

4:05 - 6:00 | Seven strategies. First, contribute early but not necessarily first. Get into the conversation before anxiety makes it harder. Second, build on others' ideas. Reference what someone said and extend it. This shows you're listening. Third, invite others in. If someone hasn't spoken, create space for them. This is leadership without domination. Fourth, suggest structure when the group is floundering. Fifth, synthesize and summarize periodically to move the conversation forward. Sixth, disagree respectfully when you have a different view. Constructive disagreement shows critical thinking. Seventh, don't fight for airtime. If several people want to talk, let others go. You'll have chances.

Common Mistakes

Three Ways Candidates Fail the TBD

Dominating

Talking too much, interrupting, or steering everything your way. This shows you'd be a difficult classmate

Staying Silent

Getting intimidated and barely speaking. You have to contribute. Jump in early before anxiety builds

Ignoring Others

Waiting for your turn without engaging with what others say. This isn't collaboration

6:00 - 7:30 | Three major ways candidates fail the TBD. Dominating: talking too much, interrupting, or steering everything your way. This backfires because it shows you'd be a difficult classmate. Staying silent: getting intimidated and barely speaking. You have to contribute, so prepare yourself to jump in early. Ignoring others: waiting for your turn to talk without engaging with what's been said. Also avoid treating it as a debate where you try to prove others wrong, and don't get stuck on one point if the group has moved past it.

The Big Picture

The Question Behind the TBD

"Would I want to learn with this person? Would they make a study group better?"

Everything you do in the TBD should answer yes to this question. Collaboration, communication, and awareness matter more than individual brilliance.

7:30 - 8:15 | Here's the question behind the entire TBD: would I want to learn with this person? Would they make a study group better? Everything you do in the discussion should answer yes. Collaborate genuinely. Communicate clearly. Show awareness of the room. This is not about individual brilliance or having the right answer. It's about being someone who makes the group smarter and more productive.

8:15 - 9:30 | Four steps to prepare. First, practice with groups. Find other Wharton applicants or friends and do mock TBDs. Practice the collaborative dynamics, not just your ideas. Second, study common prompt types: resource allocation problems, ethical dilemmas, and strategic decisions. For each, know how to identify the core tension and suggest evaluation criteria. Third, build a flexible toolkit of discussion frameworks you can apply without forcing them. Fourth, manage your nerves. The group setting is anxiety-inducing, but remember that everyone is nervous. A calm, collaborative presence stands out.

Preparation

Common Prompt Types

Resource Allocation

"How should a foundation allocate $1M among five causes?" Suggest criteria: impact, feasibility, urgency

Ethical Dilemma

"A profitable product has health risks. What should the company do?" Identify stakeholders, weigh tradeoffs

Strategic Decision

"Expand into a new market or deepen the existing one?" Define key tradeoffs, consider both before deciding

9:30 - 10:30 | Three common prompt types. Resource allocation: how should a foundation prioritize its budget? Approach this by suggesting evaluation criteria like impact, feasibility, and urgency, then systematically apply them. Ethical dilemmas: a company faces a tension between profit and responsibility. Identify stakeholders, weigh short-term versus long-term consequences. Strategic decisions: should a company expand or deepen? Define the tradeoffs and encourage the group to consider both options before deciding. When you first read any prompt, spend your thinking time identifying the core tension, two or three criteria, and your initial position with one supporting reason.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • The TBD evaluates collaboration, not individual brilliance
  • Build on others' ideas to show you're listening
  • Contribute early and consistently, but don't dominate
  • Invite quiet participants in and suggest structure when needed
  • Practice with groups to develop collaborative discussion skills
  • Show you'd be a great learning partner for your classmates
10:30 - 11:00 | Key takeaways. The TBD evaluates collaboration, not individual brilliance. Build on others' ideas to show you're listening and engaged. Contribute early and consistently, but don't dominate. Invite quiet participants in and suggest structure when the group needs it. Practice with groups because this is a skill you can develop. And above all, show that you'd be a great learning partner. That's what Wharton wants to see.
