Probability:
AND/OR & Complements

0:00-0:30 | Hook: What is the probability of rolling two sixes? Of rolling at least one six? AND and OR problems look similar but require different rules. Understanding when to multiply vs add is crucial for GRE probability. This video gives you the decision framework.

Probability Basics

The foundation

$P(\text{Event}) = \frac{\text{Favorable outcomes}}{\text{Total outcomes}}$   Always between 0 and 1

Marble Draw

4 red, 6 blue marbles

$P(\text{red}) = \frac{4}{10} = $ $\frac{2}{5}$

Die Roll

Fair six-sided die

$P(3) = $ $\frac{1}{6}$

0:30-1:30 | Probability basics: favorable over total. P(red marble) = 4/10 = 2/5. P(rolling a 3) = 1/6. Probability is always between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain).

Part 1

The AND Rule

Multiplication for joint events

1:30-1:40 | Transition to the AND rule (multiplication).

AND Rule

Independent events: multiply

$P(A \text{ AND } B) = P(A) \times P(B)$   when events are independent

Two Heads

$\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2}$

$\frac{1}{4}$

Two Sixes

$\frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6}$

$\frac{1}{36}$

Four Heads

$\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^4$

$\frac{1}{16}$

1:40-3:00 | For independent events (outcome of one doesn't affect the other), multiply probabilities. Two heads: 1/2 x 1/2 = 1/4. Two sixes: 1/6 x 1/6 = 1/36. Four heads: (1/2)^4 = 1/16.

Example 1

Without replacement (dependent)

Problem

A bag has 5 red, 3 blue marbles. Two drawn without replacement. P(both red)?

Solution

P(1st red) $= \frac{5}{8}$

P(2nd red | 1st red) $= \frac{4}{7}$

Answer: $\frac{5}{8} \times \frac{4}{7} =$ $\frac{5}{14}$

3:00-3:50 | Without replacement: probabilities change. First red is 5/8. Given first was red, second red is 4/7. Multiply: 5/8 x 4/7 = 20/56 = 5/14. GRE trap: always check whether the problem says "with replacement" or "without replacement." Without replacement means both the numerator and denominator decrease. Many students forget to reduce the denominator.

Part 2

The OR Rule

Addition for alternative events

3:50-4:00 | Transition to the OR rule (addition).

OR Rule

Mutually exclusive vs overlapping

Exclusive

$P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)$

P(2 or 5 on a die) $= \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = $ $\frac{1}{3}$

Overlapping

$P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$

P(heart or queen) $= \frac{13}{52} + \frac{4}{52} - \frac{1}{52} = $ $\frac{4}{13}$

4:00-5:30 | OR rule: add probabilities. If events are mutually exclusive, just add. If they overlap, subtract the intersection. Rolling a 2 or 5: mutually exclusive, 1/6 + 1/6 = 1/3. Heart or queen: overlap at queen of hearts, 13/52 + 4/52 - 1/52 = 4/13.

Decision Framework

When to multiply vs add

Keyword Rule Formula
AND, both, all Multiply $P(A) \times P(B)$
OR, either (exclusive) Add $P(A) + P(B)$
OR, either (overlapping) Add − overlap $P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$
At least one Complement $1 - P(\text{none})$

Watch Out Overlap? Subtract $P(A \cap B)$.   Dependent? Use $P(B|A)$ instead of $P(B)$.

5:30-6:10 | Quick reference table: AND means multiply, OR means add. For OR with overlapping events, subtract P(A intersect B) to avoid double-counting. "At least one" means use the complement. Watch for dependent events (adjust second probability).

Part 3

The Complement Rule

The shortcut for "at least one" problems

6:10-6:20 | Transition to the complement rule.

Example 2

At least one six in three rolls

Problem

Roll a die 3 times. What is P(at least one 6)?

Solution

Direct approach requires 3 separate cases.

P(no 6s) $= \left(\frac{5}{6}\right)^3 = \frac{125}{216}$

Answer: $1 - \frac{125}{216} =$ $\frac{91}{216}$

6:20-7:20 | Complement approach: P(at least one 6) = 1 - P(no 6s). P(no 6 on one roll) = 5/6. P(no 6s in 3 rolls) = (5/6)^3 = 125/216. Answer: 1 - 125/216 = 91/216. This pattern appears frequently on the GRE: anytime you see "at least one," the complement is almost always the faster path. The direct approach would require calculating P(exactly 1) + P(exactly 2) + P(exactly 3) separately.

Complement Rule

More "at least one" problems

Coin Flip

5 flips: P(at least one head)?

P(no heads) $= \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^5 = \frac{1}{32}$

$1 - \frac{1}{32} = $ $\frac{31}{32}$

Defective Items

2% defect rate, 3 items: P(at least 1 bad)?

P(all good) $= (0.98)^3 \approx 0.941$

$1 - 0.941 \approx $ $0.059$

7:20-8:20 | Two more complement examples. 5 coin flips: P(at least one head) = 1 - (1/2)^5 = 31/32. Factory defects: P(at least one bad) = 1 - (0.98)^3 = about 5.9%.

Example 3

Matching socks

Problem

10 socks (4 black, 6 white). Two drawn without replacement. P(they match)?

Solution

P(both black) $= \frac{4}{10} \times \frac{3}{9} = \frac{12}{90}$

P(both white) $= \frac{6}{10} \times \frac{5}{9} = \frac{30}{90}$

Matching = both black OR both white

Answer: $\frac{12}{90} + \frac{30}{90} =$ $\frac{7}{15}$

8:20-9:30 | Combined example using both rules. P(match) = P(both black) + P(both white). Both black: (4/10)(3/9). Both white: (6/10)(5/9). These are mutually exclusive, so add: 12/90 + 30/90 = 42/90 = 7/15. Notice the structure: AND within each case (both black), then OR between cases. This AND-then-OR pattern is the standard framework for combined probability problems on the GRE.

Key Takeaways

Probability: AND/OR & complements

  • AND means multiply - P(A and B) = P(A) x P(B) for independent events
  • OR means add - P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B), subtract overlap if events are not exclusive
  • Dependent events - Without replacement, adjust the second probability: P(B|A)
  • Complement shortcut - P(at least one) = 1 - P(none), avoids counting multiple cases
  • Combined problems - Use AND within each case, then OR to add mutually exclusive cases
9:30-10:00 | Summary: AND means multiply, OR means add, at least one means complement. For dependent events, adjust the second probability. Combined problems use both rules together.
