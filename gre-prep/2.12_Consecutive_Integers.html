Counting Integers

The fencepost formula

Number of integers from $a$ to $b$, inclusive = $b - a + 1$

Think of fenceposts: to build 10 feet of fence with posts every foot, you need 11 posts (one at each end).

From To Formula Count
$1$ $10$ $10 - 1 + 1$ $10$
$5$ $12$ $12 - 5 + 1$ $8$
$7$ $83$ $83 - 7 + 1$ $77$

Sum, middle term, and product rules

Example 1

Sum of consecutive integers

Problem

What is the sum of integers from $1$ to $100$?

$\text{Sum} = n \times \frac{\text{first} + \text{last}}{2}$

Solution

Count: $n = 100$

$\text{Sum} = 100 \times \frac{1 + 100}{2}$

$= 100 \times 50.5$

Answer: $5{,}050$

2:40-3:40 | Example 1: Sum from 1 to 100. Use the formula: n times (first + last) / 2. Click to reveal. Click: n = 100. Click: plug into formula. Click: multiply. Click: answer is 5,050. This is a classic GRE problem.

Example 2

Finding the middle integer

Problem

The sum of 5 consecutive integers is 45. What is the middle integer?

Solution

Sum = $n \times$ middle (for odd $n$)

$45 = 5 \times$ middle

The integers: $7, 8, 9, 10, 11$

Answer: $9$

3:40-4:30 | Example 2: Sum of 5 consecutive integers is 45. For an odd count, sum = n times the middle term. Click to reveal. Click: formula. Click: 45 = 5 times middle, so middle = 9. Click: verify the sequence. Click: answer is 9.

Properties

Product divisibility rule

The product of $n$ consecutive integers is always divisible by $n!$

Consecutive Set Divisible By Example
Any 2 consecutive $2! = $ $2$ $4 \times 5 = 20$
Any 3 consecutive $3! = $ $6$ $4 \times 5 \times 6 = 120$
Any 4 consecutive $4! = $ $24$ $3 \times 4 \times 5 \times 6 = 360$
Why

In any $n$ consecutive integers, one is divisible by 1, one by 2, one by 3, and so on up to $n$.

4:30-5:30 | Product divisibility: product of n consecutive integers is divisible by n!. Why: each set contains one multiple of 1, 2, 3, etc. Walk through the table examples. This is a popular GRE number properties fact.

Part 3

Spacing and Distance

Equally spaced objects on a number line

5:30-5:40 | Transition: The fencepost idea extends to spacing problems with equally spaced objects.

Example 3

Trees equally spaced in a row

Problem

8 trees are planted in a row, equally spaced. The first and last trees are 35 meters apart. What is the distance between adjacent trees?

Gaps = Objects $- 1$

Solution

Number of gaps: $8 - 1 = 7$

Distance per gap: $\frac{35}{7}$

Answer: $5$ meters

5:40-6:30 | Example 3: 8 trees, 35 meters total. Click to reveal. Click: gaps = 8 - 1 = 7. Click: distance per gap = 35 / 7. Click: answer is 5 meters.

Example 4

Counting posts along a fence

Problem

Posts are placed every 4 feet along a 100-foot fence, including both ends. How many posts are there?

Objects = $\frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Spacing}} + 1$

Solution

Number of gaps: $\frac{100}{4} = 25$

Posts = gaps $+ 1$: $25 + 1$

Answer: $26$ posts

6:30-7:20 | Example 4: 100-foot fence with posts every 4 feet. Click to reveal. Click: 100 / 4 = 25 gaps. Click: posts = gaps + 1. Click: answer is 26 posts. Same plus 1 pattern as counting integers.

Key Takeaways

Consecutive integers & number lines

  • Count (inclusive) from $a$ to $b$ = $b - a + 1$
  • Sum formula = $n \times (\text{first} + \text{last}) / 2$
  • Middle term shortcut for odd $n$: sum = $n \times$ middle
  • Product of $n$ consecutive is always divisible by $n!$
  • Gaps = objects $- 1$, and objects = distance/spacing $+ 1$
  • Common trap: forgetting the "$+ 1$" when counting endpoints
7:20-8:00 | Summary: Five key formulas. Counting inclusive uses b - a + 1. Sum uses n times average of endpoints. Middle term shortcut for odd count. Product divisibility by n factorial. Spacing uses gaps = objects - 1. The most common trap is forgetting the plus 1 when counting endpoints. [Leland+ Tip] For counting multiples in a range (e.g., multiples of 3 between 100 and 200), first adjust the range so both endpoints are multiples of 3, then use (last - first)/3 + 1. The GRE frequently tests "less than" vs "less than or equal to" to trip up students on the boundary.
