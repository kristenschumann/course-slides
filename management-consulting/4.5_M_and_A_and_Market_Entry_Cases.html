The M&A Framework

Four areas of acquisition analysis

0:30 - 0:40 | Transition to the M&A framework. Every M&A case at top firms comes down to four questions.

Four Areas of M&A Analysis

01

Market Attractiveness

Market size, growth rate, margins, competitive intensity, and regulatory environment.

02

Target Attractiveness

Financials, market share trajectory, management quality, unique assets, and culture fit.

03

Synergies and Risks

Revenue and cost synergies, integration complexity, and cultural alignment challenges.

04

Financial Implications

Purchase price vs. standalone value, expected ROI, payback period, and deal financing.

0:40 - 2:00 | Walk through each area. Market attractiveness applies market sizing from 4.3. Target attractiveness is separate from the market. Synergies are where strong candidates stand out. Financials check if the deal works on paper. Key move: lay out all four areas, then ask the interviewer which to explore.

Strategic Deal Types

Horizontal Acquisition

Buying a competitor to gain market share and consolidate the industry.

Vertical Integration

Acquiring a supplier or distributor to control more of the value chain.

PE Buyout

Private equity firm buying a company to improve operations and exit at a profit.

2:00 - 2:30 | Always clarify the strategic rationale first. The deal type determines which branches of the framework matter most. Horizontal focuses on market share synergies. Vertical focuses on cost control. PE focuses on operational improvement.
Part 2

Synergy Analysis

Revenue vs. cost synergies

2:30 - 2:40 | Transition to synergy analysis. Synergies are the heart of any acquisition thesis.

Break Into Management Consulting

Revenue vs. Cost Synergies

Revenue Synergies

  • Cross-selling to each other's customer base
  • Geographic expansion via new channels
  • Product bundling into combined offerings
  • Pricing power from increased market share

Cost Synergies

  • Eliminating redundant functions (HR, IT)
  • Economies of scale in purchasing
  • Facility consolidation and closures
  • Technology consolidation onto one platform
2:40 - 3:45 | Revenue synergies grow the top line but are speculative. Cost synergies eliminate duplication and are more predictable. BMS-Celgene realized $2.5B in annual cost savings on schedule. Salesforce-Slack targeted $1.5B in revenue synergies, which was aspirational. Flagging that cost synergies carry less risk shows judgment interviewers reward.

Break Into Management Consulting

Common M&A Traps

  • Forgetting standalone value. Know what the target is worth before evaluating synergies.
  • Ignoring integration costs. IT merges, severance, rebranding all eat into projected savings.
  • Assuming the answer is "acquire." Sometimes the right call is to walk away.
  • Not asking about price. Even a great target is a bad deal if the acquirer overpays.
  • Skipping alternatives. Could organic growth or a partnership achieve the same goal?
3:45 - 4:25 | Five traps candidates walk into on M&A cases. Nearly half of respondents in a Bain study cited cultural fit as the primary reason past deals failed. Interviewers respect it when analysis supports walking away.

Break Into Management Consulting

The Acquisition Decision Rule

Acquire if: Standalone Value + Synergies - Integration Costs > Purchase Price

If the left side is smaller than the purchase price, the acquirer is overpaying, no matter how attractive the target looks on paper. This is the mental model to anchor every acquisition recommendation.

4:25 - 4:45 | This is the core mental model. Standalone value plus synergies minus integration costs must exceed the purchase price. If it doesn't, the answer is don't acquire.
Part 3

Market Entry Framework

Four pillars of market entry decisions

4:45 - 4:55 | Transition to market entry. M&A is one way to grow. Market entry is the broader question: should the client enter at all, and how?

Break Into Management Consulting

Four Pillars of Market Entry

01

Market Attractiveness

Total addressable market, growth rate, profitability of existing players, regulatory complexity.

02

Competitive Landscape

Number and strength of incumbents, barriers to entry, brand loyalty, and switching costs.

03

Company Capabilities

Skills, distribution, technology, and regulatory approvals needed to compete effectively.

04

Financial Feasibility

Expected revenues, entry costs, projected ROI, and time to break even.

4:55 - 6:00 | Two pillars overlap with M&A (market attractiveness and financials). The logic works like a decision tree: if the market is attractive AND the client can compete AND returns justify investment, enter. If any pillar fails, the answer is "don't enter" or "enter differently."

Buy vs. Build vs. Partner

Criterion Build Buy Partner
Speed Slow (years) Fast (months) Moderate
Control Full Full (post-integration) Shared
Cost High upfront High (acquisition premium) Lower
Risk Execution risk Integration risk Alignment risk
Best When Core IP, no targets Speed matters, targets exist Limit risk, not core
6:00 - 7:30 | Once you decide a market is worth entering, the next question is how. Build when capability is core to your competitive advantage. Buy when speed matters and attractive targets exist. Partner when you need market access quickly but want to limit risk. The strongest answers evaluate all three before recommending one. These two frameworks are deeply linked: every M&A case has a market entry dimension, and many market entry cases lead to M&A.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • M&A has four areas: market, target, synergies, financials. Present all four, then go deep where the interviewer directs.
  • Cost synergies are more reliable than revenue synergies. Flag the uncertainty to show judgment.
  • Run the acquisition math: Standalone Value + Synergies - Integration Costs must exceed Purchase Price.
  • Market entry has four pillars, then a build, buy, or partner decision. Evaluate all three modes.
  • These frameworks are connected. M&A cases contain market entry questions, and vice versa.
7:30 - 8:00 | Five takeaways. M&A four areas. Cost vs. revenue synergy reliability. The acquisition decision rule. Market entry four pillars plus entry mode. Frameworks are cousins, treat them as one toolkit.
