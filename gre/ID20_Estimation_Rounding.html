The Power of Estimation

Why Calculate When You Can Estimate?

Problem

What is $4{,}987 \times 0.198$?

(A) 98.7
(B) 487
(C) 988
(D) 4,870
(E) 9,870

15-Second Estimate

$4{,}987 \approx 5{,}000$ and $0.198 \approx \frac{1}{5}$

$5{,}000 \times \frac{1}{5} = 1{,}000$

Answer: (C) 988

Strategy

When to Estimate (and When Not To)

When to Estimate

  • Spread-apart answers: Choices differ by magnitudes
  • Time-consuming math: Exact calculation would take too long
  • "Approximately" questions: The question asks for an estimate
  • Running low on time: Quick elimination beats guessing

When NOT to Estimate

  • Close answer choices: Values differ by small amounts
  • Exact value required: Question asks for a specific number
  • Precise relationships: Problem depends on exact quantities

Rounding Rules

Standard Rules

  • Look at the digit to the right of your rounding position
  • 5 or greater: round up
  • 4 or less: round down

Fundamentals

Strategic Rounding for the GRE

Round to numbers that are easy to work with:

Awkward number

$4{,}987 \rightarrow$ 5,000

Decimal to fraction

$0.198 \rightarrow$ $\frac{1}{5}$

Percent to fraction

$33\% \rightarrow$ $\frac{1}{3}$

Near a multiple

$49 \rightarrow$ 50

Speed Tip

If you round one number UP, try to round another DOWN to reduce error.

Estimation Strategies

Strategy 1

Benchmark Numbers

Use friendly numbers as reference points.

Example A

Is $\frac{7}{13}$ greater or less than $\frac{1}{2}$?

$\frac{1}{2}$ of 13 = 6.5

$7 > 6.5$, so $\frac{7}{13} > \frac{1}{2}$

Example B

Compare $0.48 \times 312$ to 150

$0.48 \approx \frac{1}{2}$

$\frac{1}{2} \times 312 = 156$

So $0.48 \times 312 \approx 150$ (slightly less since $0.48 < 0.5$)

Strategy 2

Order of Magnitude

Problem

$0.023 \times 4{,}200 = \;?$

(A) 0.966
(B) 9.66
(C) 96.6
(D) 966
(E) 9,660

Solution

$0.02 \times 4{,}000 = 80$

Answer must be close to 80

Answer: (C) 96.6

Strategy 3

Percent Estimation

Example

What is 18% of 495?

$18\% \approx 20\% = \frac{1}{5}$

$495 \approx 500$

$\frac{1}{5} \times 500 = 100$, actual is slightly less: approximately 90

10% / 5% Shortcut

10% of any number = move decimal one place left

5% = half of 10%

15% of 80:

10% of 80 = 8

5% of 80 = 4

15% = 8 + 4 = 12

5:50 - 7:00 | Walk through the percent estimation example. Then teach the 10%/5% building block method. This is one of the most useful shortcuts on the GRE.

Example 1

Total Revenue

Problem

A store sells 487 items at an average price of \$19.85. What is the approximate total revenue?

(A) \$5,000
(B) \$7,500
(C) \$10,000
(D) \$15,000
(E) \$20,000

Solution

$487 \approx 500$ and $\$19.85 \approx \$20$

$500 \times \$20 = \$10{,}000$

Answer: (C) \$10,000

Example 2

Fraction Scaling

Problem

If $\frac{3}{7}$ of a number is 258, what is approximately $\frac{5}{7}$ of that number?

Solution

$\frac{5}{7}$ is $\frac{5}{3}$ times $\frac{3}{7}$

$258 \times \frac{5}{3} \approx 260 \times \frac{5}{3} \approx 433$

Answer: approximately 430

Example 3

Square Root Estimation

Problem

$\sqrt{399} = \;?$

(A) 17
(B) 18
(C) 19
(D) 20
(E) 21

Solution

$20^2 = 400$

399 is just under 400, so $\sqrt{399}$ is just under 20

Answer: (D) 20 (closest)

Summary

Estimation and Rounding

  • Friendly numbers — Round awkward values to easy ones ($4{,}987 \rightarrow 5{,}000$)
  • Benchmarks — Use $\frac{1}{2}$, $\frac{1}{3}$, $\frac{1}{4}$ as reference points for fractions
  • Order of magnitude — When answers differ by 10x, rough math is enough
  • Percent shortcuts — Build from 10% and 5% to find any percentage fast
  • Golden rule — Round one number up, another down to minimize error
