Part 1

Operations Cases

Diagnosing processes and fixing the right constraint

0:25 - 0:40 | Operations cases test whether you can diagnose why a process isn't working and fix the right part. That distinction is everything.

Break Into Management Consulting

The Bottleneck Principle

A

100 units/hr

Excess capacity sits idle waiting for Step B

B

60 units/hr

Bottleneck determines total system throughput

C

80 units/hr

Waits for Step B, cannot run at full speed

System output = 60 units/hr. Only bottleneck improvements increase total throughput.

0:40 - 2:00 | Three-step production line. Answer is 60, not 100 or 80. Step B is the bottleneck and determines max throughput. Improving non-bottleneck steps does nothing. Once you fix one bottleneck, a new one emerges. The system always has a limiting step.

Break Into Management Consulting

People, Process, Technology

People

Staffing levels, skill gaps, shift scheduling, turnover, and absenteeism.

Process

Workflow steps, handoffs, rework loops, standardization, bottleneck location.

Technology

Equipment age, maintenance, automation potential, downtime, information systems.

2:00 - 2:50 | Once you've found the bottleneck, diagnose why it's constrained. PPT gives MECE coverage. People: understaffed, untrained, absenteeism? Process: unnecessary steps, rework loops, standardized or ad hoc? Technology: outdated equipment, breakdowns, untapped automation?

Operations Formulas

Formula Definition Example
Capacity Max output per unit of time 24 hrs x 5 units/hr = 120 units/day
Utilization Actual Output / Max Capacity 60 of 120 possible = 50%
Cycle Time Time to complete one unit at a step 12 min/unit = 5 units per hour
Throughput Actual rate through entire system Equals bottleneck capacity
2:50 - 3:25 | Four formulas you need cold. Capacity is max output per time. Utilization is actual over max, step closest to 100% is your bottleneck. Cycle time is how long one unit takes. Throughput is actual rate and equals the bottleneck's throughput.

Break Into Management Consulting

Common Operations Traps

  • Improving a non-bottleneck: making a fast step faster does nothing for total output
  • Skipping process mapping: you cannot find the bottleneck without knowing the steps
  • Forgetting to check demand: extra capacity is waste if there is no demand to absorb it

Supply chain cases are a common variant involving supplier optimization, facility relocation, or disruption risk. The same approach applies: map the process, find the constraint, quantify the impact.

3:25 - 4:05 | Three traps that sink operations cases. Improving a non-bottleneck is the most common mistake. Always ask the interviewer to walk through the process first. Increasing capacity only matters if demand exists. Supply chain cases use the same bottleneck-first approach.
Part 2

Pricing Cases

Determining what the client should charge

4:05 - 4:20 | Operations asks "how do we make this work better?" Pricing asks "what should we charge?" You already know pricing from the profitability tree: Revenue = Price x Quantity. Now we go deeper on price.

Break Into Management Consulting

The Pricing Range

Floor: Cost-Based

Cannot price below cost. With 20% margin: Cost / (1 - margin) = minimum viable price.

Position: Competitor

Where competitors price. Differentiated product prices above. New entrant or commodity prices at or below.

Ceiling: Value-Based

Maximum the customer would rationally pay. Leave enough value that switching still benefits them.

4:20 - 5:30 | Three approaches define a single range. Cost-based sets the floor ($30 cost with 20% margin = $37.50). Value-based sets the ceiling ($120 savings, realistic pricing $90-100). Competitor-based positions within ($70-90). Where you land depends on the strategic objective. First question: "What is the company's pricing objective?"

Break Into Management Consulting

Pricing Formulas

Formula Definition Example
Contribution Margin Price - Variable Cost per Unit $80 - $30 = $50 contribution
Break-Even Volume Fixed Costs / Contribution Margin $500K / $50 = 10,000 units
Price Elasticity % Change Quantity / % Change Price 15% drop / 10% hike = 1.5 (elastic)

Elasticity below 1 means demand is inelastic and a price increase raises revenue. Above 1 means elastic, and the hike reduces total revenue.

5:30 - 6:00 | Three formulas for every pricing case. Contribution margin is price minus variable cost. Break-even volume is fixed costs divided by contribution margin. Price elasticity is % change in quantity over % change in price. Below 1 = inelastic, above 1 = elastic.

Break Into Management Consulting

Common Pricing Traps

  • Using only one approach: triangulate cost, value, and competitor pricing to find a range
  • Confusing cost with value: a $10 product that saves the customer $100 should not be priced at $12
  • Not quantifying: "$54/unit, +8%, with 5% volume loss for $2.4M profit gain" beats "raise prices"
6:00 - 6:30 | Three pricing traps. Most candidates calculate cost-based and stop. That is the floor, not the answer. A low-cost product with high value should be priced on value. Always quantify: price, percentage change, volume impact, and profit outcome.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Find the bottleneck first. The step with the lowest capacity determines system throughput.
  • Use People, Process, Technology to diagnose why the bottleneck is constrained.
  • Build the pricing range. Cost-based floor, value-based ceiling, competitor-based positioning.
  • Tie pricing to the strategic objective. Maximizing profit and market share lead to different price points.
  • Quantify everything. Put a number on your recommendation and you sound like a consultant.
6:30 - 7:00 | Five things to lock in. Operations: bottleneck first, PPT for diagnosis. Pricing: build the range with all three approaches, tie to objective. Both: quantify everything.
