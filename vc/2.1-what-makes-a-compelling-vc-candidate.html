Section 1

Domain Expertise

[1:00] Transition into domain expertise.

Your Knowledge Edge

The Unfair Advantage VCs Actually Hire For

"I don't need another smart generalist. I need someone who knows fintech cold, the regulatory landscape, who the incumbents are, where the white space is, and which founders are building the interesting stuff."

That is a direct quote from a partner hiring conversation. Domain expertise is what makes your judgment useful. Without it, you are just another person with opinions about TAM.

[1:00-1:45] Here is a conversation I have had a dozen times. A partner says: "I don't need another smart generalist." They need someone who knows a sector cold. A Stanford/NBER study of 885 VCs found that the management team is rated more important than the product when making investment decisions. But how do you evaluate a management team if you don't understand the space?

Depth Levels

Three Levels of Domain Knowledge

Table Stakes

You read industry news and can name the major players. Everyone applying has this. It is not a differentiator.

Working Knowledge

You have worked in the sector. You spent years at a fintech startup or inside a health system. You know the operational reality.

Proprietary Insight

You have seen what breaks, what scales, what customers pay for. You know things that are not in any pitch deck.

[1:45-2:30] There are levels to domain expertise. [Click] At the bottom: you read TechCrunch and can name the players. That is table stakes. [Click] In the middle: you have actually worked in the sector. You know the operational reality. [Click] At the top: proprietary insight. You have seen what breaks and what scales. Candidates who get hired live in that middle-to-top range. And here is the key: domain expertise is the most buildable of the four attributes. We will cover how in the next few videos.
Section 2

Network

[2:45] Transition into network.

Deal Flow Multiplier

In VC, Your Rolodex Is Part of the Job

Why It Matters

Deal sourcing is one of the three main drivers of value creation at VC firms, per the Stanford survey. VCs source deals primarily through their professional networks, not cold inbound or databases.

What Firms Evaluate

Can you get a warm intro to a hot founder? Do you know operators who will give honest references? Are you plugged into communities where the next companies are being built?

[2:45-3:15] In VC, your network is not a nice-to-have. It is core infrastructure. [Click] The Stanford survey found that deal sourcing through professional networks is a primary driver of value creation. [Click] When a firm hires you, they are partly hiring your Rolodex. Can you get warm intros to founders? Do you know operators who will give honest back-channel references?

What You Bring

Every Background Has a Network

Investment Banker CFOs, corporate development teams, later-stage founders
Consultant C-suite executives, industry specialists, corp strategy leads

[3:15-4:00] Every background maps to a different network. Notice what is not on this list: "I went to a top school." An alumni network is a starting point, not a differentiator. Everyone applying to Sequoia went to a good school. The network that matters is the one you built by doing things: working at startups, advising founders, showing up in communities where builders spend their time. If your network is limited to people with the same job title as you, it is not VC-relevant.

Hustle

[4:15] Transition into hustle.

Demonstrated Intensity

Show Your Work Before You Get the Title

When asked what separates good candidates from great ones, a partner gave a one-word answer: "Receipts."

Hustle means you have been doing the work of a VC before anyone gave you the title. Not "I read Stratechery," because everyone reads Stratechery. Demonstrated interest means you have artifacts.

[4:15-4:45] I once asked a partner what separates good candidates from great ones. One word: "Receipts." Hustle is the easiest attribute to describe and the hardest to fake. It means you have been doing the work already. You have artifacts to prove it.

Proof Points

Six Types of VC-Adjacent Evidence

  • Angel investments, even $1K checks on AngelList or Republic
  • A venture scout or fellow position (Sequoia, Lightspeed, On Deck)
  • Published investment memos or market maps
  • A Substack, blog, or Twitter thread series on a sector
  • Advisory roles with early-stage startups
  • Sourced deal flow for an existing VC, even informally
[4:45-5:30] Here is what counts as receipts. [Click] Angel investments, even tiny ones. [Click] Scout or fellow positions at established funds. [Click] Published investment memos or market maps. [Click] A consistent blog or newsletter on a sector. [Click] Advisory roles with early-stage startups. [Click] Sourced deal flow for a VC, even informally. The job is unstructured. Nobody tells you what to do on Tuesday morning. Hustle before the job is the best evidence you will hustle after the job.

Section 4

Investment Judgment

[5:45] Transition into the most important attribute.

The Core Skill

Judgment Is Synthesis, Not Opinion

Opinion (Not Enough)

"Is this a cool idea?" or "Is the market big?" Those are inputs. Every candidate can say "AI is a big market." That tells a partner nothing.

Judgment (The Goal)

"The product is strong but the go-to-market is wrong. They are selling to enterprise but the product is bottoms-up. They need to pick a lane." That is synthesis. That is what gets you hired.

[5:45-6:30] This is the attribute partners care about most. [Click] Most candidates walk in with opinions. "I think AI is big." That is an input, not a conclusion. [Click] Judgment is the synthesis. It is looking at a company and forming a defensible view on whether it will work, and being able to articulate why. The Stanford research confirmed that deal selection is the single most important driver of value creation at VC firms.

Deliberate Practice

Three Ways to Develop Investment Judgment

Study Deals Retrospectively

Pick ten companies that raised Series A three years ago. Read about them. Look at what happened. Who scaled? Who died? What signals did you miss?

Write Investment Memos

One-page memo for a company you find interesting. Thesis, risks, market size, competitive landscape. If you cannot articulate it in writing, you do not have a thesis.

Build a Paper Portfolio

Track your "picks" over time. Go back and check. Your hit rate does not matter. What matters is whether your reasoning was sound.

[6:30-7:15] Three ways to develop judgment before you have invested a dollar. [Click] First, study deals retrospectively. Pick ten Series A companies from three years ago. What happened? Could you have predicted it? This is deliberate practice for pattern recognition. [Click] Second, write investment memos. Thesis, risks, market, competition. Share them with people who will push back. The memo forces clarity. [Click] Third, build a paper portfolio. Track your picks over time. Good judgment sometimes produces bad outcomes. Over a large enough sample, the signal emerges.
