Section 1

VC Modeling vs. Banking Modeling

1:15 | Transition into the key differences between VC and IB modeling.

VC Recruiting Course

Precision vs. direction

Banking Models

Precision instruments. Hundreds of line items. Working capital, depreciation schedules, debt covenants. Goal: a defensible valuation to the nearest dollar. Makes sense for mature companies with decades of history.

VC Models

Directional tools. Goal: understand the relationship between assumptions and outcomes. What happens if growth slows from 100% to 60%? What if margins never exceed 65%? The model answers "what if," not "how much."

1:15 - 1:45 | The core distinction. [Click] Banking models are precision instruments. Hundreds of line items, three-statement flows, detailed schedules. The goal is valuation to the nearest dollar. That makes sense for mature companies with decades of data. [Click] VC models are directional tools. The goal is understanding how assumptions drive outcomes. What happens if growth slows? What if margins stall? The model answers "what if," not "how much exactly."

VC Recruiting Course

Three things that change everything

P&L Is Primary

In banking you build all three statements. In VC, the balance sheet is rarely relevant. Cash flow is derived from the P&L plus burn rate analysis.

Wide Uncertainty Bands

Banking projects 5 years with high confidence. VC projects 5-7 years for a company that's 18 months old. Year-5 projections are scenarios, not predictions.

Assumptions Over Formulas

In banking, getting formulas right is critical. In VC, the inputs are the game. 80% vs 120% growth changes exit value by billions.

Fewer than 10% had a balance sheet. [Click] Time horizon is 5-7 years with wide uncertainty. A Series A model projects into year 5 for a company that's 18 months old. These are scenarios, not predictions. [Click] Assumptions matter more than formulas. The formula for revenue is trivial. The hard part is deciding whether 80% or 120% growth is right. That's where judgment lives, and that's what interviewers test.

Revenue Modeling

3:30 | Transition into the two revenue modeling approaches.

VC Recruiting Course

Bottom-up: Start with the unit economics

Sales-Led SaaS

Sales reps x deals per rep per quarter x average contract value. Every assumption is observable and testable. "Is it realistic that each rep closes 4 deals at $50K ACV?" is a concrete question with a concrete answer.

Product-Led Growth

Website visitors x signup conversion x free-to-paid conversion x ARPU. Each variable can be benchmarked against current performance and industry data, then projected forward with informed judgment.

You can benchmark rep productivity against current data and industry averages. [Click] For PLG: visitors times signup conversion times paid conversion times ARPU. Same principle: each variable is testable. The power of bottom-up is that every assumption is concrete, not abstract.

VC Recruiting Course

The most sophisticated approach: Cohort-based

Instead of modeling revenue as a single number, model each customer cohort independently. Each cohort has its own acquisition date, ACV, retention rate, and expansion rate. Stack them up for total revenue.

This approach naturally incorporates retention and expansion dynamics, which are the variables that matter most. If a firm asks you to build a revenue model and you use a cohort-based approach, you're signaling that you think like an investor, not an accountant.

VC Recruiting Course

Top-down: Start with the market

The Approach

TAM is $10B. Company captures 2% in 5 years. That's $200M revenue. Useful for sanity-checking bottom-up projections and for very early-stage companies with insufficient data for bottom-up.

The Limitation

Disconnected from execution. "2% of a $10B market" tells you nothing about how. No customer acquisition details, no sales capacity, no competitive dynamics. A pitch deck uses top-down to impress. Diligence uses bottom-up to verify.

5:00 - 5:30 | Top-down works from the market downward. [Click] TAM times market share equals revenue. If bottom-up says $500M year-5 revenue in a $2B TAM, that implies 25% market share. For a 3-year-old company? Probably not realistic. The top-down check saves you from fantasy. [Click] The limitation: it's disconnected from execution. Saying "2% of $10B" sounds plausible but tells you nothing about how. The best models use both: build bottom-up, cross-check top-down. If they converge, confidence increases. If they diverge, figure out which is wrong.

Operating Model and Scenario Analysis

6:15 | Transition into the operating model below the revenue line.

VC Recruiting Course

Startup p&l: Four cost categories

COGS

Hosting, infrastructure, support. Gross margin should be 70%+ for true SaaS. Below 60% means a services component that caps profitability.

Sales & Marketing

Usually the largest line at 40-60% of revenue in growth phase. The question isn't how much, it's what you get per dollar spent.

R&D

Product and engineering. 20-30% of revenue at scale. Should decline as a percentage over time as the product matures.

G&A

Finance, legal, HR. 10-15% of revenue, declining over time. If G&A grows faster than revenue, operational discipline has failed.

6:15 - 7:00 | Four standard cost buckets for a startup P&L. COGS: direct delivery costs. Gross margin must be 70%+ for real SaaS. Sales and marketing: usually the largest line, 40-60% of revenue during growth. R&D: product and engineering, 20-30% at scale, declining as a percentage. G&A: overhead at 10-15%, also declining. The relationship between these buckets and revenue is where operating leverage lives.

VC Recruiting Course

Operating leverage: Why software gets premium multiples

Software has high fixed costs and low variable costs. Serving the 10,000th customer costs almost nothing more than the 1,000th. As revenue grows, margins expand. That is operating leverage.

In the model, operating leverage shows up as expenses growing slower than revenue. If revenue doubles and expenses grow 50%, margins expand. If expenses grow at the same rate as revenue, there is no operating leverage, and the business is not truly a software company from a financial perspective. Your model should show this trajectory.

7:00 - 7:30 | Operating leverage ties the cost structure together. Software has high fixed costs, low variable costs. The 10,000th customer costs almost nothing more than the 1,000th. Revenue doubles, expenses grow 50%, margins expand. In your model, this shows up as expense growth lagging revenue growth. If both grow at the same rate, there's no operating leverage, and you should ask why. This is the core reason software companies earn premium multiples.

Bull, base, and bear: Three scenarios

Bull Case

Everything goes right. Growth accelerates, retention improves, expansion into adjacent markets. What's the exit? What return does the fund generate?

Base Case

The company executes its current plan. Growth continues on current trajectory, retention holds, costs scale historically. The "most likely" outcome.

Bear Case

Growth slows. A competitor enters. Key segment churns. Additional fundraise at lower valuation. Can the fund still generate a return, or is the investment wiped out?

7:30 - 8:00 | Scenario analysis turns the model into a decision tool. [Click] Bull: everything works. Growth accelerates, retention improves, new markets open. What does the exit look like? [Click] Base: current trajectory continues. Growth holds, costs scale in line. The most likely outcome. [Click] Bear: growth slows, competition increases, customers churn. What's the downside? Can the fund still make money? Good scenario analysis isn't about optimism and pessimism. It's about identifying which variables drive the widest range of outcomes.

VC Recruiting Course

Which assumptions matter most

Build a Sensitivity Matrix

Pick the two highest-impact variables, usually growth rate and exit multiple. Build a matrix showing returns across combinations. A 2-variable sensitivity table is the most information-dense visual in a VC model.

Partners Love This

It answers "what has to go right for this to work?" in a single glance. Highlight the zone of acceptability where returns exceed the threshold. A deal that only works in the top-right corner of the table is a market-timing bet, not an investment.

VC Recruiting Course

Two hidden variables that change everything

Dilution Doubles the Bar

If the fund owns 20% at Series B but gets diluted to 10% by IPO, the required exit valuation doubles. A company needing two more rounds will dilute existing investors 30-50%. The model must account for future fundraising.

Exit Multiple Vulnerability

A company at 20x revenue in a hot market might trade at 8x in a correction. If the model only works at 20x, there is no margin of safety. Use the bear-case multiple. If the deal requires a bull-market multiple to work, you're making a market-timing bet.

VC Recruiting Course

Think like an investor, not an analyst

The analyst builds a model and reports the output. The investor asks "what does the output need to be?" and works backward to test feasibility. That shift in framing is the difference between someone who crunches numbers and someone who makes investment decisions.

For interview take-homes, this reverse-engineering exercise is often the most impressive component. It shows you understand the investment question. Instead of building a forecast and hoping the exit looks good, you start with what the exit needs to look like and ask whether it's achievable. That's rigorous thinking.

Key takeaways

  • VC modeling is about assumptions, not formulas. Anyone can build a spreadsheet. The skill is knowing which assumptions drive the outcome and setting them realistically. Interviewers test judgment, not Excel skills.
  • Build bottom-up, sanity-check top-down. Ground the revenue model in observable unit economics, then cross-reference against TAM. The gap between the two approaches is where the most important conversations happen.
  • Work backward from the exit. "What does this company need to look like for the investment to work?" turns an abstract growth story into a concrete, testable claim. This is the most practical exercise in VC modeling.
  • Scenario analysis identifies the variables that matter. A useful bull case names the specific driver of upside. A useful bear case identifies the specific risk. Test sensitivity on the highest-impact assumptions.
  • The biggest mistake is over-engineering. A clean 30-row model that answers the investment question beats a 500-row three-statement model every time. Build the model that answers the question. Nothing more.
