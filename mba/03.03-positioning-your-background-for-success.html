Applicant Pool

Three Paths to Admission

High-Potential Path

Consulting, banking, tech. Differentiate through your unique story, passions, and extracurriculars beyond work.

Program Match

Prove you fit a specific school's profile. Align your background with their unique emphases and resources.

Diverse Background

Non-traditional paths like entrepreneurship, military, or nonprofit. Stand out by proving credibility and unique perspective.

1:00 - 2:30 | MBA programs recruit from three types of candidates. First, high-potential career paths like consulting, banking, or tech. If this is you, differentiate through your unique story, passions, and extracurriculars. Second, program match. Each school has unique emphases. Proving you fit a specific school's profile strengthens your application. Third, diverse or differentiated backgrounds. If you are on a less traditional path, you can stand out through your uniqueness, but you need to prove your path's credibility and what you'll bring to the class.

Brand Positioning

Four Principles for Your Application

Be Distinctive

What makes you different from other applicants with similar backgrounds?

Be Credible

Every claim is supported by evidence and real accomplishments.

Be Relevant

Aligned with what your target schools value and prioritize.

Be Memorable

Easy to recall and creates a lasting impression in committee.

2:30 - 3:30 | Just like brand positioning in marketing, your personal positioning should follow four principles. Be distinctive: what makes you different from other applicants? Be credible: every claim supported by evidence. Be relevant: aligned with what schools value. Be memorable: easy to recall, creates a lasting impression. Before you submit, ask yourself whether your application meets all four.

Stand Out

The "Spike" Concept

Instead of being good at everything, have one standout area that makes you memorable and easy to advocate for in committee.

When the committee discusses candidates, they advocate for people. It is easier to advocate for someone with a clear spike: "She's the one who started that nonprofit" or "He's the one with the patent for that medical device."

6:30 - 7:30 | Instead of being good at everything, consider having one standout area. A spike is something you are notably excellent at. It makes you memorable and gives the admissions committee a reason to fight for you. When the committee discusses candidates, they advocate for people. It is easier to advocate for someone with a clear spike: She is the one who started that nonprofit, or He is the one with the patent for that medical device. Your spike does not have to be professional. It can be athletic achievement, artistic accomplishment, community impact, or personal resilience.

Your Spike

Where to Look for It

  • Quantifiable impact: Built a team from 3 to 30 people, grew revenue 4x
  • Unique experience: Worked on something few people have done
  • Recognition: Award, fellowship, publication, or patent
  • Distinctive skill: Speaking four languages, competitive athletics

Don't manufacture a spike. If you don't have something genuinely standout, focus on being strong across the board with an authentic story.

7:30 - 8:45 | Where do you look for your spike? Quantifiable impact, like building a team from 3 to 30 people. Unique experience, something few people have worked on. Recognition, like an award, fellowship, or publication. Or a distinctive skill, like speaking four languages fluently. Your spike does not have to be dramatic. It could be growing revenue at a family restaurant from 200K to 800K or publishing peer-reviewed research. Look for where your impact is quantifiable and your commitment is undeniable. But do not manufacture a spike. If you do not have something genuinely standout, focus on being strong across the board with an authentic story. A fake spike is worse than no spike.

Bringing It Together

Three Questions Before You Submit

01

One-Phrase Test

What one phrase would an admissions officer use to describe you? If you cannot answer, your positioning is not clear enough.

02

Uniqueness Test

Could another applicant from your background submit the same essays? If yes, you have not differentiated enough.

03

Spike Test

Does your spike come through in at least one essay and one recommendation? If not, it is a footnote, not a spike.

8:45 - 9:30 | Before you finalize, ask three questions. First, the one-phrase test: what one phrase would an admissions officer use to describe you? If you cannot answer this, your positioning is not clear enough. Second, the uniqueness test: could another applicant from your background submit the same essays? If yes, you have not differentiated enough. Third, the spike test: does your spike come through in at least one essay and one recommendation? If not, it is a footnote, not a spike. Your positioning should combine your narrative themes, your differentiation within your category, and authenticity. The goal is not to become someone you are not. It is to help admissions see the full, unique picture of who you already are.
