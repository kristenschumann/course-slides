Critical Rule

Don't Write the Letter Yourself

Letters written by applicants are much weaker than letters written by recommenders themselves. It's awkward to brag about yourself, and you lose the recommender's unique perspective and voice.

Instead, use a recommender prep document. It still requires significant effort on your part, but it makes their job easier and produces a better recommendation.

1:00 - 2:00 | Before we get into how to prepare them, let's address the biggest pitfall: writing the letter yourself. Do everything you can to avoid this. Letters written by applicants are weaker for two reasons. First, it's awkward to brag about yourself and put someone else's name on it. Second, you lose the recommender's unique perspective and voice. Instead, use a recommender prep document. It still requires significant effort, but it makes their job much easier and ensures a better result. If you don't provide help, recommenders often feel overwhelmed about where to start and may ask you to write it for them.

Your Narrative

Question Guidance

2:00 - 3:15 | Your prep document should have three sections. First, an overview: the schools you're applying to, application deadlines, what strong recommendations look like, and what makes these programs selective. This helps your recommender understand the stakes and caliber required. Second, your narrative and themes: your career goals, the 2-3 themes you're emphasizing in your application, and how you want to be positioned. This ensures their letter reinforces rather than contradicts your story. Third, question-by-question guidance: walk them through each type of question with specific stories and examples they can draw from. This document lets you control and guide their writing while still allowing them to be creative with their words.

The Five Prompt Types

What your recommenders will be asked and how to help

3:15 - 3:20 | Now let's go through the five types of prompts your recommenders will face and how to support them on each one.

Five Prompt Types

What Recommenders Are Asked

  • History: How do you know the applicant? Brief context on the relationship.
  • Comparison: How does the applicant compare to peers? The most important prompt.
  • Criticism: What constructive feedback have you given? Shows growth.
  • Behavioral: "Tell us about a time when..." Requires the STAR method.
  • Optional: Anything else we should know? Only for genuine clarifying info.
3:20 - 4:30 | There are five main prompt types. History: a brief question about how they know you, typically limited to a short answer. Comparison: how you compare to peers. This is the most important prompt with the largest word count. Criticism: what constructive feedback they've given you and how you responded. Behavioral: tell us about a time when, requiring specific stories. And optional: anything else we should know. Let's look at how to support each one.

Comparison Prompt

The Pattern That Works

The Formula

  • Make a bold claim about the applicant
  • Back it up with a specific story
  • Repeat this pattern 2-3 times
  • No more than 3-4 stories total

In Your Prep Doc

  • Recall specific praise they've given you
  • Include compliments that compared you to others
  • Offer at least one strong story per claim
  • Give them concrete details and numbers

This prompt usually has the largest word count. Set up your recommender to fill that space with examples and details.

4:30 - 5:45 | The comparison prompt is the most important. Nine of the top ten programs ask it. Here is the pattern that works: make a bold claim about the applicant, then back it up with a specific story and examples. You can repeat this pattern 2-3 times. We don't recommend more than 3-4 stories because there won't be enough room to properly explain them. In your prep doc, think about specific praise this person has given you, especially any compliment that compared you to others. Offer at least one strong story per claim, with concrete details and numbers. This prompt usually has the largest word count, so set your recommender up to fill that space.

Criticism and Behavioral Prompts

Showing Growth Through Stories

Criticism Prompt

Remind your recommender of specific feedback they gave you. Then share what you did to change and improve. Choose the criticism where you showed the most growth.

STAR Method

Situation, Task, Action, Result. Use this structure for all behavioral prompts. Spend extra time on Action and include numbers in Result.

Every Prompt Is Behavioral

Whether or not the question explicitly asks for a story, the strongest answers always include at least one powerful anecdote.

The Optional Prompt

Only use this for genuine clarifying info (personal circumstances, gaps in record). If nothing to add, suggest they leave it blank.

5:45 - 7:30 | The criticism prompt can be uncomfortable because it requires vulnerability. But for admissions committees, these can be the most moving parts of the entire application. Think of times you received criticism from your recommender, write down what you did afterward, and choose the one where you showed the most growth. For behavioral prompts, use the STAR method: Situation, Task, Action, Result. Spend extra time on the Action portion and include numbers in the Result. Here's a big secret: almost every recommender prompt is a behavioral question in disguise. The strongest answers always include a powerful story. For the optional prompt, only use it for genuine clarifying information like personal circumstances. If there's nothing to add, suggest they leave it blank. Don't treat it as extra bragging space.

Last Resort

If You Must Write Your Own

Guideline Why It Matters
Don't feel guilty It's not immoral as long as you don't fabricate and they approve it
Study their writing style Read their old emails or publications to match their tone and voice
Be bolder than you think You are more likely to undersell yourself when writing your own letter
Get a second opinion Someone who knows you both can spot what sounds like you vs. them

After giving them your prep document, most recommenders will no longer ask you to write it. But if they still insist, follow these guidelines.

9:00 - 10:30 | Sometimes a recommender will ask you to write the letter for them. Do your best to avoid this, but if they insist after receiving the prep document, here are guidelines. Don't feel guilty. It's not immoral as long as you don't fabricate anything and they approve the final version. Study their writing style by reading old emails or publications. Match their tone, whether serious, lighthearted, or humorous. Be bolder than you think. You will almost certainly undersell yourself when writing your own recommendation. Get out of your own head and make yourself look good. Finally, get a second opinion from someone who knows you both. Ask them what parts sound like the recommender and what parts sound like you.

The Big Picture

Your Effort Equals Their Quality

The more work you put into preparing your recommenders, the stronger their letters will be. A detailed prep document, a walkthrough meeting, and regular check-ins transform a generic letter into a powerful advocate statement.

Your recommenders tell stories about you that others can't tell, and that you can't tell yourself without seeming arrogant. Give them the raw material to tell those stories powerfully.
