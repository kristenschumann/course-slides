The Portfolio Approach

Diversify Like an Investment

Reach Schools

Your stats are below the class average. Competitive but acceptance is unlikely. 2-3 schools.

Target Schools

Your profile matches the class average. Reasonable shot at 25-40% odds. 3-4 schools.

Safety Schools

Your profile exceeds the average. Schools you'd genuinely be happy attending. 1-2 schools.

1:00 - 2:15 | Think of your school list like an investment portfolio. You want diversification across risk levels. Reach schools are where your stats fall below the class average. You are competitive but acceptance is not likely. Plan on 2-3 of these. Target schools are where your profile matches the average, giving you roughly a 25 to 40 percent chance. Aim for 3-4 targets. Safety schools are where your profile exceeds the average. These should be schools you would genuinely be happy attending. Include 1-2 of these. Total: 6-9 schools. More than that and quality suffers. Fewer and you are taking unnecessary risk.

Evaluation Factors

What Actually Matters

  • Career outcomes: Where do graduates end up? Target industry placement and median salary.
  • Class profile: Work experience, international mix, class size, industry backgrounds.
  • Culture and teaching style: Collaboration vs. rigor, case method vs. lecture.
  • Geography: Regional recruiting pipelines and where you want to live.
  • Cost and ROI: Net cost after scholarships, realistic debt-to-income ratio.
2:15 - 4:00 | Five factors beyond rankings. Career outcomes are the big one. Look at employment reports for your target industry. If you want consulting, check that top firms recruit on campus. If you want tech in the Bay Area, Berkeley Haas has geographic advantages. Class profile matters too: average work experience, international mix, and class size shape your daily experience. Stanford GSB has 430 students per class, Harvard has 940, very different. Culture is distinct at every school. Kellogg is collaborative, Booth is analytical, Stanford focuses on personal development. Visit if you can. Geography matters for recruiting pipelines. West Coast: Haas, Stanford. East Coast finance: Columbia, Wharton. And finally, cost and ROI. Total cost ranges from $100K to $300K. Focus on net cost after scholarships and expected ROI for your specific career path.

Self-Assessment

Five Dimensions to Evaluate

Academics

Is your GMAT/GRE and GPA at or above the school's median?

Professional Impact

Can you point to 2-3 quantifiable achievements?

Leadership

Have you led people or projects, not just participated?

Differentiation

What makes you different from the typical applicant?

Fit

Can you articulate specific reasons this school is right for you?

Strong on all 5: safety. Strong on 4: target. Strong on 2-3: reach.

5:15 - 6:30 | For each target school, rate yourself on five dimensions. Academics: are your scores at, above, or below median? Professional impact: can you point to 2-3 specific, quantifiable achievements? Leadership: have you led people or projects, not just participated? Differentiation: what makes you different from the typical applicant in your category? And fit: can you articulate specific reasons this school is right for you? If you are strong on all 5 of these, the school is a safety. Strong on 4, it is a target. Strong on 2-3, it is a reach. This framework is more reliable than comparing a single GMAT number to a median.

6:35 - 8:00 | Four steps. Step 1: Start with your goals. What do you want to do post-MBA? Which schools have the strongest pipelines to that outcome? A Common Trap

More Applications Is Not Better

Each application takes 50 to 100 hours when done well. Ten mediocre applications lose to five excellent ones every time.

Be strategic about where you invest your application energy. Every school on your list should have a genuine "why this school" answer you can articulate clearly.

8:00 - 9:15 | One of the biggest traps: thinking more applications means better odds. Each application takes 50 to 100 hours when done well. You need to customize essays, research the school, prepare for interviews. Ten mediocre applications will lose to five excellent ones every time. Be strategic. Every school on your list should have a genuine "why this school" answer that you can articulate clearly. If you cannot explain specifically why a program fits your goals, it probably should not be on your list.

Common Misconceptions

Myths to Leave Behind

  • "Top 5 or nothing" ignores incredible programs with great outcomes outside the T5.
  • "Rankings are everything" overlooks that the gap between #7 and #12 is often noise.
  • "Apply everywhere" spreads you thin and prevents meaningful customization.
9:15 - 10:30 | Three misconceptions to let go of. "Top 5 or nothing" is ego talking. Texas McCombs is phenomenal for energy. Indiana Kelley dominates in marketing. There are incredible programs beyond the top 5. "Rankings are everything" misses the point. The difference between number 7 and 12 is often noise. Focus on fit, outcomes, and culture. And "apply everywhere" spreads you thin. You cannot customize enough for 12 schools. Cast a strategic net, not a wide one.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Build a portfolio: mix reach, target, and safety schools.
  • Focus on outcomes, not just rankings or prestige.
  • Culture and geography matter more than most applicants realize.
  • Be honest about where you stand using the five-dimension framework.
  • Quality over quantity: 6-9 schools, done well.
  • Your school list is a strategic document. Build it carefully.

Culture and geography matter more than most applicants realize, you will spend two years there. Be honest about your competitiveness using the five-dimension framework. And remember, quality over quantity. Six to nine schools, done well, is the sweet spot. Your school list is a strategic document.
