MBA vs. Job Resume

What Admissions Committees Care About

Adcoms care about leadership and impact, not technical skills or industry jargon. They want to see you zoom out from individual contributor to higher-level business impact.

Engineers get into business school by proving they see the broader vision of why their work helped the organization, not by proving they are the best engineer. Translate technical accomplishments into business results.

1:00 - 2:15 | Your MBA resume should be different from your job resume. If you are applying for investment banking, you highlight deal experience and finance buzzwords. But for business school, your resume should be practically void of industry-specific jargon. Admissions committees care about leadership and impact. They want to know why the CEO cared about your work. Engineers get into business school by proving they see the broader vision, not by proving they are the best engineer. Adcoms do not care that you improved ML model processing time by 150 percent. They want to know your improvements saved the business 5 million dollars. Translate technical accomplishments into business results.

Resume Structure

The Four Sections

01

Education

GPA, scholarships, awards, campus involvement. Show academics plus community engagement.

02

Experience

The core of your resume. Reverse chronological. Focus on results and impact, not responsibilities.

03

Service & Leadership

Volunteer work, community projects, nonprofit roles. Show a pattern of service and initiative.

04

Skills & Interests

Add personality. Highlight achievements over labels. "5x Music Award" beats "Cello player."

2:15 - 3:45 | We recommend four sections. Education: include GPA, scholarships, awards, and campus involvement. This is not just academics. Highlight clubs, organizations, and events to show you will be an active community member. Experience: the core of your resume, at least a quarter of the page. Go reverse chronological. Focus on results and impact, not responsibilities. Service and Leadership: volunteer work, community projects, nonprofit roles. Schools like HBS say "educate leaders who make a difference in the world." This section shows that side of you. Skills and Interests: add personality. Mention achievements over labels. "5x Superior Cello Music Award" is stronger than "cello player for 10 years."

Part 2

Writing Strong Bullet Points

Results over responsibilities

3:45 - 3:50 | Now the most powerful thing you can do for your resume: write strong bullet points that focus on results, not responsibilities.

Bullet Points

Weak vs. Strong: Software Engineer

Weak

  • Pitched product to investors to get grants
  • Managed inventory and supply for main product
  • Helped patients remember to take pills through new technology

Strong

  • Won $65K grant by pitching to investors for program with <2% acceptance
  • Led team of 5 to source $10K+ in product and build 2,000-person pre-order list
  • Increased pill compliance from 55% to 95%+ through patented technology

Start with strong action verbs. Quantify impact with specific numbers. Keep each bullet to one line when possible.

3:50 - 5:15 | Here is a software engineering intern example. On the left, weak bullets. Pitched product to investors, managed inventory, helped patients take pills. These describe tasks, not results. On the right, strong versions. Won a 65K grant by pitching to investors for a program with less than 2 percent acceptance. Led a team of 5 to source over 10K in product and build a 2,000-person pre-order list. Increased pill compliance from 55 percent to 95 percent through patented technology. The difference: specific numbers, strong action verbs, and clear impact. You do not need to write about tasks and responsibilities. Write about results. What you achieved, what impact you made, how you helped clients and colleagues.

Bullet Points

Weak vs. Strong: Business Analyst

Weak

  • Part of group that made customer segmentation model
  • Online customer care quality management

Strong

  • Redesigned Fortune 500 support strategy by building segmentation model for 10 customer groups
  • Led 2 airlines in social media strategy by surveying 1K+ complaints
  • Developed staffing strategy saving $100K through minimized downtime

Each position should have at least 2 bullets. Remove fluff and passive language. Lead with the result or impact.

5:15 - 6:30 | Another example, this time a business analyst. The weak version says "part of group that made customer segmentation model" and "online customer care quality management." These are vague and passive. The strong version: redesigned Fortune 500 support strategy by building a segmentation model analyzing 10 customer groups. Led two global airlines in developing social media response strategies by surveying over 1,000 complaints. Developed a staffing strategy that saved 100K through minimized downtime. Each position should have at least two bullets. Remove fluff. Lead with the result or impact, not the task.

Career Framing

Three Profiles Schools Look For

School-Specific Fit

Align your resume to each school's unique emphases, priorities, and specialties.

High-Potential Path

Established career in consulting, banking, or tech. Complement with your unique story and interests.

Diverse Background

Non-traditional path with fresh ideas. Prove you are grounded, realistic, and high-impact.

6:30 - 7:45 | To frame your career path strategically, understand the three profiles schools look for. School-specific fit: each business school has unique emphases. Find out what they prioritize and align your resume accordingly. High-potential path: if you are in consulting, banking, or tech, you check this box, but complement your established path with your unique story and diverse interests to avoid blending in. Diverse background: business schools want applicants who bring fresh ideas but are also grounded and realistic. Prove your path's credibility and potential for impact. You may need to write slightly different versions for each school.

Formatting

Rules to Follow

  • One page maximum: No exceptions. Every word should be intentional.
  • Consistent formatting: Same font, spacing, and bullet style throughout.
  • Reverse chronological: Most recent experience first within each section.
  • No objective statements: Skip summaries and objectives. Let your content speak.
7:45 - 9:00 | Key formatting rules. One page maximum, no exceptions. This does not mean filling the page with filler. Every bullet and every word should be intentional. Consistent formatting throughout, same font, spacing, and bullet style. Reverse chronological order within each section. And no objective statements or professional summaries. Let your content speak for itself. Keep the layout clean, straightforward, and easy to scan. Too much fancy design distracts from the content. Submit as PDF unless the school specifies otherwise.

Common Mistakes

What to Avoid on Your MBA Resume

Avoid Do Instead
Industry jargon and buzzwords Plain language with business impact
Listing tasks and responsibilities Quantified results and achievements
Over-relying on AI for bullet points Your own specific details refined with AI as needed
Inflated titles or fudged dates Accurate information (schools verify)
Relevant coursework section Campus involvement and leadership roles
9:00 - 10:30 | Common mistakes to avoid. Do not use industry jargon and buzzwords. Use plain language with business impact instead. Do not list tasks and responsibilities. Quantify your results and achievements. Be thoughtful about using AI for your bullet points. Admissions committees are aware of AI-generated language patterns, and generic, overly polished language can work against you. Use AI to refine your writing if helpful, but make sure the content reflects your actual experiences. Do not inflate titles or fudge dates. Most MBA programs conduct background checks on admitted students. Inflating from associate to senior associate or hiding a gap can get your admission rescinded. And do not include a relevant coursework section. Use that space for campus involvement and leadership roles instead.

Review Process

Before You Submit

Read your resume from the adcom's perspective. Remove details they will not care about. Draw out points that relate to your career path and each specific school.

Ask trusted colleagues and mentors to review it. You may need slightly different versions for each school. A powerful resume grounds the story you share in essays and recommendations.

10:30 - 11:30 | Before you submit, read through your resume several times from the adcom's perspective. Remove details they will not care about and draw out points that relate to your career path and each specific school. Ask multiple trusted colleagues and mentors to review it. You may need slightly different versions for each application. And proofread carefully for consistent formatting. A powerful resume will ground the rest of your story in your essays and recommendations and share objective impact that would not easily fit in essay form.
