Why Essays Matter

"Your essays are the only part of the application where you're a person, not a data point."

0:30 - 1:30 | Let that sink in. Your GMAT is a number. Your GPA is a number. Your resume is a list. The essays are the only place where the committee hears your voice — the way you think, the choices you've made, and why you made them. Every piece of advice I'm going to give you today flows from this idea. If your essay reads like a resume in paragraph form, you've wasted the one opportunity to be a person in this process.

"One story, told well, beats ten mentioned briefly."

HBS: 943 spots from ~8,000 applications. The essays that stick share one specific moment — not ten bullet points in prose.

1:30 - 2:30 | This is the single most important principle I teach. When I read a draft, the first thing I look for is whether there's one clear story or five competing ones. HBS gets roughly 8,000 applications for 943 spots. Readers are scanning hundreds of essays. The ones that stay with them — the ones they advocate for in committee — are the ones where one specific moment reveals something true about who you are. Not a list of achievements. A moment. We'll come back to this idea throughout the video.

Show, Don't Tell

Open With a Scene

Reads Like a Resume

"I learned leadership at Deloitte where I managed a team of 12 across multiple workstreams, driving operational improvements that resulted in a 15% efficiency gain."

Reads Like a Story

"At 11 PM on a Thursday, I realized we'd been solving the wrong problem for three weeks — and I had to tell the partner before the client meeting in nine hours."

2:30 - 4:00 | Look at the difference. On the left, we have a perfectly accurate statement. But it reads like a resume bullet — they already have your resume. They know you managed a team. What they don't know is what you did at 11 PM on that Thursday when the project was going sideways. The opening on the right puts the reader in the room with you. It creates tension. It makes them want to know what happened next. That's the difference between an essay that gets skimmed and one that gets remembered. Every one of my clients starts with something like the left. Our job together is to find the moment underneath it — the real story.

The Formula

What Makes an Essay Land

1

A specific moment

Not "during my time at McKinsey" — a date, a room, a decision point.

2

A real decision

What did you actually choose, and what did you give up by choosing it?

3

What it changed in you

Not the business outcome — how it changed how you think, lead, or see the world.

4:00 - 5:30 | Three things. Every great essay I've read has these three things. First, a specific moment. Not "during my time at McKinsey." Give the reader a date, a room, a decision point. Ground them in reality. Second, a real decision. Not just what happened — what did you choose? And what did you give up by choosing it? That's where your judgment shows. Third — and this is where most essays fall short — what it changed in you. I don't mean the business outcome. I mean: how did this experience change how you think? How you lead? How you see the world? That's what Stanford is asking with "what matters most." That's what HBS is asking with "growth."

"Write your ending first."

If you can't state your point in one sentence, you're not ready to draft.

7:30 - 8:30 | This is the practical advice I give every client before they write a single word. Write your ending first. The ending is the point. It's the "so what." If you can't state it in one sentence — the one thing you want the reader to take away — then you haven't thought deeply enough yet. Go back to reflection. Talk to someone. Journal. But don't start drafting. I've seen clients spend weeks on drafts that wandered because they started writing before they knew where they were going. The ending is the compass.

Authentic Voice

Sound Like Yourself

Press Release Voice

"Leveraged cross-functional synergies to optimize operational efficiency across three business units, resulting in measurable improvements to stakeholder satisfaction."

Real Person Voice

"I called a meeting with engineering because our teams hadn't talked in three months. It was uncomfortable. It was also the most productive hour of my entire year."

8:30 - 9:30 | Voice is everything. The left side is technically accurate — but it could have been written by anyone. Or by a chatbot. "Leveraged cross-functional synergies" — that's not a person talking, that's a press release. The right side tells you exactly what happened. "I called a meeting because our teams hadn't talked in three months." You can see this person. You can imagine being in that meeting. My test: read your essay aloud to a friend. If any sentence makes you cringe or sounds like a corporate memo, rewrite it. Your voice — your real voice, imperfections included — is what makes an essay genuine. That's especially important now that committees are looking for AI-generated patterns.

Watch Out

Three Mistakes That Sink Essays

1

Listing achievements instead of showing judgment

They have your resume. Show what you chose, not what you accomplished.

2

Submitting the same essay to every school

HBS tests leadership. Stanford tests values. Wharton tests community. Same essay won't work.

3

Polishing until your voice disappears

The tenth revision is often worse than the third. Know when to stop editing.

9:30 - 11:30 | Three mistakes I see every cycle. First, listing achievements instead of showing judgment. "I delivered a $5M project on time and under budget." Great — that's your resume. What the committee wants to know is: what did you decide? What trade-off did you make? What did you learn when things didn't go as planned? Second, submitting the same essay everywhere. I can't say this enough. HBS, Stanford, and Wharton are evaluating different things. An essay that works for Stanford's "what matters most" will fail at HBS, which wants business-mindedness. You need to research each school's criteria. Third — and this one surprises people — polishing until your voice disappears. I've seen clients revise ten times and the essay gets worse. The seventh draft sounds corporate. Keep the version that sounds like you.
