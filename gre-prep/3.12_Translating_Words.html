Example 1

Age problem

Problem

Poorni's brother is three times as old as Poorni. Six years ago, her brother was 12 years older than four times Poorni's age. How old is the brother now?

Solution

Poorni $= x$, Brother $= 3x$

Six years ago: $3x - 6 = 4(x - 6) + 12$

$3x - 6 = 4x - 24 + 12$, so $x = 6$

Brother $= 3(6) =$ $18$

1:30-3:30 | Example 1: age problem. Let Poorni = x, brother = 3x. "Six years ago" means subtract 6 from each. "Was" means equals. "12 years older than four times Poorni's age" means 4(x-6) + 12. Solve to get x = 6, brother = 18.

Fewer Variables

The LEGO inference

The Rule

If parts must add to a total, express one part as total minus the others.

If $A + B = \text{Total}$, then $B = \text{Total} - A$

Why It Helps

1 variable needs 1 equation.

2 variables need 2 equations.

Fewer variables $=$ simpler solve.

3:40-4:30 | The LEGO inference. If parts add to a total, express the last part as total minus the others. This reduces your variable count. 1 variable needs 1 equation, 2 variables need 2 equations. Keep it simple.

Example 2

Trail mix with LEGO inference

Problem

32 cups of trail mix at \$12 total. Peanuts (\$0.10), raisins (\$0.30), chips (\$1.00). Twice as many peanuts as raisins. How many cups of peanuts?

Solution

R $= r$, P $= 2r$, Chips $= 32 - 3r$

$0.20r + 0.30r + (32 - 3r) = 12$

$0.50r + 32 - 3r = 12$, so $r = 8$

Peanuts $= 2(8) =$ $16$ cups

4:30-5:50 | Example 2: trail mix. Use one variable: raisins = r, peanuts = 2r (twice as many), chips = 32 - 3r (LEGO). Set up cost equation and solve. r = 8, so peanuts = 16 cups.

Two-Variable Problems

Example 3

Ticket revenue

Problem

GA (\$16) and box seats (\$40). 300 tickets sold, \$8,160 revenue. What was GA revenue?

Solution

$G + B = 300$ and $16G + 40B = 8{,}160$

$B = 300 - G$: $16G + 40(300-G) = 8{,}160$

$-24G = -3{,}840$, so $G = 160$

GA revenue $= 160 \times \$16 =$ $\$2{,}560$

6:00-7:30 | Example 3: two equations, two unknowns. Total tickets = 300, total revenue = $8,160. Substitute B = 300 - G into the revenue equation. Solve for G = 160. GA revenue = $2,560.

Example 4

Awkward variables

Problem

A 100 ft rope makes a square and a circle (radius $r$). Express total area in terms of $r$.

Solution

Constraint: $4s + 2\pi r = 100$

Solve: $s = 25 - \frac{\pi r}{2}$

Area $= \pi r^2 + s^2$

$= \pi r^2 +$ $\left(25 - \frac{\pi r}{2}\right)^2$

7:30-9:00 | Example 4: weird-looking variables, but the algebra is basic. The rope constraint gives us 4s + 2 pi r = 100. Solve for s, then total area = pi r squared plus s squared. Do not be intimidated by pi and r.

Key Takeaways

Translation toolkit

  • Read literally - Translate a few words at a time into math symbols
  • Use as few variables as possible - Express related quantities in terms of one variable
  • LEGO inference - If parts sum to a total, write one part as total minus the others
  • Two equations for two unknowns - Look for two "equals" statements in the problem
  • Do not be intimidated - Weird symbols and variables still follow basic algebra rules
9:00-10:00 | Summary: read literally, use few variables, apply the LEGO inference, and do not be intimidated by fancy notation. These techniques work on every GRE word problem.
