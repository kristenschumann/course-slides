The Real Question

Who Are You at Your Core?

This isn't about accomplishments. It's about identity. What do you value? What drives you? What experiences shaped who you've become?

Schools are building communities, not collecting resumes. The best answers reveal something deeply true about the writer. Not impressive. Not polished. True.

2:15 - 3:45 | Most people struggle because they don't know what matters most to them. Here's a four-step process. First, start with moments, not abstractions. Don't ask "What are my values?" Instead ask "What experiences have stuck with me?" A conversation with a grandparent. A failure that changed your perspective. A choice that surprised you. Second, ask "why" repeatedly. Take a resonant moment, ask why it matters, then ask why that matters. Keep going. You're looking for the root. Third, look for patterns across your life. What connects your experiences? Fourth, test for authenticity. Is this actually true? Would your closest friends recognize this? If you're performing, the essay won't work.

Structure and Depth

Essay Structure

Four Parts of a Strong Essay

01

The Hook

Start with a specific moment, not a summary. Put the reader in a place and time. Create an image that draws them in.

02

The Build

Expand from your opening. Add context, connect experiences, show how your understanding has evolved over time.

03

The Depth

Answer "why" in a way that's genuinely revealing. Show the tensions and complexities you've wrestled with.

04

Connection to Now

Bring it to the present. How does this show up in your life today? How does it connect to your future?

5:20 - 7:00 | Four parts. The hook: start with a specific moment. Bad opening: "Family has always been important to me." Good opening: "I was twelve when my grandmother first put her hands over mine on the piano keys." The good opening creates an image and draws the reader in. The build: from your opening, expand. What context does the reader need? What other experiences connect to this theme? The depth: this is where you answer "why" in a genuinely revealing way. Show tensions, complexities, vulnerability. Depth isn't about length, it's about honesty. The connection to now: bring it forward to the present. How does this show up in your life today?

Writing with Depth

Surface vs. Deep Writing

Surface Level

"I learned the importance of perseverance when I trained for a marathon. It was hard, but I pushed through and finished."

Generic. Could be written by anyone. No real insight.

With Depth

"At mile 22, I understood something I'd been avoiding: I don't know how to quit. Even when quitting is rational. I finished on stress fractures, and I'm still not sure if that makes me determined or stubborn."

Specific, complex, self-aware. Only this person could write it.

7:00 - 8:15 | Let me show you the difference between surface and deep writing. Surface level: "I learned the importance of perseverance when I trained for a marathon. It was hard, but I pushed through and finished." Generic. Could be anyone. With depth: "At mile 22, I understood something I'd been avoiding: I don't know how to quit. Even when quitting is rational. I finished on stress fractures, and I'm still not sure if that makes me determined or stubborn." The second version reveals complexity and self-awareness. It's specific enough that only this person could have written it.

Writing Techniques

Four Ways to Go Deeper

  • Show the tension: Real values are complicated. Show where they conflict and where you've struggled.
  • Include doubt: You don't have everything figured out. Questions can be more revealing than answers.
  • Be specific: Replace "my family is close" with a specific tradition, conversation, or moment.
  • Write what scares you: The most authentic material is often what you're hesitant to share.
8:15 - 9:30 | Four techniques for going deeper. Show the tension. Real values are complicated. Show where they conflict, where they've been tested. Include doubt. You don't have to have everything figured out. Questions can be more revealing than answers. Be specific. Replace generic statements with concrete details. Instead of "my family is close," describe a specific tradition or conversation. Write what scares you. Often the most authentic material is the stuff you're hesitant to share. That hesitation is a signal that you've found something real.

Common Pitfalls

Three Traps to Avoid

Impressive Over True

If your authentic answer feels too personal or too small, you've probably found the right topic.

All Career, No Self

This essay isn't about work. It might include work, but the core should be about you as a human.

Stopping Too Soon

Most applicants stop at the first level of "why." The good stuff is three or four levels down.

9:30 - 10:45 | Three common pitfalls. First, picking impressive over true. If your authentic answer feels too personal or too small, that's usually a sign you've found the right topic. Picking "global health" when what keeps you up at night is being a good father won't work. Second, making it all about your career. This essay isn't about work. It might include work, but the core should be about you as a human being. Third, stopping too soon. Many applicants stop at the first level of "why." Keep going. The good stuff is three or four levels down. If you haven't surprised yourself, you haven't dug deep enough.

Writing Timeline

The 8-Week Process

Week Activity Goal
Week 1 Reflect Think, journal, have conversations. Sit with the question.
Week 2-3 Brainstorm List specific moments and turning points. Don't filter.
Week 3-4 Find the thread Look for patterns. Articulate your deeper "why."
Week 4-5 Draft and deepen Write messy. Then push further on the surface-level sections.
Week 6-8 Refine, feedback, polish Get trusted readers. Tighten language. Read aloud.
10:45 - 12:15 | Here's the 8-week process. Week 1: don't write yet. Think. Journal. Have conversations. Ask yourself "what matters?" and sit with the discomfort of not knowing. Weeks 2 and 3: brainstorm specific experiences, memories, and turning points. Don't filter. Get everything down. Weeks 3 and 4: look for patterns. What connects your moments? Start to articulate your "why." Weeks 4 and 5: write a rough draft. It will be messy. Then read it fresh and push the surface-level sections deeper. Weeks 6 through 8: refine structure and flow. Get feedback from trusted readers. Does it feel like you? Then final polish. Tighten language, cut unnecessary words, read aloud.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Start with specific moments, not abstract values
  • Ask "why" repeatedly to find the root of what matters
  • Structure: hook with a scene, build context, go deep, connect to now
  • Depth means showing complexity, tension, and vulnerability
  • Avoid impressive-sounding topics that aren't authentically you
  • Give yourself 6-8 weeks. This essay cannot be rushed
