Why They Matter

Past Behavior Predicts Future Behavior

Interviewers don't want to know what you think leadership is. They want to see that you've already done it. Always give a specific example.

Behavioral questions reveal how you actually behave in situations, your real skills, how you think about challenges, and what you've learned from experience.

1:00 - 2:00 | Behavioral questions are based on a simple premise: past behavior predicts future behavior. When they ask "tell me about a time you led a team," they don't want hypotheticals. They want proof you've done it. The biggest mistake candidates make is talking about what they think leadership is instead of describing what they actually did. Always give a specific example. Always.

STAR Framework

The Four Parts of Every Answer

S

Situation

Briefly set the scene. Where were you? What was the context? Keep this to 10-15 seconds

T

Task

Your specific responsibility. What was your role? Distinguish your job from the team's

A+R

Action + Result

What YOU did (spend 60% here) and the outcome with what you learned

2:00 - 3:30 | STAR stands for Situation, Task, Action, Result. Situation: briefly set the scene. Just enough context so the interviewer understands the scenario. Keep it short. Task: what was your specific responsibility? This distinguishes what you did from what the team did. Action: what did YOU do? This is the heart of your answer. Use "I" not "we." Be specific with active verbs: I proposed, I organized, I convinced. Result: what was the outcome? Quantify when possible. And include your reflection on what you learned.

STAR Framework

Where to Spend Your Time

Component Time What to Include
Situation ~20% Context in 1-2 sentences. Where, when, what was happening
Task ~10% Your specific role and responsibility
Action ~50% 3-5 specific things YOU did. Use active verbs, be concrete
Result ~20% Quantified outcome + what you learned

Target 2 minutes total per answer. Too short feels underprepared; too long loses the interviewer.

3:30 - 4:30 | Here's the time allocation that most candidates get wrong. They spend 80% on Situation and Task and rush through Action and Result. Flip it. Spend about 20% on Situation, 10% on Task, 50% on Action, and 20% on Result. The Action section is where you demonstrate your skills and judgment. That's where the interviewer learns the most about you. Aim for about 2 minutes total per answer.

Common Mistakes

Five STAR Mistakes to Avoid

  • Too much setup: Spending 80% on Situation and Task leaves no room for Action.
  • Using "we" instead of "I": They want to know what you did, not the team.
  • Vague actions: "I communicated effectively" means nothing. What did you actually do?
  • Forgetting the result: Always land the plane. What happened? What changed?
  • Weak story choice: If the story doesn't demonstrate the skill asked about, pick a different one.
4:30 - 6:00 | Five common STAR mistakes. First, too much setup. Don't spend the bulk of your answer on context. Second, using "we" instead of "I." Even in collaborative situations, describe your specific contribution. Third, vague actions. "I communicated effectively with stakeholders" tells the interviewer nothing. What specifically did you do? Did you send a weekly update? Set up a meeting? Fourth, forgetting the result. Always land the plane with what happened and what you learned. Fifth, choosing a weak story that doesn't actually demonstrate the skill being asked about.

When the Question Doesn't Match

Adapt Your Story

Most stories demonstrate multiple qualities. A leadership story often shows conflict resolution too. Lean into the angle that fits the question

Ask for a Moment

"That's a great question, let me think about the best example." Take five seconds to scan your bank. Better than rambling unprepared

7:30 - 8:30 | What if the question doesn't match your prepared stories? This will happen. You have two moves. First, adapt. Most stories demonstrate multiple qualities. If you're asked about innovation but your best example also involves teamwork, lean into the innovation angle and emphasize the creative thinking. Second, ask for a moment. Say "That's a great question, let me think about the best example." Then take five seconds to scan your story bank. This is far better than rambling into an unprepared answer.

Failure Questions

How to Answer "Tell Me About a Time You Failed"

Don't Do This
  • Pick a fake failure ("I worked too hard")
  • Humble brag about a big achievement
  • Blame others for what went wrong
  • Skip the reflection and learning
Do This Instead
  • Choose a genuine failure with a real mistake
  • Take ownership of your part
  • Explain what you learned honestly
  • Show how you changed your behavior
8:30 - 10:00 | Failure questions are the hardest because candidates want to look good. But the question is testing self-awareness, not perfection. What not to do: don't pick a fake failure like "I worked too hard." Don't humble brag. Don't blame others. What to do: pick a genuine failure where you made a real mistake. Describe what happened honestly. Take responsibility. Then explain what you learned and how you changed. The formula is: real failure, ownership, reflection, change. That's what impresses interviewers.

Practice Tips

How to Practice Effectively

  • Practice out loud: Thinking through answers is not the same as speaking them.
  • Time yourself: Aim for 2 minutes per answer. Adjust for pacing.
  • Get feedback: Practice with a friend, family member, or coach.
  • Record yourself: Watch for filler words, nervous habits, and pacing.
10:00 - 11:15 | Four ways to practice effectively. Practice out loud, because thinking through an answer is not the same as speaking it. Time yourself and aim for 2 minutes per answer. Too short feels underprepared, too long loses the interviewer. Get feedback from a friend, family member, or coach. Ask them: was that clear? Did you understand what I did? Was it too long? And record yourself. Watch for filler words, nervous habits, and pacing. You'll notice things you can improve.
