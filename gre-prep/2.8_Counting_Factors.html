Factor Counting

The factor counting formula

If $n = p_1^a \times p_2^b \times p_3^c \times \ldots$ then Number of factors $= (a+1)(b+1)(c+1)\ldots$

Why It Works

Take $12 = 2^2 \times 3^1$

Each factor uses 0, 1, or 2 copies of 2 $\rightarrow$ 3 choices

Each factor uses 0 or 1 copy of 3 $\rightarrow$ 2 choices

Verification

Total factors $= 3 \times 2 = 6$

Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12

0:40-2:00 | Introduce the formula. If n has prime factorization p1^a x p2^b x p3^c, total factors = (a+1)(b+1)(c+1). Walk through why using 12 = 2^2 x 3^1. Each factor chooses how many copies of each prime to include. 3 choices for 2, times 2 choices for 3, gives 6 total factors.

Perfect Squares

The even exponents rule

A number is a perfect square if and only if ALL exponents in its prime factorization are even.

Perfect Square

$36 = 2^2 \times 3^2$

Exponents: 2 (even), 2 (even)

$\sqrt{36} = 2^1 \times 3^1 = 6$

Not a Square

$72 = 2^3 \times 3^2$

Exponent on 2 is 3 (odd)

3:30-4:30 | The rule: a number is a perfect square if and only if ALL exponents in its prime factorization are even. Show 36 = 2^2 x 3^2, both even, so perfect square. Show 72 = 2^3 x 3^2, the 3 is odd, so not a perfect square. To take the square root, divide each exponent by 2.

Example 3

Smallest k for a perfect square

Problem

What is the smallest positive integer $k$ such that $180k$ is a perfect square?

Solution

Step 1: Factorize: $180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5^1$

Step 2: Odd exponent: 5 has exponent 1

Step 3: Need one more 5: $k = 5$

Check: $900 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5^2$ $\checkmark$

4:30-5:30 | Example 3: Find smallest k so 180k is a perfect square. Step 1: factor 180 = 2^2 x 3^2 x 5^1. Step 2: the exponent on 5 is odd. Step 3: multiply by 5 to make it even. Check: 900 = 2^2 x 3^2 x 5^2, all even.

Perfect Squares

Odd number of factors

Perfect squares have an ODD number of factors. All other positive integers have an EVEN number.

Why

Factors pair up: $(1, 36), (2, 18), (3, 12), (4, 9)$

But the square root pairs with itself: $(6, 6)$ counts as one factor

GRE Trick

"Odd number of factors" = "perfect square." The GRE loves to disguise this.

5:30-6:30 | Key property: perfect squares have an odd number of factors because the square root pairs with itself. For 36: pairs are (1,36), (2,18), (3,12), (4,9), and (6,6). That last pair is only one factor. The GRE loves disguising this.

Example 4

Integers with odd factor counts

Problem

How many positive integers less than 100 have an odd number of factors?

Solution

Translate: How many perfect squares are less than 100?

$1^2 = 1, \; 2^2 = 4, \; 3^2 = 9, \;\ldots\; 9^2 = 81$

Answer: $9$

6:30-7:15 | Example 4. Translate the question: odd number of factors means perfect square. Perfect squares less than 100 are 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81. That is 9 integers.

Example 5

GRE factor counting problem

Problem Select all that apply

If $n = 2^a \times 3^b$, where $a$ and $b$ are positive integers, and $n$ has exactly 12 factors, which could be $n$?

☐ 48
☐ 72
☐ 96
☐ 108
☐ 144

Solution

Setup: $(a+1)(b+1) = 12$

$48 = 2^4 \times 3^1 \rightarrow (5)(2) = 10$ factors $\times$

$72 = 2^3 \times 3^2 \rightarrow (4)(3) = 12$ factors $\checkmark$

$96 = 2^5 \times 3^1 \rightarrow (6)(2) = 12$ factors $\checkmark$

$108 = 2^2 \times 3^3 \rightarrow (3)(4) = 12$ factors $\checkmark$

$144 = 2^4 \times 3^2 \rightarrow (5)(3) = 15$ factors $\times$

7:15-9:00 | Example 5: GRE select-all-that-apply problem. We need (a+1)(b+1) = 12. Check every answer choice: 48 = 2^4 x 3^1 gives (5)(2) = 10 factors, wrong. 72 = 2^3 x 3^2 gives (4)(3) = 12 factors, correct. 96 = 2^5 x 3^1 gives (6)(2) = 12 factors, correct. 108 = 2^2 x 3^3 gives (3)(4) = 12 factors, correct. 144 = 2^4 x 3^2 gives (5)(3) = 15 factors, wrong. Three answers are correct: 72, 96, and 108. On select-all questions, always check every choice.

Key Takeaways

Counting factors & perfect squares

  • Factor counting formula. For $p_1^a \times p_2^b \times p_3^c$, total factors $= (a+1)(b+1)(c+1)$
  • Always prime factorize first. The formula requires the prime factorization, not the number itself
  • Perfect square test. All exponents in the prime factorization must be even
  • Making a perfect square. Multiply by primes needed to make every exponent even
  • Odd number of factors. Only perfect squares have an odd factor count
9:00-10:00 | Summary: The factor counting formula multiplies (exponent+1) for each prime. Perfect squares have all even exponents. Only perfect squares have an odd number of factors. Always prime factorize first. [Leland+ Tip] This formula appears frequently in QC problems. For example, comparing the number of factors of 84 vs 90: both have 12 factors despite 90 being larger. The GRE exploits the assumption that larger numbers have more factors. Always prime factorize to verify.
