Financial Aid Systems

Two Fundamentally Different Models

Merit-Based

Awarded based on application strength. No separate application needed. Amount reflects how much the school wants you. Used by most T15 schools.

Need-Based

Determined by financial need: income, assets, family obligations. Application quality has no bearing on the amount. Used by HBS and Stanford GSB.

Some schools like Columbia and Yale SOM offer both, which can result in a larger total package.

1:00 - 2:30 | There are two fundamentally different systems. Merit-based aid is awarded based on your application strength. You don't apply separately. The amount reflects how much the school wants you. Most T15 schools use this. Need-based aid is determined entirely by your finances. Your application quality has no bearing on the amount. Harvard and Stanford use this model, where roughly 50% of students receive fellowships. Some schools like Columbia and Yale offer both types.

Part 1

The Negotiation

When it works, how it works, and what to say

2:30 - 2:35 | Now let's talk about when and how to negotiate.

Negotiation Success Factors

What Makes It Likely to Work

01

Peer-School Offer

A competing offer from a similarly ranked school is the single most important factor.

02

Genuine Peer Status

The competing school must be a real peer. A full ride from a school ranked 30th won't move an M7.

03

Existing Award

Increasing an existing scholarship is far easier than getting one created from scratch.

2:35 - 4:00 | Three factors make negotiation likely to succeed. First, you need a competing offer from a peer-ranked school. This is the single most important factor. If Booth offers you $40K and Kellogg offers $70K, Booth has a reason to reconsider. Second, the competing school needs to be a genuine peer. Schools understand their competitive set and respond to peer pressure, not offers from schools they don't consider competitors. Third, increasing an existing award is much easier than creating a new one. If you weren't offered money at admission, it's rarely given after the fact.

Framing Your Request

Five Rules for the Email

  • Lead with enthusiasm: Express genuine appreciation and excitement about the program.
  • Frame as a personal decision: Share that it's a tough choice, not a threat or leverage play.
  • Be transparent on amount: State the competing offer clearly; they'll likely ask anyway.
  • Stay vague on the school: Say "another top program" unless the peer is clearly higher-ranked.
4:00 - 5:45 | Five rules for your negotiation email. Lead with genuine enthusiasm. Frame it as a tough personal decision, not leverage. Be transparent about the competing offer's amount, they'll ask for documentation anyway. Stay vague about which school, say "another top program" unless the competing school is clearly higher-ranked, in which case naming it can strengthen your position. And keep the whole email under 200 words. Brief, honest, and clear.

Sample Request

Keep It Under 200 Words

Express excitement about the program, share that you received a generous offer from another top program, explain the financial gap is significant for your family, and ask the committee to review your package.

Attach documentation. Don't threaten, don't overexplain, and don't name the competing school unless it clearly strengthens your position.

5:45 - 7:00 | Your email should express excitement about the program, share that you received a generous offer from another top program, explain that the financial gap is significant for your family, and ask the committee to review your package. Attach documentation. Don't threaten. Don't overexplain. Most schools respond within one to two weeks. The outcome will be an increased offer, a polite decline, or a request for more information. If they decline, accept it gracefully. Pushing harder rarely works and can damage the relationship.

Common Mistakes

Negotiation Pitfalls to Avoid

Mistake Why It Hurts You
Bluffing about offers Schools talk to each other. Dishonesty will come back to hurt you.
Being aggressive or demanding The people reading your email will be your administrators for two years.
Using the same offer at multiple schools Be genuine about your interest in each school you contact.
Assuming it will work Prepare for "no" and know your decision in that scenario.
7:00 - 8:15 | Four pitfalls to avoid. Don't bluff about offers, schools talk to each other and dishonesty will come back to hurt you. Don't be aggressive, these people will be your administrators. Don't use the same offer at multiple schools without genuine interest in each. And don't assume it will work. Roughly 50% of requests with a competing peer offer result in some increase, but that means half don't. Prepare for no and know what your decision will be.

Part 2

The Full Financial Picture

Evaluating total cost, loans, and ROI

8:15 - 8:20 | Whether or not your negotiation succeeds, you need to evaluate the full financial picture before deciding.

Total Cost of Attendance

Compare the Full Picture, Not Just Tuition

  • Tuition and fees: Compare the 2-year total after scholarships, not sticker price.
  • Living expenses: Costs vary enormously by location. Factor in 2 years of rent and cost of living.
  • Loan burden: If total loans are less than expected first-year salary, you're in manageable territory.
  • Career ROI: Compare median salaries at each school for your target industry.
8:20 - 10:00 | Don't compare tuition numbers. Compare total cost of attendance. That means tuition and fees for 2 years after scholarships, living expenses which vary enormously by location, your loan burden (a good rule of thumb is that total loans below your expected first-year salary are manageable), and career ROI. Compare median salaries in your target industry at each school. A school that costs $40K more but places 30% of graduates in your target industry at higher salary may have better ROI. But if both schools lead to similar outcomes, the cheaper option is the smarter financial decision.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Merit-based aid is potentially negotiable; need-based aid (HBS, Stanford) is not
  • You need a competing peer-school offer as the strongest basis
  • Frame it as a tough personal decision, not leverage
  • Keep the email under 200 words and don't overexplain
  • Compare total cost of attendance, not just tuition
  • Accept "no" gracefully and don't damage relationships
10:00 - 10:45 | Key takeaways. Merit-based aid is negotiable. Need-based aid at HBS and Stanford is not. You need a competing peer-school offer as your strongest basis. Frame it as a tough personal decision, not leverage. Keep the email under 200 words. Compare total cost of attendance, not just tuition. And if the answer is no, accept it gracefully. Financial aid negotiation isn't about being clever. It's about being honest about your situation and asking a reasonable question.
