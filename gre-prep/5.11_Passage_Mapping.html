Passage Mapping
in 30 Seconds

0:00 - 0:05 | Title slide. This video teaches a fast passage-mapping technique for GRE Reading Comprehension.

The Skill

Map it, don't memorize it

Fast readers don't try to memorize the passage. They build a quick mental outline of what each paragraph does so they can navigate back when questions ask.

What a Map Gives You
  • What the passage is about
  • How it's structured
  • Where to find specific information
What You Don't Need
  • Every detail memorized
  • Exact wording recalled
  • Specific data points retained
0:05 - 1:00 | Set up the concept. A passage map is a quick mental outline. Think of it like scanning a map before a road trip. You don't memorize every turn, you get the general route.

The Process

Your 30-second map

1

First Paragraph

Read carefully. Find the main idea and the author's angle. (10 sec)

→
2

First Sentences

Skim the first sentence of each remaining paragraph. (15 sec)

→
3

Final Paragraph

Read the conclusion for the author's final position. (5 sec)

Result

30 seconds, and you have a roadmap. You know the topic, the structure, and where to look for details.

1:00 - 3:00 | Walk through the three steps. Step 1: Read the opening paragraph carefully for the main idea. Step 2: Skim opening sentences of middle paragraphs for function. Step 3: Read the conclusion for the author's final stance.

Labels

Paragraph function tags

Tag What It Does Signal Words
Intro/Setup Introduces topic or problem "Scholars have long debated..."
Background Provides context or history "Historically...", "Traditionally..."
Evidence Supports a claim with data "For instance...", "Studies show..."
Counter Presents an opposing view "However...", "Critics argue..."
Conclusion Summarizes or states a position "Thus...", "Ultimately..."
3:00 - 5:00 | Teach the labeling system. As students map, they should tag each paragraph with a quick mental label. Walk through each tag and its signal words. Emphasize that these tags make function questions trivially easy.

Example

A sample passage map

A 4-paragraph passage about a scientific debate:

P1

Intro

Presents the debate about X

P2

View A

Some scholars think X means Y

P3

Counter

Others argue X means Z

P4

Author's Take

Author sides with View A because...

Part 2

What to Notice While Mapping

6:00 - 6:05 | Section divider. Now cover the four things to pay attention to while building a map.

Observation

4 things to track

Author's Tone

Neutral, positive, negative, or skeptical? Words like "unfortunately" or "importantly" reveal tone.

Structure Type

Compare/contrast, problem/solution, chronological, or argument with supporting evidence.

Transition Words

"However" signals a shift. "Moreover" continues. "Therefore" concludes. "Although" concedes.

Key Players

Who or what is discussed? Are there multiple perspectives or just one?

6:05 - 7:30 | Cover the four observation categories. Tone is revealed through evaluative language. Structure type helps predict what paragraphs will do. Transition words signal shifts. Key players help you track perspectives.

Application

Using your map for questions

Main Idea Questions

Your P1 and conclusion notes have the answer.

Detail Questions

Paragraph labels tell you where to look.

Function Questions

Labels directly answer "What is the purpose of P3?"

Inference Questions

Locate the paragraph, read closely for supported conclusions.

Time Saver

The map saves time because you never re-read the whole passage for every question.

7:30 - 9:00 | Show how the map applies to each question type. Main idea comes from the overview. Detail questions use paragraph labels. Function questions are answered by the labels themselves. Inference questions narrow down where to read closely.

Key Takeaways

Passage mapping in 30 seconds

  • Read the first paragraph carefully - find the main idea and the author's angle in the opening sentences
  • Skim first sentences of middle paragraphs - tag each one with its function: background, evidence, counter, or conclusion
  • Read the final paragraph's conclusion - identify the author's ultimate position or takeaway
  • Track tone, structure, transitions, and players - these four observations make every question type easier
  • Use your map to navigate, not recall - know WHERE information lives so you never re-read the entire passage
9:00 - 10:00 | Summary slide. Reinforce: the passage map takes 30 seconds and saves minutes on questions. Label paragraphs, track tone and transitions, and navigate back to the right section for each question.
