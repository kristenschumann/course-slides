Why Goals Matter

What Admissions Committees Assess

Fit

Does this person know what they want, and does our program help them get there?

Trajectory

Is this person thinking clearly about their future? Vague goals suggest vague thinking.

Contribution

What will this person bring to our community from day one?

1:00 - 2:15 | Admissions committees are trying to answer one question: will this person succeed? Your career goals help them assess three things. First, fit: does this person know what they want, and does our program help them get there? If you want to break into tech and you are applying to a school known for finance, that is a mismatch. Second, trajectory: is this person thinking clearly about their future? Vague goals suggest vague thinking. Third, contribution: what will this person bring to the community? Clear direction means you can contribute to recruiting events, clubs, and classroom discussions from day one.

Short-Term Goal

What Makes a Strong Post-MBA Goal

  • Specific: Name the industry, function, and type of company
  • Realistic: Achievable given your background plus an MBA
  • Logical: Connects to your past experiences and recurring themes
  • Exciting: Shows real ambition and the impact you want to have
2:15 - 3:30 | Your short-term goal is what you plan to do in the first 3 to 5 years after graduation. Four qualities make it strong. Specific: not "I want to work in consulting" but "I want to join a strategy consulting firm like Bain or McKinsey, focusing on healthcare clients." Realistic: the goal needs to be achievable given your background plus the MBA. Logical: it should connect to your past experiences with a clear thread. And exciting: show ambition, show the impact you want to have.

Short-Term Goal

A Formula That Works

"After my MBA, I plan to join [type of company] in [function/role], focusing on [specific area], where I can leverage my experience in [relevant background] while developing [new skills the MBA provides]."

This structure shows specificity, connection to your past, and a clear reason why you need the MBA.

3:30 - 4:30 | Here is a formula that works well. "After my MBA, I plan to join [type of company] in [function or role], focusing on [specific area], where I can leverage my experience in [relevant background] while developing [new skills the MBA provides]." This structure shows specificity, connection to your past, and a clear reason why you need the MBA. You do not have to use these exact words, but hit all of these elements.

Long-Term Goal

Your 10+ Year Vision

Visionary

Dream bigger. Lead a division, launch a startup, or shape policy at a government agency.

Connected

A logical extension of your short-term goal. The path from one to the other should make sense.

Impactful

Show you want to make a difference, not just advance your own career.

Authentic

Goals you genuinely care about come through in writing and interviews. Borrowed goals do not.

4:30 - 5:30 | Your long-term goal is the 10+ year vision. This is where you show big-picture thinking. It should be visionary, meaning you can dream bigger here. Maybe you want to lead a division, launch a startup, or shape policy. It should be connected, a logical extension of your short-term goal. If your short-term is healthcare consulting, your long-term might be COO of a health system. It should be impactful, showing you want to make a difference. And it should be authentic, because goals you genuinely care about come through in your writing and interviews. Borrowed goals never work.

5:30 - 7:00 | Here is a five-step process for developing your goals. Step 1, start with your values. What matters to you? What problems do you care about? Step 2, research the possibilities. Look at LinkedIn profiles of people 5 and 10 years ahead of you. Search for your target role, filter by education, and notice patterns. Step 3, identify the thread. What recurring themes do you see in your work and interests? Step 4, test for fit. Is an MBA the best path? Does this school specifically help? And Step 5, be willing to evolve. Your goals do not have to be perfect. They have to be thoughtful. Many students change their plans during business school, and that is fine.

Common Misconceptions

What Applicants Get Wrong

Misconception Reality
"My goals must be set in stone" Schools know plans change. They want thoughtful direction, not predictions.
"Say what they want to hear" Inauthentic goals show in essays and interviews. Write what you actually care about.
"More specific is always better" "Head of Product at Spotify" is too narrow. "Product at a consumer tech company" is right.
"I need unique goals" Consulting and banking are fine. What differentiates you is why and what you bring.
"Traditional goals are always safe" If your target industry is being reshaped by AI, acknowledge the transformation.
7:00 - 8:15 | Let us address some common misconceptions. First, your goals do not have to be set in stone. Schools know plans change. They want to see that you can articulate a direction. Second, do not say what you think they want to hear. If you are not genuinely interested, it shows. Third, more specific is not always better. There is a balance between "Head of Product at Spotify" and "I want to work in tech." Fourth, you do not need unique goals. Many people want consulting or banking, and that is fine. What differentiates you is why you want that path. Fifth, traditional career goals are not always safe. If your target industry is being reshaped by AI, acknowledging that transformation shows you are thinking forward.

Red Flags

Goals That Raise Concerns

  • No MBA needed: If you can achieve objectives without it, why invest $100K-$300K?
  • Disconnected from background: Random pivots without explanation are not compelling.
  • Too vague: "I want to be a leader" says nothing specific.
  • Unrealistically specific: "CEO of Apple by 2035" is not credible.
  • Only about money: "Maximize earning potential" may be true, but do not lead with it.
8:15 - 9:15 | Here are goals that raise concerns. Goals that do not require an MBA. If you can achieve your objectives without business school, why invest two years and $100K to $300K in tuition? Frame the MBA as a necessary accelerant. Goals disconnected from your background. Random pivots without explanation are not compelling. Goals that are too vague. "I want to be a leader" says nothing. Goals that are unrealistically specific. "CEO of Apple by 2035" is not credible. And goals that are only about money. "Maximize earning potential" may be true, but it is not what you should lead with.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Specificity matters: name the industry, function, and type of company
  • Connection matters: your goals should link to your past
  • Credibility matters: the path from here to there should make sense
  • Authenticity matters: you have to actually care about this direction
  • Evolution is okay: your goals can and probably will change
  • The goal is to demonstrate thoughtful direction, not a perfect 10-year plan
9:15 - 10:00 | Five things to remember. Specificity matters: name the industry, function, and type of company. Connection matters: your goals should link to your past. Credibility matters: the path from here to there should make sense. Authenticity matters: you have to actually care about this direction. And evolution is okay: your goals can and probably will change. The goal is not to have a perfect 10-year plan. It is to demonstrate that you think seriously about your future and know how to chart a course.
