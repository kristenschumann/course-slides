Part 1

Where the Money Comes From

LP-GP structure and capital sources

[1:00] Transition into LP/GP structure.

Fund Structure

LPs Provide Capital, GPs Deploy It

Limited Partners (LPs)

Institutional investors who commit capital to a venture fund. They are passive -- they do not make investment decisions.

General Partners (GPs)

The VC firm itself. GPs deploy LP capital into startups. They are intermediaries between institutional capital and early-stage companies.

GPs commit 1-3% of the fund alongside LPs ("skin in the game").

[1:00-1:45] VCs are not investing their own money -- they invest other people's money. That single fact explains almost everything about how the industry operates. LPs commit capital; GPs deploy it. GPs themselves put in 1-3% of the fund -- meaningful personal commitment, but a small fraction of the total. [Click] LPs. [Click] GPs.

Capital Sources

Types of Limited Partners

LP Type Examples Typical VC Allocation
University Endowments Yale, Stanford, MIT, Harvard 5-16%
Foundations Ford Foundation, Gates Foundation 3-8%
Pension Funds CalPERS, CalSTRS 1-5%
Family Offices HNW family investment vehicles 5-15%
Sovereign Wealth Funds GIC (Singapore), Mubadala (Abu Dhabi) 2-5%
Insurance Companies MetLife, Prudential 1-3%
Fund-of-Funds HarbourVest, Hamilton Lane 100%
[1:45-2:30] Most LPs allocate 5% or less to VC. Endowments pioneered heavy VC allocation -- the Yale Endowment under David Swensen allocated ~16% to VC and generated 18%+ annualized returns over multiple decades. Every other endowment tried to copy it; most couldn't because they lacked access to top-tier funds. Fund-of-funds allocate 100% because that's their entire strategy.

Return Expectations

Capital Calls and Target Returns

Target Returns

LPs expect 12-15% net annualized returns, roughly 500-800 basis points above the S&P 500. That premium compensates for illiquidity, risk, and a 10+ year time horizon.

Capital Calls

LPs don't write a check on day one. GPs issue drawdown notices as investments close. A $10M commitment is called over the first several years, just in time. Cash management is a constant LP concern.

[2:30-3:30] LPs lock up capital for 10+ years and expect 12-15% net annualized returns for that illiquidity premium. Key detail: capital is not deployed all at once. GPs issue capital calls -- drawdown notices -- over the first several years. If an LP has committed $50M across six funds and three issue capital calls in the same quarter, they need that cash ready.
Part 2

The 2 and 20 Model

How GPs get compensated

[3:30] Transition into GP compensation.

GP Compensation

Management Fees Keep the Lights On

2% of Committed Capital

Annual fee paid to the GP covering salaries, office space, travel, legal, and accounting. On a $100M fund: $2M/year.

Fees Step Down Over Time

During the investment period (first ~5 years), fees are on committed capital. After that, fees reduce ~25 bps/year or switch to invested capital. Total fees over 10 years on a $100M fund: ~$15-18M.

[3:30-4:30] Two components of VC compensation. Management fee is the operational engine -- 2% of committed capital annually. [Click] $100M fund = $2M/year. [Click] Fees step down in the back half of the fund's life as workload shifts from new investments to managing/exiting. Over 10 years, total fees run $15-18M -- that's a lot of capital going to overhead before a dollar returns to LPs. Which is why LPs care so much about carry.

The Incentive

Carry Is Where GPs Get Rich

Carried interest is the GP's share of the profits -- 20% of net profits after LPs have received their capital back.

Management fees keep the lights on. Carry is what makes venture capital venture capital. The GP only gets rich if the LPs get rich first.

[4:30-5:00] This is the core incentive alignment of the entire model. Carry = 20% of net profits, but only after LPs have gotten their money back. That alignment -- GP only wins when LP wins -- is the backbone of the industry.

Worked Example

A $100M Fund That Returns 3x

Component Amount
Fund Size (Committed Capital) $100M
Total Management Fees (10 yrs) ~$17M
Capital Deployed into Startups ~$83M
Total Fund Returns (Exit Proceeds) $300M
Return of LP Capital $100M
Profit $200M
GP Carry (20% of Profit) $40M
LP Share of Profit $160M
Total LP Distributions $260M
[5:00-5:45] Walk through the math. $100M fund, ~$17M goes to management fees, leaving $83M deployed. If exits generate $300M total -- [Click] -- LPs get their $100M back first, then 80% of the $200M profit = $160M. Total LP distributions: $260M. GPs earn $40M in carry on top of $17M in fees. That's the model working well.

Key Provisions

Nuances of Carried Interest

Premium Carry

Top-performing GPs negotiate 25-30% carry on subsequent funds. Strong track record = LPs happily pay more because they're still earning exceptional returns.

Hurdle Rates

Common in PE (8% preferred return), but rare in VC. VC returns are lumpier, take longer to materialize, and power law dynamics make hurdles less practical.

[5:45-6:20] Two important carry nuances. [Click] Premium carry -- if you returned 5x on Fund I and 4x on Fund II, LPs will happily pay 25% on Fund III. [Click] Hurdle rates are standard in PE (8% pref) but rare in VC because returns are lumpy and power-law driven.

Key Provisions

Clawbacks and Waterfall Structures

Clawback Provisions

If early exits look great but the fund ultimately underperforms, the GP must return excess carry. Ensures carry is calculated on whole-fund performance, not cherry-picked wins.

Waterfall Structures

European (whole-fund): GP receives carry only after all LP capital is returned. American (deal-by-deal): GP takes carry on individual profitable exits. American is more common in US VC but comes with stronger clawbacks.

[6:20-7:00] Two more carry nuances. [Click] Clawback provisions protect LPs -- GP must return excess carry if early wins don't hold up across the whole fund. [Click] Two waterfall structures: European = whole-fund carry, American = deal-by-deal. American is more GP-friendly and more common in US venture.
Part 3

The Power Law

The most important concept in venture capital

[7:00] Transition into the most important concept in the module.

Core Concept

Not a Bell Curve -- A Power Law

Normal Distribution

Most outcomes cluster around the average. A few slightly better, a few slightly worse. Predictable. (Think: diversified bond portfolio.)

Power Law Distribution

The vast majority of investments return very little or nothing. A tiny handful produce absolutely enormous, fund-defining outcomes. (Think: venture capital.)

[7:00-7:45] If you take one idea from this entire module, make it this. VC returns follow a power law distribution -- not a bell curve. [Click] Normal distribution: outcomes cluster around the average. [Click] Power law: most investments return little or nothing, but a tiny handful produce enormous outcomes. This changes everything about how VCs think and behave.

Portfolio Reality

What a Typical Fund Looks Like

Outcome # of Companies Return Multiple What It Means
Total losses 6-8 0x Complete loss of invested capital
Partial returns 4-6 0.5-1x Essentially break-even
Moderate winners 3-5 2-5x Solid but not fund-makers
Strong winners 1-2 10-20x Meaningful fund contributors
Grand slam 0-1 50-100x+ Single investment defines the fund
[7:45-8:30] Here's what a typical 20-company portfolio actually looks like. [Click] 6-8 companies are total losses. [Click] Another 4-6 essentially break even. [Click] 3-5 are moderate winners -- solid but not fund-makers. [Click] 1-2 strong winners that meaningfully contribute. [Click] And 0-1 grand slams that define the entire fund. The top one or two investments generate more return than everything else combined.

Key Insight

Slugging Percentage, Not Batting Average

Peter Thiel + Facebook

$500K invested in 2004. Stake eventually worth $1.1 billion -- a 2,200x return. Founders Fund could have lost money on every other investment and still returned an extraordinary result.

The Implication

VCs don't optimize for the highest percentage of successful investments. They optimize for finding the one or two companies that return 50x or 100x. A 10% chance at $10B beats a 90% chance at $100M.

[8:30-9:15] This is called the Babe Ruth effect -- Ruth led the league in strikeouts AND home runs. The home runs more than compensated. [Click] The most famous example: Peter Thiel invested $500K in Facebook in 2004, eventually worth $1.1B -- 2,200x. One bet. [Click] This means VCs would rather invest in a company with a 10% chance of being worth $10B than a 90% chance at $100M. Expected value is everything in a power law world.

Fund Lifecycle

The J-Curve

Fund returns follow a J-shaped curve: negative in the early years as fees are charged and bad investments surface, then rising sharply as winners emerge and exits materialize. We'll visualize this in detail in Lesson 1.3.

  • Years 1-3: Fund shows negative returns -- management fees, capital deployment, and early failures
  • Years 4-10: Winners take time to emerge, but breakout companies can transform the portfolio
[9:15-10:00] The J-curve is a direct consequence of the power law. In years 1-3, fund returns look terrible -- fees are charged, capital is deployed, and the only news is bad news. Bad investments reveal themselves fast. "Lemons ripen early" is the industry saying. But by year 4-6, the winners start to emerge. The portfolio that looked like a graveyard transforms when two companies break out and return more than the entire fund. We'll cover the J-curve in much more visual detail in Lesson 1.3.
Part 4

Performance Metrics

How LPs evaluate fund performance

[10:00] Transition into fund evaluation metrics.

Fund Evaluation

Metrics LPs Use to Judge Funds

Metric What It Measures "Good" Benchmark
DPI Cash returned to LPs / capital called > 1.0x
TVPI (Distributions + unrealized value) / capital called > 2.0x
Net IRR Annualized return to LPs, net of fees and carry > 15%
Gross MOIC Gross multiple on capital deployed into deals > 3.0x
[10:00-10:30] Four key metrics. DPI: actual cash returned divided by capital called. TVPI: realized distributions plus unrealized portfolio value divided by capital called. Net IRR: annualized rate of return after fees and carry. Gross MOIC: raw multiple on deployed capital before fees.

Most Important Metric

DPI Is the Ultimate Scorecard

"Marks are opinions. DPI is fact."

A DPI of 1.5x means for every dollar an LP put in, they've received $1.50 back in their bank account. Real cash, not paper markups. Funds with a 4x TVPI on paper can ultimately return just 1.2x in actual cash.

[10:30-11:00] DPI is the only metric that doesn't lie. Paper valuations can be wildly optimistic -- a company marked at $500M today might IPO at $2B or go to zero. DPI measures what actually landed in LP bank accounts. When evaluating funds or discussing them in interviews, anchor on cash returned, not unrealized markups.

Supporting Metrics

Context Around Each Metric

TVPI

Includes realized returns + estimated value of companies still private. Captures unrealized upside but can be misleading. LPs view with healthy skepticism.

Net IRR

The annualized return after all fees and carry. Gross IRR looks better but isn't what LPs take home. LPs care about net.

Gross MOIC

Raw multiple on capital deployed. If $80M invested across 20 companies is now worth $240M, MOIC = 3.0x. Useful for evaluating investment selection skill.

[11:00-11:20] Quick context on each. [Click] TVPI includes unrealized value -- useful but can be misleading. [Click] Net IRR is what matters to LPs. Always specify net vs. gross. [Click] Gross MOIC isolates the GP's investment selection skill before fees.

Critical Context

Always Compare by Vintage Year

Never evaluate a fund in isolation. Compare to other funds raised in the same year -- the same "vintage."

A 2.5x TVPI might be outstanding for a 2019 vintage but mediocre for a 2012 vintage. Market conditions and exit environments vary enormously. Cambridge Associates and PitchBook publish the industry-standard vintage year benchmarks.

[11:20-11:30] Final detail on metrics: vintage year benchmarking. A 2.5x TVPI means nothing without context -- you compare against all funds raised the same year. Cambridge Associates and PitchBook are the standard sources.

Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • VCs invest other people's money. That single fact explains 90% of their behavior. Know who the LPs are and what they expect.
  • The GP only gets rich if LPs get rich first. Management fees keep the lights on; carry is the real payoff, and it only materializes when the fund performs.
  • The power law is the operating principle of VC. VCs need home runs, not base hits. Every investment decision comes back to this.
  • DPI separates real performance from paper performance. Anchor on cash returned, not unrealized markups.
  • Connect economics to behavior. "Why do VCs care about market size?" Because the power law means you need companies that can return the entire fund -- a $50M outcome in a $500M fund doesn't move the needle. That's the answer that gets you hired.
[11:30-12:00] Five takeaways. [Click] VCs invest OPM -- other people's money. [Click] GP-LP alignment: GP only wins when LP wins. [Click] The power law governs everything -- home runs, not base hits. [Click] DPI is the metric that doesn't lie. [Click] When someone asks "Why do VCs care about market size?" -- it's because the power law means you need companies that can return the entire fund. A $50M exit in a $500M fund doesn't move the needle. That's the interview answer that gets you hired.
