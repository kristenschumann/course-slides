Section 1

The Five Core Activities

Where all the time goes

1:00 | Transition. Every junior VC's week revolves around five core activities. The proportions shift by firm, seniority, and time of year -- but these five buckets capture where all the time goes.

Core Activity 1

Sourcing New Deals

25-30% of Your Time

Finding new companies to potentially invest in -- attending demo days, fielding warm referrals, cold outreach, and reviewing inbound pitches.

Key Stat: 26% from Cold Inbound

HBS research shows only ~26% of funded deals come from cold inbound. The vast majority come through warm referrals and active prospecting.

1:00-1:45 | Click 1: Sourcing is 25-30% of time. Activities include startup events, demo days, warm referrals from portfolio founders and other VCs, cold outreach via Twitter/Product Hunt/research databases, and reviewing inbound pitches (hundreds to thousands per year at a well-known firm). Click 2: HBS stat -- only 26% of funded deals come from cold inbound. Network building is a core part of the job, not a nice-to-have. Also: most inbound decks get roughly 6 minutes of screening time. You develop rapid pattern-matching for what's worth 30 minutes.

Core Activity 2

Deal Evaluation and Diligence

25-30% of Your Time

Starts with an initial screen -- read the deck, check team background, market size, traction metrics. Then a 30-minute founder call. Most deals die here by design.

Deep Diligence

Customer reference calls (5-15 per deal), market sizing, competitive analysis, financial modeling, and investment memos that go before the partnership for a vote.

Five Screening Dimensions: Team, Market, Product, Traction, Deal Terms -- covered in detail in Module 3.

1:45-2:30 | Click 1: Deal evaluation is another 25-30% of time. The initial screen is fast -- deck, team, market, traction. If it passes the filter, a 30-min founder call. Most deals die here. Click 2: Surviving deals enter deep diligence. Customer reference calls (5-15 customers), market sizing, competitive analysis, financial modeling on unit economics, and investment memos -- detailed written cases for and against investing. Five screening dimensions: team, market, product, traction, deal terms (covered in Module 3).

Core Activity 3

Supporting Portfolio Companies

  • Hiring support -- sourcing candidates and making introductions to the firm's talent network
  • Board meeting prep -- preparing materials and taking detailed notes during meetings
  • KPI monitoring -- reviewing quarterly updates and dashboards to flag concerning trends early
  • Strategic introductions -- connecting founders with potential customers or domain experts
2:30-3:00 | 15-20% of time. VCs are expected to be value-add investors. As an analyst/associate, you execute on that promise. Click 1: Hiring support. Click 2: Board meeting prep and note-taking. Click 3: KPI monitoring across portfolio. Click 4: Strategic introductions -- e.g., connecting a founder with an enterprise customer or a go-to-market expert.

Core Activities 4 & 5

Meetings and Market Research

Internal Meetings (10-15%)

Monday partnership meeting to review deal pipeline and make investment decisions. Weekly team syncs, sector deep-dive sessions, and LP reporting work. At firms like Benchmark, the Monday meeting is practically sacred.

Market Research (10-15%)

Reading industry reports, following founders on X, tracking public market comps, and building sector maps and competitive landscapes. This is where you develop genuine expertise that makes you indispensable.

3:00-3:30 | Click 1: Internal meetings are 10-15% of time. The Monday partnership meeting is the most important event of the week -- full team reviews deal pipeline, debates active opportunities, makes investment decisions. Also weekly syncs, sector deep-dives, and LP quarterly reporting. Click 2: Market research is another 10-15%. The analyst who develops genuine expertise in a vertical -- e.g., vertical SaaS for construction, AI-native dev tools, climate tech supply chains -- becomes the person partners turn to before writing a $10M check.

Section 2

Monday Through Friday

A day-by-day walkthrough at a Series A/B-focused firm

3:30 | Transition. Now the theory becomes concrete -- a day-by-day walkthrough at a Series A/B-focused firm showing how these activities weave together.

Monday

Pipeline and Partnerships

  • 9:00 AM -- Partnership Meeting. The whole investment team gathers to review the deal pipeline, debate active deals, and make investment decisions. At Benchmark, partners present deals they're championing and the team votes.
  • 11:00 AM -- Inbound Deck Review. Screen 5-8 new pitch decks. Quick assessments: thesis fit, market size, traction. Pass along 1-2 that warrant a closer look.
  • 2:00 PM -- Founder Intro Call. A warm referral from a portfolio CEO. 30-minute call to learn the company, ask probing questions, assess whether to bring to the broader team.
  • 4:00 PM -- Competitive Landscape. Build a competitive landscape slide for a deal in diligence -- mapping 15-20 companies, their positioning, funding history, and relative strengths.
3:30-4:15 | Click 1: Monday starts with the partnership meeting -- marquee event of the week. Full team reviews pipeline, debates active deals, makes investment decisions. Click 2: After the meeting, screen 5-8 inbound decks from the weekend. Quick filter: thesis fit, market size, traction. Click 3: Afternoon founder intro call, typically from a warm referral. Active listening is the key skill -- understand the founder's vision, not pitch them on the firm. Click 4: End of day, competitive landscape work for a deal moving into deeper diligence.

Tuesday

Diligence and Networking

Morning

Diligence Deep Dive

9:00 AM -- Customer Reference Calls. Speak with 3-4 customers -- how they discovered the product, what they used before, how embedded it is, what they'd do if it disappeared.

11:30 AM -- Deal Memo Work. Write up notes from customer calls and update the investment memo. Clarity and intellectual honesty matter -- the memo argues both sides.

Afternoon

Network & Support

1:00 PM -- Peer Lunch. Meet a junior VC from another firm. Trade notes on the market, compare impressions on companies, and build the peer network that surfaces deals early.

3:00 PM -- Portfolio Support Call. Join a call with a Series A founder and partner to brainstorm enterprise go-to-market strategy and offer introductions.

4:15-5:00 | Click 1: Tuesday AM is customer reference calls. 3-4 calls, asking about edge cases, what broke, what they'd change -- that's where the real signal on product-market fit lives. Then deal memo writing. The memo goes before the partnership, so every sentence needs to be airtight. Click 2: Peer lunch with a VC from another firm. These relationships surface early deal flow and back-channel references. Then portfolio support -- brainstorming GTM strategy with a Series A company.

Wednesday

Board, Strategy, and Modeling

10:00 AM

Board Meeting

Attend a portfolio company board meeting alongside the partner. Take detailed notes, help prepare materials in advance, flag follow-up items.

1:00 PM

Sector Deep Dive

Weekly 90-minute team session focused on trends in AI infrastructure. Everyone comes prepared with readings, data points, and takes on market direction.

3:00 PM

Relationship Coffee

Meet a founder who isn't fundraising yet. Pure relationship building -- no pressure, no agenda. The best deals come from relationships built 12-18 months before a raise.

5:00 PM

Financial Modeling

Build a 3-year revenue model for a growth-stage deal. Revenue build, cohort analysis, unit economics.

5:00-5:45 | Click 1: Wednesday starts with a board meeting -- incredible learning opportunity to see how experienced operators and investors discuss strategy and metrics at the highest level. Click 2: Weekly sector deep dive -- 90 minutes, AI infrastructure focus. Collective intelligence sharpens individual conviction. Click 3: Relationship coffee with a pre-fundraising founder. No agenda. The best deals often come from relationships built 12-18 months before a round. Click 4: Financial modeling -- 3-year revenue model, cohort analysis, unit economics. Translating operating data into a view of the business at scale.

Thursday

Data Rooms and Demo Days

Morning: Diligence Marathon

9:00 AM -- Data Room Review. Deep dive into a data room for a deal nearing close. Review legal documents, parse the cap table, verify financial data against founder presentations. Meticulous, detail-oriented work.

Afternoon: Events & Research

1:00 PM -- Demo Day. See 8-12 companies present. Note the 2-3 most interesting and follow up with warm outreach. Hallway conversations with other investors are often the real value.

4:00 PM -- Market Map. A senior partner flagged a new sector. Build a brief market map: key players, positioning, venture activity, and whitespace opportunities.

5:45-6:25 | Click 1: Thursday AM -- diligence marathon. Data room review for a deal nearing close. Legal docs, cap table, verifying financials. Meticulous, detail-oriented work. Click 2: Afternoon demo day -- 8-12 companies presenting. Note the 2-3 interesting ones and follow up. The hallway conversations between presentations are often where the best signals come from. Then build a market map for a sector a senior partner flagged -- AI-powered drug discovery, vertical fintech, etc. Key players, positioning, venture activity, whitespace.

Friday

Review and Reflection

  • 9:00 AM -- LP Call Prep. Create materials for a quarterly update to the firm's limited partners -- performance summaries, portfolio highlights, and market commentary. The firm itself has investors too.
  • 11:00 AM -- Pipeline Review. Step back and review the week. Update the deal pipeline tracker with notes on every company evaluated. Prioritize follow-ups for next week.
  • 2:00 PM -- Mentorship. 1-on-1 with a senior partner. Ask questions, get feedback on deal analysis, discuss professional development.
  • 4:00 PM -- Reading Time. Industry newsletters, founder blog posts, research papers. StrictlyVC, The Generalist, Meritech's weekly. Staying current builds the intellectual breadth that makes a better investor.
6:25-7:00 | Click 1: Friday starts with LP call prep -- the part of VC most people forget. The firm has its own investors (LPs) and keeping them informed is non-negotiable. Click 2: Pipeline review -- the bookkeeping that keeps everything from falling through the cracks. Click 3: Mentorship -- 1-on-1 with a senior partner. Partners with 20+ years of experience have seen more failure modes than you can imagine. Click 4: Close the week with reading time. StrictlyVC, The Generalist, Meritech's weekly, founder posts, research papers.

Section 3

How It Differs by Fund Type

Seed, growth, and multi-stage firms

7:00 | Transition. The walkthrough describes one version of the job. The week feels fundamentally different depending on the type of fund. Picking the right fund type is as important as getting into VC in the first place.

Fund Comparison

Seed Fund: Speed and Network

Volume Is Everything

Evaluate 5-10 new companies per week. Seed investments can go from first meeting to term sheet in 1-2 weeks. Diligence is conviction-based, not spreadsheet-based.

Week Tilts Toward Sourcing

~40% of time on sourcing and founder relationships. Less financial modeling, more meetings, events, and developing market theses. Speed and judgment are the currencies.

7:00-7:40 | Examples: First Round Capital, Precursor Ventures. Click 1: At seed, volume is everything. 5-10 new companies per week. Decisions happen fast -- 1-2 weeks from first meeting to term sheet. No massive data rooms because companies are often pre-revenue. Conviction-based diligence -- betting on the founder and the market, not a spreadsheet. Click 2: Week tilts heavily toward sourcing (~40%) and relationship building. Much less time on modeling (~10%). If you agonize over decisions, seed investing will be painful.

Fund Comparison

Growth Fund: Analysis and Rigor

Fewer, Deeper Deals

Work on just 1-3 new deals per month. Each involves detailed financial modeling -- revenue builds, cohort analysis, public comps, unit economics. Diligence cycles last 4-8 weeks.

Week Tilts Toward Modeling

~35% of time in Excel and data rooms. More structured, PE-style evaluation. Preparing detailed investment committee memos. Analytical rigor is the currency.

7:40-8:20 | Examples: General Atlantic, Insight Partners. Click 1: Growth investing is almost a different profession. 1-3 deals per month. Deep financial analysis with revenue builds, cohort analysis, public comps. Diligence cycles are 4-8 weeks and far more structured -- resembles PE. Click 2: Week tilts toward modeling (~35%). Much less time sourcing (~15%). If you love forensic analysis on a company's numbers, growth equity is where you'll thrive.

Fund Comparison

Multi-Stage: Breadth and Versatility

A Mix of Both

Get exposure across stages, but your week depends on which team you're assigned to. A seed team at Sequoia operates very differently from the growth team.

Most Structured Training

Largest platform resources -- dedicated talent teams, marketing support, operating partners. Broadest exposure, but you may specialize earlier than expected.

8:20-8:45 | Examples: a16z, Sequoia, Bessemer. Click 1: Multi-stage gives a mix of both seed and growth styles, but it depends on team assignment. The partner focused on crypto has a different week than the partner focused on enterprise SaaS. Click 2: Most structured training programs, biggest platform resources. Great place to learn. But go in with eyes open about which team you want -- you may specialize earlier than expected.

Section 4

Is This Career Right for You?

An honest self-assessment

9:00 | Transition. Now that the realistic day-to-day is clear, the honest question: is this actually the right fit? VC attracts talented people, but it's genuinely not the right path for everyone. Knowing that early saves time and energy.

Career Fit

Signs VC Might Be Right

  • You love learning about new industries and technologies every week -- the breadth is energizing, not exhausting
  • You're genuinely excited by meeting founders and hearing their visions
  • Ambiguity doesn't paralyze you -- you make the best call with 60% of the information
  • You see patterns where others see noise -- spotting connections across companies, markets, and trends
  • You can write clearly and argue persuasively -- memos and partnership debates are how decisions get made
9:00-9:20 | Click through each sign. Click 1: Love of breadth -- new industries every week. Click 2: Genuine excitement about founders. Click 3: Comfort with ambiguity -- 60% information is the norm. Click 4: Pattern recognition across companies and markets -- core intellectual skill. Click 5: Clear writing and persuasive argumentation -- the medium for VC decisions.

Career Fit

Signs VC Might Not Fit

  • You want to build something yourself -- in VC, you're on the other side of the table from founders
  • You prefer depth over breadth -- you want to become the foremost expert on one domain
  • You're uncomfortable saying "no" 99% of the time -- most of the job is passing on companies
  • You want highly structured, predictable daily work -- this role is anything but
9:20-9:40 | Click through each sign. Click 1: If the itch is to create, consider being a founder or operator. Click 2: If deep specialization is the goal, a startup role may be better. Click 3: Saying no is the default mode -- you need to be at peace with that. Click 4: No two days look alike, as the walkthrough demonstrated. Being honest about these tradeoffs helps decide whether to pursue VC -- and also helps articulate a more authentic, credible "Why VC?" story in interviews.

Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • Five activities define the job. Sourcing, diligence, portfolio support, internal meetings, and market research -- in roughly that priority order.
  • Fund type changes everything. Seed is sourcing-heavy and conviction-driven. Growth is modeling-heavy and process-driven. Pick the one that matches how your brain works.
  • No two days look alike. If you need predictability, this isn't the career. If you thrive on variety, there's no better seat in finance.
  • Relationships and judgment are the foundation. Sourcing, diligence, portfolio support -- it all comes back to trust, accurate assessment, and clear communication.
  • Knowing the daily reality wins interviews. When a partner asks "Why VC?" -- specific answers about customer calls, Monday partnership debates, and early-stage sourcing get offers.
9:40-10:00 | Five takeaways, click through each. Click 1: Five core activities in priority order. Click 2: Fund type dramatically changes the balance -- seed vs growth vs multi-stage. Click 3: Embrace the unpredictability. Click 4: Relationships and judgment underpin everything. Spreadsheets matter, but people skills matter more. Click 5: The candidates who speak to the real day-to-day stand out immediately. This is what Module 5's "Why VC?" narrative building will draw on.
