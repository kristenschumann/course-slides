Core Formula

The d = r x t table

$D = R \times T$   |   $R = \frac{D}{T}$   |   $T = \frac{D}{R}$

Rate Time Distance
Object 1 $r_1$ $t_1$ $r_1 \times t_1$
Object 2 $r_2$ $t_2$ $r_2 \times t_2$

Fill in what you know, then set up an equation based on how the distances relate.

0:30-1:30 | The D = R x T table. Fill in rate, time, and distance for each object. Then ask: how do the distances relate? That gives you your equation. Coaching tip: distance and time are directly proportional (same speed). Time and speed are inversely proportional (same distance). For inverse relationships, the product stays constant: if you double speed, time halves.

Distance Relationships

Three scenarios

Same Direction

Chasing or catching up

$d_1 = d_2$

Opposite Directions

Moving apart

$d_1 + d_2 = \text{total}$

Round Trip

Going and returning

$d_{\text{go}} = d_{\text{return}}$

1:30-2:00 | Three scenarios. Same direction: distances are equal when one catches the other. Opposite directions: distances add to the total separation. Round trip: each leg has the same distance.

Part 1

Same Direction

Set distances equal

2:00-2:10 | Transition into same-direction problems.

Example 1

The chase problem

Problem

Leslie left at 50 mph. Lester left 1 hour later at 60 mph on the same route. How long for Lester to catch up?

Solution

Leslie: $50t$. Lester: $60(t-1)$

Set equal: $50t = 60(t-1)$

$50t = 60t - 60$, so $t = 6$ (Leslie's time)

Lester's time $= 6 - 1 =$ $5$ hours

2:10-3:30 | Example 1: Leslie at 50 mph for t hours, Lester at 60 mph for (t-1) hours. Same distance when he catches up. Solve 50t = 60(t-1) to get t = 6. Lester traveled 5 hours.

Part 2

Opposite Directions

Add the distances

3:30-3:40 | Transition into opposite-direction problems.

Example 2

Trains moving apart

Problem

Train east at 75 mph. Another leaves 2 hours later west at 35 mph. When are they 2,130 mi apart?

Solution

East: $75t$. West: $35(t-2)$

$75t + 35(t-2) = 2{,}130$

$110t - 70 = 2{,}130$, so $110t = 2{,}200$

Answer: $t = 20$ hours (since first train left)

3:40-4:50 | Example 2: opposite directions. Distances add up to 2,130 miles. East train travels 75t, west train travels 35(t-2). Add them and solve for t = 20 hours.

Part 3

Round Trips

Same distance, different rates

4:50-5:00 | Transition into round-trip problems.

Example 3

Average speed trap

Problem

Maggie drove 10 mi to town at 30 mph and returned at 20 mph. What was her average speed?

Solution

Going: $t = \frac{10}{30} = \frac{1}{3}$ hr. Return: $t = \frac{10}{20} = \frac{1}{2}$ hr

Total: $D = 20$ mi, $T = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{5}{6}$ hr

Avg speed $= \frac{20}{5/6} = 20 \times \frac{6}{5}$

Answer: $24$ mph (not $25$!)

Common Error

Average speed $\neq$ average of speeds. Use total distance $\div$ total time.

5:00-6:30 | Example 3: round trip average speed. Going takes 1/3 hr, return takes 1/2 hr. Total = 20 mi in 5/6 hr. Average speed = 24 mph, NOT 25. You spend more time at the slower speed.

Part 4

Relative Rate

Closing or widening the gap

6:30-6:40 | Transition into relative rate problems.

Relative Rate

Closing the gap

Same Direction

Relative rate $=$ faster $-$ slower

The gap shrinks by this amount each hour.

Opposite Directions

Relative rate $=$ rate$_1$ $+$ rate$_2$

The gap grows by this amount each hour.

Time to close the gap $= \frac{\text{Gap}}{\text{Relative Rate}}$

6:40-7:20 | Relative rate. Same direction: subtract rates to find how fast the gap closes. Opposite directions: add rates. Time to close = gap divided by relative rate.

Example 4

Police chase

Problem

Bonnie and Clyde drive at 60 mph. Police, 30 mi behind, chase at 80 mph. How long until caught?

Solution

Relative rate: $80 - 60 = 20$ mph

Time $= \frac{30}{20} = 1.5$ hours

Answer: $1.5$ hours

7:20-8:10 | Example 4: police chase. Same direction, so relative rate = 80 - 60 = 20 mph. Gap is 30 miles. Time = 30/20 = 1.5 hours.

Example 5

Distance before being caught

Problem

How far will Bonnie and Clyde travel before the police catch them?

Solution

They travel 1.5 hours at 60 mph

$D = 60 \times 1.5$

Answer: $90$ miles

8:10-8:40 | Example 5: follow-up. Bonnie and Clyde travel 1.5 hours at 60 mph = 90 miles before being caught.

Converging Objects

Heading toward each other

Problem

Two trains leave stations 300 miles apart, heading toward each other at 60 mph and 40 mph. When do they meet?

Solution

Step 1: Combined rate $= 60 + 40 = 100$ mph

Step 2: They close 100 miles every hour

Time $= \frac{300}{100}$

Answer: $3$ hours

8:40-9:30 | Converging objects. When two objects head toward each other, add their rates to get the combined rate of 100 mph. The 300-mile gap closes in 3 hours. This is the opposite-direction formula applied to meeting problems.

Round Trip Shortcut

The harmonic mean formula

Average speed for a round trip $= \frac{2ab}{a + b}$

Problem

Drive to work at 30 mph, return at 60 mph. What is your average speed for the round trip?

Solution

$\frac{2(30)(60)}{30 + 60} = \frac{3{,}600}{90}$

Answer: $40$ mph (not $45$!)

Common Error

The simple average $(30 + 60) \div 2 = 45$ is wrong. You spend more time at the slower speed, so the true average is always less than the midpoint.

9:30-10:30 | Harmonic mean shortcut. For round trips at different speeds, average speed = 2ab/(a+b). Here: 2(30)(60)/(90) = 40 mph. The simple average of 45 is always wrong because you spend more time at the slower speed. This formula saves time on the GRE.

Key Takeaways

D = r x t toolkit

  • Set up the table - Fill in Rate, Time, Distance for each object
  • Same direction - Distances are equal when one catches the other
  • Opposite directions - Distances add to the total separation
  • Round trip - Same distance, use total distance $\div$ total time for average speed
  • Converging objects - Add rates to find combined closing speed, then divide the gap
  • Harmonic mean - Round-trip average speed $= \frac{2ab}{a+b}$, never the simple average
  • Relative rate - Same direction: subtract. Opposite: add. Time $= \frac{\text{Gap}}{\text{Relative Rate}}$
8:40-10:00 | Summary: always set up the D = R x T table first. Identify the scenario (same direction, opposite, or round trip) to determine how distances relate. Use relative rate for gap problems.
