Test Structure

GMAT Focus vs. GRE at a Glance

GMAT Focus Edition

2 hours 15 minutes, 64 questions

  • Quantitative: 45 min, 21 questions
  • Verbal: 45 min, 23 questions
  • Data Insights: 45 min, 20 questions
  • Score range: 205-805

GRE

1 hour 58 minutes, 55 questions + essay

  • Analytical Writing: 1 essay, 30 min
  • Verbal: 2 sections, 27 questions
  • Quantitative: 2 sections, 27 questions
  • Score range: 130-170 per section
1:00 - 2:30 | Both tests have been updated recently. The GMAT Focus Edition is 2 hours and 15 minutes with three sections: Quantitative Reasoning at 45 minutes with 21 questions, Verbal Reasoning at 45 minutes with 23 questions, and Data Insights at 45 minutes with 20 questions. That is 64 total questions with scores from 205 to 805. The GRE is 1 hour and 58 minutes with Analytical Writing, two Verbal sections totaling 27 questions, and two Quant sections totaling 27 questions. That is 55 questions plus the essay. The GRE is about 17 minutes shorter overall, which matters if test endurance is a concern.

Key Differences

Five Factors That Affect Your Choice

Feature GMAT GRE
Calculator Data Insights only All Quant sections
Navigation Cannot go back (3 changes max) Skip and return freely
Adaptivity Within each section Section by section
Unique content Data Insights (multi-source analysis) Vocabulary-heavy Verbal
Writing No essay section 30-minute essay
2:30 - 4:30 | Five differences really matter. Calculator access: the GRE provides an on-screen calculator for the entire Quant section, while the GMAT only allows one for Data Insights. If mental math is not your strength, the GRE is more forgiving. Navigation: on the GRE you can skip and return to questions within each section. On the GMAT, once you answer, you move on with only 3 allowed changes per section. Adaptivity: the GRE adapts section by section, while the GMAT adapts within each section. Unique content: the GMAT has Data Insights which tests multi-source data analysis. The GRE emphasizes vocabulary more heavily. Writing: the GRE includes a 30-minute essay while the GMAT Focus Edition has no essay.

4:35 - 6:00 | The GRE may be better if you prefer having a calculator for all math, like being able to skip and return to questions, have a strong vocabulary, want a shorter test, or are also considering non-business graduate programs. The GMAT may be better if you excel at mental math, perform well under pressure when you cannot go back, enjoy analyzing data from multiple sources, or want to avoid the essay. My honest take: for most applicants, the GRE is the more approachable test. The calculator, navigation flexibility, and shorter duration make it less stressful.

What Schools Care About

Both Tests Are Equally Accepted

Every major MBA program accepts both tests equally. Harvard, Stanford, Wharton, and all other top programs have explicitly stated they have no preference. Your percentile is what matters.

If you are applying to dual-degree programs or keeping options open for law, policy, or other graduate programs, the GRE covers all of them. The GMAT is only accepted at business schools.

6:00 - 7:15 | Let us address the anxiety many applicants have. Will schools judge me for taking the GRE instead of the GMAT? No. Every major MBA program accepts both tests equally. Harvard, Stanford, Wharton have all explicitly stated they have no preference. They evaluate applicants holistically, and your test score is just one component. What matters is your percentile. A 90th percentile score is a 90th percentile score on either test. The historical perception that the GMAT is the "real" business school test is outdated. One practical note: the GRE covers business, law, policy, and other graduate programs. The GMAT is only accepted at business schools.

Retakes and Reporting

Know Your Options

  • GRE: Retake every 21 days, up to 5 times per year. ScoreSelect sends only your best.
  • GMAT: 5 attempts per year, 16 days between. You can cancel scores you do not want seen.
  • Improvement: Many applicants score significantly higher on their second attempt.

Do not view your first test as your only shot. Build retake time into your schedule.

10:00 - 11:30 | A few things to know about retakes. The GRE allows retakes every 21 days, up to 5 times in 12 months. You can use ScoreSelect to send only your best scores. The GMAT allows 5 attempts in 12 months with 16 days between. You can cancel scores you do not want schools to see. Many applicants improve significantly on their second attempt. Do not view your first test as your only shot.

Alternative Option

The Executive Assessment

For EMBA, part-time, or online MBA applicants with 8+ years of experience, the Executive Assessment is just 90 minutes. A growing number of full-time programs also accept it.

Accepting schools include Columbia, NYU Stern, Darden, Duke Fuqua, McCombs, and Georgetown. Check whether your target programs accept the EA.

11:30 - 12:15 | If you are applying to an Executive MBA, part-time MBA, or online MBA program, you may have a third option: the Executive Assessment. The EA is designed for experienced professionals with 8+ years of experience. It is only 90 minutes and covers similar content to the GMAT but with less frequency of the most challenging concepts. A growing number of full-time programs also accept the EA, including Columbia, NYU Stern, Darden, Duke Fuqua, McCombs, and Georgetown. If you are targeting an EMBA or part-time program, this may be your most efficient path.
