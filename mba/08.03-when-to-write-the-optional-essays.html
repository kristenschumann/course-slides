What Optional Essays Are For

Designed For
  • Explaining gaps or anomalies
  • Providing context the committee needs
  • Addressing a weakness proactively
  • Sharing circumstances that affected performance
Not Designed For
  • Adding more accomplishments
  • Writing another "why this school" essay
  • Repeating points made elsewhere
  • Demonstrating extra commitment

If you don't have something specific that needs explaining, skip it. A pointless optional essay is worse than none.

1:00 - 2:15 | Optional essays exist for a specific reason: to let you share information that doesn't fit elsewhere. They are designed for explaining gaps or anomalies, providing context, addressing weaknesses, and sharing circumstances that affected your performance. They are not designed for adding more accomplishments, writing another why-this-school essay, repeating points from elsewhere, or demonstrating extra commitment by writing more. The key principle: if you don't have something specific that needs explaining, skip it. A pointless optional essay is worse than no optional essay.

When You Should Write One

  • Employment gaps: Periods where you weren't working and the reason isn't obvious.
  • Low GPA or test score: Academic performance below school averages with context to provide.
  • Unusual career path: Frequent job changes, non-linear progression, or layoffs to explain.
  • Choice of recommenders: Not using your current manager when schools request it.
2:15 - 3:45 | Write an optional essay if you have any of these situations. Employment gaps where the reason isn't obvious. A low GPA or test score where there's context to share. An unusual career path with frequent job changes or a non-linear progression. Choice of recommenders, especially if you're not using your current manager when schools request it. Also consider writing one if personal circumstances affected your experience, like health issues, family responsibilities, or financial hardship. Or if you have a major update since submission, like a promotion or completed project.

When to Skip It

Nothing to Explain

Your application is straightforward, your profile is strong, and everything speaks for itself.

Tempted to Brag

Extra accomplishments belong in your resume or main essays. This isn't the place.

Filling Space

A pointless optional essay signals poor judgment more than extra effort.

3:45 - 4:45 | Don't write an optional essay just because the space exists. Skip it if you don't have anything to explain and your application speaks for itself. Skip it if you're tempted to add more brags. Those belong in your resume or main essays. Skip it if you're just filling space to show extra commitment. Admissions officers see through this. A pointless optional essay signals poor judgment more than extra effort. Many strong applicants don't write optional essays and get admitted. It's genuinely optional.

How to Write It Well

Principle Example
Be brief A few paragraphs at most. Make your point and move on.
Be matter-of-fact "My 2.8 GPA reflects a difficult freshman year. By junior year, I maintained a 3.6 in my major."
Take responsibility "I could have managed my course load better in my first year."
Show growth Whatever the issue was, explain what's different now.
Don't over-explain One concise paragraph often beats a long defensive essay.
4:45 - 6:00 | If you do have something to address, here's how to do it well. Be brief, typically a few paragraphs at most. Be matter-of-fact, not defensive. Don't apologize excessively. Compare "I'm so sorry my GPA is low, I should have worked harder" with "My 2.8 GPA reflects a difficult freshman year as the first in my family to attend college. By junior and senior year, I maintained a 3.6 in my major." Take responsibility when appropriate, because it shows maturity. Show growth or resolution. And don't over-explain. One concise paragraph is often enough. Long defensive essays raise more concerns than they resolve.

Supplementary Letters

Usually no. Most schools discourage extra recommendations. Sending them looks like you can't follow instructions. The exception: a credible alum or respected figure who spontaneously offers to send a brief, unsolicited note.

This only works with a genuine relationship, an organic-feeling note, and a person credible to the committee. If you don't have this situation, don't force it. Most successful applicants don't have supplementary letters.

6:00 - 7:00 | A separate question: should you have someone send an additional note on your behalf? Usually no. Most schools explicitly discourage additional recommendations outside the application. If you send extra letters, it looks like you're not following instructions. The exception is if someone highly credible, a respected alum or a well-known figure, spontaneously offers to send a brief note. The key is that it should appear unsolicited, it requires a genuine relationship, and the person must be credible to the committee. If you don't have this situation, don't force it. Most successful applicants don't have supplementary letters.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Optional essays are for explaining, not for bragging
  • Write one if you have gaps, anomalies, or context to provide
  • Skip it if your application speaks for itself
  • Be brief and matter-of-fact, never defensive or apologetic
  • Supplementary letters only work if genuinely unsolicited
  • Many successful applicants skip optional essays entirely
7:30 - 8:00 | Key takeaways. Optional essays are for explaining, not bragging. Write one if you have gaps, anomalies, or context to provide. Skip it if your application speaks for itself. Be brief and matter-of-fact, never defensive or apologetic. Supplementary letters only work if genuinely unsolicited from a credible source. And remember, many successful applicants skip optional essays entirely. Use the space purposefully, or not at all.
