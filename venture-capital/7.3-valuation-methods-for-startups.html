Section 1

Pre-Money and Post-Money

1:15 | Transition into the most fundamental concept in venture financing.

VC Recruiting Course

Two formulas you must know cold

Post-Money = Pre-Money + Investment

Company has a $40M pre-money. Lead investor writes a $10M check. Post-money is $50M.

Ownership = Investment / Post-Money

$10M / $50M = 20%. The investor owns 20% of the company after the round closes.

1:15 - 1:45 | The most basic concept in venture financing, and I've watched MBA candidates stumble on it. [Click] Post-money equals pre-money plus new investment. $40M pre-money, $10M check, $50M post-money. [Click] Investor ownership equals investment divided by post-money. $10M / $50M = 20%. Simple. But there are three traps that make it complicated in practice.

VC Recruiting Course

Trap 1: The option pool shuffle

VCs require the company to expand its employee option pool before a round, on a pre-money basis. The dilution comes out of the founders' ownership, not the new investor's.

If a company has a $40M pre-money but creates a new 10% option pool before the round, the effective pre-money for existing shareholders is really $36M. The new investor still gets 20% on a $50M post-money, but the founders gave up more than they realized. Understanding this signals you know how deals actually work.

1:45 - 2:15 | Trap 1: the option pool shuffle. VCs almost always require an option pool expansion before the round, on a pre-money basis. That means the dilution comes out of the founders' ownership, not the new investor's. $40M pre-money with a 10% pool expansion means founders' effective pre-money is $36M. This is one of the most common negotiation dynamics. Understanding it signals real deal knowledge.

VC Recruiting Course

Traps 2-3: SAFEs in the Cap Table

A SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) is a contract that converts to equity at a future funding round. SAFEs are not debt — they carry no interest and no maturity date.

SAFE Conversion at Series A

If a company raised $2M on SAFEs before the Series A, those convert into equity at the round. Depending on valuation cap and discount rate, SAFE holders get shares below the Series A price. Those converted shares add to the fully diluted share count, lowering everyone else's ownership.

Post-Money vs. Pre-Money SAFEs

YC's post-money SAFE defines the cap as post-money including the SAFE. A $10M cap with $1M invested means exactly 10% ownership. Clean. Older pre-money SAFEs are messier because ownership depends on total SAFE amounts raised, which may be unknown at signing.

VC Recruiting Course

Private comps: Normalize by multiples, not raw valuations

Don't compare raw valuations. Compare multiples. "Companies at this stage and growth rate are raising at 30-50x ARR" is actionable. "Company X raised at $50 million" without context is not.

PitchBook, Crunchbase, and fund-level databases track recent rounds. The challenge is that private data is noisy. A "$50M Series A" means very different things depending on whether the company had $1M or $5M in ARR. Normalizing by multiples makes the data useful.

The Venture Capital Method

5:30 | Transition into the workhorse valuation approach.

VC Recruiting Course

Working backward from the exit

If I need a target return of Xx on my investment, and I believe the company will be worth $Y at exit, then the maximum I should pay today is $Y / X (adjusted for dilution).

The VC method is the workhorse valuation approach for early-stage deals. It makes every assumption explicit: exit value, required return, dilution. Interviewers expect you to know this cold.

5:30 - 6:00 | The VC method is the most important single valuation framework you'll use. The logic: if I need Xx return on my investment, and the company will be worth $Y at exit, the maximum price today is $Y / X, adjusted for dilution. It works backward from the exit instead of forward from today.

VC Recruiting Course

Four steps of the VC method

1. Estimate Exit Value

Project revenue at Year 5-7. Multiply by an exit multiple from public comps. Example: $80M revenue x 8x = $640M exit value.

2. Set Required Return

Seed: 20-30x. Series A: 10-15x. Series B: 5-8x. Growth: 3-5x. These reflect probability-weighted expected value given failure rates at each stage.

3. Calculate Post-Money Today

Divide exit value by required return. $640M / 10x = $64M post-money. If investing $8M, pre-money is $56M.

4. Adjust for Dilution

Expect 40% dilution from future rounds. Adjusted post-money = $640M / (10 / 0.60) = $38.4M. Pre-money: $30.4M. Meaningfully different from $56M.

Scorecard and DCF

7:30 | Transition into the three other methods you should know.

VC Recruiting Course

Why DCF doesn't work for early-stage startups

  • DCF requires projecting cash flows 5-10 years out
  • Early-stage startups have no reliable revenue history and uncertain futures
  • Small changes in assumptions create wildly different valuations (false precision)
  • "Garbage in, garbage out" — a 10% change in terminal growth rate can 2x the valuation
  • VCs don't use DCF at seed or Series A — it signals misunderstanding of early-stage investing
  • DCF becomes more relevant at growth stage and beyond, where cash flows are more predictable

VC Recruiting Course

How to answer valuation questions

  • Ask clarifying questions. "What stage? What's the revenue profile? Recent comparable transactions?" This shows context determines method.
  • State your method and why. "Series B with $12M ARR, so I'd anchor to public comps with a private discount and cross-check with the VC method."
  • Walk through the math. Make assumptions explicit. Don't hide your inputs. Invite pushback.
  • Give a range, not a point estimate. "$80-120M pre-money." Ranges are honest. Point estimates are false precision.
  • Connect to the investment decision. "At $100M pre-money, we own 12% for our $12M check. At our expected exit, that's 7x. The deal works at this price."
10:30 - 11:15 | The five-part answer for valuation questions in interviews. Ask clarifying questions first. State your method and why. Walk through the math with explicit assumptions. Give a range, not a point estimate. And connect back to the investment decision: does this return justify the risk at this price? That's the complete answer. Method, math, and investment judgment. That's what gets offers.

Key takeaways

  • Pre-money and post-money are the vocabulary of every venture deal. Get the option pool shuffle and SAFE conversion right, and you'll demonstrate more practical knowledge than most first-year associates on day one.
  • Use the right tool for the stage. Berkus and Scorecard for pre-revenue. VC method for seed through Series A. Comps for Series B and beyond. DCF only when a company has stable, forecastable cash flows.
  • The VC method is the most important single valuation framework. It makes every assumption visible and turns valuation into a structured conversation instead of a fight over a number.
  • Valuation is a negotiation, not a calculation. VCs anchor on expected exits and ownership targets. The models inform the conversation. The outcome is determined by leverage, competition, and conviction.
  • Your framework matters more than your number. State the method, make assumptions explicit, give a range, and connect it to the investment decision.
