Section 1

What a Thesis Actually Is

1:00 | Transition into defining an investment thesis.

VC Recruiting Course

Headlines are not theses

What Most People Say

"I'm bullish on AI." "Healthcare is ripe for disruption." "Fintech is the future." Those are headlines. Everyone in the room agrees. They tell an interviewer nothing about your judgment.

What a Thesis Sounds Like

"Vertical AI companies with proprietary training data will capture market share from incumbent horizontal platforms over 3-5 years, because AI-native architectures require fundamentally different data infrastructure."

1:00 - 1:45 | Almost every candidate I have interviewed makes the same mistake. [Click] They think a thesis is a hot take. "AI is going to be big." Those are headlines. [Click] A thesis is a structured argument with a worldview, a timeframe, and specific criteria. "Vertical AI companies with proprietary data will win over 3-5 years because..." That is specific enough to be evaluated.

VC Recruiting Course

Five components every thesis needs

Component What It Answers Example
Market Belief What structural shift is happening? Enterprise is moving to AI-native architectures
Timing Argument Why now, and for how long? 3-5 year window while incumbents are caught flat-footed
Winning Profile What kind of company captures value? Vertical AI with proprietary data + outcome-based pricing
Evidence Base What supports this belief? Adoption data, customer interviews, comparable transitions
Key Risks What would make this wrong? Incumbents move faster; regulation changes the data landscape
1:45 - 2:45 | Five components. [Click] Market belief: what structural shift is happening. [Click] Timing argument: why now, how long is the window. Without timing, it is just an observation. [Click] Winning profile: what kind of company captures the value. Without this, you cannot evaluate deals. [Click] Evidence base: what supports your belief. [Click] Key risks: what would make you wrong. Without risks, it is wishful thinking.

Why Thesis Matters Most in Recruiting

2:45 | Transition into why thesis matters for getting hired.

VC Recruiting Course

"Does this person have the judgment to pick winners?"

The Stanford/NBER study of 885 VCs found that deal selection is rated the most important driver of value creation. More important than sourcing. More important than board support.

When a partner interviews you, they are trying to answer one question: can this person help us pick the right companies? Everything else is secondary. Your thesis is the best proxy for that judgment.

Section 3

Fund-Level vs. Personal Thesis

4:30 | Transition into the two types of theses.

VC Recruiting Course

Find the white space within the fund's mandate

Explore What They Haven't Touched

Look at the fund's portfolio and identify areas within their mandate they have not invested in yet. Then ask why. Is the market too small? Too early? Or is it genuinely underexplored? That answer tells you something important.

Stay Within the Mandate

Don't pitch something totally outside the fund's focus. Find white space within it. A candidate who spots an unmet area inside the fund's thesis shows they understand the fund deeply, not just the market in general.

5:15 - 5:45 | Don't just align your thesis to the fund's mandate. Dig deeper. [Click] Explore areas the fund has not invested in yet and ask why. That research signals you understand their portfolio, not just their brand. [Click] The goal is white space within the mandate, not outside it. A candidate who identifies what a fund is missing inside their own thesis shows real depth. That's the kind of insight that gets you hired.
Section 4

Building Your Thesis from Scratch

5:45 | Transition into the step-by-step process.

VC Recruiting Course

Four to eight weeks of focused work

  • Pick a domain. Your thesis should live where you have genuine knowledge or experience. Authenticity matters because every interviewer probes beneath the surface.
  • Map the landscape. Major players, what has been funded, where the gaps are. Bessemer's public memos show how professionals structure their thinking.
  • Identify a structural insight. Not describing a market. Making a claim about what is changing and why that change creates an investment opportunity.
  • Define your criteria. If your thesis is right, what kind of company should you invest in? Revenue threshold, customer segment, moat characteristics.
  • Name your companies. A thesis without named companies is an essay. You should have three companies ready when an interviewer asks "Give me a name."
5:45 - 7:00 | Five steps. [Click] Pick a domain where you have real depth. If you spent three years in healthcare, your thesis should be in healthtech. [Click] Map the landscape. Use Bessemer's public memos, CB Insights, PitchBook. [Click] Identify a structural insight. The best insights come from primary research: conversations with operators, customers, and founders. [Click] Define criteria. "B2B with $1M+ ARR, mid-market, with workflow integration that increases switching costs." [Click] Name companies. A thesis with five companies you are tracking is a working portfolio. The whole process takes 4-8 weeks.

VC Recruiting Course

Primary research separates good from great

Weak Evidence

Assembled from blog posts, podcasts, and TechCrunch articles. Anyone can synthesize secondary sources. It reads like a book report and partners can tell immediately.

Strong Evidence

"I talked to 15 procurement managers at mid-market companies, and 12 told me their current software cannot handle X." Primary research makes a thesis credible and defensible.

7:00 - 7:15 | The single biggest differentiator between a good thesis and a great one is the evidence. [Click] A thesis built from blog posts reads like a book report. [Click] A thesis grounded in conversations with 15 procurement managers is credible. Partners can tell the difference instantly. This is where the track record from 2.4 comes to life.

VC Recruiting Course

Four questions to test any thesis

Can you state it in three sentences?

If you need a paragraph, it is not crisp enough.

Does it have a clear "why now"?

If the same thesis was true five years ago and will be true in ten, it is not a thesis. It is a trend.

Can someone disagree with it?

Consensus does not generate outsized returns. When Peter Thiel asks "What truth do few people agree with you on?" he is asking for a thesis.

Would you put money behind it?

If someone handed you $100K and said "invest according to your thesis," could you? If yes, it is real.

7:15 - 7:30 | Four-question litmus test for any thesis. [Click] Can you state it in three sentences? [Click] Does it have a "why now"? A thesis without timing is just a trend. [Click] Can someone disagree? Consensus does not generate outsized returns. [Click] Would you put money behind it? That is the final test.

Key takeaways

  • A thesis is not a hot take, a market summary, or a prediction. It is a structured argument with a market belief, timing logic, winning profile, evidence, and risks.
  • Thesis quality is the number one signal partners use to evaluate candidates. The Stanford/NBER study confirmed what practitioners already knew: deal selection drives returns.
  • Build your thesis in a domain where you have real depth. The worst interview move is presenting a thesis you only know from TechCrunch. The best is a thesis grounded in operating experience and primary research.
  • A good thesis is specific enough to be wrong. If you cannot identify a scenario where it fails, you have not thought hard enough.
  • Start now. This takes weeks, not days. The thesis you bring to your first interview should be the product of deliberate work, not something drafted the night before.
