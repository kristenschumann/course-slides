What Is an Issue Tree?

  • Hierarchical breakdown of a problem into sub-questions, then into specific testable items.
  • Custom-built for every case. Branches reflect the unique dynamics of that business situation.
  • Dual purpose: a roadmap for investigation and a communication tool for the interviewer.

Adding "color commentary" to explain why each branch matters for this specific case is what separates top candidates.

2:40 - 4:30 | Explain the issue tree concept. - Start with core question at top, branch into sub-questions - Every tree is custom, no single tree works for all problems - Serves as roadmap (what to investigate) and communication tool (shows interviewer your thinking) - Color commentary: explaining why each branch matters shows business intuition

The 4-Phase Process

1

Listen & Clarify

Restate the problem, ask 2-3 narrowing questions.

2

Build the Tree

60-90 seconds of silence. Core question plus 3-4 MECE branches.

3

Decompose

Add 2-3 specific, testable sub-questions per branch.

4

Present Top-Down

State a hypothesis, walk through your logic at each level.

4:40 - 6:00 | Walk through the 4-phase process in detail. - Phase 1: Restate and clarify. "Domestic or global? Timeframe? Financial target?" - Phase 2: Take 60-90 seconds. Interviewers expect and respect thinking time. Sketch on paper. - Phase 3: Sub-questions should be specific enough to answer with data. - Phase 4: Don't read a list. Narrate your thinking. State which branch you'd investigate first and why. - Note: experienced hires can add industry knowledge. Undergrads focus on clean logic.

Break Into Management Consulting

The MECE Quality Test

Mutually Exclusive

  • Each branch must be distinct with no overlap.
  • Test: "Could this item fit under another branch?"
  • If yes, redefine until each item has one home.

Collectively Exhaustive

  • Branches must cover all possible angles.
  • Test: "Would full investigation give a complete answer?"
  • If a topic has no home, add a branch.
6:00 - 7:00 | Explain MECE as the gold standard for logical structures. - ME: no overlap. "Revenue per customer" includes "pricing," so they can't be separate branches. - CE: no gaps. Profit decline analysis without examining costs has a hole. - Natural MECE dichotomies: revenue vs cost, internal vs external, supply vs demand.

Break Into Management Consulting

Common MECE Violation

Mixing "what" and "why" at the same level. "Market share decline" is what happened. "Competitor pricing" is why it happened. They cannot sit side by side as branches.

Shortcut: Start with natural MECE dichotomies. Revenue vs. cost for profitability. Internal vs. external for cause analysis. Supply vs. demand for market dynamics.

7:00 - 7:30 | Most common violation: mixing what happened with why it happened. - Keep each level of the tree at the same logical type - Natural dichotomies give you a starting point, then customize from there

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Stop reciting frameworks. Naming a memorized framework signals you're not thinking about the specific problem.
  • Build issue trees. Start with the core question, create 3-4 MECE branches, decompose into testable sub-questions.
  • Test with MECE checks. Overlap test: could any item fit elsewhere? Completeness test: would you have a full answer?
  • Practice on unfamiliar problems. Build a presentable structure in 60-90 seconds on cases you have never seen before.
9:30 - 10:00 | Wrap up with four key takeaways. - Frameworks as ingredients, not recipes - Issue tree is your replacement tool - MECE is the quality gate - Real skill develops from practicing on unfamiliar problems
