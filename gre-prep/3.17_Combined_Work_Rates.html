Work Rates

0:00-0:30 | Hook: Alex can paint a room in 6 hours. Ben can paint it in 4 hours. How long working together? The wrong answer is averaging to get 5 hours. The right method uses work rates: 1/6 + 1/4 = 5/12 per hour, so 12/5 = 2.4 hours. This video teaches the work rate formula.

Core Formula

The work rate framework

Key Insight: Rate = $\frac{1}{\text{Time to complete the job}}$. If Alex finishes in 6 hours, Alex's rate is $\frac{1}{6}$ per hour.

Two Workers

$\frac{1}{A} + \frac{1}{B} = \frac{1}{T}$

A, B = individual times. T = combined time.

Shortcut

$T = \frac{A \times B}{A + B}$

Works only for exactly two workers.

0:30-2:00 | The key insight: rate equals 1 over time. Combined work formula: 1/A + 1/B = 1/T. The shortcut formula T = AB/(A+B) works for two workers. Walk through both forms. Coaching tip: to avoid fractions entirely, take the LCM of the given times as your total work units. If A takes 20 days and B takes 30 days, call the job 60 units. Then A does 3 units/day and B does 2 units/day. Combined: 5 units/day, so 60/5 = 12 days. No fractions needed.

Example 1

Two workers combined

Problem

Alex paints a room in 6 hours. Ben paints it in 4 hours. How long working together?

Solution

Step 1: Rates: $\frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{4}$

Step 2: Common denominator: $\frac{2}{12} + \frac{3}{12} = \frac{5}{12}$

Step 3: Flip the rate: $T = \frac{12}{5}$

Answer: $2.4$ hours

Three or More Workers

Extending the formula

3:00-3:10 | Transition: the formula extends naturally to any number of workers.

Example 2

Three machines working together

Problem

Three machines complete a job in 2, 3, and 6 hours respectively. How long working together?

Solution

Step 1: Add rates: $\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{6}$

Step 2: Common denominator: $\frac{3}{6} + \frac{2}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{6}{6} = 1$

Combined rate = 1 job per hour

Answer: $1$ hour

3:10-4:10 | Example 2: three machines. Click to reveal solution. Step 1: add all three rates. Step 2: common denominator gives 6/6 = 1. Combined rate equals exactly 1 job per hour. Answer: 1 hour.

Example 3

Partial work and handoff

Problem

Alex (rate $\frac{1}{6}$) works for 2 hours, then Ben (rate $\frac{1}{4}$) finishes alone. How long does Ben need?

Solution

Step 1: Alex's work: $\frac{1}{6} \times 2 = \frac{1}{3}$

Step 2: Remaining: $1 - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{2}{3}$

Step 3: Ben's time: $\frac{2/3}{1/4} = \frac{2}{3} \times 4 = \frac{8}{3}$

Answer: $\frac{8}{3}$ hours (2 hr 40 min)

4:10-5:30 | Example 3: partial work. Click to reveal. Step 1: Alex completes 1/3 of the job in 2 hours. Step 2: 2/3 remains. Step 3: divide remaining work by Ben's rate. Answer: 8/3 hours, about 2 hours 40 minutes.

Part 3

Negative Work (Leaks)

When one element works against the others

5:30-5:40 | Transition: sometimes one element works against the others, like a drain emptying a tank while a pipe fills it.

Pipes and Tanks

Positive and negative rates

Positive Rate

A pipe fills a tank.

Rate = $+\frac{1}{\text{fill time}}$

Negative Rate

A drain empties a tank.

Rate = $-\frac{1}{\text{drain time}}$

Net formula: $\frac{1}{\text{Fill}} - \frac{1}{\text{Drain}} = \frac{1}{T}$

5:40-6:30 | Positive rates for filling, negative rates for draining. Net rate formula: 1/Fill minus 1/Drain equals 1/T. If the drain is faster than the fill, the tank never fills.

Example 4

Pipe and drain

Problem

A pipe fills a tank in 5 hours. A drain empties it in 8 hours. Both open, how long to fill?

Solution

Step 1: Net rate: $\frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{8}$

Step 2: Common denominator: $\frac{8}{40} - \frac{5}{40} = \frac{3}{40}$

Step 3: Flip: $T = \frac{40}{3}$

Answer: $13\frac{1}{3}$ hours

6:30-7:30 | Example 4: pipe and drain. Click to reveal solution. Step 1: net rate is 1/5 minus 1/8. Step 2: common denominator gives 3/40. Step 3: flip to get 40/3. Answer: 13 and 1/3 hours.

Example 5

Two pipes and a drain

Problem

Two pipes fill a tank in 3 and 4 hours. A drain empties it in 6 hours. All three open, how long to fill?

Solution

Step 1: Net rate: $\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{6}$

Step 2: $\frac{4}{12} + \frac{3}{12} - \frac{2}{12} = \frac{5}{12}$

Step 3: Flip: $T = \frac{12}{5}$

Answer: $2.4$ hours

7:30-8:30 | Example 5: two pipes plus a drain. Click to reveal. Step 1: add fill rates, subtract drain rate. Step 2: common denominator gives 5/12. Step 3: flip to get 12/5. Answer: 2.4 hours.

Watch Out

When the drain is faster

Warning

If the drain rate exceeds the fill rate, the tank will never fill.

Fill in 10 hours (rate $\frac{1}{10}$), drain in 5 hours (rate $\frac{1}{5}$)

Net rate: $\frac{1}{10} - \frac{1}{5} = \frac{1}{10} - \frac{2}{10} = $ $-\frac{1}{10}$

A negative net rate means the tank is emptying, not filling. On the GRE, check whether the answer makes physical sense.

8:30-9:30 | Warning case: if the drain is faster, net rate is negative and the tank never fills. Fill in 10 hours minus drain in 5 hours gives negative 1/10. Always check that your answer makes sense.

Common Trap

Watch your units

Key point: Rates may be given in minutes but the answer is in hours — always check and convert before solving.

Problem

A standard machine fills at 1 gallon per 4 minutes. A deluxe machine fills at twice that rate. How many hours for both machines to fill 135 gallons?

Solution

Standard rate: $\frac{1}{4}$ gal/min. Deluxe rate: $\frac{1}{2}$ gal/min.

Combined rate: $\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{4}$ gal/min

Time: $\frac{135}{3/4} = 180$ minutes $= $ $3$ hours

GRE Trap

Unit mismatch is a common GRE trap — convert before solving. If the problem asks for hours, convert your rate or your time before the final step.

Unit conversion trap: rates given in minutes, answer expected in hours. Always identify the unit the question asks for, then convert. Here: 180 minutes divided by 60 = 3 hours. Students who leave the answer in minutes will choose a wrong answer choice.

Key Takeaways

Combined work rates

  • Rate = 1/Time for each worker. Add rates for combined work.
  • Two workers shortcut: $T = \frac{A \times B}{A + B}$
  • Three or more: $\frac{1}{A} + \frac{1}{B} + \frac{1}{C} = \frac{1}{T}$
  • Partial work: Work done = Rate $\times$ Time, then find remaining.
  • Negative work: Drains subtract. Net rate = fill rates minus drain rates.
  • Sanity check: Combined time is always less than the fastest individual time.
