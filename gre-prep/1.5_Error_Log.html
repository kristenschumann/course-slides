Why You Should Use
an Error Log

0:00 - 0:30 | Hook. An error log separates students who improve quickly from those who plateau. Tracking mistakes systematically is one of the most powerful things you can do to raise your score.

The Problem

Every wrong answer is information

Most students make a mistake, feel bad, glance at the right answer, and move on. A week later, they make the exact same mistake.

The Common Trap

Without tracking errors, the patterns stay invisible. You keep making the same 3-5 types of mistakes over and over.

Maybe you always mess up percent change, pick trap answers on inference, or rush through first sections.

The Error Log Solution

An error log helps you see the patterns. Once you see them, you can fix them.

If you had a list of the exact 10 things costing you the most points, you would study those. Your error log creates that list.

0:30 - 2:00 | Explain the common trap cycle. Most students make the same 3-5 types of mistakes. An error log makes patterns visible so you can fix them.

The Template

What to record

For every wrong answer, capture these fields. A downloadable template is included in the course materials.

Field What to Write
Date & Source When and from which drill or test
Topic Question type
What Went Wrong Your actual mistake, be specific
What to Remember The rule or approach for next time
Redo Date When to retry this problem
Tip

A few sentences per entry is enough. Writing forces you to think about what went wrong.

Example Entry

What a real entry looks like

Field Your Entry
Date & Source March 3, PowerPrep Test 1, Q14
Topic Algebra: Exponents & Roots
What Went Wrong "If $x^2 = 16$, what are all possible values of $x$?" I answered 4. Correct answer: 4 and −4. Forgot the negative root.
What to Remember Always consider $\pm$ when taking square roots. $x^2 = k$ has two solutions.
Redo Date March 10
Error Type

This is a concept error, not careless. The student didn't know the rule. One log entry, one rule learned.

3:00 - 4:00 | Walk through a real example entry. The student forgot that squaring produces both positive and negative roots. Categorizing as a concept error tells them they need to review the rule, not just slow down. One entry, one takeaway.

Weekly Review

Finding patterns and fixing them

Once you have 15-20 entries, patterns emerge. Spend 15 minutes each week reviewing your log.

Count by Error Type

Content gaps = study concepts. Process errors = slow down. Strategy errors = review approach.

Count by Topic

More geometry errors? More sentence equivalence? This tells you where to focus.

The Redo System

Set a redo date one week later. Try the problem again without notes.

Got it right? Move to monthly review.

Wrong again? Redo in 3-4 days. Spaced repetition cements learning.

Weekly Question

"If I could fix three things before my next test, what would have the biggest impact?"

4:00 - 6:30 | Explain pattern analysis: count by error type and topic. Introduce the redo system as spaced repetition. Weekly question guides study priorities.

Summary

The bottom line

  • Every wrong answer is data about what you need to learn.
  • An error log turns that data into patterns you can act on.
  • Recording what went wrong forces you to actually understand it.
  • Weekly review guides your study priorities so you focus on what matters most.
  • Redoing problems cements learning through spaced repetition.
6:30 - 7:00 | Recap five key takeaways. Download the error log template and start with just 5 entries. Even a small amount of tracking reveals useful patterns. [Leland+ Tip] Categorize errors as conceptual (did not know the rule), careless (knew the rule but made a mistake), or strategic (ran out of time or misread the question). Most students find that 50% or more of their errors are careless or strategic, not conceptual. Target these first for the fastest score improvement.
