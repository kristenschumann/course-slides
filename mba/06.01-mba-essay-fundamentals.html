The Word-Count Math

Stanford: 650 words

Spend ~390 words (60%) on one story's action and consequences.

HBS: 300 words

Room for exactly one story and one insight per essay.

Darden: 200 words

About one paragraph. Every sentence must earn its place.

Essay Structure

Three Principles That Work

01

Open With a Scene

Not "I learned leadership at Deloitte." Instead: "At 11 PM on a Thursday, I was staring at a deliverable that was wrong."

02

Write Your Ending First

The conclusion is the point. If you can't state it in one sentence, you're not ready to draft.

03

Allocate Word Count

In 500 words: max 75 on setup, 250+ on action, the rest on reflection. Don't waste space on context.

11:30 - 13:00 | Three structural principles I use with every client. First, open with a scene. Not "I learned leadership at Deloitte." Put the reader in the room with you â€” the specific moment, the stakes. Second, write your ending first. When I read a draft, the first thing I look for is whether the ending delivers a clear "so what." If you can't state it in one sentence, you're not ready to draft. Third, allocate word count ruthlessly. In 500 words, I tell clients: max 75 on setup, 250+ on action and decisions, the rest on reflection. The most common mistake I see with new clients is a 200-word setup that leaves no room for the insight that actually matters.

Authentic Voice

Write Like Yourself, Not a Template

Sounds Like a Press Release

  • "Leveraged cross-functional synergies"
  • Buzzwords without substance
  • Sentences that impress but say nothing
  • Accomplishments without context

Sounds Like a Real Person

  • "Called a meeting because our teams hadn't talked in 3 months"
  • Details only you would know
  • Your actual thought process, not just outcomes
  • Moments of doubt or uncertainty

Read your essay aloud. If any sentence sounds like a corporate memo, rewrite it.
