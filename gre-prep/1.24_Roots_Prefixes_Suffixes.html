Using Roots, Prefixes,
& Suffixes

0:00-0:45 | Hook: You will encounter words on the GRE you have never seen before. No matter how many flashcards you study, there will be unfamiliar words. But if you understand word parts, you can often figure out what an unknown word means. Let's build that skill.

Core Strategy

One root unlocks many words

Breakdown

in + cred + ible

in- = not

cred = believe (Latin "credere")

-ible = able to

Word Family

credible = able to be believed

credulous = too willing to believe

incredulous = not willing to believe

incredible = not able to be believed

You do not memorize each word separately. You learn the building blocks and combine them.

0:45-2:15 | Break down "incredible" into three parts. Show how the root "cred" unlocks credible, credulous, incredulous. This is the power of word parts: learn building blocks, not individual words.

Part 3

High-Frequency Suffixes

Endings that signal part of speech

8:15-8:25 | Transition: Suffixes come at the end and often tell you the part of speech.

Suffixes

Identifying part of speech

Noun Suffixes

-tion / -sion (creation)

-ment (judgment)

-ness (darkness)

-ist (scientist)

Adjective Suffixes

-ful (hopeful)

-less (careless)

-ous (famous)

-able / -ible (visible)

-ive (creative, active)

Verb Suffixes

-ize (realize)

-ify (simplify)

-ate (create)

8:25-10:00 | Noun suffixes signal a thing or state. Adjective suffixes describe qualities. Verb suffixes indicate actions. Knowing the part of speech helps you place a word correctly even if you are unsure of its exact meaning.

Application

Decoding unfamiliar words

Example 1

circumlocution

circum- = around

locu = speak

-tion = noun suffix

"Speaking around something" = indirect speech

Example 2

pusillanimous

pusill- = very small

-anim- = spirit, life

-ous = adjective suffix

"Small-spirited" = timid, cowardly

Even partial understanding helps. Knowing a word is positive or negative often lets you eliminate wrong answers.

10:00-11:15 | Practice decoding. Circumlocution: circum (around) + locu (speak) + tion (noun) = speaking around something, indirect speech. Pusillanimous: pusill (small) + anim (spirit) + ous (adjective) = small-spirited, timid. Even partial understanding helps eliminate wrong answers.

Practice

Decode with roots

The artist's early work was considered _______, but her later paintings demonstrated remarkable originality and vision.

(A) innovative

(B) derivative

(C) abstract

(D) controversial

(E) profound

11:15-12:15 | Read the sentence aloud. Give viewers ~30 seconds to think. The contrast word "but" is the key signal here. What would be the opposite of "originality and vision"? Pause before advancing.

Practice

Answer: (B) derivative

Root Breakdown

de- + riv + -ative

de- = from, away

riv = stream, flow (Latin "rivus")

-ative = adjective suffix

Why It Works

"Flowing from something else" = copied, unoriginal

The contrast word "but" signals the blank is the opposite of "originality"

Root knowledge lets you decode even unfamiliar vocabulary on test day

Strategy: Spot the contrast signal, then use root knowledge to confirm the meaning. Two tools working together.

12:15-13:15 | Reveal the answer: B, derivative. Walk through the root breakdown step by step. "De-" means from or away. "Riv" comes from the Latin for stream. So "derivative" literally means flowing from something else — copied, unoriginal. The contrast word "but" told us the blank had to be the opposite of originality. This is how root knowledge and sentence structure clues work together on the GRE.

Key Takeaways

Roots, prefixes, and suffixes

  • Prefixes change meaning: un-, in-, dis- (not); pre-, post- (time); sub-, super- (position)
  • Roots carry core meaning: cred (believe), dict (say), vert (turn), ben/mal (good/bad)
  • Suffixes signal part of speech: -tion (noun), -ous (adjective), -ize (verb)
  • Break unfamiliar words into parts, then combine meanings for an educated guess
  • Partial understanding is often enough to eliminate wrong answer choices
13:15-14:00 | Summary: Prefixes change meaning, roots carry core meaning, suffixes signal part of speech. When you encounter an unfamiliar word, break it into parts and combine. Even partial understanding lets you eliminate wrong answers. You cannot memorize every word, but you can learn to decode them. [Leland+ Tip] Focus on the 20 most productive Latin and Greek roots first: bene/mal, dict, vert/vers, cred, duc/duct, voc/voke, spec/spect, graph/gram, path, phil/phob, chron, gen, morph, log/logue, aud, scrib/script, port, rupt, sent/sens, and fac/fact. These roots appear in hundreds of GRE words. Learn them in context by reading one quality article per day (The Atlantic, The Economist) and circling unfamiliar words to break apart. This builds root recognition far faster than memorizing root lists in isolation.
