Inference
The No-Spin Rule

0:00 - 0:05 | Title slide. This video teaches the no-spin rule for GRE inference questions.

The Problem

Why students get inferences wrong

Inference questions are tricky because the answer isn't directly stated. You have to figure out what's implied. But most students go wrong by adding their own spin.

What Inference Means on the GRE

Something that MUST be true based on what's stated. A logical conclusion that follows directly from the text.

What Inference Does NOT Mean

Something that might be true, is probably true, is true in the real world, or that you think the author would agree with.

0:05 - 1:30 | Set up the problem. Define inference precisely. Like math: "All dogs are mammals" lets you infer any specific dog is a mammal, but not that dogs are the only mammals or the best mammals.

The Rule

Add nothing. Subtract nothing.

Adding Spin

You bring in outside knowledge or assumptions.

Passage: "Sales increased after the new ad campaign launched."

Wrong: "The campaign caused sales to increase."

Valid: "Sales were lower before the campaign launched."

Subtracting Spin

You ignore part of what the passage says.

Passage: "Most scientists agree, though some debate the timeline."

Wrong: "All scientists agree about climate change."

Valid: "Not all scientists agree on every aspect."

1:30 - 3:30 | Teach the core rule with two examples. Adding spin means bringing in assumptions (correlation is not causation). Subtracting spin means ignoring qualifiers like "most" vs "all."

Traps

4 ways students go wrong

The Logical Leap

Taking a small observation and drawing a large conclusion. "Hired ten engineers" does not mean "growing rapidly."

The Assumed Agreement

Assuming the author endorses something they merely describe. Reporting critics' views is not agreeing with them.

The Extreme Extension

Taking a moderate statement to an extreme. "Many health benefits" does not mean "everyone should exercise daily."

The Timeframe Error

Assuming something true now was true before. "Current policy requires training" does not mean training was always required.

3:30 - 5:30 | Walk through the four traps. Each one represents a different type of spin that students add. Logical leaps are the most common. The assumed agreement trap is especially subtle.

Part 2

Testing Your Inference

5:30 - 5:35 | Section divider. Now teach students how to verify their inferences with three tests.

Verification

3 tests before selecting an answer

The "Must" Test

MUST this be true, or could it be false while the passage remains true? If it could be false, it's not valid.

The "Prove It" Test

Can you point to specific words that prove this? Paraphrasing is good. Stretching beyond the text is not.

The "Alternative" Test

Is there any other explanation? If the passage doesn't rule out alternatives, the inference may be too strong.

5:35 - 7:30 | Teach the three verification tests. The "Must" test is the most powerful. If an inference could be false while the passage remains true, it's spin. The "Prove It" test grounds you in actual text. The "Alternative" test catches causal assumptions.

Example

Applying the three tests

Passage: "The restaurant was empty when we arrived at 7 PM."

Proposed inference: "The restaurant is unpopular."

Test results:

  • Must test: Could be a slow night, holiday, or reservation-only
  • Prove it: Nothing says anything about popularity
  • Alternative: Many explanations exist
Valid Inference

"The restaurant had no customers at 7 PM." This is directly supported by the text with no added spin.

7:30 - 8:30 | Walk through the example. The "unpopular" inference fails all three tests. The valid inference restates what the passage says without adding any spin.

Practice

Museum extended hours

The museum's decision to extend its hours until 9 PM on weeknights initially met with skepticism from staff. However, attendance during the extended hours now accounts for 18% of total visits, with the majority being working professionals who could not visit during traditional hours.

Which of the following is most strongly supported by the passage?

  • (A) Working professionals are the museum's most important demographic.
  • (B) Museums should prioritize accessibility over operational costs.
  • (C) There was unmet demand for museum access outside traditional hours.
  • (D) Staff skepticism was based on concerns about work-life balance.
  • (E) The extended hours will eventually attract even more visitors.
9:15 - 10:15 | Practice problem. Give students ~1 minute to read and choose. Tell them to apply the no-spin rule: which answer sticks closest to what the passage actually says?

Practice

Answer: spot the spin

(A) "Most important demographic" — adds a ranking the passage never makes.

(B) "Should prioritize" — makes a recommendation the passage never makes.

(D) "Concerns about work-life balance" — guesses at staff motivations not mentioned in the passage.

(E) "Will eventually attract even more" — predicts the future with no textual support.

Answer: (C)

18% of visits now happen during extended hours, mostly from people who "could not visit during traditional hours." That directly supports unmet demand — no spin added.

10:15 - 11:15 | Walk through each wrong answer and name the spin. A adds a superlative. B adds a recommendation. D invents motivations. E predicts the future. C is the only answer that restates what the passage says without adding anything.

Key Takeaways

The no-spin rule

  • Inferences must be proven by the passage - you should be able to point to specific text that directly supports your answer
  • Add nothing - don't bring in outside knowledge, assumptions, or causal reasoning the passage doesn't support
  • Subtract nothing - don't ignore qualifications, conditions, or hedging language like "most" or "some"
  • Use the three tests - Must it be true? Can you prove it? Are there alternatives the passage doesn't rule out?
  • Choose the most conservative inference - the right answer takes the smallest logical step from what's stated
11:15 - 12:00 | Summary slide. The no-spin rule is simple: add nothing, subtract nothing. If you can't point to specific text that supports your answer, you've probably added spin.
