Strategy

Read for structure, not details

First Read (1-3 min)

  • Identify the main point
  • Note the author's stance
  • Understand each paragraph's purpose
  • Don't memorize, just locate

What to Mark Mentally

  • The main claim or thesis
  • Major transitions ("however," "in contrast")
  • Strong opinions or evaluative language
  • Examples (note them, don't memorize)
Goal

Build a mental map so you can navigate back quickly, not recall every detail.

Watch Out

5 common RC traps

Trap What Happens How to Avoid
True but not stated Answer is true in the real world but not in the passage Only use passage evidence
Too extreme Words like "always," "never," "all," "none" Watch for absolute language
Right detail, wrong question Info from the passage that doesn't answer THIS question Re-read the question stem
Half-right Part is correct, part is wrong or unsupported Check every part of the answer
Outside knowledge You know more about the topic and use it Ignore what you know

Practice Passage

Read this short passage

GRE Reading Passage

The concept of keystone species, first articulated by ecologist Robert Paine in 1969, holds that certain organisms exert an influence on their ecosystems far out of proportion to their abundance. When sea otters were hunted to near extinction along the Pacific coast, sea urchin populations exploded, decimating the kelp forests that had sheltered hundreds of marine species. The resulting ecological collapse demonstrated that removing a single predator could unravel an entire food web. Subsequent research has revealed that keystone dynamics operate across nearly every biome, from tropical forests where fig trees sustain dozens of frugivores during seasonal food shortages, to freshwater systems where beaver dams create wetland habitats for amphibians and waterfowl.

Your goal: On the GRE, you would spend 2-3 minutes reading a passage like this. Focus on structure, not memorization.

4:15 - 5:15 | Present the passage. Give students 15-20 seconds to read it silently. This is a realistic GRE-style short passage: dense academic prose, one paragraph, natural science topic. Point out that this is similar to what they will see on test day. The passage has a clear thesis (keystone species matter disproportionately), evidence (sea otters, fig trees, beavers), and academic tone.

Worked Example

What is the primary purpose of this passage?

Based on the keystone species passage:

  • (A) To argue that sea otters are the most important marine species
  • (B) To explain how the removal of predators benefits ecosystems
  • (C) To illustrate that certain species have disproportionate ecological impact
  • (D) To compare the ecological roles of sea otters, fig trees, and beavers
  • (E) To challenge Robert Paine's original definition of keystone species

Answer: (C)

The passage defines keystone species and provides multiple examples showing their outsized influence.

Why others fail

  • (A) Too narrow. Sea otters are one example, not the main point.
  • (B) Reverses the argument. Removal of predators causes harm, not benefit.
  • (D) Right details, wrong focus. The comparison serves the larger thesis, not the other way around.
8:15 - 9:30 | Walk through the worked question. First, identify the type: this is a Main Idea question. Then apply the process. The main claim is in the first sentence. Choice (A) is the "too narrow" trap, focusing on one example. Choice (B) is the "reversal" trap. It sounds like it matches but gets the direction wrong. Choice (D) is the "right detail, wrong question" trap. Choice (E) contradicts the passage. Choice (C) captures the full scope of the passage.

Practice

Inference: stick close to the text

The city's new recycling program has increased participation rates from 35% to 68% of households in its first year. Officials attribute this success to the simplified sorting requirements and curbside pickup service.

Which of the following can be properly inferred?

  • (A) The previous recycling program required more complex sorting.
  • (B) Curbside pickup is more popular than drop-off recycling.
  • (C) The city will eventually achieve 100% participation.
  • (D) Recycling reduces landfill costs significantly.
  • (E) Environmental awareness has increased among residents.

Answer: (A)

Officials credit "simplified sorting requirements" for the program's success, which directly implies the old program had more complex sorting. This is a safe, text-supported inference.

Why others fail

  • (B) The passage never compares curbside to drop-off popularity.
  • (C) "Eventually 100%" goes far beyond any stated evidence.
  • (D) True in the real world, but not stated in the passage.
  • (E) Participation rose, but the passage doesn't say why residents joined — only what officials changed.
9:30 - 11:30 | Practice problem. Give students ~1 minute to read the passage and select an answer. Then reveal the answer. Key teaching point: (A) is the only choice directly supported by the text. "Simplified sorting" implies sorting was previously more complex — that's a safe inference. The other choices either add outside knowledge (D), speculate about the future (C), or introduce comparisons not in the passage (B, E). This ties back to the reading approach: stick close to the text, make only inferences directly supported by what's written, and avoid answers that add information not in the passage.

Key Takeaways

How to approach reading questions

  • Read for structure, not memorization - identify the main point, author stance, and paragraph purposes on your first read
  • Build a mental map - know WHERE information lives so you can navigate back quickly
  • Identify the question type first - each type requires a different approach and different evidence
  • Budget ~1.5-2 minutes per RC question - invest time in your first read to save time answering questions
  • Always support answers with the passage - if you can't point to specific text, the answer is probably wrong
  • Watch for extreme language - GRE passages rarely make absolute claims, so answers with "always" or "never" are usually traps
11:30 - 12:00 | Summary slide. Reinforce the key message: RC is about strategic reading. Build a map, identify question types, and always ground your answers in the passage text.
