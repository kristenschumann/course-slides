The "+1" Trap

Why Most Students Get It Wrong

How many integers from 7 to 83, inclusive?

Common wrong answer:

83 − 7 = 76

Correct answer:

83 − 7 + 1 = 77

The Fencepost Analogy

To build 10 feet of fence with posts every foot, you need 11 posts (one at each end).

Count from a to b (inclusive) = b − a + 1

The "+1" accounts for including both endpoints

Counting Integers

Three Scenarios

Inclusive (Both Endpoints)

"from a to b, inclusive"

b − a + 1

Example: From 10 to 50, inclusive
50 − 10 + 1 = 41

Exclusive (Neither Endpoint)

"strictly between a and b"

b − a − 1

Example: Strictly between 10 and 50
Integers: 11, 12, ..., 49
50 − 10 − 1 = 39

One Endpoint Only

"from a to b" (unclear) or "a < x ≤ b"

b − a

Example: 10 < x ≤ 50
Integers: 11, 12, ..., 50
50 − 10 = 40

Watch the wording: "From 1 to 10" is ambiguous. "From 1 to 10, inclusive" or "including 1 and 10" is clear.

Properties

Sum Formula and Middle Term

Sum of Consecutive Integers

Sum = n × $\frac{\text{first} + \text{last}}{2}$

Or: Sum = n × (average of endpoints)

Example: Sum of integers from 1 to 100

n = 100
Sum = 100 × $\frac{1 + 100}{2}$ = 100 × 50.5 = 5,050

Middle Term Shortcut

Sum = n × (middle term)

For odd n: middle term = average

Example: Sum of 5 consecutive integers is 45. Find the middle integer.

45 = 5 × middle
middle = 9
The integers: 7, 8, 9, 10, 11

Quick trick: For consecutive integers, the sum divided by the count equals the middle value (or average).

Properties

Product of Consecutive Integers

KEY RULE

The product of n consecutive integers is always divisible by n!

Why This Works

In any set of n consecutive integers:

  • One is divisible by 1
  • One is divisible by 2
  • One is divisible by 3
  • ...and so on up to n

Examples

Consecutive Set Divisible By
Any 2 consecutive integers 2! = 2
Any 3 consecutive integers 3! = 6
Any 4 consecutive integers 4! = 24
Any 5 consecutive integers 5! = 120

Example: 4 × 5 × 6 = 120

120 ÷ 6 = 20 ✓ (divisible by 3! = 6)

5:00 - 5:45 | Product divisibility: product of n consecutive integers is divisible by n!. Why: each set contains one multiple of 1, 2, 3, etc. Examples: 2 consecutive ÷ 2, 3 consecutive ÷ 6.

Spacing

Equally Spaced Objects

Number of gaps = Objects − 1

Example 1: Trees Problem

8 trees planted in a row, equally spaced. First and last are 35 meters apart. Distance between adjacent trees?

Gaps = 8 − 1 = 7
Distance = 35 ÷ 7 = 5 meters

Objects = $\frac{\text{Total distance}}{\text{Spacing}}$ + 1

Example 2: Fence Posts

Posts every 4 feet along a 100-foot fence (including both ends). How many posts?

Gaps = 100 ÷ 4 = 25
Posts = 25 + 1 = 26 posts

Same "+1" pattern: Just like counting integers, counting objects includes both endpoints.
