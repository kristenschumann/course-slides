Why Segmentation Works

Without Segmentation

Uses a single average for the entire population. Hides critical differences between groups.

"The average American drinks X cups of coffee per day. Multiply by 330 million."

With Segmentation

Breaks the population into groups with meaningfully different behaviors. Each group gets its own estimate.

"Adults 16-30 drink moderately. Adults 31-65 are heavy consumers. Estimate each group separately."

0:50 - 1:20 | The first approach is what most candidates do. The second gets offers. Averages hide critical differences. A 10-year-old and a 35-year-old professional have completely different coffee consumption.

The Segmentation Sweet Spot

Aim for 3 to 5 segments. Fewer than three is too rough. More than five adds complexity without improving accuracy. Three to five shows structured thinking without burning interview time on arithmetic.

This applies to any market sizing question. Whether you're segmenting by age, geography, or usage pattern, the sweet spot stays the same.

1:20 - 1:40 | The 3-5 segment rule. Fewer is too rough, more adds complexity without improving accuracy. This is the sweet spot for structured thinking.

Two Ways to Attack Any Market Sizing

Top-down from the population, or bottom-up from a single unit.

1:40 - 1:50 | Transition. Two approaches: top-down and bottom-up. Knowing when to use each separates solid answers from great ones.

Break Into Management Consulting

Top-Down vs Bottom-Up

Top-Down

  • Start with a big, known number (e.g., 330M US population)
  • Filter down through relevant segments and usage rates
  • Best for revenue and volume questions
  • Most common approach interviewers expect

Bottom-Up

  • Start with a single small unit (one store, one day)
  • Scale up by multiplying units across the market
  • Best for capacity and supply-side questions
  • Stronger when physical constraints are easy to picture
1:50 - 2:30 | Top-down is most common. Bottom-up works for physical constraints. Caution on top-down: each filter introduces an assumption, and errors compound. If you overestimate two filters by 20% each, you're 44% off.

Know Which Type You're Solving

Identify the question type before you start calculating.

Four Market Sizing Types

Revenue

Estimate both volume and price. Most common type. Use top-down segmentation.

Volume

Units only, no price. Segment by consumer group and usage frequency.

Population

Estimate demand for a service, then calculate how many providers are needed.

Capacity

Start from physical constraints like runways or turnaround time, not demand.

2:40 - 3:15 | Before any calculation, identify the type. Revenue and volume use top-down segmentation. Population chains demand and supply. Capacity starts from physical constraints.

Calculate Annual Revenue

Weekly purchases: Heavy (53M x 5) + Moderate (79M x 2) + Light (53M x 0.5) = roughly 450 million per week.

Annualize and price: 450M x 52 weeks = ~23.4 billion purchases. At ~$5 average transaction, that is roughly $117 billion in annual revenue.

The Step Most Candidates Skip

A 15-second sanity check shows commercial awareness, not just arithmetic skills.

6:30 - 6:40 | Transition. Sanity checks. Most candidates build a clean, structured estimate then just stop. That leaves points on the table.

Break Into Management Consulting

Five Essential Sanity Checks

  • Per-capita test. Divide your total by the population. Does the per-person number feel right?
  • Cross-method check. Sketch the opposite approach. If both land in the same ballpark, you're solid.
  • Benchmark against known players. If your total is smaller than one major company's revenue, something is wrong.
  • Order of magnitude check. Step back. Is your number in the right range: thousands, millions, or billions?
  • Analogous comparison. Compare to a similar market you know. Does your number scale proportionally?
6:40 - 7:15 | Five sanity checks. You don't need all five in an interview. Pick two that fit. The per-capita test and benchmark check are the fastest and most powerful combination.

Break Into Management Consulting

Where Candidates Go Wrong

  • Not segmenting at all. Using a single average for 330 million people is lazy and inaccurate. Always segment into 3 to 5 groups.
  • Confusing revenue and volume. If the question asks for dollars, you need volume and price. If it asks for units, do not multiply by price. Clarify if unsure.
  • Skipping the sanity check. You spent three minutes building a structured estimate. Take 15 more seconds to validate it. The sanity check is part of the method.
7:15 - 7:40 | Three common mistakes. Not segmenting, confusing revenue vs volume, and skipping the sanity check. These come up repeatedly in interviews.

Key Takeaways

Market Sizing Mastery

  • The method matters more than the number. Interviewers score your structure and logic. A well-built estimate that is 20-30% off beats a lucky guess every time.
  • Segment into 3-5 groups. Break the population into groups with meaningfully different behaviors. This is the most important move in any market sizing answer.
  • Know when to go top-down vs bottom-up. Top-down from population for revenue and volume. Bottom-up from a single unit for capacity. Both for cross-validation.
  • Always sanity check. Per-capita test, benchmark against known companies, order of magnitude gut check. Fifteen seconds of validation turns a good answer into a great one.
7:40 - 8:00 | Four key takeaways. Method over number. Segment into 3-5 groups. Top-down vs bottom-up. Always sanity check.
