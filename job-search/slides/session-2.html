Job Search Bootcamp

Two Stories About Networking

Transactional

Reach out only when you need something. Copy-paste the same message to 50 people.

"Hi, I saw you work at Google. Can you refer me?"

Relational

Build genuine connections. Lead with curiosity about their work.

"Hi Sarah, your talk on ML infrastructure was fascinating. I'd love to hear more about how your team approaches X."

5:15 - 7:00 | Contrast the two approaches. Let people see themselves in the left column without shame. COACH: "Be honest. Which one feels more like what you've done in the past? Most of us default to the left column because we're anxious and rushing. But look at the difference. One gets ignored. The other starts a conversation." Key point: Networking fails when it's transactional. It works when it's relational.

Who to Contact

Peers

People 1-2 years ahead of you in your target role. Easiest to reach, most relatable advice.

Hiring Managers

The person who'd manage the role you want. Harder to reach, highest impact.

Connectors

People who know many others at the company. Alumni, community leaders, active on LinkedIn.

Job Search Bootcamp

Anatomy of a Great Message

What to Include

  • Personalized hook (why this person, specifically)
  • Credibility line (your relevant background in 1 sentence)
  • Specific ask (15-min call, one question, not "pick your brain")

What to Skip

  • Your full resume or life story
  • Flattery without substance ("I'm a huge fan")
  • Vague asks ("I'd love to connect sometime")
15:15 - 17:00 | Walk through the anatomy. Emphasize brevity and specificity. COACH: "Great outreach has three parts: a personalized hook, a quick credibility line, and a specific ask. That's it. Most bad messages fail because they skip the hook or make the ask too vague."

The Informational Interview

A 5-question framework that leads to referrals

55:00 - 55:15 | Section transition.

Job Search Bootcamp

The 5-Question Framework

  • "What does a typical day look like in your role?" (opens the conversation)
  • "What surprised you most once you were actually in the role?" (reveals insider info)
  • "What's the team culture like day to day?" (tests culture fit)
  • "What skills or experiences made you successful here?" (shows what they value)
  • "Who else would you recommend I talk to?" (the referral ask)
55:15 - 58:00 | Walk through each question. Explain why each one matters. COACH: "Five questions. Memorize them. The first four build rapport and gather real intel. The fifth is the magic one. 'Who else should I talk to?' This is how one conversation turns into three." Key point: Question 5 is how you build a network from a single conversation. Never skip it.

Job Search Bootcamp

Informational Interview Rules

Timing

Keep It to 20 Minutes

Respect their time. If they want to keep talking, they will.

Prep

Research Before You Ask

Know their background. Don't ask questions you could Google.

Listen

Talk Less, Listen More

Your job is to learn, not to pitch yourself. 80% listening.

Follow Up

Thank-You Within 24 Hours

Short, specific, and reference something they said. If there's a role open at their company, mention it: "I noticed [Company] is hiring for X — I'd love your perspective on whether it might be a fit."

58:00 - 60:00 | Walk through the four rules. Quick, punchy. One example each. COACH: "Four rules. Keep it short, do your research, listen more than you talk, and always follow up. The follow-up is where most people drop the ball. A two-sentence thank-you keeps the door open." TRANSITION: "Let's practice. You're about to role-play an informational interview."

Common Mistakes

Mistake Why It Hurts What to Do Instead
Asking for a job directly Puts pressure on them. They retreat. Ask to learn about their experience.
Talking too much about yourself They feel like a captive audience. Share only when asked. Keep it brief.
No follow-up You become forgettable. Send a thank-you within 24 hours.
Skipping question 5 You miss the referral. Always ask who else to talk to.
