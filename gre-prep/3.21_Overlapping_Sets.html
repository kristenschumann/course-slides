Core Formula

Inclusion-exclusion principle

When items belong to multiple groups, adding the groups counts some items twice. Subtract the overlap.

The Formula

$\text{Total} = A + B - \text{Both} + \text{Neither}$

A = count in group A (includes overlap)

B = count in group B (includes overlap)

0:30-2:00 | The inclusion-exclusion formula: Total = A + B minus Both plus Neither. A and B each include the overlap, so we subtract it once to avoid double-counting. Walk through each variable. For three-set problems on the GRE: if you know exactly-2 and need exactly-3, use the relation: sum of sets = exactly-1 + 2(exactly-2) + 3(exactly-3). Combined with total = E1 + E2 + E3, you can solve for any unknown.

Example 1

Finding the overlap

Problem

In a class of 30 students, 18 play soccer and 15 play basketball. If 5 play neither sport, how many play both?

Solution

Step 1: $30 = 18 + 15 - \text{Both} + 5$

Step 2: $30 = 38 - \text{Both}$

Answer: Both $= 8$

Verify

Soccer only: 10 + Basketball only: 7 + Both: 8 + Neither: 5 = 30

3:00-4:00 | The Venn diagram has four regions: A only, B only, Both, and Neither. Strategy: start with what you know (usually Both), then fill in the rest. All four regions must sum to the total.

Example 2

Finding neither

Problem

Of 100 customers, 60 bought coffee, 40 bought tea, and 25 bought both. How many bought neither?

Solution

Step 1: Coffee only: $60 - 25 = 35$

Step 2: Tea only: $40 - 25 = 15$

Step 3: $100 - (35 + 25 + 15)$

Answer: Neither $= 25$

4:00-5:00 | Example 2: finding neither. Click to reveal. Step 1: coffee only = 35. Step 2: tea only = 15. Step 3: total minus all three regions. Answer: 25 bought neither.

Key Relationship

"At least one" = total - neither

At Least One

$\text{At Least One} = \text{Total} - \text{Neither}$

80 people, 10 own neither: At least one $= 80 - 10 = $ $70$

Find Both

$\text{Both} = A + B - \text{At Least One}$

$45 + 30 - 70 = $ $5$

5:00-6:00 | "At least one" equals Total minus Neither. Once you know "at least one," you can find Both by subtracting from A + B. Walk through both relationships.

Example 3

Working with fractions

Problem

Every student studies French, Spanish, or both. If $\frac{3}{4}$ study French and $\frac{2}{3}$ study Spanish, what fraction study both?

Solution

Step 1: Neither $= 0$ (everyone studies at least one)

Step 2: $1 = \frac{3}{4} + \frac{2}{3} - \text{Both}$

Step 3: $\text{Both} = \frac{9}{12} + \frac{8}{12} - \frac{12}{12}$

Answer: $\frac{5}{12}$

6:10-7:30 | Example 3: fractions. Click to reveal. Step 1: Neither is 0, everyone does at least one. Step 2: plug into the formula with 1 as total. Step 3: find common denominator 12. Answer: 5/12.

Example 4

Multi-part problem

Problem

Of 200 employees, 120 speak English, 90 speak Spanish, and 40 speak both. Find only English, only Spanish, and neither.

Solution

Only English: $120 - 40 = $ $80$

Only Spanish: $90 - 40 = $ $50$

Neither: $200 - (80 + 50 + 40)$

Neither = $30$

7:30-8:45 | Example 4: multi-part. Click to reveal. Only English = 80, only Spanish = 50. Neither = 200 minus 170 = 30. Always verify all four regions sum to the total.

Common Trap

A and B already include the overlap

Wrong

Treating A and B as "only" counts.

Soccer: 18 does NOT mean "18 play only soccer."

Right

A includes those in Both.

Soccer: 18 means 18 total, including those who also play basketball.

Always subtract the overlap: A Only $= A - \text{Both}$

8:45-9:30 | Common trap: forgetting that A includes those in Both. When a problem says "18 play soccer," that includes students who play both sports. Always subtract the overlap to get "only A." When the problem has no overlap possible (like comedy vs horror films), use a two-way table instead of a Venn diagram. Fill in row and column totals first, then work inward.

Key Takeaways

Overlapping sets

  • Inclusion-exclusion: Total $= A + B -$ Both $+$ Neither
  • Four Venn diagram regions: A only, B only, Both, Neither (must sum to Total)
  • A only $= A -$ Both. Always subtract the overlap from each group.
  • At least one $=$ Total $-$ Neither. A fast shortcut for many problems.
  • Works with fractions and percents: Use 1 or 100 as the total.
