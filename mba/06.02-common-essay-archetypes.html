Personal Statement

Who Are You at Your Core?

The deep, reflective essay. Stanford's "What matters most to you, and why?" is the canonical example. Schools want self-awareness, values, and authenticity.

Start with real moments from your life and ask "why" repeatedly until you reach the root. The biggest mistake is picking what sounds impressive instead of what's actually true. Deep-dive in Video 7.1.

Core Archetypes

Goals, Fit, and Leadership

Career Goals

Clear, specific goals with a logical past-to-future thread. Address "why MBA, why now?" with real substance.

Why This School

Go beyond rankings. Name specific courses, professors, clubs. Show what you'll contribute, not just gain.

Leadership

Show influence in action, not just a title. Use STAR format with emphasis on Action and what you learned.

4:00 - 6:30 | Three more core archetypes. The career goals essay asks about your professional aspirations, why you need an MBA, and how it fits your trajectory. Remaining Archetypes

Behavioral and Optional Essays

Behavioral Essays

Questions about failure, challenges, or collaboration. For failure questions, pick a real failure, not a humble brag. Focus on your process and what you learned.

Optional/Wildcard

Use optional space to explain gaps or weaknesses, not to add more brags. For wildcard prompts, show genuine personality. If you have nothing meaningful to add, skip it.

6:30 - 8:15 | Two more archetypes. Behavioral essays ask about specific experiences: a failure, a challenge, a time you collaborated. For failure questions, pick a real failure, not a humble brag. Focus on your process and what you learned, not just the outcome. STAR format works well for all of these. Optional and wildcard essays give you space to share something else. Use optional essays to explain gaps in your resume, address weaknesses, or provide important context. Don't use them to add more brags. For wildcard prompts like "What makes you feel alive?", show genuine personality. If you don't have something meaningful to add, skip the optional essay entirely. A pointless one is worse than none.

Watch Out

Top Pitfall for Each Archetype

  • Personal statement: Picking what sounds impressive over what's true.
  • Career goals: Being too vague about function, industry, or company type.
  • Why this school: Generic praise that could apply to any top program.
  • Leadership: Telling about leadership instead of showing it in action.
  • Behavioral: Humble-bragging on failure questions instead of sharing real failure.

Practical Tip

Identify the Archetype First

Before you start writing, classify each prompt. Ask: Is this a personal statement, goals, why-school, leadership, behavioral, or optional? The archetype tells you what approach to take.

Some prompts blend archetypes. A "why this school + career goals" hybrid should address both, but lead with whichever element the prompt emphasizes more.

Key Takeaways

  • Most prompts fit a few archetypes: personal, goals, why-school, leadership, behavioral, optional

  • Goals essays need specificity and a clear past-present-future connection
  • "Why this school" essays demand real research. Name courses, professors, clubs
  • Leadership and behavioral essays use STAR. Emphasize action and learning
  • Optional essays are for explanation, not extra brags
