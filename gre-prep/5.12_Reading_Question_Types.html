Why This Matters

Every question type has a strategy

Not all reading questions are the same. The GRE asks several distinct types, and each requires a slightly different approach. Identify the type first, then apply the right strategy.

Main Idea

Overall purpose of the passage

Detail

What the passage explicitly states

Inference

What the passage implies

0:05 - 0:30 | Set the stage. There are five major question types. Being able to identify the type immediately tells you what strategy to use and what kind of evidence to look for.

Type 1

Main idea questions

How to spot them:

  • "The primary purpose of the passage is to..."
  • "The passage is mainly concerned with..."
  • "Which best states the main idea?"

Strategy

The answer must cover the WHOLE passage, not just one part.

Use your passage map. Think: What is the author trying to do overall?

Traps

Too narrow (only about one paragraph), too broad (beyond what the passage discusses), or wrong focus (right topic, wrong purpose).

Example 1

Evaluating the answer choices

(A) describe the effects of storms on coral populations

Too narrow. This only describes storms, not the full argument about biodiversity and changing scientific views.

(B) explain why coral reefs are declining worldwide

The passage never mentions decline. Outside knowledge, not passage content.

(C) present a revised understanding of what sustains reef biodiversity

Covers the whole passage: old view challenged, new hypothesis presented. This is correct.

(D) argue that storms are beneficial to ocean ecosystems generally

Too broad. The passage discusses reef biodiversity specifically, not all ocean ecosystems.

Answer: (C) It captures the full arc: old assumption challenged, new hypothesis introduced.

2:30 - 4:00 | Walk through elimination. (A) is too narrow — it only describes storms, not the whole argument. (B) introduces outside knowledge — the passage says nothing about decline. (D) is too broad — it generalizes to all ocean ecosystems. (C) captures the full passage arc: old view challenged, new view presented. This is the hallmark of a correct main idea answer.

Type 2

Detail questions

How to spot them:

  • "According to the passage..."
  • "The author states that..."
  • "The passage mentions which of the following?"

Strategy

The answer is directly stated in the passage. Go back and find it.

Use your passage map to locate the relevant section, then read carefully.

Traps

True but not stated (true in general, not in the passage), misquoted (details slightly changed), or wrong section (from a different part).

4:00 - 5:00 | Detail questions have one right answer that paraphrases passage text. Always go back and verify. Don't rely on memory for these.

Type 3

Inference questions

How to spot them:

  • "It can be inferred from the passage that..."
  • "The passage suggests that..."
  • "The author would most likely agree that..."

Strategy

Inference = one small logical step from what's stated.

You should be able to point to specific text that supports your inference.

Traps

Too big a leap (goes far beyond the passage), contradicted (passage rules it out), or possible but unsupported (could be true, but no evidence).

5:00 - 6:00 | Inference questions require one small logical step. The answer must be supported by evidence. The key test: "Must this be true based on what the passage says?"

Example 2

Applying the "must be true" test

(A) were unaware of cognitive biases in decision-making

Too big a leap. They "dismissed" behavioral research, but that does not mean they were unaware of it.

(B) generally considered rational-actor models sufficient for market analysis

Directly supported: they argued rational models "adequately predicted" outcomes. Must be true.

(C) had no experimental data on financial decision-making

Unsupported. The passage says evidence grew by the 1990s, not that none existed before.

(D) believed that individual decisions do not affect markets

Contradicted. They said rational models predicted "aggregate outcomes," not that individual decisions were irrelevant.

Answer: (B) Apply the "must be true" test. The passage states they argued rational models were adequate. (B) is a close paraphrase of that.

6:30 - 8:30 | Walk through the "must be true" test on each choice. (A) is tempting — they dismissed behavioral research — but "dismissed" and "unaware" are different things. You can dismiss something you know about. (C) goes beyond the text — the passage only says evidence grew, not that zero evidence existed. (D) is contradicted by the passage. (B) is a direct paraphrase of "argued that rational-actor models adequately predicted." That's the hallmark of a good inference answer: one small step, fully supported.

Types 4 & 5

Function and strengthen/weaken

Function Questions

"The author mentions X in order to..."

Ask: WHY did the author include this? What role does it play in the argument?

Trap: Describing content instead of purpose.

Strengthen/Weaken

"Which of the following, if true, would most strengthen/weaken?"

Identify the argument, then find new info that supports or undermines it.

Trap: Choosing answers irrelevant to the specific argument.

8:30 - 9:30 | Cover the last two question types. Function questions ask WHY, not WHAT. Strengthen/weaken questions require identifying the argument first, then finding new information that affects its persuasiveness.

Special Format

Select-all-that-apply

Some questions give you three options (A, B, C) where you must select ALL correct answers. There may be one, two, or three correct answers.

Right Approach

Treat each option as a separate true/false question. Check each one independently against the passage.

Wrong Approach

Assuming there's only one correct answer or picking based on what "feels" right without checking the passage.

9:30 - 10:15 | Address the select-all format. Key insight: treat each option independently. Don't assume only one is correct. Check each against the passage as its own true/false question.

Special Format

Select-in-passage questions

The GRE has a unique format where you click on a sentence in the passage itself rather than choosing from (A)-(E) options.

How It Works

The question says: "Select the sentence that..." followed by a description.

Each sentence in the passage becomes clickable. You select the one that matches the description.

Strategy

Re-read each sentence individually and test it against the question stem.

The correct sentence must fully match the description, not just partially relate to the topic.

Tip: These often ask for the sentence that provides evidence, states a conclusion, or introduces a counterargument.

10:15 - 11:15 | Explain the select-in-passage format. This is unique to the GRE — no other major test does this. Students click on a sentence in the passage. The strategy: read each sentence individually and test it against the question stem. Common types ask you to find the sentence that states the author's conclusion, provides evidence for a claim, or introduces a contrasting view.

Example 3

Select-in-passage in action

Passage

[1] Early theories of plate tectonics were met with widespread skepticism in the geological community. [2] Critics argued that no known mechanism could generate sufficient force to move continental masses. [3] The discovery of mid-ocean ridges in the 1960s, however, provided direct physical evidence that new crust was forming and pushing plates apart. [4] Within a decade, plate tectonics had become the unifying framework of modern geology.

Select the sentence that provides the most direct evidence against the critics' position.

Answer: Sentence [3]

The critics said no mechanism could move continents. Sentence [3] describes the discovery of mid-ocean ridges, which demonstrated exactly such a mechanism. Sentence [4] describes an outcome, not evidence.

11:15 - 12:30 | Walk through the select-in-passage example. The question asks for evidence against the critics. Sentence [1] just sets the scene. Sentence [2] IS the critics' position. Sentence [4] is an outcome, not evidence. Sentence [3] is the answer — it describes a discovery that directly contradicted what critics claimed. This is the key: match the sentence's function to what the question asks for.

Practice

Identify the question type

Studies show that bilingual children often outperform monolingual children on tasks requiring attention switching. Researchers believe this advantage stems from the constant practice bilinguals get in switching between languages.

Which of the following must be true based on the passage?

(A) Bilingual education should be mandatory in schools.

(B) All bilingual children are better at attention switching than all monolinguals.

(C) Language switching provides cognitive exercise.

(D) Monolingual children cannot develop attention-switching skills.

(E) Learning a second language always improves cognitive function.

12:30 - 13:30 | Practice problem. Let the student read the passage and try the question. The key skill here is recognizing the question type before diving into the choices. "Must be true" is the signal — this is an inference question.

Practice

Answer walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify the type: "Must be true" signals an inference question. Pick the most cautious, directly supported answer.

(A) Bilingual education should be mandatory

A recommendation — the passage never prescribes policy.

(B) All bilinguals are better than all monolinguals

Uses "all" — the passage says "often," not "always."

(C) Language switching provides cognitive exercise

Directly supported: the advantage "stems from constant practice in switching."

(D) Monolinguals cannot develop these skills

"Cannot" is absolute — the passage never rules this out.

Answer: (C) Eliminate absolutes (B, D, E) and recommendations (A). (C) is a cautious paraphrase of what the passage states.

13:30 - 14:30 | Walk through the answer. First identify the question type — "must be true" means inference. Then eliminate: A is a recommendation, B/D/E use absolutes that go beyond the passage. C is the most cautious, directly supported answer. Strategy connection: recognizing the question type told us exactly what to look for.

Key Takeaways

Types of reading questions

  • Main Idea - the answer must cover the whole passage, not just one paragraph or detail
  • Detail - go back and find the answer in the passage text, don't rely on memory
  • Inference - one small logical step from what's stated, use the "must be true" test
  • Function - ask WHY the author included something, not WHAT it says
  • Select-in-Passage - test each sentence individually against the question stem
  • Identify the type first - then apply the right strategy for that type
