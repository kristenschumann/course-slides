Paired Passages
Agreement vs. Disagreement

0:00 - 0:05 | Title slide. This video teaches how to approach paired passage questions on the GRE.

The Format

Two passages, one set of questions

Some GRE reading questions give you two short passages instead of one. The passages are related, and the questions test whether you can figure out how.

Key Question

Do these passages agree, disagree, or something in between? Get that right, and the questions become much easier.

Relationships

How paired passages relate

Relationship Description What to Look For
Agreement Both make the same point from different angles Shared conclusions, overlapping evidence
Disagreement Opposing positions on the same issue Contrasting claims, different recommendations
Extension One builds on or adds to the other New evidence supporting the same idea
Refinement One modifies or nuances the other's position "Yes, but..." or partial agreement
Shared Theme Different topics connected by a broader theme Parallel structures, analogous situations

Part 2

Agreement and Disagreement

4:00 - 4:05 | Section divider. Now cover the two most common relationships in detail.

Agreement

When passages share a position

Common question stems:

  • "Both passages suggest that..."
  • "Which statement would both authors support?"
  • "What do the passages have in common?"
Trap

Shared topic does not mean shared position. Two passages can discuss the same topic with different conclusions.

Example

Passage A: "Solar energy is becoming cost-effective due to technological advances."

Passage B: "Government subsidies have made solar energy increasingly viable."

Agreement: Both say solar is becoming more viable. Difference: They emphasize different causes.

4:05 - 5:30 | When passages agree, find the shared conclusion. They might use different evidence or emphasis, but the position is the same. Biggest trap: confusing shared topic with shared position.

Disagreement

When passages take opposing positions

Common question stems:

  • "The authors disagree about..."
  • "Unlike Passage A, Passage B argues that..."
  • "The author of B would most likely criticize A for..."
Trap

Passages may agree on some points while disagreeing on others. Find the specific point of disagreement.

Example

Passage A: "Economic growth requires deregulation."

Passage B: "Economic growth requires targeted government investment."

Disagreement: The role of government. Agreement: Both assume growth is desirable.

5:30 - 7:00 | When passages disagree, pinpoint the specific point of disagreement. Ask: What claim does Author A make? Does Author B reject it? What does B believe instead? Passages may agree on some things and disagree on others.

Practice

Remote work and professional relationships

Passage A

The rise of remote work has fundamentally altered the dynamics of professional relationships. Without the serendipitous encounters of the office—chance meetings in hallways, informal conversations by the coffee machine—workers lose opportunities for the spontaneous exchanges that often spark innovation.

Passage B

Predictions about remote work's negative impact on workplace relationships have proven largely unfounded. Research indicates that distributed teams can develop strong bonds through intentional digital communication practices.

The passages differ primarily in their views on

  1. the importance of professional relationships to organizational success
  2. whether effective collaboration requires physical presence
  3. the role of technology in modern communication
  4. the frequency of interactions in remote versus office settings
  5. the challenges facing managers of distributed teams
9:00 - 10:00 | Show the practice problem. Give students 30 seconds to read both passages and identify the relationship before moving on. Ask: Do these passages agree or disagree? About what specifically?

Practice

Answer walkthrough

Correct: B

Passage A argues physical presence IS necessary — spontaneous encounters spark innovation. Passage B argues it is NOT — intentional digital practices can substitute. The core disagreement is about whether physical presence is required.

Why not the others?

  • (A) Both passages assume relationships matter — no disagreement there
  • (C) Neither passage focuses on technology's role in communication
  • (D) Frequency of interactions is not the central debate
  • (E) Neither passage discusses management challenges
Strategy

For paired passages, always identify the relationship first: Do they agree, disagree, or discuss different aspects? Here it's a clear disagreement about one specific claim — whether physical presence is required.

Key Takeaways

Paired passages strategy

  • Read each passage and identify its main point - know what position each author takes before looking at questions
  • Determine the relationship - agree, disagree, extend, refine, or shared theme between the passages
  • Single-passage questions use one passage only - don't bring in evidence from the other passage
  • Comparison questions require both passages - check each passage separately, then compare their positions
  • Don't overstate the relationship - shared topic does not mean shared position, and partial agreement is not full agreement
