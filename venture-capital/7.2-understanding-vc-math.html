Fund-Level Return Metrics

1:15 | Transition into the three metrics LPs care about.

VC Recruiting Course

TVPI: Total value to paid-in capital

The Formula

(Realized Proceeds + Unrealized Value) / Total Capital Called

A fund with $300M called, $200M in realized exits, and $400M in portfolio markups has a TVPI of 2.0x.

The Catch

That $400M in markups is based on the most recent round's pricing, not what anyone has paid in cash. Markups are opinions. A company valued at $500M in its Series C could be worth $500M or $50M. You won't know until there's an exit.

1:15 - 2:00 | LPs care about three numbers. That's it. First: TVPI. Total Value to Paid-In Capital. Realized proceeds plus the marked value of unrealized investments, divided by total capital called. A 2.0x TVPI looks great. But the catch: markups are opinions based on the last round's pricing. TVPI looks great during bull markets and collapses when reality hits.

VC Recruiting Course

DPI and IRR: Cash and time

DPI: Distributions to Paid-In

Only counts cash returned to LPs. Not marks, not paper gains. A fund with 4x TVPI and 0.3x DPI has made LPs feel wealthy on paper but has barely returned anything spendable. DPI is the only metric that doesn't lie.

IRR: Internal Rate of Return

Accounts for the time value of money. A 3x return in three years is spectacular. A 3x in twelve years is mediocre. Top-quartile funds target 20-25% net IRR. The median VC fund returns roughly 1.5x net TVPI and mid-single-digit IRR.

The Power Law

3:30 | Transition into the power law and why it drives everything.

VC Recruiting Course

A small number of deals drive almost all returns

The top 10% of deals in a given fund vintage account for 60-70% of total returns across the entire VC industry. At the individual fund level, one or two deals routinely generate more returns than the other twenty or thirty combined.

Most people think a good VC picks mostly winners. The reality is the opposite. Most startups fail. Many return some capital but nothing meaningful. A few become massive. And "massive" means a single company can return your entire fund, or several times your fund.

3:30 - 4:15 | The power law is the single most important concept in venture capital. Cambridge Associates data shows the top 10% of deals account for 60-70% of total returns industry-wide. At the fund level, it's even more extreme. One or two deals routinely outperform twenty or thirty others combined. Most startups fail. A few become massive. That's not bad management. That's the fundamental nature of startups.

How the power law shapes every decision

Deal Selection

Every investment needs the theoretical potential to return the entire fund. A company that can only become a $200M business doesn't move the needle, even if it's "safe."

Pricing Tolerance

VCs pay what looks like absurd prices for 100x potential. A $100M pre-money for pre-revenue AI sounds insane until you model the $10B exit scenario.

Portfolio Management

Once a GP identifies a winner, they pour resources into it. Follow-on capital, board attention, recruiting help. Winners get more of everything.

Ownership Economics

VC Recruiting Course

One equation starts every deal

Ownership % = Investment Amount / Post-Money Valuation

Invest $10M at a $40M post-money and you own 25%. Simple. But watch what happens to that 25% over time. Each new round dilutes your stake. By exit, your 25% might be 10-12% depending on how many rounds happened and whether you followed on.

Follow-on changes the math

With Follow-On

Reserve 50-60% of fund capital for follow-on. If you follow on pro rata in Series B and C, you maintain 18-20% ownership at exit. Required exit drops from $2.4B to $1.5-1.7B. More achievable.

Return Multiple Formula

Return Multiple = Exit Value x Ownership at Exit / Total Invested Capital. If exit is $1.5B and you own 18% ($25M invested total), proceeds are $270M. That's 10.8x. One deal returns 90% of a $300M fund.

The bar for every deal

If your $300M fund needs $900M in proceeds to hit 3x net, and the power law says your top 3 deals drive 70% of returns, those 3 companies each need to exit at $1.4-1.75 billion.

That's the bar. Every deal you evaluate, the first question is: can this company reach a $1.5B+ exit? If the honest answer is no, the deal doesn't fit the portfolio math regardless of how compelling the team or technology might be.

Why leading rounds matters

The 10% Ownership Floor

For most institutional VCs, 10% at entry is the minimum. Below that, even a massive exit doesn't generate meaningful fund-level returns. If you own 5% of a $1B exit, your $50M proceeds represent only 0.17x on a $300M fund.

Leading vs. Co-Investing

Leading gives you the allocation for meaningful ownership. Participating as a small co-investor gives exposure but not the economics to drive fund returns. At the portfolio construction level, leading is not optional.

VC Recruiting Course

Follow-on capital goes to winners

The best funds are ruthless about where they follow on. They concentrate reserves in their winners, the companies showing breakout growth, strong retention, and clear paths to large outcomes. The rest get no follow-on.

They don't protect ownership in every company equally. The power law expresses itself through capital allocation. If you exercise pro rata at each round, you maintain higher ownership but deploy more capital. The trade-off: follow on to maintain ownership, or accept dilution and conserve capital for new deals.

Everyone else gets no follow-on and ownership dilutes. That's not neglect. It's the power law expressing itself through capital allocation.

Key takeaways

  • DPI is the only fund metric that measures reality. TVPI includes paper gains that may never materialize. In an interview, if someone asks which metric matters most to LPs, the answer is DPI.
  • The power law is the operating math of venture capital. Two or three deals drive 70%+ of your fund's returns. Every investment thesis, pricing decision, and follow-on allocation flows from this reality.
  • "Return the fund" is the minimum bar for every deal. Run the ownership math first. Can this company exit at a valuation that, at your expected ownership, returns the entire fund?
  • Portfolio construction is strategy expressed as arithmetic. Number of bets, check sizes, reserve ratios, and follow-on decisions are all interconnected. Know the fund you're interviewing at.
  • Follow-on capital goes to winners, not evenly across the portfolio. VCs concentrate reserves in their best companies because that's where the returns are.
