Decision Rule

Does order matter?

Scenario Order? Type
Assigning 1st, 2nd, 3rd place Yes Permutation
Choosing a committee No Combination
Creating a password Yes Permutation

Quick Test Swap two selected items. Different result? Permutation. Same result? Combination.

0:30-2:00 | The key question: does changing the order create a different outcome? Walk through the table of scenarios. The swap test: if swapping two items gives something different, it is a permutation. If swapping gives the same thing, it is a combination.

Part 1

Permutations

Arrangements where order matters

2:00-2:10 | Transition to permutation formula.

Permutations

The formula and shortcut

$P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$   Shortcut: multiply $n$ down for $r$ terms

Medal Awards

Gold, silver, bronze from 10 runners

$P(10,3) = 10 \times 9 \times 8 = $ $720$

Letter Arrange

4-letter arrangements from ABCDEFG

$P(7,4) = 7 \times 6 \times 5 \times 4 = $ $840$

2:10-3:30 | Permutation formula: P(n,r) = n!/(n-r)!. The shortcut: just multiply n down for r terms. Medal example: 10 x 9 x 8 = 720. Letter arrangement: 7 x 6 x 5 x 4 = 840. GRE speed tip: the "multiply down" shortcut is faster than computing full factorials. On test day, never calculate n! and (n-r)! separately when you can just count down r terms from n.

Part 2

Combinations

Selections where order does not matter

3:30-3:40 | Transition to combinations.

Combinations

Divide out the duplicates

$C(n, r) = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} = \frac{P(n,r)}{r!}$   Divide permutation count by $r!$ to remove order

Committee

Choose 3 from 10 people

$C(10,3) = \frac{10 \times 9 \times 8}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{720}{6} = $ $120$

Pizza Toppings

Choose 4 toppings from 10

$C(10,4) = \frac{10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7}{4!} = \frac{5040}{24} = $ $210$

3:40-5:30 | Combination formula: C(n,r) = P(n,r) / r!. Why divide by r!? Because {A,B,C} is one committee, but the permutation formula counts ABC, ACB, BAC, BCA, CAB, CBA (3! = 6 arrangements). Committee: 720/6 = 120. Pizza: 5040/24 = 210.

5:30-6:30 | Keyword signals for quick identification. Permutation keywords: arrange, rank, code, different roles. Combination keywords: choose, committee, subset, handshakes.

Example 1

Counting handshakes

Problem

8 people meet. Each person shakes hands with every other person once. How many handshakes?

Solution

A handshake between A and B is the same as B and A, so order does not matter.

$C(8,2) = \frac{8 \times 7}{2 \times 1}$

Answer: $28$ handshakes

6:30-7:10 | Handshake example: a handshake between A and B is the same as B and A, so this is a combination. C(8,2) = 28.

Example 2

Five-card poker hands

Problem

From a 52-card deck, how many 5-card hands are possible?

Solution

Order does not matter (same hand regardless of deal order).

$C(52,5) = \frac{52 \times 51 \times 50 \times 49 \times 48}{5!}$

Answer: $2{,}598{,}960$ hands

7:10-7:50 | Card hands: order does not matter. C(52,5) = 2,598,960. This is a classic combination problem.

Example 3

Combining both principles

Problem

A committee of 5 is chosen from 8 men and 6 women. Must have exactly 3 men and 2 women. How many committees?

Solution

Choose 3 men: $C(8,3) = 56$

Choose 2 women: $C(6,2) = 15$

Both must happen (AND), so multiply.

Answer: $56 \times 15 =$ $840$

7:50-9:20 | Combined problem: choose 3 men from 8 AND 2 women from 6. Each selection is a combination (order does not matter within the group). Then multiply because both selections must happen. 56 x 15 = 840. The GRE often tests this pattern: "at least one" of a type on a committee. For those, use the complement: total committees minus all-of-one-type committees. Much faster than listing every qualifying case.

Key Takeaways

Permutations vs combinations

  • Permutation (order matters) - $P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$, multiply $n$ down for $r$ terms
  • Combination (order does not matter) - $C(n,r) = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$, divide permutation by $r!$
  • The swap test - Swapping creates something different? Permutation. Same thing? Combination
  • Keywords - Arrange/rank/code = permutation. Choose/committee/group = combination
  • Combined problems - Use combinations for each independent selection, then multiply
9:20-10:00 | Summary: permutations for ordered arrangements, combinations for unordered selections. Use the swap test to decide. For combined problems, multiply independent combination counts.
